# Lavatory Smoke

**Catalog ID:** 0245  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Lavatory Smoke — smoke detected in a lavatory, often from a waste-bin fire or electrical fault in transport aircraft, requiring immediate cabin crew action, flight crew emergency procedures, and clear ATC communication. The H130 typically has no airline lavatory; this lesson still teaches ICAO exam English and maps the same radio structure to helicopter-relevant smoke locations such as cabin or baggage areas.

Lavatory smoke is frequently associated with waste container fires. On airliners, cabin crew fight the fire while the flight crew plans priority landing if the fire is not immediately confirmed out. Your English must state nature, location, and intentions without delay.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master the standard phraseology, then always be ready to transfer the template to avionics or cabin smoke — the events you are more likely to see in the H130.

## 📖 Meaning

Lavatory Smoke is the detection of smoke or fire indications in an aircraft lavatory, commonly involving a waste receptacle or electrical component, requiring immediate firefighting by cabin crew, flight crew emergency assessment, possible diversion or priority landing, and clear notification to ATC of the nature and status of the event.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Fumaça no toalete / Lavatory Smoke.

Detecção de indicações de fumaça ou fogo em um toalete da aeronave, comumente envolvendo o recipiente de lixo ou um componente elétrico, exigindo combate imediato ao fogo pela tripulação de cabine, avaliação de emergência pela tripulação de voo, possível desvio ou pouso prioritário, e notificação clara ao ATC sobre a natureza e o estado do evento.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When lavatory smoke occurs, the crew should:

- coordinate with cabin crew for immediate firefighting and status reports
- apply the applicable QRH/AFM smoke or fire procedures
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday based on whether the fire is controlled
- request priority landing or diversion if fire is not confirmed extinguished
- report nature (lavatory smoke), location (lavatory), intentions; souls/fuel when asked
- for H130 training: practice the same structure for cabin smoke without a lavatory

Lavatory smoke reporting commonly includes:

- smoke in the lavatory / lavatory smoke warning
- fire extinguished or fire continuing
- request priority landing / diversion
- souls on board and fuel remaining
- request emergency services on arrival

**Important:** If the fire is out and verified, you may continue with caution or still divert depending on procedure and damage. If the fire is not confirmed out, land immediately. On the ICAO speaking test, use "lavatory smoke" correctly; in H130 line flying, prefer cabin/avionics smoke scenarios as your primary mental model while retaining exam vocabulary.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to lavatory smoke reports to:

- provide priority handling for possible onboard fire
- alert fire and rescue for arrival
- request whether the fire is extinguished or continuing
- obtain souls and fuel
- coordinate rapid landing clearance and taxiway support for evacuation if needed

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: cabin crew reports smoke in the aft lavatory. They fight the waste-bin fire with the appropriate extinguisher.

You transmit while assessing:

Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, lavatory smoke, cabin crew fighting fire, request priority vectors for precautionary landing.

Cabin crew then reports fire extinguished, smoke clearing.

You update:

ANAC123, lavatory fire extinguished, smoke clearing, still request priority landing for inspection.

If the fire were not out:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, lavatory smoke, fire not extinguished, request immediate vectors to land.

For H130 reality, replace "lavatory" with "cabin" or "avionics" — keep the urgency logic identical.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan lavatory smoke, say if fire is under control.
2. Approach: ANAC123, priority handling approved, expect straight-in runway two seven.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
4. Tower: ANAC123, fire services standing by, advise if evacuation required.
5. Approach: ANAC123, report fire extinguished or continuing.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, lavatory smoke, request priority landing.
2. Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, lavatory fire not extinguished, request immediate vectors to land.
3. ANAC123, lavatory fire extinguished, smoke clearing, continuing priority approach.
4. ANAC123, souls one four zero, fuel one hour forty minutes.
5. ANAC123, request emergency services on arrival, no evacuation planned at this time.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Lavatory smoke is exam vocabulary with real airline blood behind it — waste-bin fires are classic.

Your job on the radio is status honesty: is the fire out, or not? That single fact chooses Pan Pan versus Mayday and chooses precautionary priority versus immediate emergency landing.

Do not use vague "we had a small problem in the back." Say lavatory smoke. Say fire extinguished or fire continuing. Say what you need from ATC.

Then — for the aircraft you fly — transfer the skill. The H130 has no lavatory aisle. It has a cabin the size of a closet and avionics in your face. Cabin smoke and avionics smoke are your operational twins. Same oxygen priority. Same checklist discipline. Same clear English.

Brazilian examiners may ask lavatory smoke even in a helicopter oral. Answer with standard ICAO phrasing, then show you understand applicability to your type.

Status. Urgency level. Landing plan. No ego. No delay.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Cabin crew reports smoke in the lavatory. What do you say to ATC, and how does urgency change if the fire is not extinguished?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would coordinate with cabin crew, apply procedures, and advise ATC of lavatory smoke with our intentions. If the fire were being fought or already extinguished, I would likely declare Pan Pan and request priority landing for precautionary landing and inspection. If the fire were not extinguished, I would declare Mayday and request immediate vectors to the nearest suitable airport. I would provide souls and fuel when asked and update whether the fire is extinguished or continuing. On a helicopter without a lavatory, I would use the same structure for cabin smoke.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "smoke in the bathroom" instead of standard "lavatory smoke."  
  ✔ Use ICAO-standard "lavatory smoke" in exams and international phraseology.

- ❌ Keeping Pan Pan when the fire is clearly not under control.  
  ✔ Upgrade to Mayday if fire continues or safe flight is in doubt.

- ❌ Failing to update ATC when the fire is extinguished.  
  ✔ Report fire extinguished, smoke clearing, and revised intentions.

- ❌ Memorizing lavatory smoke without linking to H130 cabin/avionics smoke.  
  ✔ Keep exam term + operational transfer ready.

**Word Stress**

- Lavatory → LAV-uh-tor-ee (stress on LAV)
- Smoke → SMOHK
- Extinguished → ek-STING-gwisht (stress on STING)
- Priority → pry-OR-ih-tee

Practice:

Together:

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**LAV** — **L**ocate the smoke (lavatory/cabin), **A**ssess fire out or not, **V**ector priority — Pan Pan if controlled, Mayday if not.
