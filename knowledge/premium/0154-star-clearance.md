# STAR Clearance

**Catalog ID:** 0154  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers STAR Clearance — the ATC authorization for an arriving aircraft to fly a Standard Terminal Arrival Route procedure into the destination airport terminal area.

The STAR defines the lateral routing from the en-route structure to the terminal area. A STAR clearance tells the crew which published procedure to follow.

At Brazilian airports including Congonhas, São Paulo, Curitiba, and Florianópolis, STAR clearances are routine IFR arrival instructions that pilots must brief, load, and execute correctly.

Crucially: a STAR clearance authorizes the lateral routing but does not automatically authorize descent to the STAR's published altitude restrictions. Altitude clearances remain under ATC control unless Descend Via is explicitly issued.

Understanding STAR Clearance — and the concept behind it — is essential for ICAO Level 4 speaking and professional IFR operations.

## 📖 Meaning

A STAR Clearance is an ATC authorization for the arriving aircraft to fly the Standard Terminal Arrival Route procedure, following the published lateral routing from the en-route structure to the terminal area of the destination airport, with altitude descent remaining under separate ATC clearance unless Descend Via is specified.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorização do controle de tráfego aéreo para a aeronave em chegada voar o procedimento de chegada padrão para área terminal, seguindo o roteamento lateral publicado da estrutura de rota até a área terminal do aeródromo de destino, com o descenso de altitude permanecendo sob autorização separada do controle, salvo se Descend Via for especificado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives a STAR clearance, they should:

- confirm the STAR name and transition in the readback
- load the STAR into the FMS or GPS
- brief the lateral routing, altitude restrictions, and speed constraints published on the chart
- note that published altitude restrictions are not yet authorized — only lateral routing is
- maintain the last ATC-assigned altitude until a descent clearance is issued
- comply with any STAR speed constraints when authorized to descend via the procedure

STAR clearance operations commonly include:

- clearance issued by en-route control during cruise
- STAR name and destination runway specified
- separate descent instruction issued alongside or after the STAR clearance
- speed reductions initiated when approaching the terminal area

**Important:** A STAR clearance is lateral routing authorization only. Until ATC assigns a descent level or issues a Descend Via STAR clearance, the crew must maintain their cruising altitude. Never confuse a STAR clearance with descent authorization.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers assign STAR clearances to:

- standardize the routing of arrivals from multiple en-route fixes into the terminal area
- reduce controller communication load through pre-coded routing designators
- coordinate with the approach sector by assigning a known, defined arrival route
- sequence multiple arrivals from different sectors onto a common arrival flow
- ensure consistent separation between aircraft on the same arrival procedure

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at flight level one two zero inbound to Florianópolis from Curitiba in your H130.

Control transmits:

ANAC123, cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, descend and maintain flight level eight zero.

You load the IDAMO ONE STAR in the GPS. You brief the routing with your co-pilot: the waypoints from the en-route sector to the approach fix.

You respond:

Cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, descend and maintain flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

You begin descent to flight level eight zero, following the IDAMO ONE lateral routing.

At flight level eight zero, you level off. ATC clears you lower through sequential altitude assignments as you approach the terminal area.

At the approach fix, the STAR is complete. The approach phase begins.

The STAR delivered you to the doorstep of the approach. ATC managed your descent through it.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

A STAR clearance and a descent clearance are not the same thing.

This confuses pilots from every country. Not only in Brazil.

The STAR gives you the road.

ATC gives you the altitude.

When you receive "cleared IDAMO ONE arrival" — you know where to go.

But you stay at your cruise altitude until ATC assigns a descent level.

The published altitude restrictions on the STAR? Those are for reference and planning.

They do not become active until ATC specifically authorizes Descend Via — which is a more powerful instruction that covers both routing and altitude profile simultaneously.

In Brazilian IFR practice, most STAR clearances come with a separate descent instruction: cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, descend and maintain flight level eight zero.

Read back both. Fly both. Precisely.

Brief the full STAR before top of descent. Know every waypoint. Know every restriction.

That way, when ATC issues a crossing clearance for one of the STAR fixes, you already have the answer in your head.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What does a STAR clearance authorize and what does it not authorize?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A STAR clearance authorizes the crew to fly the published lateral routing of the Standard Terminal Arrival Route from the en-route structure to the terminal area of the destination. It does not automatically authorize descent to the published altitude restrictions on the STAR. The crew must maintain their last assigned altitude until ATC issues a separate descent clearance. The altitude restrictions on the STAR plate become active only when ATC issues a Descend Via clearance, which is a specific instruction authorizing both the lateral routing and the published altitude profile simultaneously. Without that instruction, ATC controls descent through sequential altitude assignments.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Descending to the first STAR restriction immediately after receiving the STAR clearance.  
  ✔ Maintain cruise altitude until ATC issues a descent clearance or Descend Via.

- ❌ Not loading the STAR before the en-route descent begins.  
  ✔ Load the STAR as soon as clearance is received — well before top of descent.

- ❌ Not reading back the STAR name and runway designation.  
  ✔ Always include both in the readback: cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four.

- ❌ Continuing on the STAR when ATC cancels it and assigns vectors.  
  ✔ Acknowledge immediately: STAR cancelled, radar vectors, descend and maintain six thousand, ANAC123.

**Pronunciation:** kleerd eye-DAH-moh WUN uh-RY-val

**Word Stress**

- Cleared → kleerd
- IDAMO → eye-DAH-moh (three syllables, stress on second; Brazilian fix name)
- ONE → WUN
- Arrival → uh-RY-val (stress on second syllable)

Practice:

Cleared... IDAMO ONE... Arrival.

Together:

Cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, descend and maintain flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

Speak the STAR name and number as a unit — IDAMO ONE — clearly and without rushing.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Florianópolis Control transmits: "ANAC123, cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, descend and maintain flight level eight zero."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete clearance.

**Your second task**

At flight level eight zero, the IDAMO ONE plate shows a restriction of six thousand at the next waypoint. May you descend to six thousand? What do you need from ATC first?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would respond: Cleared IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, descend and maintain flight level eight zero, ANAC123. At flight level eight zero I may not descend to six thousand based on the STAR chart restriction alone. My cleared altitude is flight level eight zero. To descend further I need ATC to issue a new altitude clearance — either a specific level, or a Descend Via clearance. I would maintain flight level eight zero and request or await the further descent clearance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

The STAR clearance is your route into the terminal area.

It shows you where to go.

ATC tells you how low to fly there.

Know the difference. Fly both precisely.

Brief the STAR before top of descent. Know every waypoint and restriction.

When ATC clears you lower — descend. When ATC holds you — hold.

That coordination between your navigation and ATC altitude management is professional IFR flying at its best.
