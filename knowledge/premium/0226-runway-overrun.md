# Runway Overrun

**Catalog ID:** 0226  
**Category:** Airport Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Runway Overrun — an event in which an aircraft continues past the end of the runway during takeoff or landing, departing the paved surface beyond the departure or arrival threshold.

Runway overrun is a specific type of runway excursion. Excursion is the broader term for leaving the runway sideways or longitudinally. Overrun means going beyond the runway end — past the stop end on landing, or past the departure end on a rejected or continued takeoff that fails to get airborne in time.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations in Brazil, overrun risk is lower than for fixed-wing jets because of shorter landing distances and the ability to stop or lift off vertically. Still, academy crews must understand the concept: contaminated runways, late go-around decisions, rejected takeoffs, and runway end safety areas (RESA) appear in briefings, incident reports, and ICAO speaking scenarios.

Understanding how to discuss runway overrun, report related hazards, and describe prevention in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 radiotelephony and Part 3 speaking proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

A Runway Overrun is an occurrence in which an aircraft fails to stop or become airborne before reaching the end of the runway, and continues beyond the runway end onto the overrun area, RESA, or terrain beyond the paved surface, during landing, rejected takeoff, or takeoff roll.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When discussing or managing runway overrun risk, the crew should:

- calculate landing and takeoff performance against available runway length before every runway operation
- account for wet, contaminated, or sloping runway conditions that increase stopping distance
- brief go-around and rejected takeoff decision points before the approach or takeoff roll
- identify RESA, stopway, and clearway on the airport chart
- report runway surface conditions that could contribute to overrun risk
- if an overrun occurs or is imminent, declare the emergency and advise ATC of position and intentions

Runway overrun risk factors include:

- long or fast landing with late touchdown
- contaminated or wet runway with reduced braking
- rejected takeoff initiated late or at high speed
- incorrect performance calculation or wrong runway length assumed
- tailwind component beyond limits
- runway end obstacles or short RESA

**Important:** Runway overrun is not the same as runway excursion in general. Excursion includes veering off the side. Overrun is specifically beyond the runway end. In ICAO speaking and incident discussion, use the precise term. For the H130, if runway length is marginal for a ground run, prefer a vertical takeoff or landing profile when performance and procedures allow — do not force a long ground roll into a short runway.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers and airport authorities address runway overrun to:

- protect aircraft that may not stop before the runway end
- manage rescue and firefighting response beyond the stop end
- close or inspect overrun areas after an event
- issue runway condition and braking action information that affects stopping distance
- coordinate go-around and missed approach when landing is unsafe

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on final to runway one seven Left at Congonhas in the H130 after a training circuit. The runway is wet. Tower reports Medium braking action.

You brief: touchdown in the touchdown zone, firm collective reduction, be ready to go around if the approach is unstable. You do not plan to use the full length for a long float.

On short final the approach becomes unstable — high and fast. You go around. Tower acknowledges. You climb away cleanly.

That decision prevented a potential overrun scenario. Wet runway, late touchdown, and residual energy beyond the stop end are how overruns begin. The go-around ended the risk before the wheels committed to a long roll.

Later, in the debrief, you discuss overrun versus side excursion: today the threat was longitudinal energy past the end, not lateral drift.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, runway one seven Left, braking action Medium, wet, caution possible overrun risk for long landing.
2. Tower: ANAC123, go around, aircraft overrun reported beyond runway two eight, runway closed.
3. Approach: ANAC123, expect runway one seven Right, runway one seven Left closed due to overrun inspection.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Overrun is energy past the end of the pavement.

Landing long, landing fast, or rejecting late turns runway length into a trap. The RESA is a last buffer — not a planned stopping zone.

Distinguish the vocabulary. If the aircraft leaves the side of the runway, say excursion or veer-off. If it goes past the stop end, say overrun. Precision matters in reports and ICAO speaking.

In the H130, you often have options fixed-wing crews do not: hover, vertical landing, short-field technique. Use them when runway length or contamination makes a long ground roll unsafe. Do not treat every runway like a jet runway.

Performance first. Brief the go-around. Touch down where you planned. If the approach is unstable, go around early — not after you have floated into the last third of the runway.

Wet Brazilian runways after afternoon storms are classic overrun setups for any aircraft that lands long. Respect braking action reports. They exist because stopping distance grows when the surface is compromised.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Explain the difference between a runway overrun and a runway excursion, and describe how you would prevent an overrun on a wet runway.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A runway excursion is any departure from the runway surface, including sideways veer-off or going beyond the end. A runway overrun is specifically continuing past the runway end during landing or takeoff. To prevent an overrun on a wet runway, I would check landing performance against available length, note braking action and contamination, brief a firm touchdown in the touchdown zone, and commit to a go-around if the approach is unstable or I am floating long. I would not attempt to salvage a high-energy landing into the last portion of the runway.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using "runway excursion" and "runway overrun" as if they mean exactly the same thing.  
  ✔ Overrun is beyond the end; excursion is the broader category including side departure.

- ❌ Continuing an unstable approach because "the helicopter can stop quickly."  
  ✔ Go around early. Short stopping distance is not a reason to land long and fast.

- ❌ Ignoring Medium or Poor braking action on a wet runway.  
  ✔ Factor braking action into landing distance and go-around decision points.

- ❌ Treating the RESA as usable landing distance in performance planning.  
  ✔ Plan to stop on the runway. RESA is emergency margin, not declared LDA.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are on final to a wet runway at Florianópolis. Braking action is reported Medium. At one hundred feet you are still high and fast.

**Your first task**

What do you say to tower as you decide not to continue the landing?

**Your second task**

After landing on the next approach, you see another aircraft has gone past the stop end into the overrun area. Tower has not yet closed the runway. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around. Second task: ANAC123, traffic appears to have overrun beyond the runway end, requesting confirmation runway status — to alert ATC immediately without assuming the runway remains usable. I would not land on a runway with an unresolved overrun until ATC confirms the runway is clear or assigns another runway.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Overrun is past the end. Excursion is leaving the runway. Know both words. Use the right one.

Prevention is performance, briefing, and an early go-around — not hope and the last meters of pavement.

In the H130, use helicopter options when a long ground roll is the wrong tool. Wet runway, late touchdown, and residual energy are the classic chain.

Stop on the runway you planned. Treat RESA as mercy, not method.

That is how you keep runway end safety from becoming an incident report.
