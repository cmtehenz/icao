# Plan Continuation Bias

**Catalog ID:** 0473  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Plan Continuation Bias — the tendency to continue with an original plan despite changing conditions that make the plan unsafe or no longer appropriate.

Plan continuation bias is sometimes called "get-there-itis." It is the pressure to finish what was started: the approach, the routing, the schedule, the passenger expectation.

In H130 operations — EMS, offshore, and charter work with tight slots — the bias is strong. Weather drops. Fuel margins shrink. An approach becomes unstable. The original plan still feels like the path of least social resistance.

Understanding how to break plan continuation bias in ICAO-standard English — including diversion requests, go-around calls, and clear intention statements — is essential for Part 1, Part 2, and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Plan Continuation Bias is a human-factors tendency to persist with a previously selected course of action even when new information indicates that continuing is no longer safe, efficient, or consistent with procedures and minima.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Viés de continuação do plano / Plan Continuation Bias.

Tendência de fatores humanos de persistir em um curso de ação previamente escolhido mesmo quando novas informações indicam que continuar não é mais seguro, eficiente ou compatível com procedimentos e mínimos.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When plan continuation bias may be driving the crew, they should:

- re-evaluate the plan against current weather, fuel, traffic, and aircraft status
- set hard gates: minima, stable-approach criteria, fuel reserves, duty limits
- verbalize the decision to change plan early, before options disappear
- request diversion, holding, or go-around without apologizing for safety
- brief the new plan as deliberately as the original one

Plan-change triggers include:

- weather below minima or rapidly deteriorating
- unstable approach criteria not met
- fuel approaching diversion or reserve triggers
- system malfunction that changes performance or landing capability
- ATC delay that invalidates the fuel or weather plan

**Important:** The original plan was correct when it was made. Conditions change. Continuing a dead plan is not professionalism — it is bias. Decide against the plan while you still have altitude, fuel, and options.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support safe plan changes when they:

- accept go-around and diversion requests without delay
- provide weather, runway, and traffic updates that force re-evaluation
- offer holding or alternate routing when the arrival plan collapses
- ask for intentions when a crew appears committed beyond safe parameters
- coordinate alternate aerodromes and approach sequencing quickly

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on final to Florianópolis in the H130. The approach is unstable — high and fast. Passengers are connecting. You feel the pull to "make it work."

Your first officer calls unstable.

You decide:

ANAC123, going around, request vectors for second approach.

Tower: ANAC123, go around, fly runway heading, climb three thousand, contact approach.

You respond:

ANAC123, going around, runway heading, climbing three thousand.

The schedule can wait. The plan just changed — correctly.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, go around, fly runway heading, climb three thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions, weather now below minima.
3. Radar: ANAC123, cleared to divert Navegantes, climb and maintain five thousand.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you are continuing, visibility reported decreasing.
5. Control: ANAC123, hold as published, expect further delay twenty minutes.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around, runway heading, climbing three thousand.
2. ANAC123, diverting to Navegantes, requesting weather and approach.
3. ANAC123, climbing five thousand, diverting Navegantes.
4. ANAC123, discontinuing approach, request alternate.
5. ANAC123, entering hold, requesting delay information and fuel considerations noted.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Plan continuation bias whispers: "Just a little more."

A little more sink rate. A little more tailwind. A little more hope on the weather.

In the H130, set gates before the flight gets emotional. If the gate is missed, the plan ends. No negotiation in the flare.

Passengers remember a late arrival. They do not thank you for a forced landing that almost worked.

Change the plan out loud. Bias hates daylight.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are on final, unstable, with passenger pressure to land. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would execute a go-around and communicate clearly: ANAC123, going around, request vectors for another approach. Passenger schedule is not a clearance. If weather or fuel then makes a second approach unwise, I would request diversion. The decision is based on gates, not on how far we have already come.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing an unstable approach because "we are almost there."  
  ✔ Go around at the gate — distance flown does not buy a landing.

- ❌ Delaying the diversion call until fuel options are poor.  
  ✔ Divert when the plan fails, while fuel and alternates remain healthy.

- ❌ Softening the go-around call with unnecessary explanation.  
  ✔ State the action first: going around, then intentions.

- ❌ Keeping the old brief after deciding to divert.  
  ✔ Rebrief the new aerodrome, approach, and fuel plan immediately.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**STOP** — **S**et hard gates before arrival, **T**est the plan against new facts, **O**pen a new plan early, **P**ronounce the change to ATC without delay.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On final to Florianópolis you are high and fast. Connecting passengers are waiting. Your first officer calls unstable.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

After the go-around, weather drops below minima. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around, request vectors for second approach. Second task: ANAC123, weather below minima, request diversion to Navegantes. Continuing because we are close is plan continuation bias. The professional call ends the old plan while options remain.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Plan continuation bias turns momentum into risk.

The antidote is pre-briefed gates and early, plain language to ATC.

In the H130, arriving late on a new plan beats arriving wrong on the old one.

When the facts change, change the plan — and say so.
