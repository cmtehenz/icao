# Slope Landing

**Catalog ID:** 0072  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers slope landing — landing on inclined terrain.

Slope Landing requires technique to manage collective, cyclic, and tail rotor clearance on uneven ground.

In the H130, slope landings are used in remote operations, military support, and emergency landings away from prepared surfaces.

You must assess slope angle, wind, and rollover risk before committing, then inform ATC.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 operational communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Slope Landing is a helicopter landing on inclined terrain, requiring technique to maintain control during touchdown and shutdown.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pouso em declive.

Pouso de helicóptero em terreno inclinado, exigindo técnica para manter controle durante o toque e o desligamento.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting a Slope Landing, the pilot should:

- assess slope angle — do not exceed aircraft limits in the flight manual
- approach upslope when possible with downhill side clear
- lower collective smoothly on the upslope skid or gear first
- inform ATC of landing intentions at non-standard sites
- secure the aircraft before shutting down on a slope

Slope Landing reporting commonly includes:

- landing slope or landing uneven terrain
- position and description of landing site
- report safe on the ground
- request go-around if slope is unsafe

**Important:** Slope angles beyond flight manual limits risk dynamic rollover. Always assess before committing.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Slope Landing reports to:

- provide traffic advisories in the area
- coordinate search and rescue if emergency landing
- maintain awareness of non-standard landing sites
- request position reports
- prepare for possible delay or go-around reports

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are conducting a remote site survey near Navegantes in your H130.

The only suitable landing point is a moderate grass slope facing northwest.

You orbit, assess the slope at approximately ten degrees, and call:

Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, landing slope remote site eight miles southeast of the airport, will report safe on the ground.

You approach upslope, touch down smoothly on the uphill skid, and lower collective gradually.

You report safe on the ground and secure the aircraft.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger slope landing, no traffic reported, report safe on the ground.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say slope angle if able and souls on board.
3. Tower: ANAC123, report go-around if unable to land safely.
4. Approach: ANAC123, wind northwest at one zero knots at the airport.
5. Approach: ANAC123, report departure when clear of the slope site.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Landing slope remote site eight miles southeast, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, landing slope, slope approximately ten degrees, will report safe on the ground.
3. ANAC123, unable slope landing, slope too steep, going around.
4. Tower, ANAC123, safe on the ground slope landing complete.
5. ANAC123, departing slope site, will report clear of the area.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Slope landing is about patience on the collective.

Rush the touchdown and you risk dynamic rollover.

The sequence:

Assess angle. Approach upslope. Upslope skid first. Lower collective slowly.

Never exceed your flight manual slope limit.

Never land with tail rotor toward uphill slope without clearance.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "pouso em rampa" on frequency.

Use landing slope — ICAO standard phraseology.

In the H130, know your maximum slope angle cold.

If in doubt — go around and find flatter ground.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What are the risks of slope landing and what would you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Risks include dynamic rollover if slope exceeds limits, tail rotor contact with terrain, and difficulty securing the aircraft. I would assess slope angle before landing, inform ATC of landing slope with position, approach upslope when possible, and report safe on the ground or go-around if the slope is unsafe.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Landing on slopes beyond flight manual limits.  
  ✔ Know maximum slope angle and go around if exceeded.

- ❌ Saying "pouso em inclinação" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use landing slope.

- ❌ Lowering collective too quickly on touchdown.  
  ✔ Lower collective smoothly — upslope contact first.

- ❌ Ignoring tail rotor clearance on uphill slope.  
  ✔ Ensure tail rotor clearance from terrain and obstacles.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

The only landing site available is a fifteen-degree grass slope near Joinville.

**Your first task**

Your flight manual limit is twelve degrees. What do you do?

**Your second task**

If the slope is within limits, what do you transmit to Approach?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If slope exceeds limits, I would go around and find alternative landing site. If within limits, I would transmit: landing slope remote site near Joinville, ANAC123, will report safe on the ground. I would approach upslope and touch down smoothly on the uphill skid first.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Slope landing demands respect for the angle.

Know your limit.

Approach upslope.

Collective down slowly.

Dynamic rollover is real.

Tell ATC where you are.

Go around if the slope is wrong.

Train on gentle slopes before you need a remote site.

Speak clearly. Land with patience.
