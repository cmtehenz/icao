# Maximum Altitude Capability

**Catalog ID:** 0178  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Maximum Altitude Capability — the highest altitude an aircraft can achieve or sustain under specific conditions of weight, temperature, and available power.

This concept is critical for flight planning and for communicating performance limitations to ATC.

For multi-engine jets, maximum altitude capability defines the service ceiling and is used in flight planning for route selection, alternate selection, and fuel calculations.

For the H130 helicopter, maximum altitude capability is acutely practical. The H130 is capable of high-altitude operations, but that capability is directly dependent on density altitude, weight, and whether all engines are available. The difference between All Engines Operating (AEO) ceiling and One Engine Inoperative (OEI) ceiling can be thousands of feet — and must be known before any flight into elevated terrain or high-altitude airspace.

Understanding how to state and discuss maximum altitude capability in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 performance knowledge.

## 📖 Meaning

Maximum Altitude Capability is the highest altitude at which an aircraft can maintain level flight under specified conditions — including current weight, atmospheric temperature, and engine configuration — representing either the absolute ceiling where rate of climb reaches zero, or the service ceiling defined by a minimum rate of climb threshold.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Altitude máxima de operação / Teto máximo operacional.

A altitude mais elevada na qual uma aeronave consegue manter voo nivelado sob condições especificadas — incluindo peso atual, temperatura atmosférica e configuração de motores — representando o teto absoluto onde a taxa de subida chega a zero, ou o teto de serviço definido por um limite mínimo de taxa de subida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When discussing maximum altitude capability with ATC, the crew should:

- state their maximum altitude capability when unable to comply with an assigned altitude
- advise ATC when cruising altitude approaches or exceeds performance ceiling
- request a lower altitude if assigned level is above current maximum capability
- provide ATC with the specific altitude they can sustain when performance is limited
- advise ATC immediately if performance degrades further in flight

In H130 operations, maximum altitude capability considerations include:

- AEO ceiling under current weight and ISA conditions
- OEI ceiling — the altitude sustainable on one engine
- density altitude effect on performance at Brazilian highland airports
- weight reduction through fuel burn and its effect on ceiling over time
- pilot's responsibility to brief and monitor performance against assigned altitudes

**Important:** In the H130, the AEO ceiling can be well above any assigned flight level. But the OEI ceiling may be far lower. Every flight above OEI ceiling is implicitly dependent on both engines operating. Know both numbers. Fly accordingly.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers request maximum altitude capability to:

- determine whether an aircraft can comply with an altitude assignment
- manage separation between aircraft at different performance levels
- accommodate aircraft that cannot achieve high-altitude routing
- facilitate block altitude assignments for performance-limited aircraft
- support emergency handling when performance is suddenly degraded

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are flying your H130 from Curitiba to São Paulo at flight level seven five. ATC requires you to climb to flight level nine five for traffic separation.

You check performance. At current weight, density altitude, and outside air temperature, your maximum altitude capability is flight level eight zero.

You advise:

ANAC123, unable flight level nine five, maximum altitude capability flight level eight zero, request flight level eight zero.

Radar responds:

ANAC123, roger, maintain flight level eight zero, traffic will pass on your right.

You respond:

Maintain flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

Later, after fuel burn has reduced your weight, you check performance again. Your ceiling has risen.

You call:

ANAC123, now able flight level nine five, request climb.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, climb and maintain flight level one one zero, confirm able.
2. Radar: ANAC123, advise maximum altitude for current conditions.
3. Control: ANAC123, traffic requires your altitude, confirm able to accept flight level nine zero.
4. Radar: ANAC123, roger, maintain flight level eight zero, advise when able higher.
5. Control: ANAC123, block altitude flight level seven five to nine five approved pending your performance confirmation.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Maximum altitude capability is not a fixed number. It changes.

It changes as you burn fuel. It changes as the temperature rises. It changes if an engine fails.

Pilots who memorize a single ceiling number and stop thinking are the ones who accept altitude assignments they cannot safely sustain.

In the H130, the pre-flight performance calculation must produce two numbers: AEO ceiling and OEI ceiling.

If the route takes you above the OEI ceiling — you are committed to both engines performing perfectly. Plan accordingly. Know the diversion options if one fails.

When ATC assigns an altitude above your capability, say so immediately. There is no shame in stating a limitation. There is significant danger in accepting an altitude you cannot sustain.

ANAC123, unable flight level one one zero, maximum altitude capability flight level eight zero.

Simple. Clear. Professional.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC assigns you flight level one one zero. Your H130 performance calculation shows a maximum altitude capability of flight level eight zero under current conditions. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would advise ATC that I cannot comply with the assigned altitude: ANAC123, unable flight level one one zero, maximum altitude capability flight level eight zero, request flight level eight zero. I would provide the specific altitude I can sustain rather than just declining the clearance. If ATC asked for confirmation of the limitation, I would explain: maximum capability due to weight and temperature conditions, flight level eight zero confirmed. I would not accept flight level one one zero and attempt the climb if performance does not support it.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting an altitude assignment above performance capability without advising ATC.  
  ✔ Advise immediately: ANAC123, unable, maximum altitude capability flight level eight zero.

- ❌ Stating only "unable" without providing the altitude you can sustain.  
  ✔ Always provide the maximum capability altitude so ATC can plan separation.

- ❌ Using only AEO ceiling and ignoring OEI ceiling in H130 flight planning.  
  ✔ Brief both AEO and OEI ceilings. Know the difference before every flight.

- ❌ Not updating ATC when performance improves and higher altitude becomes available.  
  ✔ When able to climb higher after fuel burn, advise ATC: ANAC123, now able flight level nine five, request climb.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You depart Curitiba in your H130 at maximum takeoff weight on a warm afternoon. ATC assigns you flight level nine five. Your pre-flight performance calculation showed an AEO ceiling of flight level eight five and an OEI ceiling of flight level four zero under those conditions.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC regarding the altitude assignment?

**Your second task**

ATC accepts flight level eight five and asks: "What is your OEI ceiling?" Why is this question relevant, and what do you answer?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

For the first task: ANAC123, unable flight level nine five, maximum altitude capability flight level eight five, request flight level eight five. For the second task: ATC is asking because if an engine fails above my OEI ceiling, I must immediately drift down. My answer: ANAC123, OEI ceiling approximately flight level four zero, current conditions. ATC needs this to plan contingency routing and separation in case of an engine failure above that level.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Maximum altitude capability is a number with consequences.

Above it, you cannot sustain level flight. If you accept that altitude, you are betting on conditions not changing — on no engine failure, no temperature rise, no weight error.

Professional pilots know both their AEO ceiling and their OEI ceiling.

They brief them before every flight. They advise ATC when they cannot comply. They update ATC when conditions improve.

The ceiling is not a limitation to hide. It is a number to share so ATC can keep you and every other aircraft separated and safe.

Know the number. State it clearly. Fly within it.
