# Hazard Identification

**Catalog ID:** 0493  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Hazard Identification — the CRM and SMS skill of recognizing conditions, objects, or events that can cause harm before they become an incident.

You cannot manage a risk you never named. Hazard identification is the first link in the safety chain: see it, say it, then assess it.

For H130 operations, hazards are often visual and immediate — wires, masts, deck motion, brownout — but also systemic: fatigue, rushed briefings, unclear clearances. Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 English must name hazards precisely.

Understanding hazard identification in ICAO-standard English is essential across Parts 1, 2, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Hazard Identification is the process of recognizing and describing any condition, object, activity, or event with the potential to cause injury, damage, or loss of control, so that risk assessment and mitigation can follow before the hazard becomes an occurrence.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Identificação de perigos / Hazard Identification.

Processo de reconhecer e descrever qualquer condição, objeto, atividade ou evento com potencial de causar lesão, dano ou perda de controle, para que a avaliação e a mitigação de risco possam ocorrer antes que o perigo se torne uma ocorrência.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When identifying hazards, the crew should:

- scan for environmental, technical, and human hazards
- say the hazard out loud — unspoken hazards stay unmanaged
- for H130: call wires, slopes, dust, deck motion, and obstacles early
- include operational hazards: late clearances, frequency congestion, fatigue
- feed identified hazards into risk assessment and briefing
- report recurring hazards through SMS after landing

Hazard identification commonly includes:

- terrain / obstacle / wire hazards
- weather and visibility hazards
- aircraft and performance hazards
- human-factor hazards
- ATC and airspace hazards

**Important:** Naming a hazard is not negativity. It is the start of professional mitigation. Silence is the real threat.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers and ATS safety systems need hazard reports to:

- learn about frequency hotspots and unclear procedures
- protect following aircraft from the same trap
- coordinate helicopter obstacle and wire awareness near pads
- adjust flow when weather hazards affect multiple flights
- support SMS between operators and ATS units

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are approaching a private helipad outside Campinas in the H130. On short final the crewman calls wires on the approach path — previously unmarked on your mental map.

You discontinue the approach, climb away, and advise Tower you are unable to land, repositioning for a different path. The hazard was identified, spoken, and acted on. After landing you file a hazard report so the next crew is not surprised.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, report intentions.
2. Tower: ANAC123, confirm go-around, advise reason if able.
3. Approach: ANAC123, hold present position, traffic.
4. Ground: ANAC123, report if any aerodrome hazard observed.
5. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to reposition, report final.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around, wires on approach path.
2. ANAC123, unable to land, requesting reposition for alternate approach.
3. ANAC123, holding present position.
4. ANAC123, wilco, will report observed hazards after landing.
5. ANAC123, repositioning, will report final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Hazard identification is seeing plus saying.

TEM starts with threats. If the threat stays in your head, the other pilot cannot help you.

In Part 1 answers, name hazards specifically: wire across final, gusty crosswind, high density altitude — not “bad conditions.”

In the H130, the crewman is often your best hazard sensor near the pad. Brief that obstacle calls are welcome and mandatory.

Some hazards belong on the radio immediately — debris, animals, wire discoveries that affect the next aircraft. That is safety citizenship, not drama.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is hazard identification and how does it help flight safety?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Hazard identification means recognizing and clearly describing anything that could cause harm — for example wires, weather, fatigue, or an unclear clearance — so the crew can assess risk and apply mitigations. It improves safety because unspoken hazards cannot be managed, briefed, or reported to protect the next flight.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Seeing a hazard but not announcing it until too late.  
  ✔ Call it early so the team can act.

- ❌ Using vague words like “complicated” instead of the real hazard.  
  ✔ Name the wire, wind, or performance limit specifically.

- ❌ Treating hazard reports as optional paperwork.  
  ✔ Recurring hazards need SMS visibility.

- ❌ Focusing only on external hazards and ignoring fatigue or rushed briefs.  
  ✔ Human-factor hazards count equally.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On short final to a private helipad your crewman identifies unmarked wires. You decide to discontinue.

**Your first task**

What do you say to Tower?

**Your second task**

What should you do after landing regarding the hazard?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around, wires on approach path, requesting reposition. Second task: File a hazard or voluntary safety report describing location and wire position so following crews and the operator can mitigate the trap. Identification without reporting leaves the next flight exposed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Hazard identification is the first safety verb: see, say, act.

Specific language beats vague worry. Early calls beat late heroics.

In the H130, wires and dust do not negotiate. Name them while you still have altitude and options.

Then assess. Then mitigate. Then, when needed, report — so the hazard does not wait for someone else.
