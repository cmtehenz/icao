# Sling Load Operations

**Catalog ID:** 0406  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Sling Load Operations — external load flights in which cargo is carried beneath the helicopter on a cargo hook and sling, requiring precise hover work, clear crew coordination, and specific ATC phraseology.

Sling load is highly relevant H130 work in Brazil: construction lifts, power-line support, remote resupply in the Amazon and highlands, and industrial sites near controlled airspace.

You must communicate load status, hover intentions, route constraints, and emergencies — including the possibility of jettison — in ICAO Part 2 and Part 3 English.

## 📖 Meaning

Sling Load Operations are helicopter flights in which external cargo is carried on a sling and cargo hook beneath the aircraft, requiring specialized hover techniques, load monitoring, and communication of load status and routing to ATC and ground crews.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Operações com carga externa / sling load.

Voos de helicóptero em que a carga externa é transportada em linga e gancho de carga sob a aeronave, exigindo técnicas especializadas de pairado, monitoramento da carga e comunicação do status e da rota ao ATC e às equipes de solo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting Sling Load Operations, the pilot should:

- advise ATC that the flight is sling load / external load
- report load on and load released at the appropriate times
- request hover or low-level routing clear of traffic and obstacles
- monitor load swing and be prepared to jettison if control is threatened
- declare urgency immediately if the load endangers flight

Sling Load reporting commonly includes:

- sling load departure, request hover taxi / departure route
- load on, proceeding to drop zone
- on station, commencing load placement
- load released, departing climb-out

**Important:** A swinging load can demand more power and attention than ATC expects. If you need an orbit, delay, or priority to stabilize, say so early.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support Sling Load Operations by:

- providing traffic separation around slow, low helicopter routes
- reserving hover areas near construction or industrial sites
- understanding reduced maneuverability with a load attached
- coordinating drop-zone activity with other aircraft
- expediting emergency landing or jettison situations

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are lifting a construction load in your H130 near Campinas, inside a control zone.

You transmit:

Campinas Tower, ANAC123, sling load, load on, request hover departure to construction site north, not above five hundred feet.

Tower clears you and advises light aircraft in the circuit. On site:

ANAC123, on station, placing load, will report load released.

After release:

ANAC123, load released, request climb one thousand five hundred, returning base.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger sling load, cleared hover departure, remain clear of runway two five final.
2. Tower: ANAC123, report load on and airborne.
3. Approach: ANAC123, sling load approved not above five hundred, report on station.
4. Tower: ANAC123, advise when load released, traffic inbound.
5. Tower: ANAC123, roger load released, cleared climb one thousand five hundred.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Sling load English is status English: load on, on station, load released.

ATC cannot see the bag under your belly. They only know what you say.

In the H130, vertical reference and power margin define the lift. If the load starts to dance, fix it or put it down. Do not drag a runaway sling through a city frequency without a call.

Near Brazilian controlled airports, stay predictable. Announce altitude caps. Report release before you climb through the circuit.

If the load threatens the aircraft, the next lesson — jettison — becomes this lesson's emergency exit. Brief it before the hook closes.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe what you report to ATC during a sling load flight from lift to release.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would identify the flight as sling load, report load on and departure route, report on station for placement, then report load released and climb-out intentions. Example: ANAC123, sling load, load on, request hover departure not above five hundred; later ANAC123, load released, request climb one thousand five hundred. Clear status calls keep ATC and traffic safe.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Departing with a load without telling ATC it is sling load.  
  ✔ State sling load on first contact.

- ❌ Climbing through the circuit immediately after release without a call.  
  ✔ Report load released, then request climb.

- ❌ Saying nothing while hovering for long periods in controlled airspace.  
  ✔ Report on station and expected delay if placement takes time.

- ❌ Using only Portuguese with the ground crew on the ATC frequency.  
  ✔ Keep ATC frequency in ICAO English; use intercom/company for hook talk.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Sling load lift inside Campinas CTR. Load begins unstable after takeoff.

**Your first task**

Initial departure call with load on.

**Your second task**

You must return to the pickup site to set the load down. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, sling load, load on, request hover departure to construction site north, not above five hundred. Second task: ANAC123, returning to pickup site, unstable load, request hover landing to release — or, if control is threatened, prepare the jettison call from the next lesson.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Sling load success is quiet hardware and loud status calls.

Load on. On station. Load released.

In the H130, power margin and swing discipline keep Brazilian industrial lifts safe.

Tell ATC what is under the hook. Release only when the site and the aircraft agree. Climb only after the frequency knows you are clean.
