# Engine Surge

**Catalog ID:** 0252  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Engine Surge — a severe airflow instability in a turbine engine, often related to compressor surge, producing bangs, thrust fluctuations, and possible flame from the exhaust, requiring immediate thrust management and clear ATC communication. Closely related to engine stall in exam English; for H130 crews, treat surge vocabulary as ICAO-essential and map it to real turbine power fluctuations, unusual noise, and parameter instability.

Engine surge sits in the same family as compressor stall. Controllers need to know you have an engine airflow problem, whether performance is affected, and what you intend to do next.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master the distinction and the shared radio structure, then transfer both to H130 engine anomaly reporting.

Understanding engine surge in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Engine Surge is a violent disruption of airflow through a turbine engine compressor, typically causing loud bangs, rapid thrust changes, and abnormal engine indications, requiring immediate crew actions per procedure and prompt advice to ATC regarding the malfunction, aircraft performance, and intentions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Surto de motor / Engine Surge (compressor surge).

Interrupção violenta do fluxo de ar através do compressor de um motor a turbina, tipicamente causando estampidos altos, mudanças rápidas de empuxo e indicações anormais do motor, exigindo ações imediatas da tripulação conforme procedimento e aviso rápido ao ATC sobre a pane, o desempenho da aeronave e as intenções.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an engine surge occurs, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control and note yaw, vibration, and thrust changes
- reduce thrust / follow engine surge or stall QRH actions
- monitor whether the surge is momentary or continuous
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday based on severity and remaining performance
- report engine surge, status (recovered / continuing), and intentions
- for H130: use the same structure for severe turbine airflow or power instability

Engine surge reporting commonly includes:

- engine surge on number one / number two
- surge recovered / surge continuing
- request level off / return / priority landing
- souls and fuel when asked
- request emergency services if landing degraded

**Important:** Surge and stall are related compressor events. In ICAO speaking, either term may appear. Use the term that matches the question or the indication, and always add performance status and intentions. Do not argue terminology while the engine is misbehaving — fly, then communicate.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use engine surge reports to:

- anticipate possible repeated thrust interruptions
- provide flexible altitude and speed assignments
- approve returns and priority approaches
- separate traffic from an aircraft with uncertain climb performance
- prepare emergency services for engine-related landings

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: in cruise climb, repeated bangs and EPR/N1 fluctuations — engine surge.

You reduce thrust as required and call:

Pan Pan Pan, Control, ANAC123, engine surge number one, request level off and vectors to return.

If surges continue and thrust is unreliable:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, engine surge continuing, request priority landing nearest suitable.

For H130 reality, describe bangs, torque/N1 instability, and power reliability with the same urgency ladder.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan engine surge, say if engine stabilized.
2. Control: ANAC123, level at flight level eight zero approved, turn right heading zero niner zero for return.
3. Approach: ANAC123, priority approach approved, expect runway one four.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Tower: ANAC123, fire services standing by, advise engine status on final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Engine surge is the engine coughing so hard the whole airframe hears it.

Your job is not to give ATC a thermodynamics lecture. Your job is status: surge, recovered or continuing, performance okay or not, return or divert.

Momentary surge that recovers can still justify a precautionary return — especially if it happened near limiting conditions. Continuous surge is a threat to thrust. Upgrade urgency when reliability disappears.

In the H130, power must be honest. If the turbine is surging or rolling, say the malfunction plainly and ask for the airspace and landing priority you need.

High priority on the exam. High honesty on the line.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is an engine surge, and how would you communicate it to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

An engine surge is a severe airflow instability in the compressor that can cause bangs and thrust fluctuations. I would control the aircraft, apply the surge procedure, and advise ATC — usually Pan Pan if controllable, Mayday if thrust is unreliable — stating engine surge, whether it has recovered or is continuing, and requesting level off, return, or priority landing as required. I would provide souls and fuel when asked. On a helicopter, I would report the same way for a serious turbine power instability.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "the engine made a big noise" without "engine surge."  
  ✔ Use "engine surge" plus recovered/continuing status.

- ❌ Keeping Pan Pan when surges continue and thrust is unsafe.  
  ✔ Upgrade to Mayday if safe flight is in doubt.

- ❌ Failing to say whether the surge recovered.  
  ✔ Always give recovered versus continuing — ATC plans from that.

- ❌ Skipping the topic as jet-only knowledge.  
  ✔ Learn for ICAO; transfer to H130 turbine anomalies.

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, EN-jin SURJ NUM-ber WUN, reh-KWEST LEV-ul OFF and rih-TURN

**Word Stress**

- Engine → EN-jin
- Surge → SURJ
- Number → NUM-ber
- Return → rih-TURN

Practice:

Engine surge number one... request level off and return.

Together:

Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, engine surge number one, request level off and return.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Repeated bangs in climb. Engine surge indications. After thrust reduction, surges stop but you elect to return.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit initially?

**Your second task**

On downwind, parameters are stable. What update do you give?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Pan Pan Pan, Control, ANAC123, engine surge number one, request level off and vectors to return. Second task: ANAC123, engine surge recovered, parameters stable, continuing priority return for landing, request emergency services on arrival. I would still treat it as a precautionary priority landing and give souls and fuel when asked.
