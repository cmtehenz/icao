# Tail Rotor Pedal Input

**Catalog ID:** 0437  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Tail Rotor Pedal Input — the anti-torque pedal application used to control yaw, trim heading in the hover, and counteract main rotor torque during power changes.

Precise pedal input is fundamental H130 flying: hover work, transitions, confined areas, and crosswind operations across Brazilian missions.

You must describe yaw control intentions, LTE-related calls, and pedal-related emergencies in ICAO English for Parts 2 and 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Tail Rotor Pedal Input is the pilot's use of anti-torque pedals to vary tail rotor or fenestron thrust, controlling aircraft yaw and heading, especially during hover, power changes, and asymmetric wind conditions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Entrada de pedal do rotor de cauda / tail rotor pedal input.

Uso dos pedais antitorque pelo piloto para variar o empuxo do rotor de cauda ou fenestron, controlando a guinada e a proa da aeronave, especialmente no pairamento, nas mudanças de potência e em condições de vento assimétrico.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing tail rotor pedal input, the pilot should:

- anticipate yaw with power changes — more collective often needs more pedal
- trim heading in the hover with precise, small inputs
- respect wind from the critical azimuth that demands large pedal
- avoid abrupt full-pedal applications that can degrade effectiveness
- report loss of yaw control immediately if pedals become ineffective

Pedal-related reporting commonly includes:

- holding hover, correcting for wind
- yaw control difficulty, going around
- loss of tail rotor effectiveness, declaring emergency
- landing with limited yaw control, request clear area

**Important:** Pedals are not optional trim. In the H130 fenestron, authority is strong within limits — but wind, weight, and high power can still demand full attention. Brief critical wind azimuths for the site.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers need pedal-related communications to:

- understand go-arounds caused by yaw control problems
- clear areas for emergency landings with limited heading control
- provide wind information critical to helicopter hover
- sequence traffic away from an unstable hover
- dispatch assistance after anti-torque emergencies

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You establish a hover at a windy helipad in your H130. A gust from the critical side demands large pedal. Authority feels marginal.

You go around before losing the nose:

Yaw control difficulty, going around, request another approach with different wind orientation — or request alternate pad.

You return with a better relative wind and stable pedal margin.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, wind two seven zero at one eight, report established hover.
2. Tower: ANAC123, roger going around, report intentions.
3. Tower: ANAC123, alternate pad available, wind more favorable, say if able.
4. Tower: ANAC123, roger emergency, cleared to land, area will be cleared.
5. Tower: ANAC123, fire services standing by, say souls and fuel when able.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Pedal input is the conversation between torque and wind.

Collective up — expect yaw. Gust on the wrong side — expect more pedal. In the H130, make the inputs early and smooth. Late full pedal is how LTE stories begin.

Part 3 loves: what do you do if you reach the pedal stop in the hover? Answer: do not sit there. Reduce power if appropriate, go around into a clearer wind, or land if already committed and controllable — communicate yaw control difficulty immediately.

Brazilian coastal crosswinds will teach you humility. Brief the wind. Fly the pedals. Leave when margin disappears.

A stable hover with spare pedal is always better than a heroic save at the stops.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are near the pedal stop in a hover and yaw control is marginal. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not remain in a marginal hover. I would go around or adjust power and orientation to regain yaw margin and transmit: yaw control difficulty, going around, request another approach or alternate pad with more favorable wind. If yaw control is lost, I would declare an emergency and land as soon as practical in a clear area.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Ignoring wind azimuth during hover planning.  
  ✔ Brief critical wind direction before the approach.

- ❌ Abrupt full pedal as the first response to every gust.  
  ✔ Smooth, anticipatory inputs; leave if at the stops.

- ❌ Not telling ATC why you went around.  
  ✔ State yaw control difficulty so they can help with wind or alternate.

- ❌ Confusing fenestron with "no LTE risk."  
  ✔ Respect limits; degraded yaw still happens.
