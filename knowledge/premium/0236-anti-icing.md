# Anti-Icing

**Catalog ID:** 0236  
**Category:** Ground Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Anti-Icing — the preventive application of fluid or the use of aircraft systems to stop ice from forming on critical surfaces before takeoff or during flight, as distinct from de-icing, which removes ice that has already formed.

Anti-icing is not the same as de-icing. De-icing removes existing contamination. Anti-icing protects clean surfaces so ice does not form again. Mixing the two concepts in radio calls or briefings confuses ATC, ground crews, and examiners.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations in Brazilian winter and cold-front conditions — Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, Florianópolis — crews must know when anti-icing is required on the ground, how it relates to holdover time, and how to discuss anti-icing clearly in ICAO English. Many light helicopters have limited or no certified flight-into-known-icing capability; ground anti-icing and timely departure remain critical.

Understanding anti-icing for Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 speaking is essential professional vocabulary.

## 📖 Meaning

Anti-Icing is the preventive treatment or system operation intended to stop ice, frost, or snow from forming or adhering to aircraft surfaces that have already been cleaned, typically by applying Type II, III, or IV anti-icing fluid on the ground or by activating aircraft anti-ice systems in flight, as opposed to de-icing which removes ice already present.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When anti-icing is required or discussed, the crew should:

- confirm surfaces are clean first — anti-icing protects a clean aircraft; it does not replace de-icing of existing ice
- request or confirm anti-icing fluid type, start of application, and holdover time with ground crew
- brief departure timing against holdover expiration before accepting takeoff clearance
- activate aircraft anti-ice systems per AFM when in or approaching icing conditions in flight, if certified
- report icing and anti-ice status to ATC when performance or systems are affected
- never confuse "request de-icing" with "request anti-icing" on the radio

Anti-icing planning includes:

- distinction from de-icing: remove first, then protect
- fluid type and concentration for expected precipitation
- holdover time tables and precipitation intensity
- taxi and takeoff sequencing so holdover does not expire
- H130 limitations: many training helicopters are not approved for flight into known icing — exit icing and report

**Important:** Anti-icing assumes a clean surface. If ice, frost, or snow is already present, de-ice first, then anti-ice if protection is still needed. On the radio, say clearly what you need: de-icing, anti-icing, or both. In Brazilian ops, coordinate with ground and ATC so departure occurs within holdover time.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers and ground coordination use anti-icing information to:

- sequence departures so aircraft leave before holdover time expires
- coordinate ground vehicle and pad availability for treatment
- understand delays related to weather and surface contamination
- prioritize aircraft approaching holdover expiration when traffic allows
- share icing and surface condition awareness across the airport

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on the ramp at Guarulhos before an IFR training departure to Curitiba. Overnight frost has been removed. Light freezing drizzle is forecast during taxi.

Ground reports the aircraft is clean. You need protection before takeoff.

You call Ground:

ANAC123, request anti-icing, Type IV, for departure runway one zero Left.

Ground: ANAC123, taxi to de-icing pad, expect anti-icing in five minutes.

After treatment, ground gives start of application and fluid type. You note holdover time and brief the departure window.

You advise:

ANAC123, anti-icing complete, ready for taxi, holdover valid.

Ground sequences you for an early departure so you remain within holdover. You take off with clean, protected surfaces — and a clear distinction between what was removed and what was prevented.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, taxi to de-icing pad via Alpha, hold short of the pad.
2. Ground: ANAC123, anti-icing in progress, report when complete and ready to taxi.
3. Tower: ANAC123, advise holdover time remaining before departure.
4. Ground: ANAC123, expect departure delay five minutes, confirm anti-icing still valid.
5. Approach: ANAC123, previous departure reported light icing at three thousand, say intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Anti-icing prevents. De-icing removes. Say the right word.

If the aircraft already has ice, frost, or snow, you need de-icing first. Anti-icing alone on a contaminated surface is the wrong mental model and the wrong request.

After the surface is clean, anti-icing fluid buys you time — holdover time — until the fluid fails under precipitation. That clock starts at the beginning of the final anti-icing application. Know it. Brief it. Depart inside it.

In the H130 academy environment, you may not always have airline-style Type IV pads. You may have limited ground support. The English still matters for exams and for professional coordination: request anti-icing, report complete, advise holdover status, and never take off with contaminated critical surfaces.

In flight, if your aircraft has anti-ice systems and you are approved to use them, follow the AFM. If you are not certified for known icing, do not treat "anti-ice on" as permission to stay in ice. Exit. Report. Divert if needed.

Clear words on the radio save minutes on the ground and arguments in the oral exam.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your aircraft has been cleaned of frost. Freezing drizzle is expected during a fifteen-minute taxi. Explain the difference between anti-icing and de-icing and what you would request.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

De-icing removes ice, frost, or snow that is already on the aircraft. Anti-icing is preventive protection applied to a clean surface so ice does not form again before takeoff. Because the aircraft is already clean and freezing drizzle is expected, I would request anti-icing, note the fluid type and start of application, calculate holdover time, and plan to depart before the fluid fails. If contamination were still present, I would request de-icing first, then anti-icing if continued protection were required.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "request de-icing" when the aircraft is already clean and only protection is needed.  
  ✔ Say "request anti-icing" for preventive treatment on a clean aircraft.

- ❌ Treating anti-icing fluid as unlimited protection with no time limit.  
  ✔ Track holdover time from start of final application until takeoff.

- ❌ Taking off after holdover expires because "the fluid is still visible."  
  ✔ If holdover has expired or is uncertain, return for re-treatment.

- ❌ Confusing aircraft anti-ice systems with ground anti-icing fluid.  
  ✔ Distinguish ground fluid protection from in-flight system operation in briefings and reports.

- Anti → AN-tee (stress on first)
- Icing → EYE-sing

- Type → TYPE

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are at Congonhas. Frost was removed. Light snow is starting. Ground asks what treatment you need.

**Your first task**

What do you say to request preventive protection only?

**Your second task**

After anti-icing, Tower offers a ten-minute departure delay. Your holdover is twelve minutes. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, aircraft clean, request anti-icing for departure. Second task: ANAC123, holdover approximately twelve minutes, able to accept short delay, request departure before holdover expires — or, if the delay would exceed holdover: ANAC123, unable delay, holdover expiring, request immediate departure or return for re-treatment. I would not take off after an expired or uncertain holdover.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Anti-icing is prevention. De-icing is removal. Holdover is the clock.

Clean first. Protect second. Depart inside the time.

On Brazilian winter mornings, the difference between the right request and the wrong one is the difference between a professional departure and a contaminated takeoff.

In the H130, respect icing limitations in the air. On the ground, respect fluid limits and language precision.

Say anti-icing when you mean protection. Say de-icing when you mean removal. Track the holdover. Take off clean.

That is how you keep winter operations honest — in English and on the aircraft.
