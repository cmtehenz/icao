# Decision Making

**Catalog ID:** 0458  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Decision Making — the structured process of choosing a safe course of action under time pressure, incomplete information, and operational constraints.

Aviation decisions are rarely between perfect and imperfect. They are between acceptable risk now and unacceptable risk later. Divert, hold, go around, continue, declare — each choice must be spoken clearly in ICAO English so ATC and the crew share the plan.

For H130 crews operating into weather-limited Brazilian destinations, offshore sites, and mountain airports, decision making links Part 1 vocabulary, Part 2 clearances, and Part 4 crew coordination. A good decision that is never transmitted is an incomplete decision.

## 📖 Meaning

Decision Making is the crew process of identifying a problem, gathering relevant information, evaluating options against safety and operational criteria, selecting and implementing a course of action, and reviewing the outcome — including communicating the decision to ATC and other crew members.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Tomada de decisão / Decision Making.

Processo da tripulação de identificar um problema, reunir informações relevantes, avaliar opções segundo critérios de segurança e operação, selecionar e implementar um curso de ação, e revisar o resultado — incluindo comunicar a decisão ao controle e aos demais membros da tripulação.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When making operational decisions, the crew should:

- define the problem clearly — weather, fuel, system, passenger, performance
- gather facts: fuel remaining, weather, NOTAMs, alternate status, ATC options
- compare options: continue, hold, divert, go around, declare
- decide, brief the plan, and fly the plan
- transmit the decision: intentions, request, or urgency call as required

Decision making includes:

- time-critical decisions — go around, reject takeoff, emergency descent
- deliberate decisions — diversion, fuel stop, delay for weather
- shared decisions — captain authority with first officer input
- review — did the decision solve the problem or create a new one

**Important:** Indecision is a decision to continue the current path. If the current path is unsafe, choose deliberately and communicate.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers need crew decisions to:

- re-sequence traffic after a go-around or diversion
- issue holds, alternates, and priority handling
- coordinate emergency services when urgency is declared
- avoid assuming the crew will continue when they should divert
- manage airspace when intentions change mid-approach

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Congonhas in the H130. Destination weather drops below minima. Alternate is open. Fuel allows one hold then divert.

You decide:

Approach, ANAC123, weather below minima, request hold, if not improving will divert to Guarulhos.

Approach: ANAC123, hold as published, expect further in ten minutes, advise intentions.

After one hold, weather remains below. You decide and transmit:

ANAC123, diverting Guarulhos, request clearance.

The decision was structured, timed, and spoken.

3. Control: ANAC123, diversion approved, cleared to Guarulhos, climb flight level six zero.
4. Approach: ANAC123, can you accept approach now, weather improving.
5. Tower: ANAC123, go around if not visual, acknowledge.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Decision making has a clock.

Some decisions need seconds — go around, climb for terrain. Some need minutes — divert, fuel plan. Match the process to the clock.

Use a simple loop: problem, options, decide, act, review. Do not invent ten options when two are real: continue safely or change the plan.

In the H130, fuel margins and weather windows can be tight. Decide early enough that ATC can help. A diversion called at the last minute creates pressure for everyone.

Invite input. The first officer may see fuel or weather you missed. Then decide. Authority is not the same as silence.

Say the decision on frequency in clear ICAO English. Intentions are part of the decision.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Destination weather is below minima. You have fuel for one hold. What is your decision process and radio call?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would state the problem: weather below minima. Options: hold once, then divert if not improving. I would transmit: ANAC123, request hold, fuel for one pattern, will divert if weather not improving. After the hold, I would decide and say: ANAC123, diverting to alternate, request clearance. I would not continue hoping without a plan.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Waiting for perfect information before deciding.  
  ✔ Decide with available facts and update as new data arrives.

- ❌ Deciding silently without telling ATC.  
  ✔ Transmit intentions: hold, divert, go around, request.

- ❌ Continuing because passengers expect arrival.  
  ✔ Safety criteria first; communicate the operational decision.

- ❌ Changing the plan without briefing the other pilot.  
  ✔ Shared decision: brief, then fly and transmit.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are twenty miles from destination in the H130. Weather is at minima and trending down. Fuel allows approach plus divert. Alternate is open.

**Your first task**

ATC asks: "ANAC123, advise intentions." What do you say?

**Your second task**

On final you are not visual at minima. What decision and call follow?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, request approach, if not visual will divert to alternate, fuel remaining one hour one five. Second task: ANAC123, going around, not visual, diverting, request clearance. The decision tree was set before the approach — execute it without hesitation.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Decision making is CRM under a clock.

Define. Option. Choose. Act. Tell ATC.

Hope is not a plan. Silence is not leadership.

In the H130, weather and fuel will force real choices on Brazilian routes. Make them early. Speak them clearly. Review them honestly.

That is how decisions keep the flight safe — and keep ATC able to help you.
