# Wilco

**Catalog ID:** 0129  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Wilco — a critical ICAO standard word that every professional pilot must use correctly.

Wilco is a contraction of will comply. It means: I have received your message and I will carry out the instruction.

Wilco is stronger than Roger. Roger confirms receipt. Wilco confirms receipt AND commitment to action.

In the H130, flying into Curitiba, Joinville, or Congonhas, knowing exactly when Wilco is the right word — and why it is different from Roger, Affirm, and a full readback — is an essential ICAO Level 4 proficiency marker.

## 📖 Meaning

Wilco is an ICAO-standard word meaning I have received your message and will comply with it. It is a contraction of will comply. It indicates both receipt and the pilot's commitment to execute the instruction as issued.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Executarei / Cumprirei.

Palavra ICAO padrão que significa "recebi a mensagem e cumprirei a instrução." É uma contração de will comply (cumprirei). Indica tanto o recebimento quanto o compromisso do piloto de executar a instrução.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When to use Wilco:

- after receiving an instruction from ATC that you intend to carry out
- when no specific readback values are required but compliance is expected
- in combination with a readback when acknowledging a complex clearance
- when ATC requests confirmation that an action will be performed

When NOT to use Wilco alone:

- in place of a required readback of specific values (altitude, runway, squawk)
- to answer a yes/no question — use Affirm
- to confirm receipt only — use Roger
- when you cannot comply — use Unable

Wilco operations commonly include:

- acknowledging frequency change instructions
- confirming you will report at a waypoint or position
- accepting a routing instruction when values are clear
- confirming you will call ATC when ready

**Important:** Wilco and Roger are never used together. ICAO states that since Wilco implies receipt, adding Roger is redundant. Say Wilco — not "Roger, Wilco."

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers request Wilco or accept it from pilots to:

- confirm the pilot will execute the instruction
- close communication loops on action items
- differentiate compliance from simple receipt
- ensure critical actions will be performed

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are tracking inbound to Curitiba in your H130.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, contact Curitiba Tower on one one eight decimal one when established on localizer.

You do not need to read back a specific value — this is an instruction to change frequency at a specific point. You respond:

Wilco, ANAC123.

This tells Approach: I received the instruction and I will contact Tower when established.

Now Approach adds:

ANAC123, report outer marker inbound.

You respond:

ANAC123, wilco.

Both uses confirm receipt and commitment to action — without requiring a full readback of specific numeric values.

If instead Approach had given you an altitude: ANAC123, descend to altitude four thousand — you would read back the altitude. Wilco alone would be insufficient because ATC needs to hear the altitude to verify it was received correctly.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Wilco is a commitment.

When you say Wilco, you are telling ATC: I received your instruction and I will carry it out.

That is a professional contract between pilot and controller.

Brazilian pilots sometimes confuse Roger and Wilco, using them interchangeably. They are not interchangeable.

Roger: I received the message.

Wilco: I received the message and I will do it.

The difference matters when ATC issues instructions like "report ready," "contact Tower," or "advise when you have information." These are action items. ATC expects compliance. Wilco signals that compliance.

Also critical: never say "Roger, Wilco." ICAO specifically prohibits this combination because it is redundant. Wilco already implies receipt. Choose one or the other based on whether compliance is expected.

In the H130, flying single-pilot, these distinctions keep you sounding professional, crisp, and in command.

Say what you mean. Use the right word.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When should a pilot use Wilco rather than Roger? Can you use them together?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Wilco should be used when ATC issues an instruction and the pilot intends to comply. Roger only confirms receipt, while Wilco confirms both receipt and commitment to action. For example, if ATC says "contact Tower when established," I would say Wilco, not Roger, because compliance is expected. ICAO states that Roger and Wilco should not be combined, because Wilco already implies the message was received. Saying "Roger Wilco" is redundant and non-standard.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using "Roger, Wilco" together.  
  ✔ Choose one: Roger for receipt only, Wilco for receipt plus compliance.

- ❌ Using Wilco in place of a readback when specific values were issued.  
  ✔ If ATC issues an altitude, you must read it back — Wilco alone is not sufficient.

- ❌ Using Roger when compliance is expected, leaving ATC unsure if you will act.  
  ✔ Use Wilco when the instruction requires a specific action from you.

- ❌ Adding "OK, Wilco, OK" with filler words.  
  ✔ Wilco, ANAC123. — clean, complete, professional.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Navegantes Tower transmits: "ANAC123, after landing, vacate via taxiway charlie and hold short of taxiway delta, call Ground on one two one decimal nine when clear."

**Your first task**

Is a full readback required, or is Wilco sufficient? What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

You land and vacate as instructed. You call Ground. Ground says: "ANAC123, taxi to parking stand bravo three via taxiway delta." Provide a correct readback and explain whether Wilco alone would be acceptable here.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

For the Tower instruction with route elements and a frequency change, I should read back the key elements: Vacate via taxiway charlie, hold short of taxiway delta, call Ground one two one decimal nine, ANAC123. Wilco alone would miss the specific route and frequency ATC needs to verify. For the Ground instruction to taxi via taxiway delta to stand bravo three, I must read back: Taxi to parking stand bravo three via taxiway delta, ANAC123. Taxi routing is a readback-required item under ICAO because runway incursions can result from missed or misheard taxi instructions.
