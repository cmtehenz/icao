# Winds Reported

**Catalog ID:** 0170  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Winds Reported — the standard ATC phraseology for communicating surface wind conditions to pilots, including direction, speed, and significant variations such as gusts, variable direction, and calm wind.

Wind information is one of the most frequently transmitted pieces of data in aviation communication.

ATC gives wind reports before landing clearances, before takeoff clearances, and on request.

Pilots who misunderstand wind reports make wrong runway choices, accept excessive crosswind components, or fail to calculate correct performance data.

In Brazil, winds at coastal airports — Florianópolis, Navegantes, Congonhas — vary significantly with sea breezes, orographic effects, and tropical convective cells. Accurate wind understanding is not optional. It is operational.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 radiotelephony involving wind reports.

## 📖 Meaning

Winds Reported is the ATC transmission of current surface wind conditions including magnetic direction in degrees, speed in knots, and relevant variations including gusts, variable direction, or calm.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Ventos reportados.

Transmissão do controle de tráfego aéreo das condições atuais de vento de superfície incluindo direção magnética em graus, velocidade em nós e variações relevantes como rajadas, direção variável ou vento calmo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ATC reports wind, the pilot should:

- note the direction in degrees magnetic
- note the speed in knots
- note any gust value: the highest speed encountered
- calculate the crosswind and headwind/tailwind components for the assigned runway
- request a different runway if the crosswind component exceeds limits
- request a wind check if conditions appear to have changed since the last report

Standard wind report format: direction — speed — variations.

Examples:

- Wind two five zero at one five: direction 250°, speed 15 knots
- Wind two five zero at one five, gusting two five: peak gust 25 knots
- Wind variable at five: direction varying, speed 5 knots
- Wind calm: no significant wind

**Important:** Wind direction in ATC reports is given in degrees magnetic, rounded to the nearest 10 degrees. Wind speed is in knots. Gust values represent the highest speed in recent observation, not the average. Always use the gust for crosswind calculation.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers report winds to:

- provide pilots with current surface conditions for runway selection
- support safe landing and takeoff decisions
- comply with mandatory wind reporting requirements before landing clearances
- advise pilots of significant changes between ATIS and actual conditions
- support go-around decisions when wind shifts suddenly

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on final approach to Florianópolis in your H130 on runway two eight.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, wind two six zero at one eight, gusting two six, runway two eight, cleared to land.

You calculate: runway two eight heading 280°. Wind from 260° — nearly aligned. Crosswind component approximately six knots left. Gusting to 26 knots — your H130 maximum demonstrated crosswind is 25 knots.

The gust exceeds your limit.

You transmit:

Florianópolis Tower, ANAC123, unable to accept crosswind gust two six, request runway one zero.

Tower responds:

ANAC123, runway one zero available, wind two six zero at one eight, gusting two six, tailwind component eight knots, advise accept or go-around.

You calculate tailwind on runway one zero: 8 knots — within your 15-knot tailwind limit.

You respond:

ANAC123, accept runway one zero, tailwind eight knots.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, wind two five zero at one five, runway two eight, cleared to land.
2. Tower: ANAC123, wind variable between two zero zero and three zero zero at eight knots.
3. Tower: ANAC123, wind two five zero at two zero, gusting three zero, caution windshear reported on final.
4. Tower: ANAC123, wind calm, runway one five, cleared for takeoff.
5. Tower: ANAC123, wind two zero zero at one five, runway two eight, crosswind component one two knots.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, cleared to land runway two eight, wind two five zero at one five.
2. ANAC123, wind variable between two zero zero and three zero zero, copied.
3. ANAC123, wind two five zero at two zero gusting three zero, unable to accept crosswind, request runway one zero.
4. ANAC123, wind calm, cleared for takeoff runway one five.
5. ANAC123, crosswind one two, runway two eight, cleared to land.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Wind reports are numbers you must act on — not just acknowledge.

Every time ATC gives you wind, do this calculation in your head:

Direction relative to runway. Speed. Crosswind component. Gust if applicable.

Brazilian pilots sometimes just say "Roger, wind copied" and proceed without actually computing the component.

That is a trap.

If the gust puts your crosswind above your limit — you must request a different runway.

The H130 has well-defined limits. Know them. Apply them.

At Florianópolis, sea breezes frequently rotate through the afternoon. The wind on the ATIS can be different from the wind on final.

Ask for a wind check if you suspect a change.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How would you communicate to ATC that the reported wind places you outside your crosswind limits?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would acknowledge the wind report and advise ATC that I am unable to accept the crosswind. For example: ANAC123, unable to accept crosswind on runway two eight with wind two five zero at two zero gusting three zero, request runway one zero or most into wind. If an alternative runway is not available, I would request a go-around and hold while waiting for conditions to improve or arrange a diversion.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Acknowledging wind without computing the crosswind component.  
  ✔ Always calculate direction, speed, and component before accepting the runway.

- ❌ Using the average wind speed instead of the gust for crosswind calculation.  
  ✔ Use the gust value — it represents the maximum crosswind encountered.

- ❌ Not requesting a wind check when conditions appear to have changed.  
  ✔ Ask for a wind check: ANAC123, request wind check.

- ❌ Saying "wind okay" instead of a proper readback.  
  ✔ Read back the key elements: cleared to land runway two eight, wind two five zero at one five, ANAC123.

**Pronunciation:** wind too fyv ZE-ro at wun fyv, GUS-ting too fyv

**Word Stress**

- Wind → wind
- Two Five Zero → too fyv ZE-ro
- At → at
- One Five → wun fyv
- Gusting → GUS-ting

Practice:

Wind... two five zero... at one five... gusting... two five...

Together:

Wind two five zero at one five, gusting two five, runway two eight, cleared to land.

Each number must be spoken individually and clearly.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**DOGS** — **D**irection in degrees magnetic, **O**nly use gust for crosswind calculation, **G**et the component before accepting the runway, **S**ay unable if it exceeds your limits.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Florianópolis Tower transmits: ANAC123, wind two zero zero at two two, gusting three two, runway two eight, cleared to land.

Your H130 has a maximum demonstrated crosswind of 25 knots. Runway two eight heading is 280°. The wind is from 200°, which gives approximately an 80° crosswind angle.

**Your first task**

Is the gust within your crosswind limit? What do you tell ATC?

**Your second task**

Tower offers runway one zero (heading 100°). Wind is still two zero zero at two two, gusting three two. What is the approximate tailwind component, and do you accept?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Runway two eight: wind from 200°, which is approximately 80° off runway heading. At that angle, nearly the full gust of 32 knots is a crosswind component — well above the 25-knot limit. I would transmit: ANAC123, unable to accept crosswind, gust three two exceeds limits, request runway one zero. For runway one zero (heading 100°), wind from 200° is approximately 100° off runway heading from behind — the full wind component is nearly a tailwind. Tailwind component approximately 22 knots. That exceeds my 15-knot tailwind limit as well. I would advise: ANAC123, unable to accept tailwind runway one zero, both runways outside limits, request holding or diversion until conditions improve.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Wind reports are math problems dressed in numbers.

Every report requires a calculation — not just a readback.

Use the gust. Check the component. Compare to your limits.

If you are outside limits — say so. Request another runway. Hold if necessary. Divert if no options remain.

The H130 has limits for a reason.

Know them. Respect them. Communicate them.
