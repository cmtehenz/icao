# Pan Pan

**Catalog ID:** 0053  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers the urgency call every pilot must distinguish from Mayday.

Pan Pan is the international urgency signal — used when an aircraft needs assistance but does not face grave and imminent danger.

Pan Pan does not override Mayday traffic.

It requests priority handling without declaring full distress.

You must know when Pan Pan is correct and when Mayday is required.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Pan Pan is the radiotelephony urgency signal indicating a condition that requires assistance but does not constitute grave and imminent danger.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Pan Pan is appropriate, the pilot should:

- transmit Pan Pan three times at the start of the message
- state the station addressed and aircraft callsign
- describe the nature of the urgency
- state intentions, position, and altitude
- provide souls on board and fuel remaining when requested

Pan Pan is used for urgency situations including:

- low fuel state
- hydraulic pressure decreasing
- landing gear unsafe
- partial electrical failure with continued safe flight
- passenger medical emergency not threatening the aircraft
- sudden decompression that is gradual, not rapid

**Important:** Pan Pan is for urgency — not distress. Say Pan Pan three times, clearly. Upgrade to Mayday if the situation deteriorates to grave and imminent danger.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Pan Pan declarations to:

- provide priority handling over normal traffic
- offer vectors and assistance without full emergency response
- coordinate landing priority when appropriate
- request additional information about the urgency
- monitor the aircraft until the situation is resolved

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are en route to Curitiba in your H130, callsign ANAC123, at two thousand feet when you notice hydraulic pressure slowly decreasing.

Control remains normal but the trend is concerning.

You transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan, Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, hydraulic pressure decreasing, request vectors to Curitiba.

Approach provides priority handling and clears traffic from your approach.

You complete a precautionary landing and report safe on the ground.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Pan Pan is the urgency call — one step below Mayday.

Say it three times.

Pan Pan Pan Pan.

Not Pan once.

Not "urgency urgency."

Brazilian pilots sometimes confuse Pan Pan with Mayday.

Know the difference:

Mayday — distress — grave and imminent danger — engine failure, fire, rapid decompression.

Pan Pan — urgency — needs help — low fuel, gear unsafe, slow hydraulic leak, passenger medical.

When the situation worsens, upgrade to Mayday without hesitation.

Controllers treat Pan Pan seriously.

Give clear information.

State nature, intentions, position.

Do not minimize a developing emergency — report early with Pan Pan rather than late with Mayday.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When should you use Pan Pan instead of Mayday?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would use Pan Pan when the aircraft needs assistance but does not face grave and imminent danger. Examples include low fuel state, hydraulic pressure decreasing, landing gear unsafe, and passenger medical emergency. I would use Mayday when safe flight is seriously in doubt, such as engine failure, fire on board, or rapid decompression. I would upgrade to Mayday if the urgency becomes distress.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using Pan Pan and Mayday interchangeably.  
  ✔ Pan Pan for urgency; Mayday for distress — know the difference.

- ❌ Saying "Pan" only once instead of three times.  
  ✔ Transmit Pan Pan three times at the start of the message.

- ❌ Using Pan Pan for engine failure or fire on board.  
  ✔ Those are distress — declare Mayday.

- ❌ Waiting too long to declare Pan Pan when a problem is developing.  
  ✔ Report early with Pan Pan — upgrade to Mayday if needed.
