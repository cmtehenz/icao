# Obstacle Avoidance

**Catalog ID:** 0081  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers obstacle avoidance — essential language and technique for low-level helicopter operations.

Obstacle Avoidance means detecting and remaining clear of terrain, buildings, towers, cranes, trees, and other hazards during flight.

In the H130, obstacle avoidance is critical during confined area approaches, urban operations, and coastal flights near Brazilian cities.

You must see and avoid, communicate traffic and obstacle concerns clearly, and go around when the approach is unsafe.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 operational communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Obstacle Avoidance is the process of detecting, assessing, and remaining clear of terrain and man-made obstacles during helicopter flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Evitação de obstáculos.

Processo de detectar, avaliar e permanecer afastado de terreno e obstáculos artificiais durante o voo de helicóptero.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Obstacle Avoidance is required, the pilot should:

- maintain visual lookout for towers, cranes, wires, and terrain
- request traffic and obstacle information from ATC when needed
- go around if obstacles make the approach unsafe
- report obstacles that may affect other aircraft
- use standard phraseology for go-around and traffic advisories

Obstacle Avoidance reporting commonly includes:

- going around due obstacles
- request traffic advisory
- obstacle on approach path
- unable to land due obstacles

**Important:** Obstacles change — construction cranes appear overnight. Always reconnoiter confined and urban sites before committing.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC supports Obstacle Avoidance by:

- providing traffic and known obstacle advisories
- clearing go-around paths
- coordinating with other aircraft in the area
- updating pilots on temporary obstacles when known
- sequencing approaches to avoid conflicts near obstacles

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are approaching a hospital helipad in São Paulo in your H130.

On final, you see a construction crane that was not on your previous briefing.

You go around and transmit:

São Paulo Tower, ANAC123, going around, obstacle on approach path, construction crane, request another approach.

Tower acknowledges and provides an alternate approach path clear of the crane.

You land safely on the second attempt.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger go-around, report when ready for another approach.
2. Tower: ANAC123, traffic advisory, crane reported north of the helipad.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions.
4. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, remain clear of the construction area.
5. Tower: ANAC123, no reported obstacles on the south approach.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Going around, obstacle on approach path, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, unable to land due obstacles, request another approach.
3. ANAC123, request traffic and obstacle advisory for hospital helipad.
4. Tower, ANAC123, obstacle clear, continuing approach.
5. ANAC123, going around due crane on final, will reattempt from the south.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Obstacle avoidance is not only seeing — it is deciding early.

The sequence:

Look. Assess. Go around if needed. Communicate.

A late go-around is better than a wire strike or crane strike.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "tem obstáculo" or "tem guindaste" on frequency.

Use obstacle on approach path or going around due obstacles — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130, urban Brazil means cranes, antennas, and unmarked wires.

Never assume yesterday's approach is clear today.

Train yourself to call the go-around early and clearly.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if you saw an unexpected obstacle on final approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would go around if the obstacle made the approach unsafe. I would transmit going around, obstacle on approach path, describe the obstacle if able, and request another approach or an alternate path. I would remain clear of the obstacle and confirm when ready for the next approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing an approach toward an unexpected crane or tower.  
  ✔ Go around early — communicate the obstacle to ATC.

- ❌ Saying "tem coisa na final" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use obstacle on approach path or going around due obstacles.

- ❌ Not reporting obstacles that could affect other aircraft.  
  ✔ Report temporary obstacles so ATC can advise others.

- ❌ Waiting until short final to decide.  
  ✔ Assess early — late decisions reduce options.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Going Around, Obstacle on Approach Path

**Pronunciation:** GO-ing uh-ROUND, OB-stuh-kul on uh-PROHCH path

**Word Stress**

- Obstacle → OB-stuh-kul
- Approach → uh-PROHCH
- Around → uh-ROUND

Practice:

Going... around...

Together:

Going around, obstacle on approach path, construction crane, ANAC123.

Speak clearly — Tower needs the reason for the go-around.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**LOOK** — **L**ocate hazards early, **O**rbit if unsure, **O**ut — go around when unsafe, **K**now and report to ATC.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On final to Congonhas helipad, you see a mobile crane in your approach path.

**Your first task**

Transmit your go-around call.

**Your second task**

Tower asks for the nature of the obstacle. What do you report?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report clearly: going around, obstacle on approach path, ANAC123. I would describe a mobile crane on final, request another approach clear of the crane, and confirm when ready.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Obstacle avoidance is early decision-making.

Look early.

Assess continuously.

Go around when unsafe.

Tell ATC why.

Report temporary hazards.

Never assume the path is clear.

Train your eyes. Speak clearly. Fly with margin.
