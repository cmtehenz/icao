# Carburetor Icing

**Catalog ID:** 0368  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Carburetor Icing — ice formation in the carburetor venturi of piston engines that restricts airflow and causes power loss. ICAO and ANAC exams still test this English extensively, even for turbine pilots.

The H130 is a turbine helicopter. It does not have a carburetor. The operational parallel for turbine crews is engine inlet icing / induction icing: ice at the engine air intake that can reduce mass flow and threaten flameout or power loss. Learn carburetor icing language for the exam; apply inlet-ice discipline in the H130.

For Brazilian operations — humid coastal air, temperature-dewpoint spreads near zero, descents through moist layers — induction icing risk remains real on turbine aircraft when flying in visible moisture near freezing.

Understanding carburetor icing phraseology and the turbine equivalent prepares you for Part 2 radio calls and Part 3 speaking questions.

## 📖 Meaning

Carburetor Icing is the formation of ice in the carburetor throat of a piston engine caused by temperature drop in the venturi and fuel vaporization, which can occur even in above-freezing ambient temperatures when humidity is high, restricting airflow and causing rough running or power loss. On turbine aircraft such as the H130, the related hazard is engine inlet or induction icing.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gelo no carburador / Carburetor Icing.

Formação de gelo na garganta do carburador de motor a pistão causada pela queda de temperatura no venturi e pela vaporização do combustível, podendo ocorrer mesmo com temperatura ambiente acima de zero quando a umidade é alta, restringindo o fluxo de ar e causando funcionamento irregular ou perda de potência. Em aeronaves a turbina como o H130, o risco correspondente é gelo na entrada de ar do motor (inlet / induction icing).

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When discussing or managing carburetor / induction icing risk, the crew should:

- for piston training or exam answers: apply carburetor heat per AFM when power loss or roughness suggests carb ice
- for H130 turbine operations: monitor for inlet icing cues in visible moisture near freezing; follow AFM anti-ice / engine ice protection procedures
- report power loss or engine icing concerns to ATC if performance or diversion is affected
- exit icing or high-humidity freezing layers when induction ice is suspected
- avoid prolonged flight in conditions conducive to inlet ice without protection selected as required
- divert if engine performance cannot be restored

Carburetor / inlet icing communication commonly includes:

- partial power loss, suspect carburetor icing (exam / piston context)
- engine inlet icing, requesting descent or diversion (turbine / H130)
- applying carburetor heat / engine anti-ice as applicable
- engine running rough, requesting priority landing

**Important:** Exam English expects carburetor icing, carburetor heat, and the classic temperature-humidity envelope. Operational English for the H130 should emphasize turbine inlet icing and AFM engine ice protection — do not invent carburetor heat on a turbine. Know both vocabularies and which aircraft you are flying.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to carburetor or engine icing reports to:

- provide priority landing or diversion when power is reduced
- clear altitude changes to warmer or drier air
- alert other aircraft to icing or induction-ice conditions
- coordinate emergency services if engine failure is possible
- understand why a pilot requests an immediate landing without assuming a full engine failure yet

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam scenario language still uses piston cues. In the H130, you translate to turbine reality.

You are descending through moist cloud toward Santos Dumont. Temperature is plus two Celsius, high humidity. You select engine anti-ice per AFM. Torque fluctuates. You suspect inlet icing.

You transmit:

Santos Approach, ANAC123, possible engine inlet icing, partial power fluctuation, request priority approach runway two zero.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Carburetor icing is exam English. Inlet icing is H130 English. You need both.

On the test: carburetor ice can form above freezing when moist air cools in the venturi. Carburetor heat melts ice and restores power. Classic answer.

In the aircraft you fly: the H130 has no carburetor. Moisture and freezing temperatures threaten the engine inlet. AFM anti-ice procedures, exit from icing, and honest radio calls about power fluctuation matter.

Brazilian coastal humidity makes induction ice a real conversation — not only winter plateau cold.

Never say "carburetor heat selected" on a turbine frequency unless you are in a piston. Say what is true: engine anti-ice on, possible inlet icing, requesting priority if power is unstable.

Examiners want the piston concept. Captains want the turbine truth. Carry both.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Explain carburetor icing and how the concept applies to a turbine helicopter like the H130.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Carburetor icing is ice forming in the carburetor of a piston engine due to cooling in the venturi, which can cause power loss even when outside air temperature is above freezing if humidity is high. Pilots apply carburetor heat to restore airflow. The H130 is a turbine and has no carburetor, but a similar risk is engine inlet or induction icing in visible moisture near freezing. I would follow the AFM engine anti-ice procedures, exit icing if performance is affected, and report power fluctuations to ATC with a request for priority landing or diversion if needed.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Applying piston "carb heat" language to the H130 on the radio.  
  ✔ Use engine anti-ice / inlet icing for turbine operations; reserve carburetor terms for exams or piston aircraft.

- ❌ Ignoring power roughness in moist near-freezing air.  
  ✔ Treat unexplained power loss as induction ice until proven otherwise; follow AFM.

- ❌ Saying only "engine problem" without icing context.  
  ✔ Specify possible carburetor or inlet icing and the operational request.

- ❌ Confusing carburetor ice with structural airframe ice.  
  ✔ Carburetor/inlet ice affects engine airflow; structural ice affects airframe and rotors — report the correct hazard.
