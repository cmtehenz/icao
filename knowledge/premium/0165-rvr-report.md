# RVR Report

**Catalog ID:** 0165  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers RVR Report — the meteorological report of Runway Visual Range, the measured distance over which a pilot on the runway centerline can see runway surface markings or runway lights.

RVR is not the same as meteorological visibility.

Meteorological visibility is a general measure of horizontal visibility in the atmosphere.

RVR is the specific measurement of how far down the runway a pilot can see from the touchdown zone — taken directly in the path of the landing or departing aircraft.

At Brazilian airports including Congonhas, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and São Paulo, RVR is reported for Cat I, Cat II, and Cat III instrument approaches and is the definitive visibility reference for approach and landing minima decisions.

## 📖 Meaning

RVR — Runway Visual Range — is the distance over which the pilot of an aircraft on the centerline of a runway can see the runway surface markings or the lights delineating the runway, measured and reported by instruments positioned adjacent to the runway touchdown zone.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives an RVR report, they should:

- confirm which runway and which measurement zone the RVR applies to: touchdown, mid-point, or roll-out
- compare the reported RVR to the published approach minima for the procedure being flown
- determine if the RVR meets or exceeds the required RVR for the approach category
- brief the crew on the visibility decision: is the RVR at or above minima?
- if RVR is below published minima, do not commence or continue the approach to landing
- advise ATC if electing to hold for improving conditions or divert

RVR reporting zones:

- touchdown zone RVR: the primary measurement — visibility at the point of landing
- mid-runway RVR: relevant for longer runways and rollout after landing
- rollout zone RVR: relevant for Cat III operations and runway exit planning

RVR values are reported in meters at Brazilian airports:

- Cat I minimum RVR: five hundred and fifty meters typical
- Cat II minimum RVR: three hundred meters typical
- Cat III minimum RVR: as low as seventy-five meters or less

**Important:** If the RVR drops below published minima after you have passed the outer marker or are on final, the decision to continue is governed by the rules of the applicable operation. In Brazil, crews must not commence an approach if RVR is below the required minimum. If RVR drops during the approach, the continued approach is governed by operational policies and ANAC regulations.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers provide RVR reports to:

- give crews the precise visibility value for the runway in use
- enable the crew to make informed approach/hold/divert decisions
- satisfy regulatory requirements for approach commencement and continuation
- update crews when RVR is improving or deteriorating
- coordinate approaches when visibility is near or below minima

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are holding at flight level eight zero awaiting weather at Congonhas in your H130.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, current RVR runway one seven, touchdown five hundred, mid-runway four hundred, improving.

You check your ILS approach plate: Cat I minimum RVR is five hundred and fifty meters at touchdown.

Current RVR is five hundred — below Cat I minimum.

You respond:

ANAC123, RVR below minima, continuing to hold.

Fifteen minutes later:

ANAC123, RVR runway one seven, touchdown six hundred and fifty, mid-runway six hundred, improving.

Now the RVR is above Cat I minimum.

You respond:

ANAC123, RVR at and above minima, request approach clearance.

Approach: ANAC123, cleared ILS runway one seven.

The RVR report gave you the precise data to make the correct, professional decision.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

RVR is the precise, instrument-measured visibility value that determines whether your approach is legal.

Not a tower observation. Not a general METAR visibility. Not what the captain thinks he can see from the holding area.

The RVR instrument at the runway touchdown zone measures exactly what matters for the approach minima.

Know your published RVR minimum for every approach you might fly. Have it memorized or immediately accessible on the approach plate.

When ATC gives you an RVR — compare it immediately to your minimum. At or above: approach is authorized. Below: hold or divert.

In Brazil, RVR values are always in meters. In the USA, they may be in feet. Know which unit applies to the airspace you are operating in.

RVR can fluctuate rapidly in fog and low-cloud conditions. A value of six hundred may drop to three hundred in five minutes. Monitor the trend: is it improving or deteriorating? ATC often provides the trend — use that information.

In the H130, flying operations in São Paulo and Congonhas in the winter fog season, RVR awareness is a daily operational requirement.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC reports the RVR at the touchdown zone is four hundred meters. Your Cat I ILS approach requires a minimum RVR of five hundred and fifty meters. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

With an RVR of four hundred meters and a Cat I minimum of five hundred and fifty meters, the current visibility is below approach minima. I may not commence the approach. I would advise ATC: ANAC123, RVR below Cat I minima, request to continue holding. I would note the RVR trend — is it improving or deteriorating? If improving, I would hold and wait for it to reach five hundred and fifty meters before commencing. If deteriorating or stable below minima, I would consider diverting to my alternate. I would not attempt the approach at four hundred meters expecting to "see enough" — the published minimum is a regulatory and safety threshold.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Confusing general meteorological visibility with RVR.  
  ✔ RVR is specific to the runway touchdown zone. It is more precise and the correct reference for approach minima.

- ❌ Commencing an approach when RVR is reported below published minima.  
  ✔ Below minima: do not commence. Hold or divert.

- ❌ Not knowing the published RVR minimum for the approach being flown.  
  ✔ Brief the RVR minimum before commencing the approach. Have it immediately accessible.

- ❌ Ignoring RVR trend information — whether improving or deteriorating.  
  ✔ Trend is operationally important. Improving RVR justifies holding. Rapidly deteriorating RVR suggests diversion.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Congonhas ATIS reports: "RVR runway one seven touchdown four hundred and fifty meters, mid-runway four hundred meters, conditions deteriorating."

Your Cat I ILS approach minimum RVR is five hundred and fifty meters touchdown.

**Your first task**

What is your immediate operational decision and what do you transmit to ATC?

**Your second task**

Twenty minutes later the RVR improves to six hundred meters touchdown, five hundred and fifty meters mid-runway. ATC transmits: "ANAC123, RVR runway one seven, touchdown six hundred, improving." What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

For the first task: with RVR at four hundred and fifty meters, below my Cat I minimum of five hundred and fifty meters, and conditions deteriorating, I would not commence the approach. I would transmit: ANAC123, RVR below Cat I minima, request hold for improving conditions or advise on alternate. The deteriorating trend adds urgency — I would also calculate my fuel state and set a decision time: if RVR does not reach minima within my hold fuel endurance, I will divert to my alternate. For the second task: with RVR at six hundred meters — above my Cat I minimum — and improving, I would request the approach: ANAC123, RVR at and above Cat I minima, request ILS runway one seven. The improving trend supports the approach commencement.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

RVR is the precise measurement that protects your approach.

It is not approximate. It is not a general estimate. It is the instrument-measured visibility at the exact point where your landing decisions are made.

Know your minimum. Compare it immediately when ATC provides the value.

At or above: you may commence. Below: you may not.

Monitor the trend. Plan the fuel. Make the decision early — not when you are already on final in deteriorating conditions.

RVR awareness is professional meteorological judgment applied to every low-visibility operation.
