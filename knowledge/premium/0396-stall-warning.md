# Stall Warning

**Catalog ID:** 0396  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Stall Warning — the aural and visual alert that the aircraft is approaching a critical angle of attack and is near aerodynamic stall.

Stall Warning is not a suggestion. It is the last reliable cue before the wing or rotor system loses lift authority.

In fixed-wing ICAO exam English, stall warning means the aircraft is approaching stall and immediate recovery action is required. For H130 helicopter operations, the equivalent discipline is recognizing low rotor RPM, settling with power, and critical angle-of-attack cues — and communicating the emergency clearly to ATC.

Understanding how to report a stall warning, declare the emergency, and state intentions in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Stall Warning is an aural and/or visual system alert that activates when the aircraft approaches a critical angle of attack, indicating that aerodynamic stall is imminent and that the pilot must reduce angle of attack and recover immediately.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Alerta sonoro e/ou visual que se ativa quando a aeronave se aproxima de um ângulo de ataque crítico, indicando que o estol aerodinâmico é iminente e que o piloto deve reduzir o ângulo de ataque e recuperar imediatamente.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a Stall Warning activates, the crew should:

- reduce angle of attack immediately — lower the nose, add power as required
- roll wings level if banked, then recover to a safe flight path
- do not chase altitude until flying speed and angle of attack are restored
- declare the emergency to ATC once the aircraft is under control
- advise intentions: continuing, going around, or diverting

Stall Warning reporting commonly includes:

- stall warning on approach or departure
- recovering, going around
- request altitude or vectors for recovery
- mayday or pan-pan if control is threatened

**Important:** Aviate first. Silence the warning by recovering the aircraft, then communicate. Never continue an approach with an active stall warning.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to Stall Warning reports to:

- clear traffic from the recovery path
- issue go-around or climb clearances without delay
- alert other aircraft to possible missed approach or unusual maneuver
- coordinate runway and airspace for an emergency return
- provide vectors and altitude blocks for safe recovery

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on final to Congonhas in a training scenario discussing fixed-wing stall warning English, while operating your H130 on a parallel IFR approach profile.

On short final, you recognize a critical low-energy condition and initiate a go-around.

You transmit:

Congonhas Tower, ANAC123, going around, stall warning, recovering, request climb two thousand.

Tower clears the missed approach and warns following traffic.

You recover, climb, and request vectors for another approach when ready.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger stall warning, cleared go-around, climb and maintain two thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions after recovery.
3. Tower: ANAC123, traffic advised, runway vacated for your return when ready.
4. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading three six zero, climb three thousand, report when ready for approach.
5. Tower: ANAC123, wind calm, runway one seven left, cleared to land when ready.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Stall warning is the aircraft telling you the wing — or your energy state — is about to quit.

In fixed-wing exam English, the response is textbook: reduce angle of attack, add power, wings level, recover, then talk.

In the H130, you may not hear a classic stick-shaker stall warning, but the principle is identical. Low rotor RPM, settling with power, or a critical approach attitude demands the same priority: fly the aircraft first.

Brazilian crews sometimes delay the call because they are still fighting the recovery. That is correct for the first seconds — then speak. ATC cannot protect you if they do not know you are recovering from a near-stall condition.

Say the words clearly: stall warning, going around, recovering. Short. Precise. ICAO Level 4.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You receive a stall warning on short final. Describe what you do and what you say to ATC.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would recover immediately by reducing angle of attack, adding power, and establishing a climb. Once the aircraft is under control, I would transmit: ANAC123, going around, stall warning, recovering, request climb two thousand. I would not continue the approach with an active stall warning. After recovery, I would advise intentions for another approach or diversion.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing the approach after stall warning because the runway is in sight.  
  ✔ Go around. Stall warning means the approach is no longer safe.

- ❌ Making a long explanation before recovering.  
  ✔ Aviate first. Communicate after the aircraft is flying again.

- ❌ Saying only "going around" without the reason.  
  ✔ State the reason: stall warning — so ATC can protect the airspace.

- ❌ Using Portuguese on the frequency during the emergency.  
  ✔ Use ICAO English: stall warning, recovering, going around.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Going Around, Stall Warning, Recovering

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, GO-ing uh-ROUND, STAWL WOR-ning, ree-KUH-vuh-ring

**Word Stress**

- Stall → STAWL
- Warning → WOR-ning (stress on first)
- Recovering → ree-KUH-vuh-ring (stress on second)

Practice:

Going around... stall warning... recovering.

Together:

ANAC123, going around, stall warning, recovering, request climb two thousand.

Stall warning is not a training buzzword. It is the edge of controlled flight.

Recover first. Speak second. Never continue an approach with the warning active.

In exam English, use the fixed-wing vocabulary ATC expects worldwide. In the H130, apply the same urgency to energy and rotor management.

Know the words. Fly the recovery. Call it clearly.
