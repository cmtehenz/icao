# Vocabulary

**Catalog ID:** 0498  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Vocabulary — the ICAO language skill that measures whether you have enough words and paraphrases to discuss common and uncommon operational topics clearly, including when standard phraseology is not enough.

Vocabulary is range plus precision. You need the right aviation term when it exists, and a plain-language paraphrase when the exact word does not come.

For H130 ICAO Delta candidates, helicopter-specific vocabulary — hover, sling, winch, confined area — must sit beside core IFR and emergency lexicon for Part 1 success.

Understanding vocabulary as an ICAO skill is essential for Part 1 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Vocabulary is the ICAO language proficiency skill that evaluates the breadth and accuracy of words and phrases a speaker can use and paraphrase to communicate effectively on familiar and unfamiliar aeronautical topics in both phraseology and plain language.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Vocabulário / Vocabulary.

Habilidade de proficiência linguística da OACI que avalia a amplitude e a precisão das palavras e expressões que o falante consegue usar e parafrasear para comunicar-se com eficácia em tópicos aeronáuticos familiares e não familiares, tanto em fraseologia quanto em linguagem clara.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When demonstrating vocabulary, the pilot should:

- use standard phraseology terms correctly first
- switch to precise plain language for abnormals
- paraphrase when a rare word will not come — do not freeze
- for H130: master hover, vortex ring, sling, winch, helideck terms
- avoid false friends from Portuguese that change meaning
- build topic banks: weather, systems, emergencies, CRM

Vocabulary work commonly includes:

- ICAO phraseology lexicon
- aircraft systems plain-language terms
- weather and aerodrome vocabulary
- paraphrase strategies (in other words...)
- precision: leak vs smoke vs fire

**Important:** Level 4 requires sufficient vocabulary for familiar and unfamiliar topics, with paraphrase ability when needed. Silence while searching for a perfect word is worse than a clear paraphrase.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers need precise pilot vocabulary to:

- distinguish urgency: medical, technical, fuel, weather
- understand helicopter-specific requests and limits
- avoid ambiguity between similar conditions
- act correctly on abnormal plain-language reports
- reduce clarification cycles on congested frequencies

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

In Part 1 you must describe an H130 with a burning smell in the cabin but no visible flames.

Weak vocabulary says “problem with smoke maybe fire.” Strong vocabulary distinguishes: burning odour, no visible smoke, no confirmed fire, requesting priority landing and emergency services as a precaution.

On frequency that precision changes the response. Vocabulary is operational, not academic.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, confirm nature of problem.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm smoke, fire, or fumes.
3. Tower: ANAC123, emergency services available, advise requirements.
4. Control: ANAC123, say again using plain language.
5. Approach: ANAC123, request souls on board and fuel remaining.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, burning odour in cabin, no visible fire, request priority landing.
2. ANAC123, fumes only, negative fire at this time.
3. ANAC123, request emergency services on arrival.
4. ANAC123, technical problem, request vectors shortest route.
5. ANAC123, souls on board five, fuel remaining five zero minutes.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Vocabulary is the right tool, not the longest word.

Examiners listen for precision: divert is not the same as delay; fumes are not the same as fire; unable is not the same as standby.

When the exact term disappears under stress, paraphrase immediately: “the system that controls the rotor speed” is better than silence while you hunt for a memory.

In the H130, build a personal lexicon for external load, EMS, and deck work. Part 1 topics love helicopter specificity — reward it with accurate words.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How can a pilot manage vocabulary limitations during an ICAO English test or on frequency?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I use standard phraseology when it fits, and precise plain language for abnormals. If I cannot recall a rare term, I paraphrase clearly instead of stopping. For example, I can describe fumes without visible fire and request priority landing. The goal is accurate meaning, not perfect wording.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Freezing when a technical word will not come.  
  ✔ Paraphrase immediately and continue.

- ❌ Using Portuguese false friends (e.g., “actual” for current).  
  ✔ Learn high-risk false friends and prefer plain clear terms.

- ❌ Overgeneralizing: “problem” for every abnormal.  
  ✔ Specify hydraulic, electrical, weather, medical when known.

- ❌ Mixing informal slang into professional answers.  
  ✔ Keep aviation-appropriate vocabulary in Part 1 and on frequency.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You detect a burning odour in the H130 cabin with no visible smoke or flames. Approach asks you to confirm the nature of the problem.

**Your first task**

What precise call do you make?

**Your second task**

If you forget the word “odour/odor” in a Part 1 answer, how do you paraphrase?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, burning odour in cabin, no visible fire, request priority landing. Second task: I would say there is a burning smell in the cabin without smoke or flames, and I am requesting priority landing — paraphrase keeps meaning moving when a single word fails.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Vocabulary is precision under pressure.

Phraseology when it fits. Plain language when it must. Paraphrase when a word disappears.

In Part 1, specific beats vague. On frequency, specific gets the right emergency response.

In the H130, learn the words for the missions you fly. The lexicon you build on the ground is the one you will still have in the climb.
