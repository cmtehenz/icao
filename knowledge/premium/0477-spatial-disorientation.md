# Spatial Disorientation

**Catalog ID:** 0477  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Spatial Disorientation — a condition in which the pilot's perception of aircraft attitude, motion, or position conflicts with reality, often without immediate self-awareness of the conflict.

Spatial disorientation is not a lack of skill. It is physiology defeating intuition when visual references are lost or misleading. Night, IMC, acceleration, and somatogravic cues can all create a false sense of climb, bank, or level flight.

In H130 operations over water, at night toward unlit pads, or in sudden IMC near terrain, spatial disorientation is a critical threat. The recovery is always the same: trust instruments, not sensations.

Understanding how to declare and recover from spatial disorientation in ICAO-standard English — including emergency climb requests and control-transfer calls — is essential for Part 1, Part 2, and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Spatial Disorientation is a state in which the pilot is unable to correctly sense the aircraft's attitude, altitude, or motion relative to the Earth, leading to inappropriate control inputs unless instruments and trained instrument procedures are followed.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Desorientação espacial / Spatial Disorientation.

Estado em que o piloto é incapaz de perceber corretamente a atitude, altitude ou movimento da aeronave em relação à Terra, levando a comandos inadequados a menos que se sigam os instrumentos e os procedimentos de voo por instrumentos treinados.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When spatial disorientation is recognized or suspected, the crew should:

- transition immediately to instruments — attitude indicator is primary
- ignore vestibular and proprioceptive "feelings" of climb or bank
- transfer control if the flying pilot is disoriented and the other pilot is oriented
- level the wings, set power, and establish a known pitch attitude
- advise ATC if climb, vectors, or priority are required to recover safely

Disorientation defenses include:

- maintaining instrument proficiency for night and IMC H130 operations
- avoiding abrupt acceleration in IMC without instrument cross-check
- using autopilot or stability augmentation if available and appropriate
- briefing night/water approaches as high disorientation risk

**Important:** Believing you are "mostly oriented" is often the trap. If instruments and body disagree, the body is wrong. Fly the instruments through the conflict until visual references are reliable again.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support disoriented crews by:

- providing no-gyro or simple heading and altitude instructions
- clearing immediate climbs away from terrain
- reducing secondary tasks and frequency changes during recovery
- offering radar vectors to VMC or a known approach
- coordinating priority handling when a pilot declares inability to maintain attitude by visual references

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Night transit over water toward an unlit coastal site. You feel a strong nose-up sensation after accelerating. You push forward. The altimeter unwinds.

Your first officer calls: "Instruments — nose low."

You recover on the attitude indicator and transmit:

ANAC123, spatial disorientation, climbing to three thousand, request vectors.

Radar: ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand, turn right heading zero nine zero, radar vectors.

You respond:

ANAC123, climbing three thousand, heading zero nine zero.

Sensation said climb. Instruments said dive. Instruments won.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand, vectors available.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able to maintain altitude by instruments.
3. Radar: ANAC123, turn right heading zero nine zero, no-gyro vectors if required.
4. Control: ANAC123, priority handling approved, say intentions.
5. Approach: ANAC123, climb immediately, terrain ahead.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, climbing three thousand, request vectors.
2. ANAC123, maintaining altitude on instruments.
3. ANAC123, heading zero nine zero, requesting no-gyro if needed.
4. ANAC123, requesting priority, recovering spatial disorientation.
5. ANAC123, climbing immediately, terrain awareness noted.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Spatial disorientation is a body lie.

Your inner ear will invent banks and climbs with perfect confidence. That confidence is the danger.

In the H130, especially at night over water, the attitude indicator is the horizon. Full stop.

If you are the disoriented pilot, say so and give control. Pride is a poor attitude reference.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

At night you feel pitched up but instruments show a descent. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would reject the sensation, fly the attitude indicator to a safe climb or level attitude, and advise ATC: ANAC123, spatial disorientation, climbing to three thousand, request vectors. If I could not reliably interpret instruments, I would transfer control to the other pilot immediately.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Trusting "seat of the pants" in IMC or on dark nights.  
  ✔ Fly attitude, power, and performance instruments as primary.

- ❌ Keeping control while disoriented to avoid embarrassment.  
  ✔ Transfer control as soon as orientation is in doubt.

- ❌ Making large abrupt corrections based on body cues.  
  ✔ Use smooth instrument recovery to a known attitude.

- ❌ Delaying the ATC call until the upset is severe.  
  ✔ Declare early and request climb or vectors while recoverable.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On a night overwater transit you feel strongly nose-up after accelerating. Instruments show a descent. Terrain is inland to your left.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC as you recover on instruments?

**Your second task**

You remain unsure of your orientation. What do you say regarding control and priority?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, spatial disorientation, climbing three thousand, request vectors. Second task: ANAC123, requesting priority, first officer flying, recovering on instruments. I would not push on a false climb sensation. Instruments and an early declaration keep the H130 out of the water.
