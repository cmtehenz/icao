# V1 Decision Speed

**Catalog ID:** 0393  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers V1 Decision Speed — the takeoff decision speed used in multi-engine aeroplane performance: the maximum speed at which the pilot must take the first action to reject the takeoff, and above which the takeoff is continued after an engine failure, within certified limits.

V1 is fixed-wing / multi-engine exam language. It appears throughout ICAO Part 2 and Part 4 performance discussions.

For H130 operations: the helicopter does not use V1. Reject or continue decisions use helicopter-specific cues — rotor RPM, engine parameters, airspeed, and takeoff profile — not V1. Learn V1 for ICAO English proficiency; do not invent V1 calls in H130 radio work.

## 📖 Meaning

V1 is the decision speed specified by the aeroplane performance calculation at which, in the event of an engine failure or other serious malfunction, the crew must have begun the reject if aborting, or otherwise continue the takeoff, based on accelerate-stop and accelerate-go performance.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When discussing V1, the crew should:

- calculate V1 with takeoff performance data (aeroplane)
- brief reject below V1 / continue above V1 before takeoff
- call "V1" as a standard callout on the roll (aeroplane SOP)
- for H130: brief helicopter reject criteria instead — not V1
- use correct exam definitions without misapplying them to the H130

V1-related concepts include:

- accelerate-stop distance available vs required
- engine failure at or after V1 — continue takeoff (aeroplane)
- wet runway and weight effects on V1
- V1 cannot exceed VR; relationships with V2
- H130: reject for engine failure, fire, or critical malfunction per AFM — no V1

**Important:** Saying "V1" on an H130 frequency confuses ATC and examiners who know your type. Use accurate helicopter language in ops; use V1 definitions in the oral exam when asked about aeroplane performance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

ATC does not assign V1, but controllers are affected when:

- a rejected takeoff occurs below V1 (aeroplane)
- an aircraft continues after engine failure and declares emergency
- runway is occupied after a high-speed reject
- emergency services are required after abort or continued takeoff with failure
- crews report performance-limited departure affecting sequence

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style aeroplane context: at V1 the captain's hand leaves the thrust levers — continue.

H130 Congonhas departure: engine parameters decay on the takeoff. There is no V1 call. You reject per helicopter procedure and advise:

ANAC123, rejecting takeoff, engine problem, stopping on runway.

Tower: ANAC123, roger, emergency services as required.

You used operational truth — not borrowed jet callouts.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is V1? Does the H130 use V1?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

V1 is the takeoff decision speed on multi-engine aeroplanes: the speed by which a reject must have been initiated if aborting, and above which the takeoff is normally continued after engine failure, based on performance calculations. The H130 does not use V1; reject or continue decisions follow helicopter AFM procedures and engine or rotor indications. I use V1 language for ICAO exams and aeroplane theory, not for H130 radio calls.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using V1 callouts in H130 operations.  
  ✔ Use helicopter reject phraseology: rejecting takeoff, [reason].

- ❌ Defining V1 only as "rotation speed."  
  ✔ V1 is decision speed; VR is rotation. They are related but not identical.

- ❌ Ignoring wet runway effects on V1 in exam answers.  
  ✔ Mention weight, wind, and surface effects on V1 calculation.

- ❌ Confusing V1 with V2.  
  ✔ V1 = decision; V2 = takeoff safety speed after liftoff.
