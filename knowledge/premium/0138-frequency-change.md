# Frequency Change

**Catalog ID:** 0138  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Frequency Change — the procedure and phraseology used when a pilot changes from one ATC frequency to another.

A Frequency Change may be initiated by ATC with a Contact or Monitor instruction, or by the pilot when leaving a frequency to check in with another unit.

In the H130, flying through Brazilian airspace between Florianópolis, Joinville, Navegantes, and São Paulo, frequency changes are a constant part of every IFR flight.

Executing frequency changes professionally — with correct readbacks, proper departure announcements, and immediate check-in procedures — is a fundamental ICAO Part 2 skill.

## 📖 Meaning

Frequency Change is the procedure by which a pilot transfers radio contact from one ATC frequency to another, either at ATC direction or by standard procedure, including readback, acknowledgment, and initial contact on the new frequency.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Mudança de frequência.

Procedimento pelo qual o piloto transfere o contato de rádio de uma frequência ATC para outra, seja por instrução do controle ou por procedimento padrão, incluindo readback, reconhecimento e contato inicial na nova frequência.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When executing a Frequency Change, the pilot should:

- read back the new frequency and unit name
- acknowledge the releasing controller (e.g., "good day")
- switch to the new frequency promptly
- make a full opening call to the receiving unit
- include callsign, altitude, routing, and any relevant status
- not leave the current frequency before readback is acknowledged

Standard Frequency Change scenarios include:

- sector handoffs during cruise
- transition from en-route to approach control
- transition from approach to tower
- transition from tower to ground
- pilot-initiated changes when entering new airspace

**Important:** Never switch frequency in silence. If ATC has not explicitly released you, announce your intention: ANAC123, leaving your frequency. This prevents controllers from calling you on an empty channel and initiating unnecessary lost contact procedures.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers manage frequency changes to:

- transfer jurisdiction to the appropriate sector
- ensure continuity of radar service and separation
- prevent aircraft from operating in a sector without check-in
- avoid frequency congestion by distributing traffic across sectors
- maintain coordination between adjacent control units

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are at flight level eight zero, twenty minutes from Joinville, talking to Curitiba Control.

Control transmits:

ANAC123, contact Joinville Approach on one two one decimal three, good day.

You reply:

Contact Joinville Approach one two one decimal three, ANAC123, good day.

You set your secondary radio to 121.3, confirm the frequency, and switch primary.

You call:

Joinville Approach, ANAC123, flight level eight zero, descending, inbound Joinville, estimating one five.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, Joinville Approach, radar contact, expect ILS runway two seven, descend to altitude four thousand.

You reply:

Expect ILS runway two seven, descend altitude four thousand, ANAC123.

Frequency change complete. Professional, fast, unambiguous.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, contact Florianópolis Approach on one one nine decimal zero, good day.
2. Approach: ANAC123, contact Florianópolis Tower one one eight decimal one, good day.
3. Tower: ANAC123, contact Congonhas Ground one two one decimal nine.
4. Control: ANAC123, leaving our frequency, squawk VFR, frequency change approved.
5. Approach: ANAC123, contact Navegantes Tower one one eight decimal three, cleared to land.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The frequency change is where many pilots lose professionalism.

They get the Contact instruction, say "Roger", switch, and call the new frequency with just their callsign.

No altitude. No routing. No position.

The receiving controller now has an anonymous blip and a callsign.

They must ask: ANAC123, say altitude?

That is an extra transmission caused by an incomplete opening call.

Train the full opening call: callsign, altitude, routing.

Also train the farewell: read back correctly, say good day if the controller did, then switch.

In the H130 operating into Congonhas or Florianópolis, approach sectors are busy. An incomplete check-in can delay your sequencing.

Give the new controller everything they need in one call.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How do you professionally execute a frequency change when ATC instructs contact on a new frequency?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I read back the unit and frequency, acknowledge the releasing controller with a good day if appropriate, then switch promptly. On the new frequency I transmit a full opening call with my callsign, current altitude, routing, and intention. I do not say just my callsign. I give the receiving controller sufficient information to identify and service my aircraft immediately.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "Roger" and switching without reading back the frequency.  
  ✔ Always read back: Contact [unit] [frequency], ANAC123.

- ❌ Opening on the new frequency with only a callsign.  
  ✔ Include altitude and routing: ANAC123, flight level eight zero, inbound Joinville.

- ❌ Switching frequency before the current controller acknowledges the readback.  
  ✔ Wait for the acknowledgment or "good day" before switching.

- ❌ Not announcing departure when leaving a frequency without explicit release.  
  ✔ Transmit: ANAC123, leaving your frequency. Especially in uncontrolled or advisory airspace.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Contact Joinville Approach one two one decimal three, ANAC123

**Pronunciation:** KON-takt ZHOIN-vil ah-PROACH WUN TOO WUN DEH-sih-mal THREE

**Word Stress**

- Contact → KON-takt
- Joinville → ZHOIN-vil (Portuguese proper noun — use natural Brazilian pronunciation)
- Decimal → DEH-sih-mal
- Three → THREE (not "tree" in Brazilian Portuguese accented English — but both acceptable in ICAO)

Practice:

Contact Joinville... Contact Joinville Approach...

Together:

Contact Joinville Approach one two one decimal three, ANAC123.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**SWITCH** — **S**ay the frequency back completely, **W**ait for acknowledgment, **I**mmediately tune and verify, **T**ransmit full opening call, **C**heck in received, **H**old ready for further instructions.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are at flight level seven zero inbound to Florianópolis. Curitiba Control says: "ANAC123, contact Florianópolis Approach on one one nine decimal zero, have a good day."

**Your first task**

What do you say before switching? What is your full opening call on the new frequency?

**Your second task**

Florianópolis Approach does not respond after two calls. The frequency seems quiet. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I transmit: Contact Florianópolis Approach one one nine decimal zero, ANAC123, good day. I switch and call: Florianópolis Approach, ANAC123, flight level seven zero, inbound Florianópolis, estimating one zero. If there is no response after two attempts, I try once more, then consider that I may have the wrong frequency. I return to the last frequency and request confirmation, or attempt the guard frequency at 121.5 to establish contact.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Every frequency change is a handshake between two controllers.

You are the carrier of that handshake.

Read back. Acknowledge. Switch. Call.

Four steps. Done in under thirty seconds.

The receiving controller has been handed a piece of paper with your callsign on it.

When you call with altitude and routing, you confirm everything on that paper.

When you call with just a callsign, you create uncertainty.

Be the pilot who eliminates uncertainty.

Every time.
