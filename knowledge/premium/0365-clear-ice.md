# Clear Ice

**Catalog ID:** 0365  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Clear Ice — hard, transparent ice that forms from larger supercooled droplets spreading before freezing, adhering strongly to the airframe and rotors and often difficult to see, especially at night or on dark surfaces.

Clear ice is high risk because it is dense, tenacious, and easy to underestimate visually. ICAO English must report clear ice or icing with intensity, altitude, and the request to exit the conditions.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, clear ice may form in freezing rain, freezing drizzle, or cumulus icing environments. Limited ice protection means early recognition and early exit.

Understanding clear ice phraseology is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Clear Ice is a dense, transparent or translucent ice formation caused by larger supercooled water droplets that freeze relatively slowly after impact, spreading over the surface and creating hard ice that strongly adheres to the aircraft and significantly degrades aerodynamic and rotor performance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gelo cristalino / Clear Ice.

Formação de gelo denso, transparente ou translúcido, causada por gotículas super-resfriadas maiores que congelam de forma relativamente lenta após o impacto, espalhando-se sobre a superfície e criando gelo duro que adere fortemente à aeronave e degrada significativamente o desempenho aerodinâmico e do rotor.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When clear ice is forming, the crew should:

- recognize rapid performance loss even if ice is hard to see
- activate ice protection if installed and approved for the conditions
- exit icing conditions with climb, descent, or diversion
- report clear ice or icing intensity, altitude, and temperature if useful
- avoid holds in continuous clear-ice accretion
- after landing, inspect for residual ice before next departure

Clear ice reporting commonly includes:

- clear ice / moderate icing / severe icing at altitude
- request climb or descent to exit icing
- request diversion
- ice accretion continuing / stopped

**Important:** Clear ice versus rime ice matters for meteorology and exams. For ATC under stress, intensity and exit request matter most — but know the term clear ice for Part 2/3 accuracy.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use icing and clear-ice PIREPs to:

- warn aircraft in the same altitude band
- approve prompt level changes
- keep holding stacks out of icing layers
- support diversion and priority handling when severe
- feed meteorology with special air reports

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

In cloud at four thousand feet near Curitiba in the H130, collective rises for the same airspeed and vibration changes. A landing light sweep shows smooth clear ice on the skid fairing.

You report moderate to severe icing, clear ice, and request descent to warmer air. Approach clears the descent. Accretion stops. You continue with a PIREP so the next aircraft does not linger in the same band.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, say type of icing and intensity if able.
2. Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain two thousand, warmer air reported.
3. Center: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported clear ice at four thousand.
4. Approach: ANAC123, divert approved, expect Approach on...
5. Approach: ANAC123, report when clear of icing conditions.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

2. ANAC123, severe icing, clear ice, request diversion.
3. ANAC123, descending two thousand, exiting icing conditions.
4. ANAC123, clear of icing, accretion stopped.
5. ANAC123, unable hold at four thousand, clear ice, request vectors.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Clear ice hides in plain sight.

It looks like wet glass. It flies like a weight and a shape change.

Say clear ice when you can identify it. Always say intensity and altitude.

Brazilian pilots often say only "gelo na célula." Prefer clear ice, moderate icing at four thousand, request descent.

In the H130, performance cues may arrive before a perfect visual ID — trust the aircraft, exit, then refine the PIREP.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is clear ice and how would you report it to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Clear ice is hard, transparent ice from larger supercooled droplets that spread before freezing and stick strongly to the aircraft. I would report clear ice with icing intensity and altitude, request climb or descent to exit the conditions, and divert if accretion continues or performance degrades.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Waiting for obvious white rime before reporting icing.  
  ✔ Clear ice may be transparent — report performance loss and icing early.

- ❌ Reporting "gelo" without intensity or altitude.  
  ✔ Include moderate/severe and altitude.

- ❌ Holding in continuous clear-ice accretion.  
  ✔ Request vectors clear of icing.

- ❌ Confusing clear ice with frost on a parked aircraft only.  
  ✔ Clear ice is an in-flight accretion hazard in supercooled droplets.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

In cloud at four thousand feet, performance degrades. You identify smooth clear ice on the airframe.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

After descent, accretion stops. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, clear ice at four thousand, moderate icing, request descent. Second task: ANAC123, clear of icing at two thousand, accretion stopped. I would divert if icing had continued or control margins had reduced further.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Clear ice is heavy, sticky, and easy to miss visually.

Trust performance. Name the ice. Exit the layer.

Report altitude and intensity so others avoid your mistake of lingering.

In the H130, early English and early descent beat late heroics.

See it or feel it — then leave it and say it.
