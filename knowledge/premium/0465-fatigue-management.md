# Fatigue Management

**Catalog ID:** 0465  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Fatigue Management — recognizing, mitigating, and communicating the effects of fatigue so that degraded performance does not silently enter the flight deck.

Fatigue slows reaction, narrows scanning, weakens decision making, and increases tolerance for risk. Crews often under-report it. ATC and operators cannot manage what the crew will not say.

For H130 operations — early EMS launches, long offshore days, multi-leg IFR — fatigue is a critical human factor. ICAO English must support Part 1 vocabulary, Part 2 operational calls when fatigue forces delay or diversion, and Part 4 crew discussion of fitness for duty.

## 📖 Meaning

Fatigue Management is the set of personal, crew, and organizational practices used to prevent, detect, and mitigate the effects of mental and physical fatigue on pilot performance, including fitness-for-duty decisions and operational adjustments when fatigue impairs safe flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gerenciamento de fadiga / Fatigue Management.

Conjunto de práticas pessoais, de tripulação e organizacionais usadas para prevenir, detectar e mitigar os efeitos da fadiga mental e física no desempenho do piloto, incluindo decisões de aptidão para o voo e ajustes operacionais quando a fadiga prejudica o voo seguro.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing fatigue, the crew should:

- assess fitness for duty before acceptance of the flight
- use controlled rest only if approved by operator SOP and regulations
- increase cross-checking and simplify tasks when fatigue is present
- avoid non-essential workload and protect sterile cockpit strictly
- if unsafe to continue, delay, divert, or remove yourself from duty and communicate

Fatigue management includes:

- sleep opportunity and circadian awareness
- caffeine timing and hydration as limited tactical tools
- task sharing to protect the flying pilot
- honest crew reports: "I need you to monitor closely" or "I am not fit"

**Important:** Fatigue is a safety report, not a weakness. Continuing while unsafe is the failure — not speaking up.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers may receive fatigue-related operational requests when crews:

- request delay before departure
- request simplified clearances or hold for rest planning on long flights
- divert due to crew duty or fitness limits
- declare inability to accept further approaches after long duty
- need priority handling when fatigue combines with weather or system issues

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

After a long multi-leg H130 day, you are offered one more IFR approach into deteriorating weather. Both pilots feel microsleep risk.

You decide fitness first.

Approach, ANAC123, unable another approach, diverting to alternate, request clearance.

Approach: ANAC123, diversion approved, climb and maintain four thousand, cleared to Congonhas.

You protected the operation. Fatigue was managed by changing the plan — and saying so.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Clearance: ANAC123, ready to copy clearance, advise when ready for departure.
2. Ground: ANAC123, expect departure delay, advise intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, expect multiple approaches due weather, advise fuel and intentions.
4. Approach: ANAC123, diversion approved, climb four thousand, contact control.
5. Tower: ANAC123, roger priority request, cleared to land runway two eight.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, request delay, not ready for departure.
2. ANAC123, unable multiple approaches, request divert.
3. ANAC123, diverting Congonhas, climbing four thousand.
4. ANAC123, request simple vectors, minimum communications.
5. ANAC123, request priority landing, crew fatigue, fuel remaining one hour.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fatigue lies. It says you are fine while your scan has already collapsed.

Respect the early signs: missed radio, fixation, irritability, long silences, slow math on fuel.

In the H130, vibration, heat, night NVG or unaided night work, and dense Brazilian traffic all amplify fatigue. Plan sleep. Protect duty limits. Do not hero through one more leg.

If you are marginal, increase monitoring and simplify. If you are unsafe, stop. The radio call is: delay, divert, or unable — not a medical essay on frequency.

Part 4 English includes telling your colleague: I need you to take the radios, or I am not fit. That sentence saves flights.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You recognize unsafe fatigue before a night approach in weather. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not commence an approach I cannot safely fly. I would coordinate with the other pilot and transmit something like: ANAC123, unable approach, request delay or divert to alternate. If already on duty limits, I would remove myself from the flight per regulations and operator policy. Safety first; clear intentions to ATC.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Hiding fatigue to appear professional.  
  ✔ Report fitness issues early; professionalism is honesty.

- ❌ Using fatigue as a vague radio story.  
  ✔ State the operational request: delay, divert, priority, unable.

- ❌ Continuing "just one more approach" when unsafe.  
  ✔ Change the plan before the approach begins.

- ❌ Skipping cross-checks because you "know the airport."  
  ✔ Increase cross-checks when fatigued; never reduce them.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Unable Another Approach, Request Divert

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, un-AY-bul uh-NUTH-er uh-PROACH, reh-KWEST di-VERT

**Word Stress**

- Unable → un-AY-bul
- Another → uh-NUTH-er
- Approach → uh-PROACH
- Divert → di-VERT

Practice:

Unable another approach... request divert.

Together:

ANAC123, unable another approach, diverting, request clearance.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You finish a long H130 duty day. Weather at destination requires a demanding night IFR approach. Both pilots feel unsafe fatigue.

**Your first task**

What operational decision do you make?

**Your second task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Do not accept the approach — delay if rest is possible, or divert/cancel per duty and fitness rules. Second task: ANAC123, unable approach due crew limitations, request diversion clearance to alternate — or request delay. Fatigue management is a decision communicated in clear ICAO English.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Fatigue management is human factors with operational teeth.

Detect it. Mitigate it. Stop when mitigation is not enough.

In the H130, long Brazilian days will tempt one more leg. Do not trade sleep debt for a night approach you cannot fly.

Say delay. Say divert. Say unable. That is professional English — and professional judgment.
