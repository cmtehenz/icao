# Standard Terminal Arrival

**Catalog ID:** 0152  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers the Standard Terminal Arrival Route — the STAR — the published instrument procedure that guides arriving aircraft from the en-route structure down into the terminal area of an airport.

The STAR connects the airways to the approach. It defines routing, altitudes, and speeds for the descent into the terminal area.

Brazilian airports including Congonhas, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and São Paulo all have published STARs that pilots must know, brief, and fly.

Understanding STARs — and how to discuss them in clear ICAO English — is fundamental to professional IFR operations.

This lesson bridges Part 2 phraseology with the Part 4 navigational knowledge required at ICAO Level 4.

## 📖 Meaning

A Standard Terminal Arrival Route is a published instrument flight procedure that routes arriving IFR traffic from the en-route phase of flight to the initial approach fix of the destination airport, providing coded lateral routing, altitude restrictions, and speed constraints published on the applicable chart.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Rota de chegada padrão para área terminal.

Procedimento de voo por instrumentos publicado que conduz o tráfego IFR em chegada da fase de rota até o fix de aproximação inicial do aeródromo de destino, fornecendo roteamento lateral codificado, restrições de altitude e limitações de velocidade publicadas na carta correspondente.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When flying a STAR, the crew should:

- load the correct STAR into the FMS or GPS before top of descent
- brief the lateral routing, the altitude restrictions at each fix, and the speed constraints
- comply with all published altitude and speed restrictions unless modified by ATC
- transition from en-route cruise altitude to terminal area descent following the STAR
- monitor FMS sequencing and cross-check published altitudes at each waypoint
- advise ATC of any inability to comply with restrictions

STAR operations commonly include:

- descending from cruise altitude through step-down altitude restrictions
- speed reductions to comply with terminal area speed limits
- routing to the initial approach fix, intermediate fix, or radar handoff point
- transition from FMS STAR guidance to vectors or approach clearance

**Important:** Receiving a STAR clearance does not authorize the crew to descend to the published altitudes. Altitude restrictions on a STAR are complied with only when the STAR is cleared and no conflicting ATC altitude assignment has been issued. ATC altitude clearances take precedence over STAR published altitudes unless Descend Via clearance is explicitly issued.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers assign STARs to:

- standardize and simplify arrival routing in complex terminal areas
- reduce controller workload by using pre-defined coded routes
- allow efficient transition from en-route airspace to approach control
- ensure structured descent profiles and speed management
- facilitate sequencing of multiple arrivals from different directions

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at flight level one two zero inbound to Curitiba in your H130.

Approach assigns you the STAR for runway one five.

Radar: ANAC123, cleared SAMOS TWO arrival, runway one five, descend and maintain flight level eight zero.

You load SAMOS TWO in the navigation display and brief the routing with your co-pilot: waypoints, altitude restrictions, the transition to the approach fix.

You respond:

Cleared SAMOS TWO arrival, runway one five, descend and maintain flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

You begin the descent, following the STAR routing toward Curitiba.

At each fix, you cross-check the published altitudes.

At the initial approach fix — the STAR is complete. The approach phase begins.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The STAR is the handshake between the en-route world and the terminal world.

It says: here is your path from the airways to the approach. Fly it.

But there is a trap that catches many pilots.

A STAR clearance is not a descent clearance.

You receive the STAR. You know the routing. But until ATC assigns you an altitude — you stay at your cleared altitude.

Unless ATC says "descend via" — which is a separate, more powerful instruction — published STAR altitudes are for guidance only until ATC assigns them.

In Brazilian practice, controllers commonly issue the STAR clearance and a separate descent instruction. Read back both.

In the H130, loading the STAR into the GPS gives you lateral guidance. But altitude management is yours — guided by ATC clearances, not solely by the published chart.

Brief the full STAR before top of descent. Know every waypoint. Know every restriction.

That way, when ATC says "cleared to cross SAMOS at or above flight level one zero zero," you already know where SAMOS is and what the restriction means.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is a STAR and how does receiving a STAR clearance differ from receiving a descent clearance?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A Standard Terminal Arrival Route is a published procedure that routes arriving IFR traffic from the en-route structure to the terminal area of the destination airport, with coded lateral routing, altitude restrictions, and speed constraints. Receiving a STAR clearance authorizes the crew to follow the published lateral routing. However, it does not automatically authorize descent to the published altitudes. ATC must issue a separate altitude clearance, or specifically clear the crew to descend via the STAR, before the altitude restrictions become binding. The crew must maintain their last cleared altitude until ATC assigns a new level, even after receiving the STAR clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Descending to STAR published altitudes immediately after receiving the STAR clearance.  
  ✔ Maintain cleared altitude until ATC issues a descent clearance or Descend Via clearance.

- ❌ Not briefing STAR waypoints and restrictions before top of descent.  
  ✔ Brief the full STAR during cruise, before descent begins.

- ❌ Failing to advise ATC when unable to comply with a STAR restriction.  
  ✔ Advise ATC promptly: ANAC123, unable to comply with SAMOS restriction, request amended clearance.

- ❌ Accepting a STAR without loading it into the navigation system.  
  ✔ Load and activate the STAR immediately after clearance receipt.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Cleared SAMOS TWO Arrival

**Pronunciation:** kleerd SAY-mos TOO uh-RY-val

**Word Stress**

- Cleared → kleerd
- SAMOS → SAY-mos (fix name, two syllables)
- TWO → TOO
- Arrival → uh-RY-val (stress on second syllable)

Practice:

Cleared... SAMOS TWO... Arrival.

Together:

Cleared SAMOS TWO arrival, descend and maintain flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

Speak the arrival designator clearly — both the fix name and the number.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**ROUTE** — **R**ead the STAR plate fully before descent, **O**wn the lateral routing, **U**nconfirmed altitudes need ATC clearance, **T**ransition to approach fix at the end, **E**xpect vectors or approach after the STAR.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Curitiba Control transmits: "ANAC123, cleared SAMOS TWO arrival, runway one five, descend and maintain flight level one zero zero."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete clearance.

**Your second task**

At flight level one zero zero, approaching SAMOS fix, you cannot meet the published restriction of eight thousand feet due to terrain avoidance. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would respond: Cleared SAMOS TWO arrival, runway one five, descend and maintain flight level one zero zero, ANAC123. If I could not meet the published restriction at SAMOS I would advise ATC: ANAC123, unable to meet SAMOS restriction, request amended clearance. I would not descend below my ATC-assigned level without a new clearance, and I would not violate the restriction without advising ATC.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

The STAR is your road into the terminal area.

Brief it fully before you need it.

Load it before top of descent.

Know every waypoint. Know every restriction.

And remember: the STAR gives you the road. ATC gives you the altitude.

Descend when cleared. Cross restrictions as assigned.

That combination — published routing plus ATC altitude clearances — is professional terminal arrival flying.
