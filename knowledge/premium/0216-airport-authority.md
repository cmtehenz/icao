# Airport Authority

**Catalog ID:** 0216  
**Category:** Ground Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Airport Authority — the aerodrome operator responsible for airport infrastructure, apron management, ground access, and operational permissions that sit outside ATC clearances alone.

Airport Authority is not the tower. Controllers manage air traffic; the airport authority manages the aerodrome as a facility — stands, gates, cargo areas, construction permits, and local operating rules. Pilots often hear "contact airport authority," "await airport authority," or "airport authority advises" when a ground issue needs the operator, not a taxi clearance.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations at Congonhas, Guarulhos, Santos Dumont, Curitiba, and Florianópolis, you may need airport authority for parking assignment, apron access after hours, temporary helipad use, or coordination when a stand is blocked. Understanding the distinction between ATC and airport authority keeps radio work clean and avoids asking tower for decisions they do not own.

This lesson builds Part 2 proficiency in recognizing, acknowledging, and correctly routing communications that involve the airport authority.

## 📖 Meaning

Airport Authority is the organization that operates and manages the aerodrome, including aprons, stands, ground access, and local aerodrome rules; in radiotelephony it refers to that operator when ATC directs the pilot to contact, await, or follow advice from the airport authority rather than from ATC alone.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Airport authority / Autoridade aeroportuária (operador do aeródromo).

Organização que opera e administra o aeródromo, incluindo pátios, posições de estacionamento, acesso de solo e regras locais; na fraseologia, refere-se a esse operador quando o controle orienta o piloto a contatar, aguardar ou seguir orientação da autoridade aeroportuária.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ATC refers you to the airport authority, the pilot should:

- acknowledge the instruction and note the frequency or contact method if given
- understand that ATC clearance alone may not authorize stand occupancy or apron access
- hold position if told to await airport authority approval
- do not assume tower can assign a closed stand or override operator restrictions
- for helicopters, confirm helipad or apron parking with the operator when local rules require it
- keep ATC informed of your position while coordinating with the authority

Airport authority involvement commonly covers:

- stand and gate assignment or reassignment
- apron access outside published hours
- temporary parking or overflow areas
- construction zones and restricted apron sectors
- local fees, handling, or ground service coordination

**Important:** ATC and airport authority have different roles. A taxi clearance to the apron does not always mean the stand is yours. If Ground says "contact airport authority for parking," do that before assuming a position. In the H130, do not hover-taxi into an unmarked apron sector without confirmation — rotor clearance and other traffic still need a clear plan.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers refer pilots to the airport authority to:

- separate traffic control from facility and apron management
- avoid issuing clearances they cannot legally or operationally guarantee
- route parking and access requests to the correct decision-maker
- coordinate temporary restrictions published by the operator
- keep the frequency free of non-ATC administrative detail when possible

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You have landed at Santos Dumont in the H130 after a training sector. Ground clears you to the apron via Alpha.

Ground: ANAC123, taxi to the apron via Alpha, contact airport authority for parking assignment.

You read back the taxi route and the instruction to contact airport authority. You taxi carefully, hold short of the unmarked stand area if needed, and obtain the parking position from the operator. Then you advise Ground you are parking as assigned.

Tower managed the movement. Airport authority managed the stand. Both pieces were required.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, taxi to the apron, contact airport authority for parking.
2. Tower: ANAC123, hold position, await airport authority approval for stand occupancy.
3. Ground: ANAC123, airport authority advises stand Bravo two closed, expect reassignment.
4. Apron: ANAC123, contact airport authority on one two one decimal seven for handling.
5. Ground: ANAC123, taxi via Charlie, airport authority reports works on the east apron.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Airport authority is the landlord of the aerodrome.

ATC owns the sky and the maneuvering area clearances. The airport authority owns how the facility is used — which stand is open, which apron sector is closed, which temporary pad is available for a helicopter.

Brazilian pilots sometimes treat tower as the answer to every ground question. That creates friction. Tower cannot invent a stand that the operator has closed. Tower cannot authorize overnight parking against local rules.

When you hear "contact airport authority," treat it as part of the clearance chain, not as optional paperwork. Complete the coordination, then update ATC with your status.

In academy operations, you will often park at known training stands. Still listen. Temporary works, VIP movements, or cargo overflow can change the plan at Congonhas or Guarulhos without warning.

Clear roles. Clear radio. Clear parking.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

After landing, Ground clears you to the apron but tells you to contact airport authority for parking. Explain what you do and why.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back the taxi clearance and the instruction to contact airport authority. I would taxi to the apron as cleared, then contact the airport authority for the parking assignment before occupying a stand. ATC controls the taxi movement, but the airport authority assigns the stand. If I am unsure whether I may enter a specific apron sector, I would hold position and confirm rather than assume. After receiving the parking position, I would advise Ground of my final parking so traffic awareness remains shared.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Asking tower to assign a stand when told to contact airport authority.  
  ✔ Follow the instruction: contact the operator for parking, then update ATC.

- ❌ Occupying any free-looking stand after a taxi-to-apron clearance.  
  ✔ Wait for parking assignment when airport authority coordination is required.

- ❌ Leaving Ground frequency without acknowledging the authority contact.  
  ✔ Read back both the taxi route and the contact instruction.

- ❌ Treating airport authority advice as optional local information.  
  ✔ Treat operator restrictions as binding for apron and stand use.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You have landed at Curitiba in the H130. Ground clears you to the apron via Bravo.

**Your first task**

Ground says: "ANAC123, taxi to the apron via Bravo, contact airport authority for parking." What do you say?

**Your second task**

Airport authority assigns stand Charlie one, but when you arrive the stand is occupied by a tow truck. What do you say to Ground?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, taxi to the apron via Bravo, will contact airport authority for parking. Second task: ANAC123, stand Charlie one occupied, request further taxi or alternate parking — to keep ATC in the loop while the authority reassigns. I would not force into the stand or leave the frequency without reporting the conflict.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Airport authority owns the facility. ATC owns the traffic.

You need both to finish a professional arrival.

When told to contact airport authority, do it. Do not invent a stand. Do not pressure tower for an operator decision.

Taxi as cleared. Park as assigned. Report when the plan changes.

That is clean ground operations — and clean ICAO English.
