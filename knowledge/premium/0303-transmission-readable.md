# Transmission Readable

**Catalog ID:** 0303  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Transmission Readable — the ICAO readability scale used to describe how well a radio transmission can be understood, typically reported as "readable," "readable with difficulty," or with a readability number from one to five. Core Part 2 radiotelephony skill for every H130 crew in Brazilian airspace: you must understand readability reports and respond when ATC says your transmission is weak, broken, or unreadable.

Transmission readable is Medium priority as a catalog item and High importance in daily radio work — without mutual readability, clearances fail.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master ICAO readability phraseology and the habit of adjusting speech, microphone technique, and frequency when readability drops.

Understanding transmission readability in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Transmission Readable describes the degree to which a radiotelephony message can be understood by the receiving station, commonly expressed with the ICAO readability scale (one to five) or plain phrases such as "readable," "readable with difficulty," "broken," or "unreadable," prompting the sender to adjust technique, repeat, or change frequency as required.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Transmissão legível / Transmission Readable (escala de legibilidade).

Grau em que uma mensagem radiotelefônica pode ser compreendida pela estação receptora, comumente expresso pela escala de legibilidade da OACI (um a cinco) ou por frases como "readable," "readable with difficulty," "broken" ou "unreadable," levando o emissor a ajustar a técnica, repetir ou mudar de frequência conforme necessário.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When dealing with transmission readability, the crew should:

- know the ICAO readability scale: 1 unreadable, 2 readable now and then, 3 readable with difficulty, 4 readable, 5 perfectly readable
- speak clearly, at moderate speed, with the microphone correctly positioned
- repeat or rephrase when ATC reports poor readability
- check volume, squelch, headset, and frequency if your reception is weak
- request "say again" when you cannot copy a clearance
- for H130: expect readability changes near terrain, cities, and during hover or high cabin noise

Readability exchanges commonly include:

- readable / readability five
- readable with difficulty / readability three
- broken / unreadable
- say again / say again all after...
- how do you read?

**Important:** "Readable" is not small talk — it is a technical assessment. If ATC says you are unreadable, fix the problem before accepting a complex clearance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use readability reports to:

- confirm whether clearances are being understood
- ask pilots to speak slower, louder, or closer to the microphone
- decide when to repeat, simplify, or change frequency
- maintain safety when reception is marginal
- document communication quality during busy traffic

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Operational scenario: approaching Congonhas in the H130. Approach asks how you read.

Approach: ANAC123, how do you read?

You respond:

ANAC123, reading you five / reading you loud and clear.

If Approach says your transmission is broken:

ANAC123, say again, you are broken.

Or Approach reports:

ANAC123, readable with difficulty, speak slower.

You slow down, shorten the next call, and confirm the clearance with a clean readback.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, how do you read?
2. Approach: ANAC123, readable, continue.
3. Approach: ANAC123, readable with difficulty, speak slower.
4. Tower: ANAC123, you are broken, say again altitude.
5. Ground: ANAC123, readability five, taxi via Alpha.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Readability is the quality check on every clearance.

If you cannot hear, say "say again." If they cannot hear you, slow down, shorten the call, check the mic. Do not pretend you copied a clearance you only half heard.

"Reading you five" means perfectly readable. "Reading you three" means difficulty — expect repeats. Use the scale when asked "how do you read?"

In the H130, rotor and cabin noise destroy lazy microphone technique. Hold the mic properly. Pause before keying. Speak like a professional, not like a passenger on a phone.

Part 2 is built on this. Master it early.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC asks "how do you read?" and later says you are readable with difficulty. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would answer with a readability assessment such as reading you five, or reading you three if reception is difficult. If ATC says I am readable with difficulty, I would speak more slowly, shorten my transmissions, check microphone position and volume, and repeat essential information clearly. I would not acknowledge a clearance I did not fully understand — I would request say again.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Answering "how do you read?" with "yes" or "ok."  
  ✔ Use readability: reading you five / readable.

- ❌ Accepting clearances when the transmission was broken.  
  ✔ Say again until the clearance is complete.

- ❌ Speaking faster when readability is poor.  
  ✔ Speak slower and clearer, not faster.

- ❌ Ignoring microphone technique in the noisy H130 cabin.  
  ✔ Mic position and keying discipline are part of readability.
