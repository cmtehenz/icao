# Windshear

**Catalog ID:** 0088  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers windshear — a sudden change in wind speed or direction that can destroy performance on takeoff or landing.

Windshear is especially dangerous for helicopters near the ground, where airspeed and altitude margins are small.

In the H130, windshear may occur near thunderstorms, frontal boundaries, and strong coastal gust fronts in Brazil.

You must recognize the signs early, go around or reject takeoff if needed, and report windshear immediately to ATC.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Windshear is a sudden change in wind speed or direction over a short distance, which can cause abrupt changes in airspeed and performance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Cisalhamento do vento (windshear).

Mudança súbita de velocidade ou direção do vento em curta distância, causando alterações abruptas de velocidade e desempenho.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Windshear is encountered, the pilot should:

- recognize sudden airspeed loss or gain near the ground
- apply maximum available power and establish a climb attitude for escape
- go around if windshear occurs on approach
- reject takeoff if windshear occurs during takeoff roll or initial climb when unsafe
- report windshear to ATC with location and altitude

Windshear reporting commonly includes:

- windshear on final or on departure
- airspeed loss or gain
- going around due windshear
- request another approach or delay

**Important:** Windshear near the ground is a survival event. Escape first — then report.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Windshear reports to:

- warn arriving and departing aircraft immediately
- delay or change runway operations if needed
- relay PIREPs with altitude and airspeed loss or gain
- coordinate go-arounds and missed approaches
- advise when windshear alerts are current

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on short final to runway two nine at Navegantes in your H130.

Airspeed suddenly drops twenty knots and the aircraft sinks.

You apply maximum power, establish a climb, and go around.

You transmit:

Navegantes Tower, ANAC123, going around, windshear on final, airspeed loss two zero knots at two hundred feet.

Tower warns following traffic and you hold for improving conditions.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger windshear, all aircraft advised.
2. Tower: ANAC123, windshear alert, advise intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported windshear on final, say if continuing.
4. Tower: ANAC123, cleared go-around, report when established.
5. Tower: ANAC123, say airspeed loss and altitude of windshear.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Navegantes Tower, ANAC123, going around, windshear on final.
2. ANAC123, windshear on departure, airspeed loss one five knots at one hundred feet.
3. ANAC123, windshear alert received, requesting delay.
4. Tower, ANAC123, windshear on final, airspeed gain then loss, going around.
5. ANAC123, unable to land due windshear, request another approach later.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Windshear is a knife near the ground.

Sudden wind change. Sudden performance change.

The sequence:

Power. Attitude. Climb. Report.

Do not chase airspeed with aggressive nose-down near the ground.

Escape the shear first.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "cortante" or "perda de velocidade" on frequency.

Use windshear — ICAO and FAA standard term.

Always report altitude and airspeed loss or gain.

In the H130, margins are small — go around early when shear is suspected.

Train escape until power and climb attitude are automatic.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if you encountered windshear on final approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would go around, apply maximum available power, and establish a climb. I would report going around due windshear on final, state the approximate altitude and airspeed loss or gain, and request another approach or delay until conditions improve.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing the approach after a significant airspeed loss near the ground.  
  ✔ Go around immediately — escape first.

- ❌ Saying "vento cortante" without the standard term.  
  ✔ Use windshear.

- ❌ Not reporting altitude and airspeed change.  
  ✔ Include altitude and airspeed loss or gain in the PIREP.

- ❌ Confusing windshear with ordinary gusts.  
  ✔ Windshear is a sudden, significant change over a short distance.
