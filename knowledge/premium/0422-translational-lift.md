# Translational Lift

**Catalog ID:** 0422  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Translational Lift — the additional lift produced as a helicopter moves from hover into forward flight and the rotor system leaves its own downwash, improving efficiency and climb performance.

Translational lift is an aerodynamic fact every helicopter pilot feels. ICAO English requires you to discuss it when explaining performance, departure profiles, and why a hover or departure is limited.

For H130 operations in Brazil — hot/high pads, rooftop departures, and coastal HEMS — you must explain performance intentions clearly when translational lift is delayed by wind, weight, or technique.

Understanding how to discuss translational lift in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Translational Lift is the increase in rotor efficiency and lift that occurs as a helicopter accelerates from a hover into forward flight, when horizontal airflow through the rotor reduces induced flow and the aircraft leaves the turbulent downwash of the hover.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Sustentação translacional / Translational Lift.

Aumento da eficiência e da sustentação do rotor que ocorre quando o helicóptero acelera do pairado para o voo à frente, quando o fluxo de ar horizontal pelo rotor reduz o fluxo induzido e a aeronave deixa a turbulência descendente do pairado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing translational lift, the pilot should:

- plan departure so the transition to forward flight occurs with adequate power margin
- use wind to assist early translational lift when available
- avoid settling or excessive pitch attitudes while seeking translational lift
- explain to ATC if departure will be a slow acceleration or require runway/helipad length
- report unable if weight/altitude/temperature prevent a safe transition

Translational lift discussion commonly includes:

- departing, accelerating through translational lift
- request departure into wind
- unable vertical departure, request air taxi / runway
- power limited, slow acceleration

**Important:** Translational lift is not a radio call by itself — it is the reason behind many performance requests. Use it to explain why you need wind, space, or a different departure profile.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers may not say "translational lift," but they support the performance need by:

- assigning into-wind departures when requested
- providing adequate departure path clear of obstacles and traffic
- allowing air taxi or runway use instead of vertical climb-out
- understanding slow initial acceleration on hot days
- sequencing so the helicopter is not rushed off a short pad

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Hot afternoon departure from a São Paulo rooftop in your H130 at high weight. Vertical climb margin is thin. You need early forward speed for translational lift.

Tower, request departure into wind, will accelerate level then climb — power limited for vertical departure.

Tower gives you the wind-favored edge. You transition through translational lift cleanly and climb away without settling.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, wind two three zero at one two, cleared for takeoff, departure into wind approved.
2. Tower: ANAC123, unable vertical, roger, air taxi to runway approved.
3. Tower: ANAC123, report airborne and clear of the pad.
4. Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain two thousand, when able.
5. Tower: ANAC123, caution wake turbulence, heavy jet departed, advise ready.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, into wind, cleared for takeoff, will accelerate then climb.
2. ANAC123, air taxi to runway, unable vertical departure.
3. ANAC123, airborne, clear of the pad, climbing.
4. ANAC123, climbing two thousand.
5. ANAC123, ready, requesting short delay for wake to clear.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Translational lift English is performance-request English.

You rarely need to lecture ATC on aerodynamics. You need the product: into wind, more space, no vertical departure.

In the H130 on Brazilian rooftops and hospital pads, density altitude and weight steal hover power. Forward flight gives it back through translational lift. Ask for the departure that lets you accelerate.

If a controller pushes for an immediate steep climb, say unable and state the alternative. Part 4 skill is negotiating the profile without sounding argumentative.

Feel the shudder and the performance gain as you leave the downwash — then climb. Do not climb hoping translational lift will appear later over obstacles.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are heavy on a hot rooftop and cannot make a vertical departure. How do you explain your plan to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would say: Unable vertical departure, request departure into wind, will accelerate to gain translational lift then climb. If needed I would request air taxi to a runway. I would not attempt a vertical climb that exceeds available power.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Attempting a vertical rooftop departure when power margin is gone.  
  ✔ Request into-wind acceleration or a runway.

- ❌ Long aerodynamic lecture on frequency.  
  ✔ State the operational need: unable vertical, request into wind.

- ❌ Ignoring wind that would give earlier translational lift.  
  ✔ Ask for into-wind departure when it matters.

- ❌ Climbing steeply before effective forward speed.  
  ✔ Accelerate first when the profile requires translational lift.

- Unable → un-AY-bul
- Vertical → VER-ti-kul
- Departure → di-PAR-cher (stress on second)

Practice:

Together:

Unable vertical departure, request into wind, will accelerate then climb.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Heavy H130 on a hot São Paulo rooftop. Wind favors one edge of the pad.

**Your first task**

What do you request from Tower?

**Your second task**

Tower asks you to climb vertically for traffic. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, request departure into wind, will accelerate then climb. Second task: ANAC123, unable vertical climb, power limited, request delay or alternate departure path into wind — do not accept a vertical profile you cannot fly.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Translational lift is free performance — if you plan for it.

Ask for wind. Ask for space. Refuse vertical when the charts say no.

In the H130 over Brazil, the transition from hover to forward flight is where many departures are won or lost.

Accelerate with a plan. Climb with a margin. Speak the limitation before the obstacle does.
