# Team Coordination

**Catalog ID:** 0489  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Team Coordination — the CRM process of synchronizing roles, timing, and information so that two or more crew members act as one system during normal and abnormal operations.

Team coordination fails when both pilots fly, both talk, or neither monitors. It succeeds when PF, PM, and any crewman know who does what, when, and how they cross-check.

For H130 operations with a pilot and crewman — sling, hoist, EMS — coordination is the mission. Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 English must keep that team aligned with ATC and with each other.

Understanding team coordination in ICAO-standard English is essential across Parts 1, 2, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Team Coordination is the structured sharing of tasks, monitoring duties, and information among crew members so that flight path control, systems management, and communications remain synchronized, cross-checked, and free of conflicting actions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Coordenação de equipe / Team Coordination.

Compartilhamento estruturado de tarefas, deveres de monitoramento e informações entre os membros da tripulação, de modo que o controle da trajetória, a gestão de sistemas e as comunicações permaneçam sincronizados, com verificação cruzada e sem ações conflitantes.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When coordinating as a team, the crew should:

- assign PF / PM and radio clearly before the critical phase
- use standard callouts and closed-loop communication
- cross-check altitude, heading, and clearance readbacks
- for H130: coordinate pilot and crewman on sling, hoist, and pad calls
- keep ATC calls consistent with the agreed plan
- recover quickly if two people start the same task

Team coordination commonly includes:

- I fly / you talk
- confirmed / acknowledged
- my radio / your controls
- callouts at gates and minima
- standby — resolving conflict

**Important:** If two crew members give opposite inputs or opposite radio intentions, freeze the conflict, stabilize the aircraft, then reset roles out loud.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers benefit from coordinated crews because:

- readbacks are consistent and timely
- only one coherent intention is transmitted
- abnormal reports arrive complete on the first call
- helicopter special ops have a clear radio and handling split
- frequency congestion falls when crews do not double-transmit

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are conducting an H130 sling operation near a construction site outside São Paulo. You fly. The crewman owns the load and the verbal clearance to lift or abort.

Before lift you brief: you control the aircraft and talk to ATC; the crewman calls load status; any “stop” call is immediate.

Approach asks you to hold east of the site for traffic. You acknowledge, the crewman pauses the lift, and both of you restate the new plan. Coordination keeps the load and the airspace safe together.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, hold east of the site, traffic crossing.
2. Tower: ANAC123, report ready for departure after load secure.
3. Control: ANAC123, confirm single pilot flying, crewman on board.
4. Approach: ANAC123, advise when sling operation complete.
5. Tower: ANAC123, vacate and report load status on this frequency.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, holding east of the site, traffic in sight.
2. ANAC123, wilco, will report ready when load secure.
3. ANAC123, affirmative, pilot flying, crewman on board.
4. ANAC123, wilco, will advise when sling complete.
5. ANAC123, vacating, load secure, ready to taxi.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Team coordination is choreography with checklists.

If nobody owns the radio, ATC waits. If everybody owns the radio, ATC hears chaos. Name the roles.

Closed-loop communication is the glue: statement, readback, confirmation. Use it between pilots and with the crewman — not only with ATC.

In Part 4 discussions, describe how the team shares monitoring. Examiners listen for cross-check language, not hero language.

In the H130, the crewman’s “stop” overrides pride. Build that rule into every briefing.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How should an H130 crew coordinate during a sling-load lift near controlled airspace?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The pilot flying should control the aircraft and handle ATC, while the crewman monitors the load and makes clear lift, stop, or abort calls. Before the lift, roles must be briefed and any ATC restriction — such as a hold for traffic — must be acknowledged by the whole team. Closed-loop callouts keep the aircraft and the load synchronized.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Both pilots transmitting different intentions to ATC.  
  ✔ One radio owner; agree the plan before keying the mic.

- ❌ Starting the lift without a clear stop call rule.  
  ✔ Brief that any stop call is immediate and mandatory.

- ❌ Assuming the other pilot is monitoring altitude.  
  ✔ Assign monitoring explicitly and use callouts.

- ❌ Switching roles silently during high workload.  
  ✔ Announce role changes out loud: I have control / you have radio.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

During an H130 sling lift, Approach tells you to hold for crossing traffic. The crewman is seconds from calling “lift.”

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

What do you say to the crewman to keep team coordination intact?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, holding east of the site, traffic copied. Second task: Hold the lift — ATC hold for traffic; we lift only after release. Team coordination means the radio restriction and the load action stay synchronized through explicit calls.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Team coordination turns individuals into one aircraft.

Name the roles. Close the loops. Honor the stop call.

On frequency, one plan. In the cabin, shared monitoring.

In the H130, sling and hoist work make coordination visible. Fly that standard on every sector — not only when a load is on the hook.
