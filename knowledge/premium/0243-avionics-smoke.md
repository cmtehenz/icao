# Avionics Smoke

**Catalog ID:** 0243  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Avionics Smoke — smoke or fumes originating from avionics, electrical, or instrument equipment, often with a distinctive electrical smell, requiring immediate crew protection, electrical load shedding per AFM, and a clear distress or urgency call stating nature, location, and intentions.

Avionics smoke is one of the most relevant smoke scenarios for H130 helicopter operations. Training helicopters may not have airline cargo holds or lavatories, but they do have avionics bays, radio stacks, and electrical equipment close to the crew. Smoke here can disable navigation and communication while impairing the pilots.

Brazilian academy IFR — Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, Florianópolis — demands Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 speaking that names avionics smoke specifically and requests immediate landing.

## 📖 Meaning

Avionics Smoke is smoke or electrical fumes produced by overheating or failing avionics, electrical, or instrument equipment, typically detected by smell, haze, or visible smoke near the instrument panel or avionics bay, and requiring immediate protective action, emergency checklists, possible electrical isolation, and priority landing.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Fumaça ou vapores elétricos produzidos por superaquecimento ou falha de equipamentos de aviônicos, elétricos ou instrumentos, tipicamente detectados por cheiro, névoa ou fumaça visível perto do painel de instrumentos ou baía de aviônicos, e exigindo ação protetora imediata, checklists de emergência, possível isolamento elétrico e pouso prioritário.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When avionics smoke occurs, the crew should:

- don oxygen or smoke goggles as equipped immediately
- apply the AFM avionics/electrical smoke checklist
- shed or isolate affected electrical equipment per procedure
- declare Mayday or Pan Pan based on severity
- request immediate vectors to land; expect possible loss of radios or navigation
- report nature (avionics smoke), location (cockpit/avionics bay), intentions; souls/fuel when asked

Avionics smoke reporting commonly includes:

- avionics smoke / electrical smoke in the cockpit
- request immediate landing / nearest suitable airport
- partial electrical failure or radio problems secondary to smoke
- smoke dissipating after power isolation
- souls on board and fuel remaining

**Important:** Avionics smoke can take your radios next. Make the emergency call early while you can still transmit. In the H130, this scenario is more operationally realistic than cargo or lavatory smoke — master this English thoroughly for exams and line flying.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to avionics smoke reports to:

- provide immediate priority and shortest path to land
- prepare for possible radio failure after the initial call
- alert emergency services for electrical/fire risk on landing
- request souls, fuel, and smoke status
- coordinate simple clearances if navigation capability is degrading

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR at five thousand feet inbound to Congonhas. An acrid electrical smell rises from behind the instrument panel. Light smoke appears.

You don oxygen, start the checklist, and call while the radio still works:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, São Paulo Approach, ANAC123, avionics smoke in the cockpit, request immediate vectors to Congonhas for landing.

Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading three three zero, descend two thousand, Congonhas twelve miles, say souls and fuel.

You give souls and fuel. After shedding equipment, smoke reduces. You update:

ANAC123, smoke dissipating after electrical isolation, continuing Mayday, request straight-in.

You land with a working plan — because the first call was complete and early.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Mayday avionics smoke, turn right heading three three zero, descend immediately.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
3. Approach: ANAC123, expect straight-in runway one seven Right, fire services alerted.
4. Approach: ANAC123, report radio if degrading, squawk emergency if able.
5. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, roll to the end, emergency services standing by.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Avionics smoke is the helicopter smoke emergency you must own.

It sits in front of you. It can kill the radios. It can kill the screens. It can kill the crew if you breathe it long enough.

Oxygen first. Checklist. Mayday while the transmitter works. Do not wait for confirmed flames.

On the radio, "avionics smoke" is better than "electrical problem" and better than bare "smoke." Controllers and examiners hear the precision. Add location and the request for immediate landing.

If equipment isolation helps, say smoke dissipating. If it does not, say smoke continuing. Either way, land.

In Brazilian dense terminal airspace, priority exists for declared emergencies — but only after you declare. Silent troubleshooting over Congonhas helps no one.

Make the call early. Fly the simplest path to the ground. After landing, shut down and get clear as the AFM and emergency services require.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You smell electrical smoke from the avionics and see haze on the panel. What is your plan and radio message structure?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would don oxygen immediately, apply the avionics or electrical smoke checklist, and declare Mayday. I would report avionics smoke in the cockpit and request immediate vectors to the nearest suitable airport. I would give souls and fuel when asked, update whether smoke is dissipating or continuing, and land as soon as practicable, expecting possible further electrical or radio degradation.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Troubleshooting silently for several minutes before calling ATC.  
  ✔ Declare early — avionics smoke can take communications next.

- ❌ Saying "electrical failure" when the primary threat is smoke.  
  ✔ Lead with avionics smoke; mention electrical isolation as secondary.

- ❌ Cancelling Mayday in the air because smoke reduced slightly.  
  ✔ Continue priority landing; update status; cancel only when safe on ground per procedure.

- ❌ Forgetting that H130 avionics bay smoke is more relevant than airline lavatory examples.  
  ✔ Train this scenario as primary helicopter smoke English.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Electrical smoke appears behind the panel on an IFR approach. Radios still work.

**Your first task**

What is your Mayday call?

**Your second task**

After load shedding, smoke decreases but instruments are degraded. What do you tell ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday Mayday Mayday, Approach, ANAC123, avionics smoke in the cockpit, request immediate vectors to land. Second task: ANAC123, smoke dissipating after electrical isolation, partial instrument degradation, request simple vectors and priority landing. I would keep the emergency active until on the ground and would not delay the first call to finish troubleshooting.
