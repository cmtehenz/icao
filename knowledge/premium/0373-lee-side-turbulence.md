# Lee Side Turbulence

**Catalog ID:** 0373  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Lee Side Turbulence — turbulent, often severe airflow on the downwind side of mountains, ridges, or large obstacles, frequently associated with rotors, eddies, and mountain wave activity.

Lee side turbulence is where helicopters get hurt in mountain flying. The H130 can experience sudden upsets, altitude loss, and high workload while close to terrain — exactly when margins are smallest.

For Brazilian operations along the Serra do Mar escarpment, approaches into valleys, and strong wind days over the Mantiqueira, crews must plan the windward side when possible, avoid hugging lee slopes, and report turbulence clearly to ATC.

## 📖 Meaning

Lee Side Turbulence is disturbed, turbulent airflow on the downwind (lee) side of a topographic barrier, caused by flow separation, rotors, and eddies, which can produce moderate to severe turbulence, abrupt altitude changes, and hazardous conditions for low-level and mountain flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Turbulência a sotavento / Lee Side Turbulence.

Fluxo de ar perturbado e turbulento no lado de sotavento de uma barreira topográfica, causado por separação de fluxo, rotores e redemoinhos, podendo produzir turbulência moderada a severa, mudanças abruptas de altitude e condições perigosas para voo em baixo nível e em montanha.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When lee side turbulence is expected or encountered, the crew should:

- approach ridges from the windward side when practical; avoid low lee-side cruise
- maintain extra altitude over ridges in strong wind
- reduce to turbulence speed guidance if severe bumps occur
- request climb, deviation, or delay if turbulence prevents safe flight
- report lee side turbulence intensity and location to ATC
- divert or reverse course rather than force a lee-side approach into rising terrain

Lee side turbulence reporting commonly includes:

- moderate or severe turbulence lee of [ridge / airport]
- request higher or deviation
- unable to continue present routing
- mountain wave / rotor suspected

**Important:** Smooth air on the windward slope does not mean the lee is safe. Wind increases, pressure drops on the lee, and rotors form. In the H130 near a Brazilian escarpment, treat strong lee-side wind as a no-go for low VFR until you have altitude and an escape route.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use lee side turbulence reports to:

- warn aircraft on downwind mountain routes and approaches
- offer alternate routing or altitudes
- delay approaches into airports on the lee of high terrain
- update other traffic with PIREPs
- understand go-arounds or deviations near ridges

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are VFR along the Serra do Mar with a strong easterly wind. Crossing to the lee, the H130 hits severe turbulence and a sharp downdraft toward rising terrain.

You transmit:

São Paulo Approach, ANAC123, severe lee side turbulence east of the ridge, requesting climb to five thousand and deviation north.

Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain five thousand, deviation north approved, report smooth.

You climb, turn toward lower terrain, and stay off the lee slope.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger severe turbulence, climb and maintain five thousand.
2. Center: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported severe lee side turbulence over the Serra.
3. Approach: ANAC123, advise able to continue approach, lee side wind strong.
4. Approach: ANAC123, deviation approved, report clear of turbulence.
5. Tower: ANAC123, delay landing, severe turbulence reported on final lee of terrain.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Lee side turbulence is mountain flying's tax collector — it takes altitude and attention when you can spare neither.

Windward: rising air, often smoother. Lee: sinking, rolling, violent.

Brazilian crews who "cut the corner" on the lee of the Serra learn this the hard way. Do not learn it that way.

Give the ridge altitude. Cross at an angle that preserves an escape to lower terrain. If it gets severe, climb or turn — then report lee side turbulence so the next helicopter does not repeat your mistake.

ATC cannot see the rotor. Your PIREP is the radar.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You encounter severe turbulence on the downwind side of a ridge. How do you describe it and what do you request?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report severe lee side turbulence with the location relative to the ridge and request an immediate climb or deviation toward lower terrain. I would reduce speed as appropriate for turbulence and avoid continuing a low lee-side track toward rising ground. I would say I am unable to continue present routing if necessary. In the H130 I prioritize terrain clearance and escape path over staying on the original scenic or short route.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Calling it only "bad air" without lee side / turbulence intensity.  
  ✔ Use: moderate/severe lee side turbulence, [location].

- ❌ Descending on the lee to "get below the bumps."  
  ✔ Bumps plus descending terrain is how CFIT happens — climb or exit laterally.

- ❌ Pressing a lee-side approach in strong wind.  
  ✔ Delay, circle windward, or divert.

- ❌ No PIREP after recovery.  
  ✔ Report intensity and location while ATC can still warn others.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Strong wind over the escarpment. On the lee you hit severe turbulence at three thousand feet near terrain.

**Your first task**

What do you say?

**Your second task**

ATC asks if you can continue the approach to a lee-side airport. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, severe lee side turbulence, requesting immediate climb and deviation to lower terrain. Second task: ANAC123, unable approach, severe lee side turbulence, request delay or diversion to windward alternate. Do not accept a lee-side final into known severe turbulence near terrain. Unable is the correct word.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Lee side turbulence lives where the wind hides behind the mountain.

Stay high. Stay windward when you can. Escape early. Report clearly.

In the H130 along Brazilian ridges, the lee is not a shortcut — it is a test. Pass it by refusing the low downwind track when the wind is strong.
