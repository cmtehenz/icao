# Black Hole Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0481  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Black Hole Approach — a night visual illusion that occurs when approaching a lighted runway over dark, featureless terrain or water, causing the pilot to perceive the aircraft as too high and fly a dangerously low approach path.

The black hole illusion is visual, not vestibular. With only runway lights ahead and darkness below, the brain misreads approach angle. Crews descend early. Terrain or water arrives before the threshold.

In H130 night operations to coastal, Amazonian, and mountain strips — or to helipads with limited surrounding light — black hole approaches are a classic CFIT setup. Approach-path instruments and disciplined go-around criteria are mandatory defenses.

Understanding how to brief and communicate black hole risk in ICAO-standard English supports Part 1, Part 2, and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

A Black Hole Approach is a night visual approach over unlighted or featureless terrain or water toward a lighted runway or pad, producing an illusion of being too high and leading pilots to fly below the correct glide path.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aproximação buraco negro / Black Hole Approach.

Aproximação visual noturna sobre terreno ou água sem iluminação ou sem referências, em direção a uma pista ou heliponto iluminado, produzindo a ilusão de estar alto demais e levando os pilotos a voar abaixo da trajetória correta.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a black hole approach is possible, the crew should:

- prefer an instrument approach or vertical guidance when available
- cross-check altitude versus distance on final — do not fly by runway-light picture alone
- brief a hard go-around gate if path cues become unreliable
- use VASI, PAPI, or flight-director glidepath as primary path reference
- advise ATC early if discontinuing for an instrument procedure or go-around

Black hole defenses include:

- calculating and monitoring altitude at known distances
- avoiding long straight-in visuals over water or jungle at night without path guidance
- treating "we look high" as a potential illusion cue, not a descent clearance
- executing go-around when visual path cannot be verified

**Important:** If the runway lights are the only lights in the windscreen, assume black hole risk. Your eyes will invite you low. Your instruments must refuse.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support black hole threat management by:

- offering instrument approaches at night when visual conditions are deceptive
- providing altitude and distance advisories on final when available
- accepting go-around and circling or instrument requests without delay
- alerting crews to unlighted terrain or water approaches
- sequencing aircraft for procedures with vertical guidance

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Night visual to a coastal runway. Dark water behind you. Bright threshold ahead. You feel high.

Your first officer checks: "Two miles — we should be at six hundred. We are at four hundred."

You decide:

ANAC123, going around, black hole illusion, request vectors for ILS.

Tower: ANAC123, go around, fly runway heading, climb three thousand, contact approach.

You respond:

ANAC123, going around, runway heading, climbing three thousand.

The picture said high. The numbers said low. The numbers won.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, go around, fly runway heading, climb three thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, cleared ILS runway one five, report established.
3. Tower: ANAC123, altitude low on final, confirm intentions.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions, visual approach available or ILS.
5. Radar: ANAC123, vectors for ILS, climb and maintain three thousand.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Black hole approaches flatter your eyes and starve your margins.

Featureless dark below. Jewels of light ahead. The brain says "too high." The aircraft sinks into the black.

In the H130, night visuals over water or unlit terrain need a path contract: altitude versus distance, or vertical guidance, or go-around. No contract, no continue.

Feeling high is not data. Altitude at two miles is data.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

On a night visual over water you feel high, but altitude versus distance shows you low. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would go around immediately and request an instrument approach: ANAC123, going around, request vectors for ILS. I would not descend further on a black hole picture. The illusion invites a low approach; the correct response is a missed approach and vertical guidance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Descending because the runway "looks high" over dark water.  
  ✔ Cross-check altitude versus distance or use glidepath guidance.

- ❌ Continuing a night visual without path references.  
  ✔ Request ILS, LNAV/VNAV, or go around when cues are insufficient.

- ❌ Waiting for terrain warning before correcting a low path.  
  ✔ Go around at the first unverified low indication.

- ❌ Briefing only the runway lights, not the illusion threat.  
  ✔ Brief black hole risk and hard gates before night coastal arrivals.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Black hole approaches punish visual confidence.

At night over featureless terrain, the pretty runway can pull you low.

In the H130, fly numbers or guidance — or go around. The illusion will always invite one more descent. Decline the invitation.
