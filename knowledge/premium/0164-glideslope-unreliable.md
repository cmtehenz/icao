# Glideslope Unreliable

**Catalog ID:** 0164  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Glideslope Unreliable — the advisory indication that the ILS glideslope signal cannot be trusted for approach guidance.

Glideslope Unreliable can mean several things: the airborne glideslope receiver has failed, the ground glideslope transmitter is unserviceable, signal interference is affecting reliability, or a NOTAM has been issued advising crews not to use the glideslope.

In each case, the result is the same: the glideslope cannot be used. The approach must be flown as a localizer-only non-precision approach.

In the H130, glideslope status is monitored throughout the approach. Pilots must recognize the glideslope unreliable indication, respond correctly with ATC, and transition smoothly to non-precision technique.

## 📖 Meaning

Glideslope Unreliable is an indication or advisory that the ILS glideslope signal is not dependable for vertical guidance on approach, requiring the crew to disregard the glideslope needle and fly the approach using the localizer only with non-precision approach minima and technique.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Glideslope não confiável.

Indicação ou aviso de que o sinal do glideslope do ILS não é confiável para orientação vertical na aproximação, exigindo que a tripulação desconsidere o agulha do glideslope e execute a aproximação utilizando apenas o localizador com técnica e mínimos de aproximação de não precisão.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew encounters a glideslope unreliable indication, they should:

- recognize the indication: a glideslope flag, an amber advisory, or an ATC advisory about glideslope unserviceability
- advise ATC if the failure is airborne: ANAC123, glideslope unreliable, request localizer approach
- confirm with ATC if they issue the advisory: acknowledge and confirm LOC approach only
- remove the glideslope from primary flight display consideration — do not follow the needle
- pull up the localizer-only approach chart for the runway
- apply non-precision approach minima: use the LOC MDA, not the ILS DA
- fly CDFA technique or step-down fixes for vertical guidance
- execute missed approach at MDA if runway environment is not in sight

Systems commonly associated with glideslope unreliable:

- glideslope receiver flag indication in the cockpit
- NOTAM: glideslope unserviceable (GS U/S)
- ATC ATIS broadcast: glideslope runway one seven out of service
- ground maintenance: the glideslope transmitter is shut down

**Important:** A glideslope unreliable indication does not mean the localizer is unreliable. The localizer remains the lateral guidance source and should be used normally. Only the vertical component is affected. Do not abandon the ILS system entirely — use the localizer portion of the ILS for lateral guidance throughout the approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers broadcast glideslope unreliable advisories to:

- protect aircraft from following a false or unserviceable vertical path
- ensure crews apply non-precision minima and technique
- coordinate the transition from ILS approaches to localizer-only approaches
- update the ATIS when glideslope unserviceability is known before flights depart
- issue NOTAMs when planned maintenance will render the glideslope unavailable

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are being vectored for the ILS runway one seven at Congonhas in your H130.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, advise, glideslope runway one seven unserviceable, cleared localizer approach runway one seven.

You respond:

Glideslope unserviceable, cleared localizer approach runway one seven, ANAC123.

In the cockpit: your co-pilot pulls up the LOC runway one seven chart. You note the MDA — eight hundred and fifty feet — versus the ILS DA of three hundred and sixty-five feet.

You set the MDA on the altitude alerter.

You continue on the localizer. Your lateral deviation needle shows centerline.

You fly the CDFA profile: from the final approach fix at fifteen hundred feet, descending at approximately seven hundred feet per minute for the required gradient.

Passing MDA: runway lights in sight.

You commence the normal landing.

No glideslope needed. Professional non-precision technique.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Glideslope unreliable is one of the most operationally significant advisories in IFR flying.

The transition from ILS to localizer-only is not just a navigation change — it is a minima change and a technique change.

Higher minima. No precision vertical path. Increased pilot workload.

The most dangerous error: receiving a glideslope unreliable advisory and continuing to follow the glideslope needle anyway. The needle may be showing you a false path. Following it below safe terrain clearance altitudes has caused accidents.

The NOTAM-savvy crew: check the ATIS and NOTAMs before departure. If the glideslope at your destination is unserviceable, brief the LOC approach before you take off — not while you are flying the approach in IMC.

In the H130, glideslope status is displayed clearly in the avionics. Know your system. Know how a glideslope flag presents in your specific aircraft.

When you transition to the localizer approach, brief your co-pilot: we are on LOC minima now. MDA eight hundred and fifty feet. No glideslope. CDFA technique.

Clear, concise, professional.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC advises you that the glideslope at your destination is unserviceable. How do you respond and how does this change your approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would acknowledge: Glideslope unserviceable, cleared localizer approach runway one seven, ANAC123. I would immediately pull up the localizer-only approach chart for the runway. I would note the new minima: the LOC MDA rather than the ILS DA. The MDA is typically significantly higher than the ILS decision altitude. I would set the MDA on the altitude alerter. I would fly the lateral guidance from the localizer precisely, and use CDFA technique or published step-down fixes for vertical guidance. I would disregard any glideslope needle movement on the PFD — it may be presenting an unreliable signal. At MDA, if the runway is not in sight, I would execute the missed approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing to follow the glideslope needle after a glideslope unreliable advisory.  
  ✔ Disregard the glideslope completely. An unreliable needle may lead below safe terrain clearance.

- ❌ Using ILS minima after transitioning to a localizer-only approach.  
  ✔ LOC MDA applies — not ILS DA. The MDA is higher and the approach is non-precision.

- ❌ Not checking the ATIS or NOTAMs for glideslope unserviceability before departure.  
  ✔ Pre-flight NOTAM and ATIS review prevents in-flight surprises. Brief the LOC approach before takeoff.

- ❌ Not briefing the co-pilot on the minima change when transitioning mid-approach.  
  ✔ Brief immediately: LOC approach, MDA eight hundred and fifty, no glideslope, CDFA technique.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Congonhas ATIS broadcasts: "ILS runway one seven glideslope out of service. Localizer approach in use."

**Your first task**

How does this ATIS information change your approach briefing before departure?

**Your second task**

On approach to runway one seven, established on the localizer, the glideslope needle in the aircraft appears to activate and shows a two-dot fly-up indication. Your co-pilot says "let's follow it a little, looks like it's working." What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

For the first task: I would brief the localizer approach runway one seven, not the ILS. I would note the LOC MDA on the approach chart — eight hundred and fifty feet versus the ILS DA of three hundred and sixty-five feet. I would set the MDA on the altitude alerter before departure. I would brief CDFA technique or step-down fixes for vertical profile. I would advise my co-pilot that no glideslope guidance will be available. For the second task: I would instruct my co-pilot to disregard the glideslope needle. The ground glideslope is declared unserviceable — any signal we receive is unreliable and potentially false. Following it could lead us below safe terrain clearance. We fly LOC minima, CDFA profile, and execute the missed approach at MDA if runway not in sight.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Glideslope unreliable changes the approach from precision to non-precision.

That change is significant. Higher minima. Increased workload. More pilot responsibility for vertical profile.

Know the change. Brief the change. Fly the change.

Do not trust a glideslope that has been declared unreliable — even if the needle appears to move correctly.

Trust the localizer for lateral guidance. Trust the non-precision plate for vertical guidance.

Professional instrument flying means knowing exactly what your guidance sources are — and which ones you can trust.
