# Speed Brake Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0269  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Speed Brake Failure — loss or malfunction of the speed brake system used to increase drag and steepen descent without increasing airspeed excessively, requiring revised descent planning and clear ATC communication when you cannot meet an expedite or crossing restriction as filed.

Speed brakes (often related to or combined with spoilers on many types) let crews lose altitude while managing speed. When they fail, you may arrive high, need more track miles, or be unable to accept a late descent clearance.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, the H130 does not have jet-style speed brakes. The useful analogy is managing a steeper or shallower descent with collective and airspeed — not a fake speed-brake lever on the helicopter. Master the ICAO English for exams and multi-type flying.

Part 2 and Part 3 reward precise calls: speed brake failure, unable to meet restriction, request delay vectors or earlier descent.

## 📖 Meaning

Speed Brake Failure is a malfunction in which the speed brake system cannot be extended or retracted normally, reducing the aircraft's ability to increase drag for descent path control, and requiring abnormal procedures plus ATC notification when descent profile, crossing restrictions, or expedite clearances cannot be met.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do speed brake / Speed Brake Failure.

Malfuncionamento em que o sistema de speed brake não pode ser estendido ou recolhido normalmente, reduzindo a capacidade da aeronave de aumentar o arrasto para controle da trajetória de descida, e exigindo procedimentos anormais e notificação ao ATC quando o perfil de descida, restrições de cruzamento ou autorizações de expedite não puderem ser cumpridos.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When speed brakes fail, the crew should:

- confirm the failure and apply the AFM abnormal checklist
- recalculate descent: more distance may be required to lose altitude
- advise ATC early if a crossing restriction or expedite cannot be met
- request earlier descent, delay vectors, or holding to lose altitude
- avoid diving to "catch" the profile at the cost of an unstable approach
- keep H130 analogies honest: collective descent flexibility ≠ speed brake hardware

Speed brake failure scenarios commonly include:

- speed brakes stuck in — excess drag, possible speed or climb limitation
- speed brakes stuck out of reach — high on profile, need track miles
- late descent clearance with failed speed brakes
- STAR crossing altitude that cannot be made
- exam radio: unable to comply, request alternative

**Important:** If you will be high on profile, say so before the restriction, not after you bust it. Unable to meet crossing restriction, speed brake failure gives ATC time to re-clear you.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use speed brake failure reports to:

- reissue descent clearances with more distance or earlier start
- remove or amend crossing restrictions the aircraft cannot meet
- avoid expedite instructions that assume high drag capability
- sequence the aircraft with extra track miles if needed
- plan for a possibly higher, hotter energy state on arrival

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on a STAR into Curitiba in a fixed-wing training scenario. Speed brakes fail retracted. You still have forty miles and a crossing at six thousand.

You call:

ANAC123, speed brake failure, unable to meet crossing restriction at six thousand, request earlier descent or delay vectors.

Radar: ANAC123, roger speed brake failure, descend now to six thousand, cancel speed restriction, expect vectors for longer final.

You answer:

ANAC123, descending six thousand, cancel speed restriction, expect longer final.

You protect the restriction by talking early. You do not dive into an unstable mess.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, roger speed brake failure, say altitude request.
2. Radar: ANAC123, descend now flight level six zero, cancel crossing restriction.
3. Approach: ANAC123, expect delay vectors to lose altitude, advise when ready.
4. Approach: ANAC123, unable expedite, maintain present rate, traffic permitting.
5. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading one five zero for longer final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Speed brakes buy descent without a screaming dive. When they fail, distance becomes your friend.

Ask for earlier descent. Ask for vectors. Ask to cancel a restriction you cannot make. Do not wait until the fix to confess.

Brazilian crews sometimes say "sem speed brake" casually. Prefer speed brake failure and the operational limit: unable to meet restriction / unable expedite.

In the H130 you can often steepen with collective. That flexibility is a teaching analogy for "I need another way to lose altitude" — not a claim that the helicopter has speed brakes.

High and fast is recoverable with plan and radio. High and silent is how unstable approaches are born.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Speed brakes fail and you cannot make a published crossing altitude. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would advise ATC of the speed brake failure and that I am unable to meet the crossing restriction. I would request an earlier descent, delay vectors, or an amended clearance so I can lose altitude without an unstable dive, then continue the approach only when the profile is under control.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Busting a crossing restriction silently, then explaining after.  
  ✔ Report unable to meet restriction before the fix.

- ❌ Diving steeply to compensate for missing speed brakes.  
  ✔ Request track miles; protect a stable approach.

- ❌ Saying only "performance problem" without naming speed brakes.  
  ✔ Use speed brake failure plus the clearance you cannot accept.

- ❌ Claiming H130 speed brake procedures in type training.  
  ✔ Keep systems factual; teach the ICAO phraseology honestly.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Speed brake failure turns descent into a distance problem.

Say unable early. Ask for miles or an amended clearance. Protect stability.

The H130 may steepen without a speed brake panel — the exam still wants the jet vocabulary.

Name the system. Name the restriction you cannot meet. Get a clearance you can fly. That is how you arrive controlled, not chased.
