# Light Turbulence

**Catalog ID:** 0087  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers light turbulence — slight, erratic changes that do not significantly affect control.

Light Turbulence causes slight strain against seat belts, with little or no change in altitude or attitude.

In the H130, light turbulence is common in daytime heating, light coastal winds, and below small cumulus clouds.

You must still report it when useful, and know when light becomes moderate.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 operational communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Light Turbulence is turbulence that causes slight, momentary changes and slight strain on occupants, without significant effect on aircraft control.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Turbulência leve.

Turbulência que causa mudanças leves e momentâneas, com pequeno esforço nos ocupantes, sem efeito significativo no controle.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Light Turbulence occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain normal aircraft control
- report light turbulence when it helps ATC or other traffic
- monitor for intensification toward moderate
- secure loose items if turbulence may increase
- continue unless conditions worsen

Light Turbulence reporting commonly includes:

- light turbulence at altitude
- light turbulence occasional
- ride smooth above or below
- turbulence increasing to moderate

**Important:** Light turbulence is still useful information — especially when it marks the edge of worse weather.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC uses Light Turbulence reports to:

- build a picture of ride conditions on the route
- advise other aircraft of expected light chop
- detect trends toward moderate or severe conditions
- refine altitude recommendations
- support pilot decision-making with PIREPs

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are climbing out of Congonhas in your H130 on a warm afternoon.

Below two thousand feet you feel light, occasional bumps from daytime heating.

You report when asked:

Congonhas Tower, ANAC123, light turbulence occasional below two thousand, smooth above.

Tower passes the information to following traffic.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, say ride conditions on departure.
2. Approach: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported light turbulence below three thousand.
3. Approach: ANAC123, advise if turbulence increases.
4. Center: ANAC123, report ride conditions at altitude.
5. Approach: ANAC123, thank you for the PIREP.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Congonhas Tower, ANAC123, light turbulence occasional below two thousand.
2. ANAC123, light turbulence, smooth above three thousand.
3. ANAC123, light turbulence continuous, still controllable.
4. Approach, ANAC123, light turbulence increasing to moderate.
5. ANAC123, ride smooth, no turbulence.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Light turbulence is the bottom of the intensity scale.

Slight strain. Little altitude change. Aircraft fully controllable.

The sequence:

Feel. Classify. Report if useful. Monitor.

Do not inflate light into moderate.

Do not ignore light when it is increasing.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "leve" or "só um chop" on frequency.

Use light turbulence — ICAO standard intensity term.

In the H130, light chop on a hot afternoon is normal — but report trends.

Train yourself to use the correct word every time.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is light turbulence and when would you report it?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Light turbulence causes slight, momentary changes and slight strain on occupants, without significant effect on control. I would report light turbulence when ATC asks for ride conditions, when it helps other traffic, or when it is increasing toward moderate. I would include altitude and whether it is occasional or continuous.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Calling light bumps "severe" or "muito forte."  
  ✔ Use light turbulence for slight strain only.

- ❌ Saying "chop leve" without standard intensity.  
  ✔ Use light turbulence.

- ❌ Not reporting when turbulence starts increasing.  
  ✔ Update ATC if light becomes moderate.

- ❌ Omitting altitude in the PIREP.  
  ✔ Always state altitude with turbulence reports.
