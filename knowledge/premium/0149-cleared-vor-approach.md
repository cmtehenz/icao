# Cleared VOR Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0149  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Cleared VOR Approach — the ATC authorization to fly a VHF Omnidirectional Range instrument approach to the assigned runway.

The VOR approach is a non-precision approach. There is no glideslope. Descent is managed by the crew using step-down altitudes published on the chart until the Minimum Descent Altitude.

At airports like Curitiba, Navegantes, and Congonhas, VOR approaches remain valid alternatives when ILS or RNAV systems are unavailable, under NOTAM, or not certified for the aircraft type.

Understanding how to receive, read back, and fly a VOR approach in ICAO English — including its key differences from ILS — is essential for professional operations.

This lesson connects Part 2 phraseology with Part 4 navigation knowledge required at ICAO Level 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Cleared VOR Approach is an ATC authorization for the pilot to commence the VHF Omnidirectional Range approach to the specified runway, tracking the VOR radial as the lateral guidance and using published step-down altitudes to descend to the Minimum Descent Altitude, where the crew must acquire visual reference and land or execute the missed approach.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorização do controle de tráfego aéreo para o piloto iniciar a aproximação VOR para a pista especificada, rastreando o radial VOR como guia lateral e usando as altitudes de step-down publicadas para descer até a Altitude Mínima de Descida, onde a tripulação deve adquirir referência visual e pousar ou executar a arremetida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives Cleared VOR Approach, they should:

- confirm the VOR frequency is tuned, identified, and the CDI course is set to the inbound radial
- review and brief all step-down altitudes on the approach plate
- identify the Final Approach Fix and confirm crossing altitude
- set the Minimum Descent Altitude on the altimeter and brief the required visual references
- brief the missed approach point and the full missed approach procedure
- at MDA — if visual reference is established, continue to land; if not, execute the missed approach

VOR approach operations commonly include:

- tracking to the VOR overhead
- inbound radial tracking on the published course
- step-down altitude management to the final approach fix
- level flight at MDA until either visual contact or missed approach point
- missed approach execution if visual reference is not achieved

**Important:** In a VOR approach there is no vertical guidance. You must level off at MDA and fly level until the missed approach point. Descending below MDA without the required visual reference is a serious violation of instrument approach procedures.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Cleared VOR Approach to:

- assign an approach when the ILS is not available or not suitable
- sequence arrivals at airports with VOR approaches as the primary procedure
- provide a non-precision approach alternative when GNSS is restricted
- assign an approach type clearly when multiple procedures exist on the same runway
- allow the crew to use the published VOR procedure for final approach

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Curitiba in your H130. The ILS is out of service under NOTAM.

You have already briefed the VOR approach to runway one five. The VOR frequency is set and identified. Step-down altitudes reviewed. MDA confirmed and set.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, cleared VOR approach runway one five, report FAF inbound.

You respond:

Cleared VOR approach runway one five, ANAC123.

You track the inbound radial. At the FAF you report:

ANAC123, FAF inbound, runway one five.

You descend following the step-down altitudes published on the plate.

At MDA — visual contact with the runway. Continue for landing.

Professionally executed. Non-precision. Fully professional.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The VOR approach is the original instrument approach.

Before ILS. Before GPS. Before RNAV. Pilots flew VOR approaches.

And many still do.

The difference from a precision approach is fundamental: there is no vertical guidance.

You manage your own descent using the published step-down altitudes.

At MDA, you stop descending. You fly level.

You look for the runway. If you see it — land. If you do not — at the missed approach point, go around.

That discipline — stopping at MDA, flying level, committing at the missed approach point — is what separates a professional from a hazardous pilot.

In the H130, the VOR approach requires solid raw-data navigation. Track the radial precisely. Monitor your DME if available. Cross-check your timing on each step-down.

One common mistake: rushing the descent below MDA in marginal conditions.

That is the mistake that kills.

MDA is a floor. Not a suggestion. A hard floor.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe the key difference between a VOR approach and an ILS approach in terms of vertical guidance and crew responsibility.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The ILS provides both lateral guidance via the localizer and vertical guidance via the glideslope, with a Decision Height at which the crew must have visual reference to land. The VOR approach provides only lateral guidance through the VOR radial. There is no vertical guidance. The crew must manage their own descent using published step-down altitudes and level off at the Minimum Descent Altitude. At MDA they fly level until visual contact with the runway is established or until the missed approach point, at which point they must execute the missed approach if visual reference has not been achieved.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Descending below MDA without visual reference.  
  ✔ MDA is a hard floor. Level off and maintain until visual or missed approach point.

- ❌ Not briefing all step-down altitudes before the approach.  
  ✔ Review every step-down altitude on the plate during the approach brief.

- ❌ Not identifying the VOR navaid before commencing the approach.  
  ✔ Always confirm the VOR Morse code identification before using the navaid.

- ❌ Confusing the missed approach point with the MDA.  
  ✔ MDA is an altitude. The missed approach point is a position fix or time. Both matter.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Cleared VOR Approach Runway One Five

**Pronunciation:** kleerd VEE-OH-AR uh-PROHCH RUN-way WUN FYVE

**Word Stress**

- Cleared → kleerd
- VOR → VEE-OH-AR (three letters, each spoken separately)
- Approach → uh-PROHCH
- One Five → WUN FYVE

Practice:

Cleared... V... O... R... Approach... Runway... One Five.

Together:

Cleared VOR approach runway one five, ANAC123.

Speak each letter of VOR distinctly — VEE, OH, AR — not as a single word "VOR."

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**STEPS** — **S**et VOR frequency and ident, **T**rack the inbound radial, **E**xecute step-down altitudes precisely, **P**lateau at MDA — no lower, **S**ee the runway or go around at MAP.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Curitiba Approach transmits: "ANAC123, cleared VOR approach runway one five, maintain four thousand until established on final, report FAF inbound."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete clearance.

**Your second task**

You reach MDA and have no visual contact with the runway. What do you do and what do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would respond: Maintain four thousand until established on final, cleared VOR approach runway one five, ANAC123. I would report: ANAC123, FAF inbound, runway one five. At MDA with no visual, I would level off and fly to the missed approach point. At the MAP with no visual contact I would initiate the missed approach and transmit: ANAC123, missed approach, climbing to four thousand as published, ANAC123. I would not descend below MDA under any circumstances without the required visual reference.
