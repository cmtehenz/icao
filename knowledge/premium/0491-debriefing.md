# Debriefing

**Catalog ID:** 0491  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Debriefing — the structured post-flight or post-event review that extracts learning from what went well, what went wrong, and what will change next time.

Debriefing closes the CRM loop that briefing opens. Without it, the same error returns on the next sector.

For H130 crews, a short honest debrief after a difficult pad, sling, or IFR arrival is worth more than a long silent walk to the hangar. Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 English all benefit from clear debrief language.

Understanding debriefing in ICAO-standard English is essential across Parts 1, 2, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Debriefing is a facilitated, non-punitive review after a flight or significant event in which the crew reconstructs key decisions, identifies effective and ineffective behaviors, and agrees specific improvements for future operations.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Debriefing / Debriefing operacional.

Revisão facilitada e não punitiva após um voo ou evento significativo, na qual a tripulação reconstrói decisões-chave, identifica comportamentos eficazes e ineficazes e concorda melhorias específicas para operações futuras.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting a debrief, the crew should:

- start with facts, then analysis, then actions
- balance what went well with what must improve
- avoid personal attacks; focus on behaviors and systems
- for H130: capture pad, hoist, and weather lessons while memory is fresh
- note any ATC communication issues for SMS or company feedback
- agree one or two concrete changes — not twenty wishes

Debriefing commonly includes:

- what was the plan versus what happened
- threats managed well / missed
- communication and assertiveness moments
- radio and readback quality
- next-time actions

**Important:** A debrief is not a courtroom. Just Culture rules apply — honest errors are learning material, not ammunition.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

ATS units and operators use debrief outcomes to:

- improve how crews phrase unable and diversion calls
- identify recurring frequency or procedure traps
- feed SMS reports after communication breakdowns
- refine helicopter special-ops coordination with towers
- close the loop between an event and a procedure change

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You have just completed a go-around at Florianópolis after an unstable H130 approach. On the ground you debrief for five minutes.

Facts: late configuration, high on profile, go-around called by PM. What went well: assertive go-around and clear Tower call. What to improve: earlier energy management and a firmer unstable call at one thousand feet.

You also note that the first go-around call to Tower was rushed. Next time: standard phrase, then intentions. The debrief turns a messy approach into a cleaner next sector.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, go around, acknowledge.
2. Tower: ANAC123, report intentions after go-around.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm reason for go-around if able.
4. Ground: ANAC123, taxi to parking, report if further assistance needed.
5. Approach: ANAC123, cleared for second approach, advise when ready.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around.
2. ANAC123, requesting vectors for second approach.
3. ANAC123, unstable approach, going around.
4. ANAC123, taxi parking, negative assistance.
5. ANAC123, ready for second approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Debriefing is where professionals get paid in learning.

Keep it short. Keep it honest. Keep it kind.

Use a simple spine: facts, good points, improvements, actions. That structure also scores well in Part 1 and Part 4 answers.

In the H130, debrief the special operation the same day — wires seen late, crewman callouts, deck movement. Memory fades; ego hardens. Debrief while both are soft.

If ATC communication was part of the problem, say so factually and fix the phraseology next time. That is operational maturity.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How should a crew debrief after a go-around caused by an unstable approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The crew should review the facts without blame, recognize what worked — such as an assertive go-around and a clear call to Tower — and identify what must change, for example earlier energy management and a firmer unstable call. They should agree specific actions for the next approach and, if needed, file a safety report under Just Culture.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Skipping the debrief because “we landed safely in the end.”  
  ✔ Safe outcomes can still hide repeatable errors — review them.

- ❌ Turning the debrief into personal blame.  
  ✔ Focus on behaviors, timing, and system traps.

- ❌ Listing twenty problems with no agreed action.  
  ✔ Choose one or two concrete improvements.

- ❌ Ignoring radio performance in the debrief.  
  ✔ Communication quality is a CRM item — review it.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

After a go-around at Florianópolis, Tower asks your intentions. You plan a second approach after a short debrief airborne on roles.

**Your first task**

What do you say to Tower?

**Your second task**

In one ICAO Level 4 sentence, what learning point would you capture in the post-flight debrief?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, requesting vectors for second approach. Second task: We will call unstable earlier and go around without delay if energy is high — the successful go-around must become an earlier intervention next time, not a late recovery.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Debriefing turns experience into improvement.

Facts first. Learning second. Actions last.

Protect honesty with Just Culture. Protect standards with clear next steps.

In the H130, five honest minutes after a hard pad beat five silent days of repeating the same mistake.
