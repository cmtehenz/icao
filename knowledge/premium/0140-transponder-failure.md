# Transponder Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0140  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Transponder Failure — the situation where the aircraft's transponder becomes unserviceable in flight, and the procedures and phraseology required to inform ATC and manage the resulting loss of radar identification.

A transponder failure removes your Mode C altitude data and, in some cases, your entire secondary radar return.

In controlled airspace around Congonhas, Curitiba, or São Paulo, operating without a transponder requires immediate coordination with ATC.

In the H130, the transponder is your radar presence. Without it, you are a primary return at best — or invisible at worst.

Knowing what to say, what ATC needs, and what options exist is a critical operational skill.

## 📖 Meaning

Transponder Failure is the loss of transponder function in flight, requiring the pilot to notify ATC, follow applicable procedures for transponder-mandatory airspace, and coordinate continuation, diversion, or priority landing as appropriate.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do transponder.

Perda da função do transponder em voo, exigindo que o piloto notifique o controle, siga os procedimentos aplicáveis ao espaço aéreo com transponder obrigatório e coordene a continuação, desvio ou pouso prioritário conforme necessário.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a transponder failure occurs, the pilot should:

- attempt to reset the transponder and restore function
- notify ATC immediately if reset is unsuccessful
- state current position, altitude, and squawk code assigned
- advise whether Mode C altitude data is also lost
- request continued clearance, priority, or diversion as required

ATC may respond by:

- assigning a primary-only radar track
- issuing procedural separation
- restricting the flight to airspace where transponder is not mandatory
- granting priority handling for landing
- coordinating with adjacent sectors for reduced separation

**Important:** In Mode S transponder environments and ADS-B airspace, the failure affects more than radar. It may also invalidate traffic awareness systems for other aircraft. ATC must know immediately. Do not attempt to continue without notification.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC needs immediate transponder failure notification to:

- apply appropriate separation standards without Mode C
- advise adjacent sectors of the non-transponding aircraft
- coordinate priority handling if required
- prevent conflicts with other transponder-equipped traffic
- document the event for airspace safety records

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at flight level eight zero en route to Florianópolis in your H130.

You notice the transponder display has gone dark — no reply light, no code displayed.

You attempt a reset. No response.

You transmit:

Curitiba Control, ANAC123, transponder failure, unable Mode C, squawk was two four zero five, flight level eight zero, sixty miles north of Florianópolis.

Control responds:

ANAC123, roger, will provide procedural separation, advise intentions. Do you require priority?

You assess the route:

ANAC123, no priority required, requesting continue to Florianópolis, VMC on top, estimate Florianópolis in three five minutes.

Control clears you to continue with revised separation and coordinates with Florianópolis Approach.

One clear transmission opened the door to a managed, safe solution.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, confirm squawk. No return observed.
2. Control: ANAC123, transponder failure noted, advise intentions.
3. Control: ANAC123, cleared to continue, procedural separation applied.
4. Approach: ANAC123, expect radar advisory only, confirm altitude at each call.
5. Tower: ANAC123, no transponder, cleared visual approach runway three two, report final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

A transponder failure is not an emergency.

But it must be treated with the same urgency of communication.

The moment you lose the transponder return, ATC loses your altitude data.

In busy airspace like the São Paulo TMA, an unidentified primary return at flight level eight zero is a problem for every controller in the sector.

Your job: notify immediately. Give your position, your altitude, your last assigned code, and your intention.

Do not reset quietly and hope no one notices.

Do not delay the call while you troubleshoot.

Call first. Troubleshoot second.

In the H130, the transponder is relatively simple — a power cycle often restores function. But you make the call before the reset, not after.

If the reset works, you tell ATC: ANAC123, transponder restored, squawking two four zero five. The issue is closed.

If it does not, ATC has already begun protecting your airspace.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you say to ATC if your transponder failed in controlled airspace?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately notify ATC: Control, ANAC123, transponder failure, unable Mode C, squawk was [code], current altitude [altitude], position [position]. I would advise my intentions and ask whether priority handling or diversion is required. I would follow ATC instructions for procedural separation and confirm my altitude verbally at each call until the transponder is restored or I land.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Resetting the transponder quietly without notifying ATC.  
  ✔ Notify ATC first. If reset is successful, advise: Transponder restored, squawking [code].

- ❌ Continuing to operate as if nothing changed.  
  ✔ ATC has lost your Mode C data. Separation standards change. They need to know.

- ❌ Not stating the last assigned squawk code in the notification.  
  ✔ Include the code: squawk was two four zero five. ATC can use the primary return with that reference.

- ❌ Declaring an emergency unnecessarily for a transponder failure alone.  
  ✔ A transponder failure is not an emergency unless combined with other factors. Request priority, not emergency, if needed.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are at flight level seven zero, forty miles south of Curitiba in your H130, inbound to Joinville. The transponder shows no reply light and the display is blank. A reset does not restore it.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit to Curitiba Control?

**Your second task**

Control asks: "ANAC123, do you require priority for landing at Joinville?" You are VMC, fuel is good, weather is clear. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I transmit: Curitiba Control, ANAC123, transponder failure, unable Mode C, squawk was three three six zero, flight level seven zero, forty miles south of Curitiba, inbound Joinville. Regarding priority — conditions are VMC, fuel normal, weather clear — I would advise: Negative priority required, ANAC123, requesting normal sequence into Joinville, will confirm altitude at each call. I continue to confirm altitude verbally until landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

The transponder is your presence on radar.

Without it, you are harder to see.

In modern airspace, that matters.

Report it immediately.

Give ATC everything they need: position, altitude, last code, intention.

Do not reset first and talk later.

The safety margin belongs to the controller who knows the problem exists.

Give them that margin.

Then troubleshoot.
