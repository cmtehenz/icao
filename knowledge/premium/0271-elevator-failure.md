# Elevator Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0271  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Elevator Failure — loss or restriction of elevator control that degrades pitch authority, one of the most serious flight-control failures, requiring immediate aircraft control, abnormal procedures, and urgent ATC communication for priority landing.

The elevator commands pitch. Without reliable pitch control, airspeed, climb, descent, and flare all become hazardous. Crews may use trim, thrust, or alternate pitch devices per AFM — but the radio must convey urgency clearly.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, pitch attitude is primarily cyclic fore-aft, not an elevator surface. Use that analogy for "degraded pitch control" without inventing elevators on the H130. ICAO exams still expect elevator failure phraseology in fixed-wing scenarios.

Part 2 and Part 3: elevator failure, limited pitch control, Mayday or Pan Pan as the situation demands, priority landing.

## 📖 Meaning

Elevator Failure is a malfunction in which the elevator system jams, disconnects, or otherwise fails to provide normal pitch control, severely affecting the ability to control attitude and airspeed, and requiring abnormal procedures and immediate ATC notification for emergency or urgency handling and landing assistance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de profundor / Elevator Failure.

Malfuncionamento em que o sistema de profundor trava, desconecta ou de outra forma deixa de fornecer controle normal de arfagem, afetando gravemente a capacidade de controlar atitude e velocidade, e exigindo procedimentos anormais e notificação imediata ao ATC para tratamento de emergência ou urgência e assistência ao pouso.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When elevators fail, the crew should:

- control pitch with remaining methods per AFM — trim, thrust, standby systems as applicable
- declare the appropriate emergency level: Mayday if control is critical, Pan Pan if still manageable but urgent
- advise ATC of elevator failure and limited pitch control
- request priority vectors to the nearest suitable airport and a long, stable final
- avoid configuration changes that suddenly change pitch moment unless checklist requires them
- H130 analogy: degraded fore-aft cyclic authority — same communication logic, different hardware

Elevator failure scenarios commonly include:

- jammed elevator requiring trim/thrust pitch control
- disconnected elevator with partial authority
- pitch trim used as primary pitch control
- inability to flare normally — long runway essential
- exam: distress call with elevator failure

**Important:** If pitch control is marginal, this is not a "continue and see" event. Declare, get priority, and land on the longest suitable runway with a stable, shallow approach. Do not accept late configuration changes that pitch the nose uncontrollably.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use elevator failure reports to:

- provide immediate priority and clear airspace for a straight-in
- assign the longest suitable runway
- avoid altitude or speed assignments that demand aggressive pitch changes
- alert airport rescue and fire fighting
- coordinate the simplest possible approach path

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in a jet training scenario at eight thousand feet near Campinas. Elevator response freezes. You stabilize pitch with trim and thrust per procedure.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, elevator failure, limited pitch control, request priority vectors for immediate landing Guarulhos, longest runway.

Approach: ANAC123, Mayday acknowledged, turn right heading one two zero, expect runway two seven Right, emergency services alerted.

You continue:

ANAC123, right heading one two zero, runway two seven Right, souls five, fuel one hour.

You fly a gentle path. You do not experiment with large pitch inputs on short final.

1. Approach: ANAC123, Mayday acknowledged, say souls and fuel when able.

3. Approach: ANAC123, descend at pilot's discretion, maintain controllable rate.
4. Tower: ANAC123, runway two seven Right, cleared to land, all traffic hold, services standing by.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Elevator failure is pitch. Pitch is life. Treat it with the seriousness it deserves.

Stabilize with the AFM method — often trim and thrust on transport types. Then talk. Mayday if you are fighting the airplane; Pan Pan if you have a workable alternate pitch method but still need priority.

Ask for the longest runway and a shallow final. A normal flare may be impossible. Plan the touchdown energy early.

In the H130, fore-aft cyclic is your pitch. Degraded cyclic pitch authority would get the same radio honesty: limited pitch control, priority landing. Do not invent elevator hardware on the helicopter for academy briefs.

Never accept a slam-dunk descent that demands pitch you do not have.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Elevator control is largely lost; trim still provides slow pitch change. What do you communicate?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would stabilize the aircraft using the AFM alternate pitch method, then declare the appropriate emergency — Mayday if control is critical, or Pan Pan if still manageable. I would report elevator failure with limited pitch control, request priority vectors for the nearest suitable airport and the longest runway, and ask for a shallow straight-in approach. I would provide souls and fuel when able.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Under-calling a pitch control failure as a minor "system problem."  
  ✔ Use elevator failure and the correct Mayday or Pan Pan.

- ❌ Accepting a steep approach that requires large elevator inputs.  
  ✔ Request shallow approach and longest runway.

- ❌ Changing flaps or gear late without considering pitch moment.  
  ✔ Follow checklist timing; avoid surprise pitch changes.

- ❌ Mixing Portuguese "profundor" only, without English on ICAO frequency.  
  ✔ Say elevator failure in standard English.
