# Distraction

**Catalog ID:** 0475  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Distraction — any event, stimulus, or secondary task that pulls crew attention away from primary flight duties at a critical moment.

Distraction is broader than channelized attention. It includes cabin calls, passenger questions, non-essential radio chatter, tablet alerts, and even well-intended troubleshooting at the wrong time.

In H130 operations — especially EMS, VIP, and training flights — the cockpit is rarely quiet. Distraction becomes lethal when it coincides with configuration changes, approach gates, or low-altitude maneuvering.

Understanding how to manage distraction in ICAO-standard English — including sterile-cockpit discipline, deferral language, and clear ATC priorities — supports Part 1, Part 2, and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Distraction is any interruption or competing stimulus that diverts crew attention from aviate-navigate-communicate priorities, increasing the risk of procedural omissions, altitude deviations, and loss of situational awareness.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Distração / Distraction.

Qualquer interrupção ou estímulo concorrente que desvia a atenção da tripulação das prioridades aviar-navegar-comunicar, aumentando o risco de omissões procedurais, desvios de altitude e perda de consciência situacional.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When distraction threatens the flight path, the crew should:

- protect critical phases with sterile-cockpit discipline
- defer non-essential conversation until after climb or after landing
- acknowledge cabin or passenger inputs briefly, then return to flying
- use checklists as recovery tools after an interruption
- advise ATC if a distraction caused a missed call or delayed readback

Distraction defenses include:

- defining sterile phases: below ten thousand feet equivalent, approach, hover, landing
- assigning who handles cabin calls
- repeating the last incomplete checklist item after any interruption
- rejecting non-urgent company messages in critical phases

**Important:** The distraction itself is often harmless. The unfinished item after the distraction is what bites — gear, clearance, fuel check, or altitude set.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers mitigate distraction effects when they:

- repeat clearances that were not acknowledged
- keep transmissions short during busy approach phases
- ask the crew to confirm unfinished instructions
- avoid non-essential frequency congestion near the runway
- provide time for the crew to recover after a missed call

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

On short final to Belo Horizonte, a passenger asks about delay times. Both pilots answer. Nobody confirms landing clearance.

Tower:

ANAC123, confirm cleared to land runway one six.

You refocus:

ANAC123, affirm, cleared to land runway one six.

After landing you brief: sterile cockpit next time — cabin questions wait until rotor brake.

The distraction was social. The risk was procedural.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, confirm cleared to land runway one six.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say again, you were blocked.
3. Tower: ANAC123, go around, traffic on the runway.
4. Radar: ANAC123, confirm you copied hold short instructions.
5. Approach: ANAC123, read back altitude, maintain four thousand.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Distraction is rarely dramatic.

It is a question from the cabin. A phone buzz. A joke on frequency. Then a missed item.

In the H130, treat sterile cockpit as a professional boundary, not rudeness. Passengers can wait thirty seconds. The landing cannot.

After any distraction, ask: "Where were we?" Then finish the line.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

On final, a passenger question distracts the crew and you miss part of a tower call. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would return to the radios immediately: ANAC123, say again, ready to copy. I would not guess the clearance. After confirming landing or go-around instructions, I would restore sterile cockpit and complete any interrupted checklist item before continuing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Answering passenger questions during short final.  
  ✔ Defer cabin conversation until after landing or a safe phase.

- ❌ Guessing a partially heard clearance.  
  ✔ Request say again and read back the full instruction.

- ❌ Restarting a checklist from memory after interruption.  
  ✔ Return to the last known completed item and continue in order.

- ❌ Treating sterile cockpit as optional on familiar airports.  
  ✔ Apply sterile discipline especially on familiar, high-threat approaches.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On short final a passenger asks about the delay. You miss tower's landing clearance transmission.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

Tower says traffic is on the runway and issues a go-around. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, say again, ready to copy. Second task: ANAC123, going around, traffic on the runway. I would not invent a clearance after a distraction. Confirm, comply, then restore sterile cockpit for the next approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Distraction steals seconds. Omissions steal margins.

In the H130, protect the critical phases. Defer the rest. After every interruption, finish the unfinished item.

Hear the clearance. Fly the aircraft. Talk to passengers later.
