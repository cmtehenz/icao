# Poor Readability

**Catalog ID:** 0304  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Poor Readability — radiotelephony reception that is difficult, broken, or only partially understandable, requiring slower speech, shorter messages, repeats, frequency checks, and refusal to accept incomplete clearances. Core Part 2 skill for H130 operations in Brazil, where terrain, distance, cabin noise, and congested frequencies regularly degrade readability.

Poor readability is High priority because a misunderstood altitude or runway instruction is a safety event, not a language inconvenience.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master ICAO phraseology for poor readability, "say again," and recovery techniques until mutual understanding is restored.

Understanding poor readability in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Poor Readability is a condition in which radio transmissions are difficult to understand — partially copied, broken, distorted, or weak — requiring the crew and ATC to slow down, repeat, simplify, check equipment and frequency, and confirm critical clearances before acting on incomplete information.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Má legibilidade / Poor Readability.

Condição em que as transmissões de rádio são difíceis de compreender — parcialmente recebidas, cortadas, distorcidas ou fracas — exigindo que a tripulação e o ATC reduzam a velocidade da fala, repitam, simplifiquem, verifiquem equipamento e frequência, e confirmem autorizações críticas antes de agir com informação incompleta.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When readability is poor, the crew should:

- advise ATC: readable with difficulty / you are broken / unreadable
- request say again, or say again all after a known word
- speak slower, use shorter transmissions, and improve microphone technique
- verify correct frequency, volume, and headset connection
- request a frequency change if the frequency is congested or weak
- never execute a clearance that was only partially understood

Poor readability exchanges commonly include:

- readable with difficulty / readability three or less
- broken / unreadable
- say again / say again all after...
- speak slower / transmit shorter
- request frequency change

**Important:** Pride is not a radio skill. If you missed the runway number, say again. Acting on a guessed clearance is how incidents start.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers manage poor readability to:

- protect clearances from being misunderstood
- instruct pilots to adjust speech or equipment
- repeat or break clearances into shorter segments
- move aircraft to a better frequency when needed
- maintain separation when communication quality is degraded

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Operational scenario: descending toward Curitiba in the H130. Approach clearance comes through broken.

You hear only fragments. You transmit:

ANAC123, say again, you are broken.

Approach repeats slower. Still marginal:

ANAC123, readable with difficulty, request say again altitude.

When you finally copy "descend and maintain four thousand," you read back fully. You do not descend on a guess.

For Brazilian terminal areas at busy times, poor readability is normal — your discipline must be better than the frequency noise.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, you are unreadable, check your microphone.
2. Approach: ANAC123, readable with difficulty, speak slower.
3. Tower: ANAC123, say again, you were broken.
4. Approach: ANAC123, transmit shorter, frequency congested.
5. Control: ANAC123, if unable to copy, standby for frequency change.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, say again, you are broken.
2. ANAC123, readable with difficulty, request say again altitude.
3. ANAC123, wilco, speaking slower.
4. ANAC123, descending four thousand, ANAC123.
5. ANAC123, request frequency change, poor readability.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Poor readability is a stop sign for assumptions.

You either copy the clearance or you do not. There is no "I think it was four thousand."

Use the tools: say again, speak slower, shorter calls, mic check, frequency change. Then give a full readback of what you finally understood.

In the H130, cabin noise will tempt you to accept garbage audio. Do not. Key deliberately. Pause. Talk across the noise, not into it.

Part 2 examiners fail candidates who invent clearances. Poor readability is where that trap appears. Be the pilot who asks again.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC issues a clearance but the transmission is broken and you miss the altitude. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not change altitude based on a partial copy. I would advise that the transmission was broken or readable with difficulty and request say again, specifically asking for the altitude if needed. I would speak clearly and slowly on my next call, then read back the complete clearance once understood. If readability remains poor, I would request a frequency change or further repeats until the clearance is confirmed.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "ok" after a broken clearance.  
  ✔ Say again until the full clearance is copied.

- ❌ Reading back an altitude you are not sure about.  
  ✔ Only read back what you actually heard.

- ❌ Blaming only ATC instead of checking your own mic and volume.  
  ✔ Check both ends — technique and equipment.

- ❌ Speaking louder and faster when nervous.  
  ✔ Slower and shorter improves readability.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Say Again, You Are Broken / Readable with Difficulty

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, say uh-GEN, yoo ar BROH-ken / REED-uh-bul with DIF-ih-kul-tee

**Word Stress**

- Again → uh-GEN
- Broken → BROH-ken
- Readable → REED-uh-bul
- Difficulty → DIF-ih-kul-tee

Practice:

Say again, you are broken... readable with difficulty... request say again altitude.

Together:

ANAC123, say again, you are broken. ANAC123, readable with difficulty, request say again altitude.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**POOR** — **P**ause before guessing, **O**wn the say-again call, **O**nly fly confirmed clearances, **R**educe speed and length of speech.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Approach issues what sounds like a descent clearance but the call is broken. You are unsure of the altitude.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Approach repeats; you now copy descend and maintain four thousand. What do you transmit?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, say again, you are broken — or ANAC123, readable with difficulty, request say again altitude. Second task: ANAC123, descending four thousand. I would not have left altitude until the number was confirmed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Poor readability is High-priority Part 2 discipline.

Broken means stop. Say again. Confirm. Then fly.

In the H130 over busy Brazilian frequencies, this skill separates Level 4 professionals from dangerous guessers.

Never invent a clearance. Never be too proud to ask again.

Detect it. Declare it. Repeat it. Read it back clean.

That is the poor readability lesson — core Part 2 safety English.
