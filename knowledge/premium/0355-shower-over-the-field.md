# Shower Over the Field

**Catalog ID:** 0355  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Shower Over the Field — a rain shower affecting the aerodrome or final approach area, reducing visibility and braking action awareness, and possibly requiring delay, orbit, or diversion until the shower moves.

A shower over the field is often temporary. Professional English reports what you see, what you need, and whether you can continue visually or must hold or divert.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, convective showers build quickly in the afternoon — Congonhas, Campinas, and coastal Santa Catarina fields included. Helicopters can sometimes wait VFR nearby; IFR flights need clear requests to Approach.

Understanding shower-over-the-field language supports Part 2 phraseology and Part 4 comprehension of weather descriptions.

## 📖 Meaning

Shower Over the Field is a rain shower occurring at or over the aerodrome that temporarily reduces visibility and may obscure the runway or helipad, requiring pilots to assess whether to continue the approach, hold, delay, or divert until conditions improve.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pancada / chuva sobre o campo / Shower Over the Field.

Pancada de chuva ocorrendo no aeródromo ou sobre ele que reduz temporariamente a visibilidade e pode obscurecer a pista ou o heliponto, exigindo que os pilotos avaliem se continuam a aproximação, entram em espera, atrasam ou desviam até as condições melhorarem.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a shower is over the field, the crew should:

- obtain current visibility and runway visual conditions from ATIS or Tower
- decide continue, hold, orbit, or divert based on minima and fuel
- report unable to continue approach if the field is not in sight when required
- request delay or hold until the shower passes
- be prepared for wet surface and reduced visibility on short final
- update ATC when the shower clears and the approach can resume

Shower-related reporting commonly includes:

- shower over the field / field not in sight
- request hold / delay for weather
- request divert
- field in sight, request continue

**Important:** A shower is not automatically a thunderstorm. If lightning, severe turbulence, or hail is present, upgrade your language and your decision — do not treat it as a light rain delay.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers manage showers over the field to:

- pass visibility and runway condition information
- sequence holds and delayed approaches
- coordinate diversions when the shower persists
- reopen the approach sequence when the field clears
- warn arrivals of temporary visibility loss on final

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Inbound to Florianópolis in the H130, you see a heavy rain shower sitting over the runway. Visibility drops. The field is not clearly in sight for a visual approach.

You report shower over the field, request hold east of the field until the shower moves. Ten minutes later the shower drifts offshore. You call field in sight and continue for landing on a wet runway with care.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, shower over the field, visibility two thousand meters, advise intentions.
2. Approach: ANAC123, hold east of the field, expect approach in one zero minutes.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway wet, shower moving east, report field in sight.
4. Approach: ANAC123, divert approved to Navegantes if required.
5. Tower: ANAC123, shower cleared, cleared to land runway one four.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, shower over the field, field not in sight, request hold.
2. ANAC123, holding east of the field, expect approach when shower clears.
3. ANAC123, field in sight, request continue approach.
4. ANAC123, unable visual, request divert Navegantes.
5. ANAC123, cleared to land runway one four, runway wet copied.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Shower over the field is a temporary door — sometimes open, sometimes closed.

Look. Decide. Say intentions.

Brazilian pilots often say "chuva no campo" without a request. Prefer shower over the field, request hold, or field not in sight, request delay.

In the H130, you may orbit VFR clear of the cell if fuel and airspace allow — still tell ATC what you are doing.

Part 4 listening often includes weather picture descriptions. Train the phrase so you recognize it instantly in ATIS-style English and in Tower calls.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

There is a shower over the destination field and you cannot see the runway. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report shower over the field and field not in sight, then request hold or delay until the shower passes. If fuel or time does not allow waiting, I would request diversion to an alternate. When the field is in sight again, I would report field in sight and request to continue.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "chuva" without intention.  
  ✔ State shower over the field and request hold, delay, or divert.

- ❌ Continuing a visual approach when the field is obscured.  
  ✔ If you need the field in sight and do not have it — hold or go IFR as cleared.

- ❌ Treating a thunderstorm as a simple shower.  
  ✔ Upgrade decision and language for convective hazards.

- ❌ Not reporting when the field becomes visible again.  
  ✔ Report field in sight to reopen the approach sequence.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**SHOWER** — **S**ee the field or not, **H**old if obscured, **O**ffer divert if needed, **W**ait for clearance, **E**nter approach when clear, **R**eport field in sight.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Inbound to Florianópolis, a rain shower sits over the runway. You are on a visual approach and lose the field.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

The shower moves. You see the runway. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, shower over the field, field not in sight, request hold. Second task: ANAC123, field in sight, request continue approach. I would divert if fuel or the shower duration made holding unsafe.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Shower over the field is usually temporary — your decision must still be firm.

If you see the field and meet minima, continue with wet-runway care. If not, hold or divert.

Say what you see. Say what you need.

In Brazilian convective seasons, this call is routine. Make it sound routine and professional.
