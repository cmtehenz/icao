# MGB Temperature High

**Catalog ID:** 0443  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers MGB Temperature High — a high main gearbox oil temperature indication that signals inadequate cooling, low oil quantity, excessive load, or internal gearbox distress.

The main gearbox is the heart of helicopter power transmission. Rising MGB temperature is not a comfort item — it is a critical systems emergency that can precede seizure or catastrophic failure.

For H130 helicopter operations, high MGB temperature demands immediate power reduction, continuous monitoring, and a landing as soon as possible with clear urgency communication to ATC.

Understanding how to report MGB temperature high in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

MGB Temperature High is an abnormal indication that main gearbox oil temperature has exceeded normal operating limits, indicating potential lubrication, cooling, or internal mechanical problems in the main gearbox.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Temperatura elevada da caixa de transmissão principal (MGB).

Indicação anormal de que a temperatura do óleo da caixa principal excedeu os limites normais de operação, sugerindo problemas de lubrificação, refrigeração ou mecânicos internos na caixa de engrenagens principal.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When MGB temperature is high, the pilot should:

- reduce power and collective demand immediately within safe flight limits
- monitor MGB oil pressure, temperature trend, and unusual vibrations
- land as soon as possible at a suitable site
- declare Mayday if temperature continues rising, pressure falls, or vibration appears
- declare Pan Pan only if temperature stabilizes after power reduction and landing is imminent

MGB temperature high reporting commonly includes:

- main gearbox temperature high
- reducing power, landing as soon as possible
- request priority or immediate landing
- souls on board and position

**Important:** Do not continue a mission with rising MGB temperature. Gearbox failure can be sudden. Aviate, reduce load, land, then communicate.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to MGB temperature emergencies to:

- clear traffic for an immediate or priority landing
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable landing site
- coordinate fire and rescue services
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- avoid any clearance that increases power demand or delay

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in cruise at two thousand feet in your H130 along the coast near Santos.

MGB oil temperature rises into the high caution range. You reduce collective, check oil pressure, and feel for vibration. Temperature continues to climb slowly.

You declare Mayday, report main gearbox temperature high, and request immediate landing at the nearest suitable site. Approach clears traffic. You land, shut down, and request fire services stand by until the gearbox cools and maintenance inspects.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Mayday, main gearbox temperature high, say souls on board.
2. Approach: ANAC123, nearest suitable is Santos, turn right heading two seven zero, eight miles.
3. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land any runway, emergency services rolling.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say temperature trend and intentions.
5. Tower: ANAC123, wind two two zero at one two, report short final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

High MGB temperature means the gearbox is telling you it is unhappy.

Oil cools and lubricates. When temperature climbs, something is wrong: low oil, blocked cooler, overload, or internal friction.

On the H130, reduce load first. High collective keeps dumping heat into an already hot gearbox. Then land — as soon as possible, not as soon as convenient.

Watch the trend. Rising temperature with falling pressure or vibration is distress. Stable temperature after power reduction still warrants a prompt landing and maintenance action.

Say "main gearbox" or "MGB" clearly. Controllers need to understand this is transmission, not engine oil alone.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

MGB temperature is rising in cruise and does not stabilize after you reduce power. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Approach, main gearbox temperature high and rising, reducing power, request immediate landing nearest suitable. I would state position, souls on board, and that I am landing as soon as possible.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing to base because the gearbox has not failed yet.  
  ✔ Land as soon as possible — rising MGB temperature is critical.

- ❌ Calling only "oil temperature high" without saying main gearbox.  
  ✔ Specify MGB or main gearbox so the emergency is clear.

- ❌ Accepting a long vector or hold.  
  ✔ Request immediate landing and decline delay.

- ❌ Shutting down in a hover over unsuitable terrain to "save the gearbox."  
  ✔ Fly to a suitable site with minimum power, then land and shut down.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Main Gearbox Temperature High, Request Immediate Landing

**Pronunciation:** MAYN GEAR-boks TEM-pruh-chur HIGH, reh-KWEST ih-MEE-dee-ut LAN-ding

**Word Stress**

- Main → MAYN
- Gearbox → GEAR-boks (stress on first)
- Temperature → TEM-pruh-chur (stress on first)
- Immediate → ih-MEE-dee-ut (stress on second)

Practice:

Main gearbox temperature high... request immediate landing.

Together:

Main gearbox temperature high, reducing power, request immediate landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

The main gearbox does not give many second chances.

High temperature is a warning. Rising temperature with pressure loss is a countdown.

Reduce load. Land soon. Speak Mayday when the trend is against you.

On the H130, protect the transmission — and get the aircraft on the ground before the gearbox decides for you.
