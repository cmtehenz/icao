# Straight-In Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0122  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers the Straight-In Approach — the procedure and flight path concept describing an approach made directly to the runway without a circling maneuver or overhead traffic pattern.

This lesson is about the procedure concept and the leg itself, not the clearance phrase.

Cleared Straight-In Approach (0103) covers the specific ATC clearance.

Here, you learn what a Straight-In Approach means operationally — how it differs from a circling approach, when it applies, and how to manage the flight path from the initial approach fix to the runway threshold.

At Florianópolis, Navegantes, and Joinville, straight-in approaches are the norm in instrument conditions.

## 📖 Meaning

A Straight-In Approach is an approach made on the final approach course aligned with the runway centerline, without the circling maneuver, proceeding directly to a landing from the instrument procedure or radar vectors.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aproximação com pouso direto (sem manobra de arredondamento).

Aproximação realizada diretamente na trajetória de final alinhada com a pista, sem manobra de circulamento, procedendo diretamente ao pouso a partir do procedimento de aproximação por instrumentos ou de vetores radar.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

A Straight-In Approach applies when:

- the final approach course is aligned within a defined angle of the runway centerline
- the aircraft can descend to the threshold without a circling maneuver
- ATC assigns or approves the straight-in procedure
- weather minima for straight-in are met or exceeded

Straight-In Approach flight path management commonly includes:

- intercepting the final approach course from a vector or IAF
- maintaining descent along the instrument approach gradient
- identifying the runway at or before the applicable minima
- landing without deviating from the final approach course alignment

**Important:** A Straight-In Approach does not require a visual circuit. If the runway cannot be identified at minimums during a straight-in instrument approach, the missed approach must be executed — not a circle.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers use Straight-In Approach procedures to:

- improve traffic flow by eliminating circling maneuvers
- reduce workload in IMC arrival sequences
- provide efficient routing for aircraft inbound on final
- align with published instrument approach procedures
- minimize exposure to low-visibility maneuvering

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Florianópolis runway one four on the ILS.

Approach has vectored you onto the localizer.

The procedure is a straight-in ILS to runway one four.

You intercept the localizer and glideslope and fly the final approach course directly to the runway.

There is no overhead pass.

No circuit.

No teardrop.

Just the straight-in path from the intercept to the threshold.

At minimums, you acquire visual contact with the runway — still aligned, still descending on profile.

You continue to land.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, continue, cleared to land runway one four.

The straight-in approach is the most efficient path to the ground.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The Straight-In Approach is the most natural path to landing.

No circles. No turns. Just a straight path aligned with the runway.

But many pilots confuse the concept with the clearance phrase.

The concept is the flight path and the procedure.

The clearance is the specific authorization issued by ATC.

Understanding the concept means knowing this: you are committed to landing on that runway.

If you lose visual contact at minimums, the path does not change — you go around.

You do not circle to find another runway.

You do not detour to a better angle.

You execute the published missed approach.

Brazilian pilots sometimes attempt to circle at minimums on a straight-in approach when the runway is not immediately visible.

That is incorrect and dangerous.

Straight-in means land straight — or miss and go around.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the difference between a straight-in approach and a circling approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A straight-in approach is made with the final approach course aligned with the runway, and the aircraft lands without performing a circling maneuver. A circling approach is used when the approach course is not aligned with the landing runway or when the aircraft must maneuver visually at low altitude to align with a different runway. In a straight-in approach, if the runway is not in sight at minimums, the missed approach must be executed immediately — there is no option to circle.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Attempting to circle visually when the runway is not acquired on a straight-in instrument approach.  
  ✔ On a straight-in approach, not seeing the runway at minimums means missed approach — not a circle.

- ❌ Confusing Straight-In Approach (the concept) with Cleared Straight-In Approach (the clearance in 0103).  
  ✔ The concept describes the flight path; the clearance is the ATC authorization.

- ❌ Breaking off the final approach course to maneuver to a better angle.  
  ✔ Maintain the straight-in course. If unsafe, go around.

- ❌ Not reporting established when instructed on a straight-in.  
  ✔ Report established on final as instructed: ANAC123, established ILS runway one four.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are on an ILS approach to Florianópolis runway one four. Approach has vectored you to straight-in. At decision height, you see only the approach lighting system — no runway threshold.

**Your first task**

What do you do?

**Your second task**

Explain the difference between this situation and a visual circling approach.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

On a straight-in approach at decision height with insufficient visual reference to identify the runway threshold, I would execute the published missed approach immediately. There is no option to circle on a straight-in procedure. In a visual circling approach, the aircraft has visual contact with the aerodrome and maneuvers at low altitude to align with the landing runway — but only with established circling minima and conditions met. The straight-in approach requires either landing on the aligned runway or going around.
