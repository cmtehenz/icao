# GPWS Warning

**Catalog ID:** 0400  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers GPWS Warning — Ground Proximity Warning System alerts that the aircraft is in a potentially unsafe proximity to terrain or an unsafe descent profile.

A GPWS warning is not a nuisance. It is a terrain or flight-path conflict demanding immediate assessment and, often, immediate climb.

In the H130, terrain awareness systems and radio altimeter cues support low-level Brazilian operations near escarpments, offshore platforms, and mountainous approaches such as those around Belo Horizonte and the Serra do Mar. ICAO English requires clear GPWS reporting for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

A GPWS Warning is an aural and visual alert from the Ground Proximity Warning System indicating potentially unsafe terrain proximity, excessive descent rate, or an unsafe terrain closure condition that requires immediate pilot action.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aviso GPWS / Ground Proximity Warning System.

Alerta sonoro e visual do sistema de aviso de proximidade com o solo indicando proximidade potencialmente insegura com o terreno, taxa de descida excessiva ou fechamento inseguro com o relevo, exigindo ação imediata do piloto.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a GPWS Warning activates, the crew should:

- respond per the alert mode — correct flight path or climb without delay
- if the warning indicates terrain conflict, apply maximum climb performance
- do not delay while analyzing charts in the cockpit
- advise ATC: GPWS warning, climbing, or going around
- level off only when clear of terrain and the warning ceases

GPWS Warning reporting commonly includes:

- GPWS warning, climbing
- GPWS, going around
- request higher altitude
- terrain warning, recovering

**Important:** Respect the warning. If visual conditions clearly show no terrain conflict and the AFM allows a caution response, still correct the profile. When in doubt, climb.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to GPWS Warning reports to:

- provide immediate climb clearance if needed
- stop descents that conflict with terrain or MSA
- alert other aircraft in the same sector
- coordinate minimum vectoring altitudes
- support emergency climb without delay for readback perfection

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are descending on vectors toward Confins in your H130 at night with rising terrain ahead.

A GPWS warning activates. You apply climb power and pitch up.

You transmit:

Confins Approach, ANAC123, GPWS warning, climbing, request three thousand five hundred.

Approach clears you immediately and issues a new heading away from terrain.

You level only when the warning stops and terrain clearance is assured.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger GPWS, climb and maintain four thousand, turn left heading three six zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, minimum vectoring altitude three thousand, say altitude.
3. Tower: ANAC123, roger going around, GPWS acknowledged.
4. Control: ANAC123, radar terrain advisory, climb immediately, confirm climbing.
5. Approach: ANAC123, when clear of terrain, expect vectors for approach.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, GPWS warning, climbing four thousand, left heading three six zero.
2. ANAC123, leveling three thousand five hundred, GPWS clear, request vectors.
3. ANAC123, going around, GPWS warning, climbing.
4. ANAC123, climbing immediately, request higher.
5. ANAC123, clear of terrain, ready for approach when able.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

GPWS speaks when the flight path and the ground are about to meet.

Do not argue with it on short final at night. Climb. Then talk.

Modes differ — sink rate, terrain, glideslope — but the communication pattern is constant: GPWS warning, climbing or going around, request altitude.

In the H130, low-level VFR along the coast can put you near cliffs and wires. If a terrain awareness alert fires, treat it as real until proven otherwise visually in daylight with clear references.

Brazilian night IFR into mountainous terminals leaves no time for debate. Climb first. Explain second.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

A GPWS warning activates on descent. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would initiate an immediate climb or correct the flight path per the warning, using maximum performance if terrain is indicated. I would transmit: ANAC123, GPWS warning, climbing, request four thousand. I would not continue the descent until the warning is clear and terrain clearance is assured. Then I would request new vectors for the approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing descent to "see if it stops."  
  ✔ Climb or correct immediately. Investigate after you are safe.

- ❌ Long explanation before stating the climb.  
  ✔ Lead with: GPWS warning, climbing.

- ❌ Confusing GPWS warning with a routine altitude alert.  
  ✔ GPWS is terrain-related urgency — treat it as such.

- ❌ Failing to tell ATC why you climbed suddenly.  
  ✔ Report GPWS so ATC understands the deviation.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Night descent into Confins. GPWS warning. Rising terrain on the radar display.

**Your first task**

What is your radio call?

**Your second task**

Approach says "confirm climbing." You are through three thousand and still in the warning. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, GPWS warning, climbing, request four thousand. Second task: ANAC123, affirmative climbing, still GPWS, request higher and turn away from terrain. Keep climbing until clear.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

GPWS warning is the ground calling your flight path unsafe.

Climb. Correct. Communicate.

Do not negotiate with terrain. In the H130, low-level Brazilian ops make this discipline non-negotiable.

Hear it. Climb it. Call it. Then rejoin the approach when clear.
