# Seizure

**Catalog ID:** 0330  
**Category:** Passenger  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Seizure — an in-flight medical event in which a passenger experiences convulsions or seizure activity and requires protection, monitoring, and often medical assistance on landing.

Seizure is High priority in ICAO communication. Examiners may use "seizure" or older source language such as "epileptic attack." Use clear modern English: passenger having a seizure.

For H130 operations without cabin crew, the pilot must keep the aircraft stable while another passenger protects the person from injury if possible. Do not restrain violently; protect the head and clear hazards. Radio the problem without medical over-detail.

Understanding how to communicate a seizure event in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Seizure describes a medical event in which a passenger experiences uncontrolled convulsions or seizure activity, requiring immediate protection from injury, monitoring after the event, and communication with ATC if medical assistance or priority handling is needed.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a passenger has a seizure, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control
- protect the passenger from sharp objects and injury; cushion the head if possible
- avoid forcing objects into the mouth
- time the seizure and monitor breathing afterward
- advise ATC of a medical problem and request assistance if the event is prolonged, repeated, or followed by poor recovery

Seizure communication commonly includes:

- medical problem on board, passenger having a seizure
- passenger convulsions, request medical assistance
- request priority landing if seizures continue or recovery is poor
- update ATC when the seizure has stopped

**Important:** On the H130, space is tight. Brief passengers not to crowd the controls. After the seizure, the person may be confused — keep them seated and secured. Prolonged or repeated seizures justify Pan Pan and priority landing.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to seizure reports to:

- arrange medical assistance on arrival
- provide priority handling when requested
- understand the medical nature of the urgency
- request souls on board and updates on passenger condition
- coordinate ambulance meeting points at airports or helipads

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Florianópolis in the H130 with three passengers.

A passenger begins convulsing in the rear seat. The other passenger protects the head and keeps the area clear.

You maintain the approach path and transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical problem on board, passenger having a seizure, request medical assistance on landing.

The seizure stops after a minute. The passenger is breathing but confused. You continue priority handling. Medical services meet the aircraft.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say souls on board and passenger condition.
2. Approach: ANAC123, medical services alerted, continue approach.
3. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, ambulance standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm seizure has stopped.
5. Control: ANAC123, priority landing approved if required.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

3. ANAC123, seizure has stopped, passenger breathing, still request medical assistance.
4. ANAC123, repeated seizures, request priority landing and ambulance.
5. ANAC123, souls on board four, medical problem, passenger seizure.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Seizure language should be calm and exact.

Say passenger having a seizure. That is better than long descriptions of shaking.

ICAO source materials sometimes say epileptic attack. In modern radio English, seizure is clearer and sufficient. You do not need to diagnose epilepsy on frequency.

Protect the person. Protect the aircraft. Then speak.

In the H130, a thrashing passenger in a small cabin is a hazard to cyclic and collective if anyone leans forward. Keep helpers in the passenger area. Secure loose objects.

If recovery is good and the person has a known history, you may only need medical meet-and-assist. If seizures continue, escalate to priority landing without hesitation.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How do you report a passenger seizure to ATC, and when do you request priority landing?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I report a medical problem on board with a passenger having a seizure and request medical assistance. I request priority landing if the seizure is prolonged, if seizures repeat, or if the passenger does not recover well afterward. I update ATC when the seizure stops.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using only "epileptic attack" without a clear request.  
  ✔ Say passenger having a seizure and request medical assistance.

- ❌ Putting fingers or objects in the passenger's mouth.  
  ✔ Protect the head; do not force anything into the mouth.

- ❌ Ignoring post-seizure confusion.  
  ✔ Keep the passenger secured and monitored until medical handover.

- ❌ Failing to update ATC when the situation improves or worsens.  
  ✔ Give a short condition update.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

A passenger has a seizure lasting under one minute, then breathes and slowly recovers. You are ten minutes from landing.

**Your first task**

What do you say initially?

**Your second task**

What update do you give after the seizure stops?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical problem on board, passenger having a seizure, request medical assistance on landing. Second task: ANAC123, seizure has stopped, passenger breathing, still request medical assistance on arrival.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Seizure is a medical storm that usually passes — but not always safely.

Protect the passenger. Protect the flight controls. Speak plain ICAO English.

On the H130, space is small and help is limited. That makes clear radio and a prepared medical meet even more important.

Seizure. Assistance. Update. Land.
