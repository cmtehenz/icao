# Standard Operating Procedures

**Catalog ID:** 0463  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Standard Operating Procedures — the agreed, documented ways a crew flies, communicates, and manages abnormal situations so that performance is consistent and predictable under pressure.

SOPs are not bureaucracy. They are shared memory. When workload rises, crews fall back on what is standardized: callouts, profiles, checklists, and radio patterns. Deviating without briefing creates surprise — and surprise creates error.

For H130 ICAO Delta operations, SOPs link Part 1 CRM language, Part 2 phraseology, and Part 4 interaction. When SOPs require a go-around or unable call, that message must be delivered clearly on frequency.

## 📖 Meaning

Standard Operating Procedures are company- or operator-approved normal and abnormal procedures that define how crews configure the aircraft, communicate, make decisions, and respond to non-normal situations, providing a common baseline that reduces variability and supports effective CRM.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When applying SOPs, the crew should:

- brief departures and arrivals using the standard profile and callouts
- follow checklist discipline — challenge and response, not memory alone
- apply approach stability gates and mandatory go-around criteria
- use standard radio calls for go-around, unable, and emergency
- brief any intentional deviation from SOP before executing it

SOPs typically cover:

- PF/PM task sharing and sterile cockpit rules
- altitude and navigation callouts
- abnormal and emergency memory items plus checklists
- communication standards with ATC and cabin

**Important:** If you must deviate from SOP for safety, brief the deviation, fly it deliberately, and return to standard as soon as practicable.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers benefit from SOP-driven crews because:

- go-around and missed approach calls are predictable
- readbacks follow expected structure
- unable and diversion decisions arrive earlier
- emergency checklists produce clearer priority requests
- traffic flow improves when aircraft behave consistently

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Your H130 SOP requires a go-around if not stable by five hundred feet IMC. At six hundred feet you are still fast.

SOP decides. You do not negotiate with hope.

Tower, ANAC123, going around, not stable.

Tower: ANAC123, roger, climb and maintain two thousand, fly runway heading, contact approach.

You fly the published missed approach profile per SOP. Predictable for the crew. Predictable for ATC.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, runway two seven cleared to land, wind two seven zero at one two.
2. Tower: ANAC123, roger go around, climb two thousand, runway heading.
3. Approach: ANAC123, radar contact, vectors for another ILS approach.
4. Approach: ANAC123, unable hold at this time, advise intentions per your fuel.
5. Control: ANAC123, cleared as filed, climb via SID, confirm SOP departure.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around, not stable, climbing two thousand, runway heading.
2. ANAC123, missed approach, request vectors for ILS.
3. ANAC123, diverting per fuel policy, request clearance to alternate.
4. ANAC123, unable that speed, request present speed per performance.
5. ANAC123, climbing via SID, cleared as filed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

SOPs are the crew's common language when English, fatigue, or weather is hard.

They tell you who flies, who talks, when to go around, and how to say it.

In the H130, operator SOPs adapt helicopter realities — profiles, performance, offshore or EMS specifics — but the principle is identical: standardize the normal so you can recognize the abnormal.

Do not invent a personal technique on every flight. Personal technique is where monitoring fails, because the other pilot cannot predict you.

If ATC issues something that conflicts with SOP safety limits — speed, altitude, configuration — say unable. SOP and clearance must both be safe. Clearance alone is not enough.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your SOP requires go-around if unstable at the gate. ATC asks you to continue. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would follow the SOP safety gate and go around. I would transmit: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. SOPs exist to prevent pressure from pushing an unsafe continuation. I would then request vectors or divert as fuel and weather require.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating SOPs as optional when "experienced."  
  ✔ Experience means executing SOPs under pressure, not skipping them.

- ❌ Deviating without briefing the other pilot.  
  ✔ Brief any intentional deviation before you fly it.

- ❌ Continuing unstable approaches to satisfy ATC flow.  
  ✔ Go around per SOP; communicate clearly.

- ❌ Mixing airline jet callouts with helicopter SOP language.  
  ✔ Use your H130 operator standards consistently.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Your H130 SOP mandates go-around if not configured and on speed by five hundred feet. Tower says traffic requires you to continue.

**Your first task**

What do you say?

**Your second task**

After the go-around, Approach asks why you abandoned the approach. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around, not stable. Second task: ANAC123, unstable approach, request vectors for another approach — or divert if needed. SOP safety criteria override traffic convenience. Communicate the reason briefly and professionally.
