# Automation Bias

**Catalog ID:** 0470  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Automation Bias — the tendency to favor automated indications or recommendations even when contradictory raw data, charts, or ATC clearances suggest the automation is wrong.

Automation bias is not laziness alone. It is a decision error: the computer said so, so it must be true. Crews follow a wrong FMS path, accept a wrong computed landing performance, or ignore a conflicting chart because the display looks authoritative.

For H130 GPS and flight-director operations, automation bias links Part 1 human factors, Part 2 confirmation with ATC, and Part 4 crew challenge. When bias is suspected, confirm clearances and reject unsafe automated guidance.

## 📖 Meaning

Automation Bias is the human tendency to over-rely on automated information or recommendations and to disregard or under-weight contradictory information from raw data, charts, other crew members, or ATC, leading to incorrect decisions or continued flight on an erroneous automated path.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Viés de automação / Automation Bias.

Tendência humana de confiar excessivamente em informações ou recomendações automatizadas e de desconsiderar ou subestimar informações contraditórias de dados brutos, cartas, outros tripulantes ou ATC, levando a decisões incorretas ou à continuação do voo em uma trajetória automatizada errônea.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When countering automation bias, the crew should:

- treat automation as one source — never the only source
- compare FMS/GPS guidance with charts and ATC clearance
- prefer raw data when sources conflict, until resolved
- challenge "the box says so" with evidence
- confirm with ATC when automated routing disagrees with the clearance

Automation bias traps include:

- following a wrong waypoint because it is displayed
- accepting computed performance that conflicts with AFM limits
- ignoring a pilot's raw-data challenge
- descending on a path that violates published altitude constraints

**Important:** If the display and the chart disagree, stop and resolve. Do not vote for the prettier screen.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers help correct automation bias when crews:

- confirm routing after an FMS/clearance mismatch
- report navigation discrepancies
- request radar vectors when GPS guidance is doubted
- correct wrong readbacks influenced by a loaded procedure
- go around after realizing the wrong approach was followed

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Your H130 FMS shows a shortcut that descends early. The charted STAR forbids it. Approach clearance matches the chart, not the FMS creative path.

Your first officer says the box wants lower. You reject the bias:

Approach, ANAC123, confirm maintain four thousand until TROPY, FMS suggests early descent.

Approach: ANAC123, affirm maintain four thousand until TROPY, descend after.

You fly the clearance and the chart. The automation is corrected to match reality — not the other way around.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, affirm maintain four thousand until TROPY, then descend three thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you are cleared via the STAR, not direct.
3. Radar: ANAC123, you appear off route, confirm navigation.
4. Approach: ANAC123, stop descent, maintain three thousand, traffic.
5. Tower: ANAC123, go around, you are aligned with the wrong runway.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Automation bias flips authority. The box becomes captain. That is backwards.

You are pilot in command. Automation is a advisor with a screen.

In the H130, GPS approaches and overlays are excellent — until a wrong approach is loaded or a constraint is coded poorly. Charts and clearances remain law. Displays remain suspects until verified.

When a crew member challenges the automation, listen. Bias often answers "but the FMS shows..." — that sentence should trigger a cross-check, not automatic obedience.

Use ATC as an independent source: confirm altitude restrictions, confirm runway, confirm routing. That is Part 2 English defeating Part 1 bias.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The FMS commands descent but the chart and your understanding of the clearance require you to maintain altitude. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not descend. I would fly the clearance and chart, correct the FMS, and if needed transmit: ANAC123, confirm maintain four thousand until TROPY. Automation bias is rejected by verifying against independent sources and confirming with ATC.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Following the FMS when it conflicts with the clearance.  
  ✔ Clearance and charts win; fix the box.

- ❌ Dismissing a raw-data challenge because "GPS is accurate."  
  ✔ Investigate every conflict between sources.

- ❌ Reading back what the FMS shows instead of what ATC said.  
  ✔ Read back the clearance; then load the box to match.

- ❌ Continuing a wrong approach because it is already coupled.  
  ✔ Go around; reload; verify; return.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Your H130 FMS shows direct to the airport and a continuous descent. ATC cleared you via STAR with a crossing restriction. Your colleague says follow the FMS to save time.

**Your first task**

What do you decide?

**Your second task**

What do you say if doubt remains?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Fly the STAR and restriction — reject automation bias. Second task: ANAC123, confirm cleared via STAR, maintain four thousand until TROPY. Independent confirmation beats a confident display.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Automation bias makes the screen the authority. Take the authority back.

Clearance. Chart. Raw data. Then the box.

In the H130, GPS will usually be right — and occasionally be dangerously wrong. Verify. Challenge. Confirm with ATC.

That is how human factors keeps automation in its place — and keeps the aircraft on the cleared path.
