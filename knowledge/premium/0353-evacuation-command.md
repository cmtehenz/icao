# Evacuation Command

**Catalog ID:** 0353  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Evacuation Command — the clear, authoritative order to leave the aircraft immediately after an emergency landing, rejected takeoff, fire, or other life-threatening ground condition.

Evacuation command is Critical because hesitation kills. The words must be standard, loud, and followed by action — then ATC must hear that evacuation is in progress so fire services protect the scene.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, the command still applies: stop, secure, open doors, get clear of rotors and fire. Airliner slide language appears on exams; map it honestly to helicopter egress in line flying.

Understanding evacuation command in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Evacuation Command is the crew order directing all occupants to leave the aircraft immediately through available exits when remaining on board is more dangerous than egress — typically due to fire, smoke, or structural threat — coordinated with a radio report that evacuation is in progress.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Comando de evacuação / Evacuation Command.

Ordem da tripulação determinando que todos os ocupantes deixem a aeronave imediatamente pelas saídas disponíveis quando permanecer a bordo é mais perigoso do que sair — tipicamente devido a fogo, fumaça ou ameaça estrutural — coordenada com um informe rádio de que a evacuação está em andamento.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an evacuation command is required, the crew should:

- stop and secure the aircraft per AFM (rotors/engines as required on H130)
- decide evacuate versus remain based on fire, smoke, and structural risk
- command evacuation clearly: evacuate evacuate evacuate / exit now
- open usable exits; direct occupants away from rotors, fire, and intakes
- advise ATC: evacuating / emergency evacuation in progress
- account for souls when able; do not re-enter for belongings

Evacuation command reporting commonly includes:

- evacuating now / emergency evacuation in progress
- fire / smoke / uncontrollable fire (reason)
- request fire services
- all souls clear / souls evacuated

**Important:** The cabin hears the command. ATC hears evacuating. Do not delay the radio call while searching for perfect wording — short and standard wins.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to evacuation reports to:

- confirm fire and rescue are on scene and positioned correctly
- hold other traffic and protect the runway or helipad
- obtain souls evacuated versus missing
- coordinate medical services for injuries
- manage airport emergency plan activation

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

After an engine fire landing at Congonhas, smoke enters the H130 cabin on the runway. You stop, shut down, and command evacuate. Doors open. Occupants move clear of the rotor disc.

You transmit that you are evacuating and request fire services. Tower already has equipment rolling. When all are clear, you report all souls clear of the aircraft.

The command inside and the call outside happen within seconds of each other.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger, evacuating, fire services on the way.
2. Tower: ANAC123, confirm all souls clear when able.
3. Tower: ANAC123, remain clear of the aircraft, equipment approaching.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and if anyone is injured.
5. Tower: ANAC123, emergency services with you, advise if fire continuing.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, evacuating now, smoke in cabin, request fire services.
2. ANAC123, emergency evacuation in progress, runway one seven.
3. ANAC123, all souls clear of aircraft, fire services required.
4. ANAC123, evacuating, engine fire, uncontrollable smoke.
5. ANAC123, souls four, all clear, one minor injury, request medical.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Evacuation command is leadership under heat and smoke.

Decide. Command. Move. Tell ATC.

Brazilian crews sometimes soften the order — "pode ir saindo" — when the situation needs evacuate evacuate evacuate.

On the ICAO oral, know airliner exit/slide vocabulary. On the H130 line, command egress through doors, away from rotors, and report evacuating without inventing slides.

Critical priority means you do not wait for a second opinion when fire or toxic smoke is present. Unnecessary evacuation has risks — but confirmed fire on the ground is not the time to debate.

Train the command in English until it is automatic.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When do you give an evacuation command, and what do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I give an evacuation command when remaining on board is more dangerous than leaving — typically fire, smoke, or structural threat after landing. I secure the aircraft, command evacuate, get occupants clear, and tell ATC that evacuation is in progress with the reason and a request for fire services. When able I report all souls clear.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Soft cabin language instead of a clear evacuate command.  
  ✔ Use evacuate evacuate evacuate — loud and standard.

- ❌ Evacuating without telling ATC.  
  ✔ Report evacuating so fire services position correctly.

- ❌ Re-entering for bags or tablets.  
  ✔ Never re-enter for belongings.

- ❌ Using only slide commands for an H130 scenario.  
  ✔ Use doors and clear-of-rotors language for the helicopter; keep slide terms for airliner exam items.

**Your first task**

**Your second task**

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Evacuation command is Critical — decide fast, speak loud, move clear.

Secure. Command. Egress. Report.

ATC cannot pull people from the cabin. They can bring fire trucks to the right place if you say evacuating.

In the H130, rotors and doors replace slides — the urgency does not change.

Command it. Clear the aircraft. Account for souls.
