# Weather Deviation

**Catalog ID:** 0186  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Weather Deviation — the intentional departure from the cleared route or track to avoid hazardous weather, typically thunderstorms, severe turbulence, hail, or embedded convective cells.

Weather deviation is not a casual heading change. It is a safety-critical navigation action that must be coordinated with ATC whenever possible, executed with clear communication of direction and distance, and closed with a return-to-route report when the hazard is clear.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations in Brazilian airspace — Congonhas, Guarulhos, Santos Dumont, Curitiba, Florianópolis — convective weather is a frequent summer and afternoon threat. The H130 can often divert around cells at lower altitudes and shorter distances than jets, but the phraseology and discipline remain the same: request, advise, deviate, and report back on course.

Understanding how to request weather deviation, report the extent of the offset, and resume the cleared route in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Weather Deviation is a deliberate lateral or vertical departure from the ATC-cleared route, track, or altitude, initiated by the flight crew to avoid hazardous meteorological conditions, coordinated with ATC when communications permit, and followed by a return to the cleared route once the weather hazard is clear of the flight path.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Desvio por condições meteorológicas / Weather Deviation.

Afastamento lateral ou vertical deliberado da rota, trilha ou altitude autorizada pelo controle, iniciado pela tripulação para evitar condições meteorológicas perigosas, coordenado com o ATC sempre que as comunicações permitirem, e seguido do retorno à rota autorizada assim que o perigo meteorológico estiver fora da trajetória de voo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When planning or executing a weather deviation, the crew should:

- identify the hazardous weather visually, by weather radar, or by PIREP and SIGMET information
- determine the safest direction and approximate distance of the offset (left or right of track)
- request weather deviation from ATC before leaving the cleared route whenever radio contact is available
- if unable to obtain clearance in time, deviate as necessary for safety and advise ATC as soon as practicable
- report the new heading or offset distance and when clear of weather
- request or accept a clearance to resume own navigation or rejoin the original route

Weather deviation operations commonly include:

- left or right of track offsets around convective cells
- altitude change requests when lateral deviation is constrained by airspace or terrain
- temporary heading assignments issued by ATC to vector around weather
- return-to-route clearances after the cell is clear
- coordination with adjacent sectors when the deviation crosses FIR or approach boundaries

**Important:** Safety of flight takes priority. If immediate deviation is required and ATC cannot be reached in time, deviate and advise as soon as possible: direction, distance, and intentions. Never fly into a thunderstorm to preserve a route clearance. In the H130, use the aircraft's maneuverability — but still communicate the deviation clearly so ATC can protect your new path from other traffic.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with weather deviation to:

- keep aircraft clear of hazardous weather while maintaining separation from other traffic
- assign headings or offsets that fit the overall traffic flow
- coordinate deviations that may enter adjacent sectors or restricted airspace
- resequence arrivals and departures when multiple aircraft deviate around the same cell
- restore aircraft to the cleared route or STAR once weather is no longer a factor

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are en route from Congonhas to Florianópolis at flight level nine zero. Ahead, a line of embedded thunderstorms blocks the direct track. Weather radar paints a solid cell twenty miles ahead, with a clearer corridor to the left.

You call radar and request weather deviation left of track. Radar clears you left of track, report when clear of weather.

You turn left twenty degrees, offset approximately fifteen miles, and remain clear of the cell. Once past the weather, you report clear and request to resume own navigation to Florianópolis.

Radar: ANAC123, resume own navigation, direct Florianópolis, report established on course.

You rejoin the route. Deviation complete. Traffic protected. Weather avoided.

That is professional weather deviation — requested early, flown deliberately, closed with a clear report.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, weather deviation approved left of track, report when clear.
2. Control: ANAC123, unable left due traffic, deviate right of track, advise distance.
3. Radar: ANAC123, fly heading one eight zero for weather avoidance, expect further vectors.
4. Approach: ANAC123, cleared to deviate as necessary for weather, remain outside restricted area bravo.
5. Radar: ANAC123, when clear of weather, resume own navigation, direct Florianópolis.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Weather deviation is a negotiation between safety and traffic.

You see the cell. You know you cannot continue on track. ATC knows the traffic picture. Your job is to state what you need — left or right, how far — early enough for ATC to protect you.

Do not wait until you are inside the rain shaft to call. Request when the cell is still twenty to thirty miles ahead. That gives ATC time to coordinate.

If ATC says unable left due traffic, accept right — or request an altitude change if lateral options are blocked. Do not argue with a thunderstorm.

In the H130, you can often take a tighter offset than a jet. That helps in busy terminal areas around Congonhas and Guarulhos. Still report the offset. ATC cannot protect what they cannot see in your intentions.

When clear of weather, say so. Resume own navigation only when cleared. Closing the loop is as important as opening it.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are on an IFR route and weather radar shows a thunderstorm directly ahead. ATC has not yet issued a deviation clearance. Describe what you do.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would assess the safest direction of deviation using weather radar and visual cues if available. I would request weather deviation early, stating left or right of track and the approximate distance needed. If ATC cannot approve immediately and the cell is an immediate hazard, I would deviate as necessary for safety and advise ATC as soon as practicable of my heading, offset, and intentions. Once clear of weather, I would report clear and request to resume own navigation or rejoin the cleared route. Safety of flight comes first, but communication must follow without delay.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "request deviation" without left/right or distance.  
  ✔ State direction and extent: request weather deviation left of track, fifteen miles.

- ❌ Deviating silently and reporting only after returning to route.  
  ✔ Advise ATC as soon as practicable when leaving the cleared track.

- ❌ Remaining on the offset indefinitely after weather is clear.  
  ✔ Report clear of weather and request resume own navigation.

- ❌ Flying into the cell because ATC said "stand by."  
  ✔ If safety requires immediate action, deviate and advise — do not penetrate the storm.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Request Weather Deviation Left of Track

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, reh-KWEST WEH-ther dee-vee-AY-shun LEFT uv TRAK

**Word Stress**

- Weather → WEH-ther (stress on first)
- Deviation → dee-vee-AY-shun (stress on third)
- Left → LEFT
- Track → TRAK

Practice:

Request weather deviation... left of track... fifteen miles.

Together:

ANAC123, request weather deviation left of track due to thunderstorm, approximately fifteen miles.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**DEVIATE** — **D**etect the cell early, **E**stimate left or right offset, **V**oice the request before you need it, **I**nitiate only with clearance or for safety, **A**dvise heading and distance, **T**ell ATC when clear, **E**stablish back on route when cleared.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are cruising from Curitiba to Florianópolis. A solid convective cell blocks your track twenty-five miles ahead. Radar has not issued any weather clearance.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit to request weather deviation?

**Your second task**

Radar replies: "ANAC123, unable left due traffic, deviate right of track, report when clear." After passing the cell, what do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, request weather deviation left of track due to thunderstorm, approximately fifteen miles. Second task: ANAC123, clear of weather, request resume own navigation, direct Florianópolis. I would not remain on the offset without a clearance to rejoin, and I would accept the right-of-track solution when left was unavailable due to traffic.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Weather deviation is professional judgment spoken clearly on the radio.

See the hazard early. Request early. State left or right and how far.

If ATC cannot approve your preferred side, take the available side — or climb or descend if that is safer and cleared.

If you must deviate for safety before clearance, do it — then tell ATC immediately.

When the weather is behind you, close the loop: clear of weather, request resume own navigation.

In Brazilian summer ops around Congonhas, Guarulhos, and the south coast, this call will come often. Make it clean every time.

That is how you keep the H130 — and the traffic around you — out of the storm.
