# Debris on Taxiway

**Catalog ID:** 0220  
**Category:** Airport Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Debris on Taxiway — the report or advisory that foreign objects, loose materials, or other debris are present on a taxiway and may damage aircraft or endanger ground operations.

Debris on a taxiway is an immediate ground hazard. It can puncture tires, damage skids or wheels, and — for helicopters — become lethal FOD in the rotor system. ATC may advise debris, close a taxiway, or ask you to report whether the path is clear. Pilots must also know how to report debris they see first.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations at Congonhas, Santos Dumont, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Guarulhos, taxiway debris is high priority: downwash can lift objects you did not notice at idle. Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 speaking both test clear hazard reporting and safe decision-making.

Understanding how to report, avoid, and respond to debris on the taxiway is essential for ICAO proficiency and real apron safety.

## 📖 Meaning

Debris on Taxiway means loose objects, fragments, or materials are present on a taxiway surface in a way that may damage aircraft, injure personnel, or require taxiway avoidance, inspection, or closure until the area is cleared.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Debris on taxiway / Detritos / objetos na taxiway.

Significa que objetos soltos, fragmentos ou materiais estão presentes na superfície de uma taxiway de forma que podem danificar aeronaves, ferir pessoas ou exigir desvio, inspeção ou interdição até a área ser limpa.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When debris is reported or observed on a taxiway, the pilot should:

- stop or avoid the contaminated section if it is on your route
- report position, taxiway designator, and brief description of the debris
- do not taxi through debris to "get it over with"
- for helicopters, avoid hover taxi that could lift and ingest debris
- accept a revised taxi route without argument
- after passing a cleared area, continue lookout — debris can migrate

Debris on taxiway commonly includes:

- metal fragments, safety wire, or tools
- plastic, paper, or cargo wrapping
- gravel and pavement pieces after works
- wildlife remains or nesting material
- parts fallen from other aircraft or vehicles

**Important:** Seeing debris and saying nothing is a professional failure. Your report protects the next aircraft. In the H130, if you are unsure whether an object is on your path, stop and tell Ground. A short delay beats a compressor or blade event.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers communicate debris on taxiway to:

- protect aircraft from foreign object damage
- close or restrict taxiways until inspection and removal
- re-route traffic quickly with shared awareness
- prompt pilot reports that supplement vehicle inspections
- prevent secondary incidents from multiple aircraft hitting the same object

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are taxiing via Alpha at Congonhas in the H130 toward the holding point.

Ahead you see a metal strip on the centerline. You stop.

You call Ground and report debris on Alpha abeam the fire station. Ground stops other traffic, sends a vehicle, and clears you via Bravo.

You did not hover over it. You did not pretend it was "probably nothing." You protected the rotorcraft and the airport.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, Alpha closed, debris on taxiway, taxi via Bravo.
2. Tower: ANAC123, taxi with caution, debris reported on Charlie, inspection in progress.
3. Ground: ANAC123, hold position, debris on taxiway ahead, vehicle en route.
4. Ground: ANAC123, confirm taxiway Alpha clear of debris from your position.
5. Tower: ANAC123, vacate via Delta, Alpha closed due debris.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

2. ANAC123, taxi with caution, debris on Charlie copied.
3. ANAC123, holding position, debris ahead.
4. ANAC123, Alpha not clear, debris on centerline abeam fire station.
5. ANAC123, vacating via Delta, Alpha closed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Debris is small until it is inside your aircraft.

On a jet, it is often tires and engines. On the H130, it is the entire rotor system and anything downwash can throw.

If you see it, report it with location and a short description: metal strip, plastic bag, gravel pile. Do not write a novel. Do not stay silent.

If ATC advises debris, treat the taxiway as contaminated until told clear. Revised routing is not an inconvenience — it is the fix.

Part 3 examiners like this topic because it tests plain English under operational pressure: what you saw, where, and what you did. Keep structure: observe, stop, report, comply with new clearance.

Never blow debris away with collective. That is how one problem becomes many.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

While taxiing you see a piece of metal on the taxiway centerline. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would stop the aircraft immediately and avoid taxiing over the object. I would report to Ground with my position and a short description — for example metal debris on the taxiway centerline abeam a known reference. I would request an alternate taxi route and would not use hover taxi over the debris because downwash could lift it into the rotors or toward people. I would wait for ATC instructions and continue only on a cleared route.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Seeing debris and continuing because "it looks light."  
  ✔ Stop, report, and request another route.

- ❌ Trying to blow debris aside with rotor downwash.  
  ✔ Never use downwash as a broom; it creates uncontrolled FOD.

- ❌ Reporting "something on the taxiway" with no location.  
  ✔ Give taxiway name and a clear position reference.

- ❌ Hover-taxiing across a debris advisory area to save time.  
  ✔ Ground taxi or reroute; protect the rotor system.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Debris on the taxiway is a stop-and-speak problem.

See it. Stop. Report location. Accept the new route.

Do not roll over it. Do not hover over it. Do not stay quiet.

In the H130, FOD discipline is survival discipline.

That is high-priority airport English — and high-priority airmanship.
