# Collective Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0415  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Collective Failure — loss of normal collective pitch control, leaving the pilot unable to command climb or descent through the collective in the usual way and requiring emergency landing procedures and clear distress communication.

Collective failure is a flight-control emergency. Lift management is compromised. The radio must be short, standard, and immediate.

For H130 operations in Brazil — city pads, coastal routes, and Amazon transfers — you must declare the emergency, state collective failure, and communicate landing intentions while flying the aircraft.

Understanding how to report collective failure in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Collective Failure is a malfunction of the collective control system that prevents the pilot from normally increasing or decreasing main rotor pitch, compromising vertical control and requiring immediate emergency procedures and an expedited landing.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do coletivo.

Malfuncionamento do sistema de comando coletivo que impede o piloto de aumentar ou diminuir normalmente o passo do rotor principal, comprometendo o controle vertical e exigindo procedimentos de emergência imediatos e pouso expedito.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When collective failure occurs, the crew should:

- fly the aircraft using remaining controls and AFM emergency procedures
- establish the best controllable flight path toward a suitable landing area
- declare mayday with nature of failure and intentions
- advise souls on board and fuel when able
- keep transmissions short until on the ground

Collective failure reporting commonly includes:

- mayday, collective failure
- making emergency landing
- position and intentions
- souls and fuel on request

**Important:** Aviate first. A perfect mayday with an unflown aircraft helps nobody. Control what you can, then speak.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to collective failure maydays to:

- clear traffic and provide priority handling
- offer nearest suitable landing sites and weather
- alert emergency services to the landing area
- provide radar vectors if the pilot requests them
- keep the frequency clear for the distressed aircraft

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are five miles west of Congonhas at two thousand feet in your H130 when collective response is lost. You enter the emergency profile per AFM and transmit:

Mayday mayday mayday, São Paulo Approach, collective failure, making emergency landing, five miles west Congonhas, two thousand feet.

Approach clears the area, alerts emergency services, and asks souls and fuel. You land in an open field and report down and safe when able.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, mayday acknowledged, all traffic cleared, say intentions.
2. Approach: ANAC123, nearest open area your twelve o'clock two miles, emergency services alerted.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
4. Approach: ANAC123, squawk seven seven zero zero if able.
5. Approach: ANAC123, roger landing, report on ground when able.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, mayday, collective failure, emergency landing west of Congonhas.
2. ANAC123, proceeding to open area twelve o'clock two miles.
3. ANAC123, souls four, fuel one hour.
4. ANAC123, squawking seven seven zero zero.
5. ANAC123, on ground, aircraft intact, request assistance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Collective failure English is mayday English.

Say the aircraft callsign, the failure, and the plan. Position helps ATC help you.

In the H130, vertical path is collective-driven. When that path is broken, every second of flying is borrowed time. Do not narrate the malfunction on frequency — fly, then state facts.

Brazilian urban ops leave few forced-landing options. Ask for the nearest open area if you need it. Accept vectors only if they reduce workload.

After landing, one short call — on ground, status — closes the emergency for ATC and starts the rescue correctly.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You lose collective control near a city. What do you transmit?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare distress: Mayday mayday mayday, collective failure, making emergency landing, position and altitude. When able I would give souls and fuel. I would keep the call short and fly the emergency procedure first.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Explaining the technical failure in long Portuguese on frequency.  
  ✔ Mayday, collective failure, intentions, position — in English.

- ❌ Delaying the mayday until a landing site is perfect.  
  ✔ Declare early; refine intentions while flying.

- ❌ Forgetting position in the initial call.  
  ✔ Position lets ATC clear traffic and send help.

- ❌ Blocking the frequency with continuous updates.  
  ✔ Fly; answer when ATC asks; report on ground when able.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Collective failure five miles west of Congonhas at two thousand feet.

**Your first task**

Give the initial mayday call.

**Your second task**

Approach asks souls and fuel while you are still flying the approach to a field. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday mayday mayday, São Paulo Approach, ANAC123, collective failure, making emergency landing, five miles west Congonhas, two thousand. Second task: ANAC123, souls four, fuel one hour — short answer, then back to flying. If workload is critical I may say: ANAC123, stand by, then give souls and fuel when the landing path is stable.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Collective failure steals vertical control. The radio must not steal your attention.

Mayday. Nature. Intentions. Position.

In the H130 over Brazilian cities, options shrink fast. Fly the procedure. Speak in facts. Land. Report on ground.

That is how control failure English keeps you alive and gets help to the right place.
