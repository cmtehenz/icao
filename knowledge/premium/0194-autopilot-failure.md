# Autopilot Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0194  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Autopilot Failure — unexpected disengagement or unavailability of the automatic flight control system, requiring immediate transition to manual flight and, when relevant, clear communication with ATC if the failure affects ability to comply with the clearance.

Autopilot failure is first a handling event, then a communication event. Fly the aircraft. Stabilize attitude, altitude, and heading. Then assess whether you need ATC assistance — altitude deviation, inability to accept an RNAV procedure that assumed automation, or a request for simplified clearances.

For H130 helicopter operations, autopilot (when installed and engaged) reduces workload in IFR cruise and approaches between Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Santos Dumont. Sudden disconnect in IMC raises workload quickly. Crews must hand-fly competently and speak clearly if the clearance must change.

Understanding how to report autopilot failure and manage the operational consequences supports Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Autopilot Failure is the unintended disengagement, malfunction, or unavailability of the aircraft's automatic flight control system, requiring the pilot to assume manual control of the aircraft and, when the failure affects navigation compliance or workload management, to advise ATC and request an amended clearance if necessary.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the autopilot fails or disconnects unexpectedly, the crew should:

- assume manual control immediately — attitude, altitude, heading, airspeed
- silence or acknowledge disconnect warnings per AFM procedure
- stabilize the flight path before troubleshooting
- determine whether the failure is a simple disconnect or a system fault preventing re-engagement
- advise ATC if altitude or track was compromised, or if you need a simpler clearance
- continue the flight manually or with remaining automation per AFM limitations

Autopilot failure scenarios commonly include:

- unexpected disconnect in IMC cruise or approach
- inability to re-engage after disconnect
- disconnect caused by trim, servo, or sensor faults
- increased workload requiring vectors instead of a complex RNAV procedure
- need to declare inability to accept certain coupled approach clearances

**Important:** Aviate first. Do not make a radio call while the helicopter is pitching or rolling uncorrected. In the H130, hand-flying skill is fundamental — autopilot is an aid, not a crutch. Once stable, tell ATC only what they need: altitude deviation if any, request for heading vectors, or that you are continuing manually.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with autopilot failure reports to:

- understand sudden altitude or track deviations
- provide simplified clearances or radar vectors to reduce crew workload
- avoid issuing complex procedures when the crew is hand-flying in IMC
- protect separation if the aircraft momentarily left the cleared profile
- coordinate priority or delay if the crew needs time to stabilize

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in IMC cruise toward Florianópolis with the autopilot engaged. The autopilot disconnects with a warning. The nose pitches slightly. You take manual control, stabilize at flight level nine zero on heading, and cancel the warning.

You assess: unable to re-engage. Workload is manageable. You are still on clearance.

You advise Radar that autopilot is inoperative and you are continuing manually. Radar offers vectors for a straight-in ILS to reduce complexity. You accept.

No drama. Aircraft first. Then a concise report. Then a simpler plan if helpful.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, roger autopilot failure, maintain present altitude, fly heading one eight zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, understand hand-flying, radar vectors for ILS, reduce cockpit workload.
3. Control: ANAC123, confirm able to maintain altitude manually, traffic nearby.
4. Approach: ANAC123, if unable complex RNAV arrival, expect vectors.
5. Radar: ANAC123, roger continuing manually, report any further problems.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Autopilot failure is a stick-and-rudder moment before it is a radio moment.

Fly the aircraft. Cross-check attitude instruments in IMC. Trim. Breathe. Then decide what ATC needs to know.

If you stayed on altitude and track, a simple advisory may be enough: autopilot inoperative, continuing manually. If you lost altitude or need a simpler arrival, say so and ask for vectors.

Do not be embarrassed to request workload relief. Hand-flying an H130 in IMC into Guarulhos weather is honest work. ATC would rather give you vectors than watch you struggle through a complex RNAV STAR.

Part 3 answers should emphasize control first, communication second, and clearance amendment when needed — not a long systems lecture on frequency.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The autopilot disconnects unexpectedly in IMC during arrival. Describe your priorities and what you tell ATC.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

My first priority is to assume manual control and stabilize attitude, altitude, heading, and airspeed. I would acknowledge the disconnect warning and follow the AFM procedure. Once the aircraft is stable, I would advise ATC that the autopilot is inoperative and that I am continuing manually. If workload is high or I cannot safely fly a complex RNAV arrival, I would request radar vectors for a simpler approach. I would not make lengthy radio calls before the aircraft is under control.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Calling ATC immediately while the aircraft is still pitching or off altitude.  
  ✔ Aviate first; communicate after the flight path is stable.

- ❌ Saying nothing after a disconnect that caused an altitude deviation.  
  ✔ Advise ATC if you left the cleared altitude or need assistance.

- ❌ Insisting on a complex RNAV STAR while hand-flying in hard IMC.  
  ✔ Request vectors to reduce workload when appropriate.

- ❌ Attempting repeated re-engagement instead of flying the aircraft.  
  ✔ Stabilize manually first; troubleshoot only when safe.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are on an RNAV arrival to Curitiba in IMC when the autopilot fails and will not re-engage. You are stable on altitude after two hundred feet of deviation that you have corrected.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit to Approach?

**Your second task**

Approach offers heading vectors for the ILS. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, autopilot failure, continuing manually, corrected altitude, request vectors if available. Second task: ANAC123, heading as assigned, vectors for ILS — reading back the specific heading Approach gives. I would prioritize manual control first, then ask for a simpler clearance rather than forcing a complex RNAV arrival hand-flown in IMC.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Autopilot failure is handled in the seat, then on the radio.

Fly the helicopter. Stabilize. Then speak.

Tell ATC what changed: autopilot inoperative, any altitude issue, and whether you need vectors.

In H130 IFR training across Brazil, manual flying competence is the real backup system. Automation helps — it does not replace scan and control.

Aviate. Navigate. Communicate. Simplify the clearance if workload demands it.

That is how an autopilot disconnect stays a manageable event — not an incident.
