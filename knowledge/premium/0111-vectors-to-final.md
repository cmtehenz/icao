# Vectors to Final

**Catalog ID:** 0111  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Vectors to Final — the radar headings ATC issues to position an aircraft precisely onto the final approach course for landing.

Vectors to Final is the active phase: ATC is turning you, descending you, and slowing you onto the localizer, RNAV final, or visual final, ready to land.

In the H130, vectors to final are issued at every controlled airport in Brazil — Congonhas, Florianópolis, Curitiba, Joinville, Navegantes, and São Paulo area.

You must follow each vector heading precisely, confirm intercept, and transition to the approach without hesitation.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and radar approach sequencing.

## 📖 Meaning

Vectors to Final are radar headings and altitude assignments issued by ATC to position an aircraft onto the final approach course, enabling intercept of the localizer, RNAV final, or visual final track.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Vetores para o final.

Rumos e altitudes de radar fornecidos pelo controle para posicionar a aeronave no eixo final da aproximação e interceptar o localizador, RNAV ou visual.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When receiving Vectors to Final, the pilot should:

- follow every heading and altitude instruction precisely
- maintain assigned speed until further advised
- configure the aircraft progressively for approach
- monitor the final approach course for intercept
- confirm established and continue the approach

Vectors to Final commonly include:

- heading turns to final intercept angle
- descent to intercept altitude
- speed reductions for sequencing
- clearance for the approach on intercept

**Important:** Do not intercept the final course until ATC issues the turn. Following vectors precisely is a safety responsibility — unauthorized early turns can create conflicts with parallel or crossing traffic.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC issues Vectors to Final to:

- sequence multiple aircraft efficiently to the active runway
- optimize intercept angle and distance from touchdown
- control speed and spacing between arrivals
- maintain separation from other traffic on final
- provide the cleared approach without the full published procedure

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are descending into Congonhas in your H130 at three thousand feet.

Approach has you on a base leg and transmits:

ANAC123, turn left heading two two zero, descend and maintain two thousand, reduce speed one six zero knots.

You comply, then Approach turns you again:

ANAC123, turn left heading one seven zero, cleared ILS runway one seven left, report established.

You intercept and report:

Established ILS runway one seven left, ANAC123.

The sequence is clean. You are on final, configured, and stable.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading two two zero, vectors to final ILS runway one seven left.
2. Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain two thousand, speed one six zero.
3. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading one seven zero, intercept localizer.
4. Approach: ANAC123, cleared ILS runway one seven left, report established.
5. Approach: ANAC123, you are eight miles from touchdown, maintain speed until five miles.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Vectors to final is sequencing in real time.

ATC is painting a picture on radar. Your job is to follow the strokes.

The sequence:

Follow headings exactly. Descend on instruction. Manage speed. Monitor course. Intercept on the turn. Report established.

Brazilian pilots sometimes anticipate the final heading and turn early.

Do not turn until the vector is given. ATC sees traffic you cannot see.

In the H130, begin configuring for approach during base leg vectors — not on final.

Arrive at the intercept point with gear down, speed set, approach checklist complete.

If you intercept above glide slope or above localizer, advise ATC and request a longer final or go around.

Train each vector readback until it is automatic.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What do you do if you are vectored to final but intercept above the glide slope?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If I am vectored to final and intercept above the glide slope, I advise ATC that I am above glide slope and request a longer final or extended vectors to allow me to establish on the correct glide path. If I cannot establish stably, I would advise going around for another approach. A high intercept is a configuration and safety risk and should not be forced to a landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Turning toward the final course before receiving the vector instruction.  
  ✔ Wait for every heading instruction before turning.

- ❌ Not configuring the aircraft during base leg vectors.  
  ✔ Use base leg vectors to extend gear, configure flaps, set speed.

- ❌ Reporting established while still intercepting or oscillating.  
  ✔ Report established only when centered and stable on course.

- ❌ Accepting a high glide slope intercept without advising ATC.  
  ✔ Advise above glide slope and request resolution early.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Left Heading One Seven Zero, ANAC123

**Pronunciation:** left HED-ing WUN SEV-en ZEE-ro, AY-EN-AY-CEE WUN TOO THREE

**Word Stress**

- Heading → HED-ing
- One Seven Zero → each digit separate

Practice:

Left heading... one seven zero...

Together:

Left heading one seven zero, intercept localizer, ANAC123.

Say each digit clearly — one seven zero, not one seventy.
