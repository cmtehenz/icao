# Microburst

**Catalog ID:** 0089  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers microbursts — intense, localized downdrafts that can exceed helicopter performance.

A Microburst is a powerful column of descending air that spreads outward at the surface, producing extreme windshear.

In the H130, a microburst near the ground can force an uncontrollable descent even at maximum power.

You must avoid thunderstorms and virga, escape early if a microburst is suspected, and report immediately to ATC.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

A Microburst is a concentrated, severe downdraft that spreads outward upon reaching the surface, creating extreme windshear over a small area.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Microburst / microexplosão.

Corrente descendente intensa e concentrada que se espalha na superfície, criando cisalhamento extremo em área pequena.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a Microburst is suspected or encountered, the pilot should:

- avoid the area — never intentionally fly under a thunderstorm or virga
- apply maximum power and climb attitude if caught near the ground
- go around or divert immediately
- report microburst or severe windshear to ATC
- delay landing or takeoff until the threat has passed

Microburst reporting commonly includes:

- microburst on final or on departure
- severe windshear, strong downdraft
- going around due microburst
- request delay

**Important:** Microbursts can reverse headwind to tailwind in seconds. Avoidance is the only reliable strategy.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Microburst reports to:

- issue immediate warnings to all aircraft
- suspend or delay arrivals and departures if needed
- relay PIREPs with location and severity
- coordinate go-arounds and holding
- advise when conditions improve

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on approach to Congonhas in your H130 with a rain shaft visible near the airport.

On short final, you encounter a violent downdraft and sudden airspeed loss.

You apply maximum power, pitch for climb, and go around.

You transmit:

Congonhas Tower, ANAC123, going around, microburst on final, severe downdraft, airspeed loss.

Tower suspends approaches and warns all traffic.

You hold until the cell moves away.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger microburst, all aircraft advised, approaches suspended.
2. Tower: ANAC123, microburst alert, advise intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported microburst on final, say if continuing.
4. Tower: ANAC123, cleared go-around, climb and maintain two thousand.
5. Tower: ANAC123, say altitude and airspeed loss.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Congonhas Tower, ANAC123, going around, microburst on final.
2. ANAC123, microburst, severe downdraft, request delay.
3. ANAC123, unable to land due microburst, holding for improving conditions.
4. Tower, ANAC123, microburst on departure path, rejecting takeoff.
5. ANAC123, clear of microburst area, request another approach when available.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

A microburst is windshear at its worst.

Descending air. Outflow. Performance collapse.

The sequence:

Avoid. Escape. Report. Delay.

Never land under a rain shaft from a thunderstorm.

Never take off into a visible microburst signature.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "downdraft forte" or "rajada de tempestade" on frequency.

Use microburst or severe windshear — ICAO and FAA standard terms.

In the H130, you may not have the power to outclimb a mature microburst near the ground.

Train avoidance until rain shafts mean go around — not continue.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is a microburst and what would you tell ATC if you encountered one on final?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A microburst is a severe, localized downdraft that spreads at the surface and creates extreme windshear. If I encountered one on final, I would go around with maximum power, report going around due microburst or severe downdraft, state airspeed loss if known, and request delay until conditions improve.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing an approach under a thunderstorm rain shaft.  
  ✔ Go around or delay — microburst risk is extreme.

- ❌ Saying only "vento forte" without the hazard name.  
  ✔ Use microburst or severe windshear.

- ❌ Not warning ATC immediately after escape.  
  ✔ Report so other aircraft are protected.

- ❌ Attempting takeoff with a microburst alert active.  
  ✔ Delay until the threat has passed.
