# Roger

**Catalog ID:** 0128  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Roger — one of the most used and most misused words in aviation radio communication.

Roger means: I have received all of your last transmission.

That is its complete meaning. Nothing more.

Roger does not mean I will comply. It does not mean I agree. It does not mean the action is done. It simply confirms receipt of a message.

In the H130, operating into Congonhas, Curitiba, or São Paulo, understanding the precise meaning of Roger — and knowing when to use it versus Wilco, Affirm, or a full readback — is fundamental to ICAO Level 4 proficiency and to safe operations.

## 📖 Meaning

Roger is the ICAO-standard word used to indicate that a received message has been received in full. It does not indicate compliance, agreement, or action. It is a receipt confirmation only.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Recebido / Entendido (no sentido de "recebi").

Palavra ICAO padrão para indicar que a mensagem recebida foi recebida integralmente. Não indica conformidade, concordância ou ação. É apenas uma confirmação de recebimento.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When to use Roger:

- when ATC provides information that requires no specific action or readback
- when ATC acknowledges a pilot report or position report
- when ATC confirms a request has been noted
- when no compliance action is expected from the message

When NOT to use Roger alone:

- in place of a readback when a readback is required (altitude, runway, squawk)
- to confirm compliance with an instruction — use Wilco
- to confirm a yes/no question — use Affirm
- after a clearance requiring specific values to be confirmed

Roger operations commonly include:

- acknowledging traffic information (traffic in sight — Roger)
- confirming receipt of weather reports
- acknowledging ATC information not requiring a readback
- responding to an advisory or notification

**Important:** Roger is not a readback. Never use Roger alone in response to a runway assignment, altitude clearance, or taxi instruction. ICAO requires you to read back the specific values. Roger only confirms the words arrived — not that the words were understood correctly.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers transmit Roger when:

- acknowledging a pilot report received with no further action needed
- confirming a request has been noted and is being worked
- acknowledging position or status information from the pilot
- closing a routine exchange that requires no specific ATC action

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are operating over the coast inbound to Florianópolis in your H130.

Approach asks if you have the weather information:

ANAC123, do you have ATIS information bravo?

You reply:

Affirm, information bravo, ANAC123.

Approach responds:

Roger, ANAC123.

That Roger means: I received your confirmation. Nothing more.

Now Approach clears you for the approach:

ANAC123, cleared ILS runway two eight, descend to altitude two thousand.

You must read back — Roger alone is insufficient:

Cleared ILS runway two eight, descend altitude two thousand, ANAC123.

Approach confirms:

Roger, ANAC123 — that readback was correct.

Roger in Approach's response is acknowledging the readback was received.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger, continue approach, report outer marker.
2. Tower: ANAC123, roger your request, standby.
3. Approach: ANAC123, roger, traffic information, Airbus in your two o'clock, five miles.
4. Control: ANAC123, roger, position noted.
5. Ground: ANAC123, roger, taxi clearance to follow shortly.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Roger, wilco, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, roger, continuing approach.
3. Roger, ANAC123 — traffic in sight.
4. ANAC123, roger, standing by.
5. Roger, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Roger is the simplest word in aviation radio.

And the most misused.

Brazilian pilots often say "Roger, ANAC123" after every ATC transmission — including runway clearances, altitudes, and taxi instructions — because it feels like an acknowledgement.

It is an acknowledgement. But it is not a readback.

ATC does not know what you heard when you say Roger.

They know the message arrived. They do not know if you wrote down the right altitude. They do not know if you heard runway one seven left or one seven right.

Only a readback tells them that.

Roger and readback are different tools:

Roger — confirms the message arrived.

Readback — confirms the message arrived with the correct content.

Use Roger for information and advisory messages.

Use a readback for clearances, instructions, and safety-critical values.

Master this distinction. It is a key ICAO Level 4 differentiator.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What does Roger mean in ICAO radio communication? How is it different from Wilco?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Roger means I have received your last transmission in full. It confirms receipt only — not compliance and not understanding of specific values. Wilco means I have received the message and I will comply. If ATC instructs me to contact approach on a new frequency, I might say Wilco to indicate I will take the action. If ATC tells me traffic information that requires no compliance, I say Roger to confirm I received it.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using "Roger, ANAC123" in place of a readback after a runway or altitude clearance.  
  ✔ Read back the specific values: Cleared to land runway two eight, ANAC123.

- ❌ Confusing Roger with Affirm — using Roger to answer a yes/no question.  
  ✔ Use Affirm for yes and Negative for no. Roger is for general receipt confirmation.

- ❌ Using Roger to indicate compliance — saying "Roger" when you mean "I will do it."  
  ✔ Use Wilco to indicate compliance with an instruction.

- ❌ Adding filler words: "OK, Roger, understood, ANAC123."  
  ✔ Keep it clean: Roger, ANAC123. — or provide the required readback.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**ROGER = Receipt Only** — Roger confirms the radio message **R**eached you. It says nothing about what you will **O**r will not do. Never replace a **G**enuine readback with just **E**xtra Roger **R**esponses.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

São Paulo Approach transmits: "ANAC123, traffic information — Cessna in your ten o'clock, three miles, northbound, altitude two thousand five hundred."

**Your first task**

Is a readback required here? What is the correct response?

**Your second task**

Approach then says: "ANAC123, descend to altitude two thousand, QNH one zero one six." Is Roger sufficient? What do you transmit?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

For the traffic information, Roger is appropriate: Roger, ANAC123 — or Roger, traffic in sight if I see the aircraft. No readback of specific values is required for advisory information. For the descent clearance, Roger alone is not sufficient. I must read back: Descend altitude two thousand, QNH one zero one six, ANAC123. ICAO requires readback of altitude assignments and QNH settings. Roger does not replace that readback.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Roger is a precision tool.

It has one meaning: message received.

Use it precisely. Use it confidently. Use it where it belongs.

When a readback is required, give the readback.

When compliance is expected, say Wilco.

When a yes/no answer is needed, say Affirm or Negative.

Roger is not a universal filler.

It is a specific confirmation.

Use it as such.
