# Assertiveness

**Catalog ID:** 0487  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Assertiveness — the CRM skill of stating concerns, limits, and required actions clearly and respectfully so that safety-critical information is heard and acted on without aggression or silence.

Assertiveness sits between passivity and aggression. The passive pilot stays quiet. The aggressive pilot attacks. The assertive pilot speaks early, factually, and firmly.

For H130 operations, assertiveness saves lives in confined areas, on short final, and when a clearance is unsafe. Part 1 speaking and Part 2 radiotelephony both demand it.

Understanding assertiveness in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 and Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Assertiveness is the ability to communicate safety concerns, operational limits, and required actions in a clear, respectful, and timely manner, ensuring that critical information is acknowledged and addressed without delaying intervention or escalating into conflict.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Assertividade / Assertiveness.

Capacidade de comunicar preocupações de segurança, limites operacionais e ações necessárias de forma clara, respeitosa e oportuna, garantindo que a informação crítica seja reconhecida e tratada sem atrasar a intervenção nem escalar para conflito.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When assertiveness is required, the crew should:

- speak up early when something feels wrong
- use clear, factual language — not hints
- escalate if the first challenge is ignored
- for H130: challenge unstable approaches, wire risks, and fuel states firmly
- on frequency: say unable when a clearance is unsafe
- remain respectful — assertiveness is not shouting

Assertive exchanges commonly include:

- I am concerned about...
- unable to comply as cleared
- go around / discontinue approach
- request alternative clearance
- we need to...

**Important:** If the aircraft is in danger, assertiveness becomes a command: go around, stop, hold position. Politeness never outranks safety.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers rely on assertive pilot communication to:

- receive early “unable” calls before a conflict develops
- re-clear aircraft that cannot accept a heading, altitude, or speed
- understand real operational limits of helicopters in terminal airspace
- avoid assumptions that silence means acceptance
- maintain safety when pilots challenge an unsafe clearance

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on short final to Congonhas in the H130. The approach is becoming unstable — high and fast. Your colleague says nothing.

You do not hint. You state: go around, we are unstable.

Tower is advised. You climb away, re-enter the pattern, and fly a stable second approach.

Later Approach offers a tight base that would put you below a safe profile for the sector. You reply unable, request extended downwind. Assertiveness on the flight deck and on the radio is the same skill.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, turn base now, reduce speed.
2. Tower: ANAC123, continue approach, traffic clearing.
3. Approach: ANAC123, descend two thousand, expedite.
4. Tower: ANAC123, go around, acknowledge.
5. Control: ANAC123, advise if unable to accept short approach.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, unable turn base now, request extended downwind.
2. ANAC123, going around, unstable approach.
3. ANAC123, unable expedite descent, request alternative.
4. ANAC123, going around, ANAC123.
5. ANAC123, unable short approach, request standard pattern.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Assertiveness is timed truth.

Hints are not CRM. “Are you happy with this?” is not a challenge. “We are unstable — go around” is a challenge.

In Brazilian training culture, some pilots fear sounding rude on frequency. Unable is not rude. Unable is professional. ATC would rather re-clear you than watch you accept an unsafe instruction.

In the H130, the window to speak is short — wires, decks, and confined pads do not wait for soft language. Use the graded assertiveness model: enquire, challenge, take control. End on action when needed.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You believe an ATC clearance is unsafe for your H130. How do you communicate assertively?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would state unable clearly and immediately, then give a short reason and a workable alternative. For example, I would say I am unable to accept the short approach because it would make the profile unstable, and I would request an extended downwind. Assertiveness means early, factual, and respectful communication — not silence and not aggression.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Staying silent to avoid conflict with the captain or ATC.  
  ✔ Speak early with facts; silence is a CRM failure.

- ❌ Using soft hints instead of a clear challenge.  
  ✔ Say the problem and the required action directly.

- ❌ Accepting an unsafe clearance and complaining after landing.  
  ✔ Say unable on frequency while there is still time.

- ❌ Confusing assertiveness with aggression or blame.  
  ✔ Stay calm, factual, and focused on the aircraft.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Approach clears you for a short approach to runway one seven Left at Congonhas. Your energy state is high and the turn would be tight for a stable H130 profile.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

Your colleague wants to continue. The approach is clearly unstable. What do you say on the flight deck, then to Tower if a go-around is required?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, unable short approach, request extended downwind. Second task: On the flight deck — we are unstable, go around. To Tower: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. Assertiveness means the unsafe path is stopped with clear words, not delayed with hints.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Assertiveness keeps the aircraft inside the safe envelope.

Speak early. Speak clearly. Offer an alternative when you can.

On frequency, unable is a professional word. In the cockpit, go around is a professional command.

In the H130, the assertive call often arrives with seconds to spare. Do not spend those seconds being polite about danger.

State the risk. Protect the flight. That is CRM.
