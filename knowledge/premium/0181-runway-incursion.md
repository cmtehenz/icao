# Runway Incursion

**Catalog ID:** 0181  
**Category:** Airport Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Runway Incursion — any occurrence at an aerodrome involving the incorrect presence of an aircraft, vehicle, or person on the protected area of a surface designated for the landing and takeoff of aircraft.

Runway Incursion is the deadliest category of airport surface event. It has caused some of the worst aviation disasters in history, including the Tenerife collision of 1977, which killed 583 people — to this day the deadliest accident in aviation history.

In Brazil, runway incursion prevention is a standing safety priority for ANAC and DECEA. Brazilian airports including Congonhas, Curitiba, and Florianópolis have implemented surface movement monitoring systems precisely because of this risk.

This lesson is Critical. Not High. Critical.

Every pilot at every airport must understand: a runway is a protected surface. You enter it only when explicitly authorized by ATC. You confirm that authorization. You verify it is correct. You cross only when you are certain.

The cost of a misheard clearance, an incorrect readback, or a wrong taxiway is measured in lives.

## 📖 Meaning

A Runway Incursion is any occurrence at an aerodrome involving the unauthorized or incorrect presence of an aircraft, vehicle, or person on the protected area of the runway, including the runway itself and its associated surfaces and safety areas, creating a collision hazard with landing or departing aircraft.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

To prevent runway incursion, the crew must:

- hold short of every runway holding position unless explicitly cleared to enter
- read back all runway crossing and runway entry clearances completely and accurately
- positively confirm ATC acknowledgement of your readback before entering the runway
- verify your position on the airport surface using charts, signage, and markings
- confirm runway designation verbally: runway one four, runway three two
- stop and query ATC if any doubt exists about authorization, position, or clearance

Prevention behaviors include:

- complete sterile cockpit discipline during taxi operations
- explicit challenge-and-response verification between pilots before runway entry
- head-up scanning of the runway environment before entry — both directions
- immediate stop if landing traffic is sighted on final during taxi to runway

**Important:** NEVER enter a runway based on an implied, partial, or unclear clearance. If you did not hear clear and complete runway entry authorization — hold short. Call ATC. Clarify. The aircraft that is already on the runway will survive. The runway incursion might not.

## 🎯 Why Prevention Matters

Runway incursion prevention is prioritized because:

- collision speed on a runway can exceed three hundred knots combined closure rate
- visual detection of conflicting traffic is unreliable in poor visibility, night, or complex aerodrome layouts
- a clearance misunderstanding takes less than two seconds to create a fatal situation
- most runway incursions involve a correctly-instructed aircraft and one that missed or misread clearance
- even a Category A runway incursion — the most serious — begins with a communication failure

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are taxiing your H130 at Congonhas after landing, following taxi instructions to the ramp.

Ground Control clears you: ANAC123, taxi to the ramp via taxiway Golf, hold short of runway one seven.

You read back: Taxi to ramp via Golf, hold short of runway one seven, ANAC123.

Approaching the holding position, you see runway one seven directly ahead.

Your co-pilot says: "I think I heard a crossing clearance earlier."

You stop. You have no current crossing clearance on record.

You call Ground:

ANAC123, holding short runway one seven, confirm, request runway crossing.

Ground: ANAC123, hold short runway one seven, traffic on short final.

Your co-pilot was mistaken. A Fokker 100 lands thirty seconds later across your path.

The stop saved both aircraft.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Runway incursion is a communication failure that becomes a collision.

The chain always starts the same way. A partial readback. A missed ATC instruction. A crew member assuming what they did not confirm. A controller assuming a readback that was not given.

Each link in that chain can be broken.

The most powerful tool you have is a complete, verbatim readback of every runway-related clearance — including the runway designation.

Not "crossing runway, ANAC123."

Crossing runway one seven, ANAC123. Clear of runway one seven, ANAC123.

Runway number. Every time. Without exception.

At night. In fog. At unfamiliar airports. At your home base where you think you know every taxiway. Especially there.

The other tool is the stop.

If doubt exists — stop. The runway will still be there after you clarify. The aircraft that has not seen you will not stop in time.

Professional pilots develop an instinct: runway ahead means confirm authorization. Not just once. Every single time.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe how you prevent runway incursion during taxi operations. Include specific phraseology behaviors.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Runway incursion prevention starts before engine start. I review the airport layout, identify hold-short points, and brief my co-pilot on the active runway and critical crossing points. During taxi, I maintain sterile cockpit discipline. I read back every runway-related clearance including the runway designation and require ATC acknowledgement before entering. Before entering any runway I verify: I have an explicit current clearance; my co-pilot confirms it; I scan the runway visually in both directions; and I verbally confirm the runway number. If any doubt exists, I stop at the hold-short line and query ATC: ANAC123, holding short runway one seven, confirm crossing clearance. I never enter a runway based on assumption.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reading back runway crossing clearances without stating the runway designation.  
  ✔ Always include the runway number: crossing runway one seven, ANAC123.

- ❌ Entering a runway because you thought you heard a clearance.  
  ✔ If uncertain, hold short and confirm: ANAC123, holding short, request clarification.

- ❌ Not reporting clear of the runway after crossing.  
  ✔ Always report: ANAC123, clear of runway one seven. ATC needs this to release the runway.

- ❌ Assuming your clearance is still valid after a long hold at the holding point.  
  ✔ Clearances can expire during holds. Confirm current authorization before entering.

- ❌ Allowing cockpit conversations during taxi to distract from ATC communications.  
  ✔ Sterile cockpit during all taxi operations. No non-essential conversation near runways.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are taxiing at Congonhas after landing. Ground clears you: "ANAC123, taxi to ramp via Golf, hold short runway one seven." You read back and proceed.

At the holding point, your co-pilot says: "I think Tower cleared us to cross earlier on the frequency."

**Your first task**

What do you do and what do you say?

**Your second task**

Ground responds: "ANAC123, negative, hold short runway one seven, departing traffic." You see an aircraft beginning its takeoff roll on runway one seven. What does this confirm, and what is the lesson?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: I would stop at the hold-short line immediately and call Ground: ANAC123, holding short runway one seven, request confirmation of crossing clearance — we believe we may have received a crossing clearance but require confirmation. I would not enter the runway based on the co-pilot's recollection. Second task: Ground's instruction to hold confirms there was no current crossing clearance. The departing aircraft confirms that entering the runway would have been a runway incursion. The lesson is absolute: never enter a runway based on a believed or partially-heard clearance. Stop, confirm, verify. The extra thirty seconds always costs less than the alternative.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

There is no lesson in this entire program more important than this one.

Runway incursion kills. It has killed hundreds. It will kill again somewhere, someday, unless every pilot holds to one absolute rule:

You do not enter a runway without explicit, confirmed, current authorization.

Not because you think you heard it. Not because you have crossed this runway a hundred times before. Not because the other pilot remembers a clearance you are not sure about.

Only when you have it. Only when you read it back. Only when ATC confirmed your readback. Only after you scanned the runway clear.

Four steps. Every time. Every runway. Every airport. Every flight.

There is no shortcut that is worth the risk.

Hold short until you are certain. Then cross. Then report clear.

That discipline is not a checklist. It is the difference between arriving and not arriving.
