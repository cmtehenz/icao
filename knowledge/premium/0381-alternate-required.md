# Alternate Required

**Catalog ID:** 0381  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Alternate Required — the operational and regulatory condition in which a destination alternate aerodrome must be filed and available because weather, fuel, or operational factors make a diversion possible.

Alternate Required is not paperwork. It is a decision that shapes fuel planning, route selection, and how you speak to ATC when destination weather deteriorates.

For H130 helicopter operations in Brazil, alternates matter on IFR flights, offshore legs, and any mission where the destination may go below minima. Congonhas, Santos Dumont, and coastal fields can change quickly with fog, rain, or low cloud.

Understanding how to discuss an alternate requirement in ICAO-standard English — filing, advising ATC, and requesting diversion — is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Alternate Required means that, under applicable regulations and the flight's weather and fuel conditions, the crew must designate at least one suitable alternate aerodrome and plan fuel and routing so that a diversion can be executed safely if the destination cannot be used.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Alternado obrigatório / Alternate Required.

Condição operacional e regulamentar em que a tripulação deve designar pelo menos um aeródromo alternado adequado e planejar combustível e rota para que um desvio possa ser executado com segurança se o destino não puder ser utilizado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an alternate is required, the crew should:

- check destination and alternate weather against minima before departure and en route
- file the alternate in the flight plan and brief the diversion route and fuel
- monitor destination TAF and METAR for deterioration toward minima
- advise ATC early if diversion becomes likely
- request clearance to the alternate with intentions clear and concise

Alternate Required planning includes:

- weather below or near destination minima
- single runway or limited approach capability at destination
- fuel policy that assumes a missed approach plus diversion
- NOTAMs that close or restrict the destination
- offshore or remote destinations with limited recovery options

**Important:** Filing an alternate does not replace continuous weather monitoring. If destination weather falls below minima, declare your intention to divert early. Do not wait until fuel is critical.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with alternate requirements to:

- anticipate diversion traffic when destination weather deteriorates
- sequence aircraft toward alternate aerodromes with adequate capacity
- coordinate between approach, radar, and destination towers
- protect fuel-critical flights that must divert without delay
- manage flow when multiple aircraft divert from the same field

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR from Curitiba to Florianópolis in the H130. Destination TAF shows temporary fog near minima. You filed Navegantes as alternate with diversion fuel.

Approaching the terminal area, Florianópolis ATIS reports visibility below your approach minima.

You call:

ANAC123, destination below minima, request diversion to Navegantes, alternate as filed.

Radar: ANAC123, roger diversion, proceed direct Navegantes, climb and maintain flight level six zero.

You respond:

ANAC123, diverting Navegantes, climbing flight level six zero.

Planned. Briefed. Executed before fuel becomes a problem.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, destination weather deteriorating, say intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, cleared to alternate Navegantes as filed, climb flight level six zero.
3. Approach: ANAC123, divert approved, contact Navegantes Approach one two four decimal seven.
4. Radar: ANAC123, confirm alternate fuel remaining.
5. Control: ANAC123, expect vectors to Navegantes, traffic diverting same field.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

An alternate is insurance you must know how to use.

Filing it is step one. Briefing the diversion route, fuel burn, and approach at the alternate is step two. Speaking clearly to ATC when you need it is step three.

Brazilian weather — coastal fog, afternoon convection, mountain cloud — can close a destination faster than you expect. If the TAF says TEMPO near minima, treat the alternate as live, not theoretical.

In the H130, range and fuel margins are tighter than in a jet. That makes early diversion communication more important, not less. Tell ATC as soon as the decision is made.

Never say you "might divert" when you have already decided. Say: diverting to [alternate], request clearance. Clear language gets clear priority.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Destination weather has gone below your approach minima. You have an alternate filed with adequate fuel. Describe what you say to ATC.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would state the situation and the intention clearly: ANAC123, destination below minima, request diversion to Navegantes, alternate as filed. I would confirm fuel remaining if asked and accept the clearance to the alternate without delay. I would not continue toward an unusable destination hoping for improvement unless I have fuel and a clear plan. Early communication protects both the flight and ATC sequencing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing to the destination hoping weather improves without advising ATC.  
  ✔ Divert early when below minima: state destination status and request alternate clearance.

- ❌ Saying only "going to alternate" without naming the field.  
  ✔ Name the alternate: diverting to Navegantes as filed.

- ❌ Waiting until minimum fuel to request diversion.  
  ✔ Divert while you still have planned alternate fuel margin.

- ❌ Not monitoring destination weather after filing the alternate.  
  ✔ Update METAR/TAF continuously and brief the diversion before you need it.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are inbound to Florianópolis. ATIS reports visibility below your minima. Navegantes is filed as alternate with diversion fuel remaining.

**Your first task**

What do you say to Approach on first contact after hearing the ATIS?

**Your second task**

Approach replies: "ANAC123, standby for diversion, expect further in two minutes." Fuel is adequate but you are inside thirty miles. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, destination below minima, request diversion to Navegantes as filed. Second task: ANAC123, standing by, able to hold briefly, fuel remaining one hour fifteen minutes — alternate fuel available. I would not press into an unusable approach. If delay continues and fuel margin shrinks, I would upgrade urgency: ANAC123, require diversion clearance now. Clear, early communication keeps the diversion controlled.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Alternate Required means you planned for the day the destination fails.

File it. Brief it. Monitor weather. Divert early.

When you speak to ATC, name the alternate, state the reason, and request clearance — not a vague intention.

In Brazilian IFR and coastal operations, weather can close a field in minutes. Your H130 fuel margin will not forgive late decisions.

Know when an alternate is required. Know when to use it. Say it clearly.

That is how diversion stays a procedure — not an emergency.
