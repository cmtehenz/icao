# Visual Meteorological Conditions

**Catalog ID:** 0183  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Visual Meteorological Conditions — VMC — the weather conditions in which pilots can see and avoid other aircraft and terrain by looking outside the cockpit, without relying on instruments for separation and navigation.

VMC is not simply "good weather." It is a legally defined set of minimums for visibility, cloud clearance, and distance from cloud that must be met for Visual Flight Rules operations to be authorized.

Understanding VMC — and its clear distinction from IMC — is fundamental to safe flying in Brazil and essential for ICAO Level 4 proficiency.

Florianópolis, Navegantes, and the Santa Catarina coast can transition between VMC and IMC rapidly due to sea fog and low coastal cloud. Pilots who cannot precisely describe VMC, recognize when conditions fall below it, and communicate that change to ATC are a danger to themselves and others.

This lesson pairs directly with Lesson 0184 — Instrument Meteorological Conditions — to give you the complete picture of weather minima and the language to describe them.

## 📖 Meaning

Visual Meteorological Conditions are meteorological conditions expressed in terms of visibility, distance from cloud, and ceiling equal to or better than specified minima, permitting flight under Visual Flight Rules where the pilot maintains visual separation from terrain and other traffic by direct outside observation.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Condições meteorológicas visuais (CMV).

Condições meteorológicas expressas em termos de visibilidade, distância de nuvens e teto iguais ou melhores que os mínimos especificados, permitindo o voo sob as regras de voo visual (VFR), onde o piloto mantém separação visual do terreno e do tráfego por observação direta.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When operating in VMC, the crew must:

- confirm visibility, cloud base, and cloud clearance meet applicable VFR minima for the airspace class
- maintain visual separation from terrain, obstacles, and other traffic
- avoid entering cloud or reducing visual reference below VMC minima
- advise ATC if conditions are deteriorating toward IMC
- request IFR clearance if conditions fall below VMC during VFR flight
- report leaving VMC: ANAC123, IMC encountered, request IFR clearance

VMC minima vary by airspace class in Brazil:

- Class C airspace: visibility 8 km, 1500 m horizontal cloud clearance, 300 m (1000 ft) vertical cloud clearance
- Class D airspace: same as Class C
- Class G airspace below 900 m AGL: visibility 1500 m, clear of cloud, in sight of surface

**Important:** VMC does not guarantee safety from collision. See-and-avoid depends on both pilots seeing each other. In VMC, you are still responsible for your own separation. The presence of VMC conditions does not remove the risk of collision — it only means the tools for visual separation are theoretically available.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers reference VMC/IMC distinctions to:

- determine which separation standards apply to the flight
- authorize or restrict VFR operations in controlled airspace
- facilitate VFR flight following or advisory services
- respond to pilot reports of deteriorating visibility
- coordinate transition from VFR to IFR when conditions change

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are operating VFR in your H130 from Navegantes toward Florianópolis, flying along the coast at one thousand five hundred feet.

The weather is beautiful: visibility thirty kilometers, no cloud below five thousand feet, blue sky.

Classic VMC. You can see Florianópolis from forty miles away.

You check in with Approach:

ANAC123, H130, inbound Florianópolis, VFR, one thousand five hundred feet, VMC, request advisory service.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, radar contact, VFR advisory service available, report abeam Biguaçu.

As you approach Florianópolis, you notice a band of low coastal cloud developing ahead. The cloud base looks around eight hundred feet. Horizontal visibility is reducing.

You assess: conditions are marginal. The gap between VMC and IMC is narrowing.

You call Approach:

ANAC123, coastal cloud developing, visibility reducing, request IFR clearance to Florianópolis.

Approach: ANAC123, roger, cleared IFR, descend and maintain one thousand, squawk zero four five five, advise airport in sight.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, VMC reported, VFR flight following approved, maintain own separation.
2. Approach: ANAC123, advise if you encounter IMC.
3. Tower: ANAC123, visual approach approved, runway one four, report final.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm VMC maintained or request IFR clearance.
5. Radar: ANAC123, other traffic VFR at two thousand, report traffic in sight.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, VMC, VFR flight following approved, own separation, wilco.
2. ANAC123, will advise if IMC encountered.
3. Visual approach approved, runway one four, wilco, ANAC123.
4. ANAC123, VMC maintained, continuing VFR.
5. ANAC123, traffic in sight.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

VMC is your permission to look out the window and manage your own separation.

It sounds simple. It is not.

VMC requires visibility. It requires distance from cloud. It requires a clear and reliable visual picture of your surroundings.

The trap pilots fall into is optimistic assessment. "It looks okay." "The cloud is thin." "I can see the ground through breaks."

VMC is not a feeling. It is a measurement. If the visibility is seven kilometers and the minimum is eight — you are below VMC. No matter how it feels.

In H130 operations along the Brazilian coast — particularly Santa Catarina and São Paulo coast — sea fog and coastal cloud can develop and thicken in minutes. A departure in clear VMC may require an IFR return thirty minutes later.

Know your escape route. If VMC begins to deteriorate — request IFR clearance before you are in cloud. Declaring IFR before entering IMC is professional. Declaring it when you are already in cloud is an emergency.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe Visual Meteorological Conditions and explain the difference between VMC and VFR.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Visual Meteorological Conditions are the weather conditions that must exist for Visual Flight Rules operations to be permitted. VMC is defined by specific minima for visibility, distance from cloud, and ceiling, which vary by airspace class. VFR is the set of rules that governs flight when VMC exists — the pilot uses visual reference to navigate and maintain separation. VMC is the weather standard; VFR is the operational rule. A pilot can be flying VFR while conditions are deteriorating toward the VMC minimum — and must request IFR clearance before conditions fall below VMC to avoid entering IMC while on a VFR clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Confusing VMC with "good weather" without reference to specific minima.  
  ✔ VMC is a defined standard: visibility, cloud clearance, ceiling. Know the numbers for each airspace class.

- ❌ Continuing VFR after conditions drop below VMC without requesting IFR clearance.  
  ✔ If conditions fall below VMC: ANAC123, IMC encountered, request IFR clearance.

- ❌ Assuming VFR flight following provides separation service equivalent to IFR.  
  ✔ VFR flight following is advisory. Separation is the pilot's responsibility in VMC.

- ❌ Not advising ATC of deteriorating visual conditions.  
  ✔ Report early: ANAC123, visibility reducing, conditions approaching IMC.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, VMC Maintained, Continuing VFR

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, VEE-em-SEE men-TAYND, kun-TIN-yoo-ing VEE-ef-AR

**Word Stress**

- VMC → VEE-em-SEE (three letters, spoken individually)
- Maintained → men-TAYND
- Continuing → kun-TIN-yoo-ing (four syllables)
- VFR → VEE-ef-AR (three letters, spoken individually)

Practice:

VMC maintained... continuing VFR.

Together:

ANAC123, VMC maintained, continuing VFR, request advisory service to Florianópolis.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**VMC** — **V**isibility meets the minimum, **M**easure cloud clearance and ceiling, **C**ontinue VFR only if all three are met — or request IFR immediately.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are flying VFR in your H130 from Navegantes to Florianópolis along the coast at one thousand five hundred feet. Visibility is thirty kilometers. You are fifteen minutes from Florianópolis when you encounter a solid bank of coastal fog. Visibility drops to two kilometers. Cloud base is six hundred feet.

**Your first task**

Are you still in VMC? What do you do?

**Your second task**

You request IFR clearance. Approach assigns you squawk zero four five five and instructs you to climb and maintain three thousand feet. You are at one thousand five hundred feet, in cloud, with low cloud below you. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: No, I am below VMC. Class C airspace requires eight kilometers visibility and three hundred meters vertical cloud clearance. I am well below both. I would immediately request IFR clearance: ANAC123, IMC encountered, request immediate IFR clearance, one thousand five hundred feet. Second task: ANAC123, IFR clearance accepted, climbing to three thousand, squawk zero four five five, ANAC123. I would climb immediately on instruments, establishing a positive instrument scan. I would not attempt to maintain visual reference with the surface while transitioning to IFR.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

VMC is your anchor to visual flight. When it holds — you fly by sight, confirm traffic by eye, and navigate by landmarks.

When it breaks — transition. Do not hesitate.

The worst position in aviation is halfway between VFR and IFR. Conditions below VMC, no IFR clearance, visual reference unreliable, instruments available but not active.

Make the call early. Request IFR before you are in cloud.

Approach does not penalize pilots for requesting IFR in deteriorating conditions. They respect it.

Know the standard. Monitor the conditions. Decide early. Communicate clearly.

VMC maintained: continue. VMC lost: request IFR. Two conditions. Two responses. No ambiguity.
