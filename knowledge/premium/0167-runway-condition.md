# Runway Condition

**Catalog ID:** 0167  
**Category:** Airport Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Runway Condition — the formal assessment of the physical state of the runway surface, communicated to pilots through ATC, ATIS, and NOTAMs.

Runway Condition is broader than Braking Action. It describes what is on the runway, not only how the braking feels.

ICAO introduced the Global Reporting Format (GRF) to standardize runway condition reporting worldwide. Under GRF, runway condition is assessed per third of the runway and classified using a Runway Condition Code (RWYCC) from 6 (dry) to 0 (Nil braking).

Brazilian pilots operating into Curitiba, Florianópolis, Navegantes, and São Paulo airports must be able to communicate clearly about runway condition — both to understand ATC advisories and to report conditions after landing.

## 📖 Meaning

Runway Condition is the assessed state of the runway surface, including the type of contamination present (if any), its depth, coverage, and the resulting effect on aircraft performance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Condição de pista.

Estado avaliado da superfície da pista, incluindo o tipo de contaminação presente (se houver), sua profundidade, cobertura e o efeito resultante no desempenho da aeronave.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When runway condition information is provided, the pilot should:

- note the condition for each third of the runway (touchdown zone, mid-runway, rollout zone)
- identify the type of contamination: wet, slush, snow, ice, standing water
- apply the appropriate performance correction factors
- verify the condition matches the ATIS or most recent report
- report any discrepancy to ATC after landing

Runway Condition Codes (RWYCC):

- **6** — Dry
- **5** — Frost or wet (less than 3mm depth)
- **4** — Wet (greater than 3mm depth) or compacted snow
- **3** — Slippery wet (damp pavement with less braking than wet)
- **2** — Slush or dry snow
- **1** — Ice or standing water
- **0** — Nil braking / wet ice

**Important:** A runway can have different condition codes along its length. The third with the worst condition determines the minimum acceptable performance margin.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers report runway condition to:

- ensure pilots have accurate surface information before landing or takeoff
- comply with mandatory ICAO GRF reporting requirements
- trigger performance recalculation by the flight crew
- coordinate runway closures when condition codes reach unsafe levels
- support ATIS updates with current runway state information

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are preparing to depart Curitiba in your H130 after an overnight frost.

The ATIS reports:

Runway one five, runway condition code five, frost on runway one five.

You check your H130 performance supplement.

For a RWYCC of 5, your accelerate-stop distance requires an additional 10% correction.

You calculate: distance is within limits.

You call Ground:

ANAC123, ready for departure runway one five, copied runway condition code five.

Ground confirms:

ANAC123, runway condition code five, frost on runway one five, expect normal braking.

You accept and depart.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Runway condition is the ground truth before you touch down.

Braking Action tells you how the previous aircraft felt.

Runway Condition tells you what is physically on the surface and where.

Both matter. Use both.

In the H130, you operate at light weight and relatively low speeds — but performance margins can still be tight at short runways like Joinville or Navegantes.

Know the RWYCC scale: 6 down to 0.

Six is ideal. Zero is dangerous.

Always verify the condition code against your aircraft manual before accepting the runway.

If the code requires a performance correction that puts you beyond limits — do not go.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What information does a Runway Condition Code provide to the pilot?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A Runway Condition Code, or RWYCC, provides the pilot with a standardized number from zero to six indicating the braking performance available on the runway. Code six means the runway is dry and braking is normal. Code zero means braking is nil and the runway is essentially unusable. The code is assessed separately for each third of the runway — the touchdown zone, the mid-runway, and the rollout zone — and includes information about the type of contamination such as frost, wet, slush, or ice. The pilot uses the code to calculate performance corrections before landing or takeoff.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Ignoring runway condition codes in the ATIS because the airport looks fine from the air.  
  ✔ Always apply the condition code correction even if the runway appears normal on approach.

- ❌ Confusing Runway Condition Code with Braking Action report.  
  ✔ RWYCC is an objective surface assessment. Braking Action is a pilot's subjective report after landing.

- ❌ Applying the correction only to the worst-condition third and ignoring the others.  
  ✔ Apply the most conservative correction that covers the full landing roll.

- ❌ Not reporting a discrepancy after landing.  
  ✔ If the actual condition differs from the reported code, advise ATC after clearing the runway.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Florianópolis ATIS reports: Runway condition code two, slush, zero to three millimeters, runway two eight.

Your H130 performance supplement indicates that RWYCC 2 requires a 25% increase in landing distance. The available landing distance on runway two eight is 1,400 meters. Your normal landing distance is 1,150 meters.

**Your first task**

Calculate whether your corrected landing distance fits within the available runway.

**Your second task**

If it does not fit, what do you tell ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Corrected landing distance: 1,150 multiplied by 1.25 equals 1,437 meters. That exceeds the available 1,400 meters. I would advise ATC: ANAC123, runway condition code two on runway two eight exceeds performance limits, unable to accept, request go-around and diversion to alternate. I would then proceed to an alternate with a dry or less contaminated runway.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Runway Condition is objective information.

It tells you what is on the surface, how much of it, and what code that assigns to your braking performance.

Numbers do not lie.

Use the code. Check the manual. Calculate the distance.

If the numbers say no — the runway says no.

Communicate clearly. Divert if necessary. That is professional decision-making.
