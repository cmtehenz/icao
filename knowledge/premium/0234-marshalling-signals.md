# Marshalling Signals

**Catalog ID:** 0234  
**Category:** Ground Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Marshalling Signals — standardized hand and wand signals used by ground personnel to direct aircraft on the apron during arrival to stand, parking, engine start, and departure push or taxi, ensuring safe movement relative to equipment, buildings, and other aircraft.

Marshalling is a visual language. In noise, on crowded aprons, and when radio is secondary to the marshaller in front of you, those signals keep the aircraft aligned with the stand and clear of obstacles. Misreading a signal can mean jet blast — or rotor wash — into equipment, or a collision with a blast fence.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, marshalling is especially important: hover-taxi to the spot, rotor clearance, and shutdown signals differ in feel from fixed-wing parking, but ICAO-standard signals remain the common framework. Academy crews must recognize standard signals and know when to stop if the signal is unclear.

This lesson builds Part 2 proficiency in discussing marshalling, coordinating with Ground, and operating safely under marshaller guidance.

## 📖 Meaning

Marshalling Signals are the standardized visual signals given by a marshaller, using hands or illuminated wands, to guide an aircraft during ground movement on the apron, including directions to proceed, turn, slow, stop, shut down engines, and other parking and departure-related instructions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When under marshalling guidance, the crew should:

- acquire the marshaller and confirm you are the aircraft being marshalled
- follow standard signals for proceed, turn, slow, and stop
- stop immediately if the signal is unclear or if you lose sight of the marshaller
- maintain responsibility for rotor and obstacle clearance even while following signals
- coordinate with ATC or apron frequency as required before moving under marshalling
- for helicopters, interpret hover and shutdown-related signals with rotor awareness

Marshalling commonly covers:

- guidance into the parking stand or helipad spot
- turns to align with the centerline or T-marking
- slow-down and stop at the correct nose position
- engine or rotor shutdown signals when applicable
- chocks in / all clear type coordination with ground crew after stop

**Important:** The marshaller assists; the pilot-in-command remains responsible for the safe operation of the aircraft. If a signal would drive you into an obstacle or exceed rotor clearance, stop. In the H130, never assume a fixed-wing-style taxi signal accounts for blade tip radius — if in doubt, stop and clarify.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Apron control and ATC related ground operations rely on marshalling to:

- position aircraft precisely on stands and spots
- prevent apron collisions with equipment and other aircraft
- standardize visual communication across languages
- support night and low-visibility apron movement with lighted wands
- coordinate start and departure sequences with ground crews

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You air-taxi to the training apron at Congonhas in the H130. Ground clears you to the stand. A marshaller waits with wands.

You follow the proceed and turn signals, slow on the slow-down signal, and stop cleanly on the stop signal over the spot. You do not continue past the stop because you "know the parking." The marshaller owns the final meters of alignment; you own the decision to refuse an unsafe signal.

After landing the skids on the spot, you wait for the signal before reducing to idle shutdown as briefed. Rotor wash is controlled. Equipment stays clear.

That is marshalling done professionally: standard signals, clear stop, PIC override if something is wrong.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, taxi to stand two four, marshaller ahead.
2. Apron: ANAC123, follow marshalling signals to the helipad spot.
3. Ground: ANAC123, hold position, marshaller not in position, standby.
4. Ground: ANAC123, after marshalling complete, contact Ground on one two one decimal niner.
5. Apron: ANAC123, marshaller reports parking complete, shutdown at your discretion.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Marshalling is precision parking with a human in the loop.

Learn the standard signals well enough that a stop is unmistakable and a turn is not confused with proceed. At night, wands help — but only if you are looking outside.

You may refuse a signal that puts the rotor disk into a blast fence or another aircraft. Stop. Make it safe. Then continue when the path is clear.

In the H130 academy, briefing who watches the marshaller matters. One set of eyes on the wands, one on instruments and clearances as needed. Do not both stare inside while the marshaller is working.

Radio clearances get you to the apron. Marshalling finishes the last meters. Both matter.

Night marshalling with wands is clearer than bare hands — still, do not fixate so hard on the lights that you lose the tail rotor and nearby equipment in your scan. Outside scan first, signals second, ego never.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe your responsibilities when a marshaller is guiding you onto a stand.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would follow standardized marshalling signals for direction, speed, and stop while keeping responsibility for obstacle and rotor clearance. I would stop immediately if I did not understand a signal or if following it would be unsafe. After stopping on the spot, I would complete parking checks and shut down according to the marshaller's signals and company procedure. The marshaller guides; I remain responsible for the aircraft.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing past a stop signal because the stand "looks close enough."  
  ✔ Stop on the stop signal. Final alignment is the marshaller's job to indicate.

- ❌ Moving toward a marshaller who is facing another aircraft.  
  ✔ Confirm you are the aircraft being marshalled before you move.

- ❌ Ignoring rotor clearance while focusing only on the wands.  
  ✔ Scan blades, tail rotor, and obstacles as well as the signals.

- ❌ Starting to taxi away without clearance after marshalling, assuming apron freedom.  
  ✔ Obtain the required ATC or apron clearance before moving again.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are cleared to the stand. A marshaller gives turn and proceed signals, then a stop. You are not yet perfectly on the T-marking.

**Your first task**

What do you do on the stop signal, and what might you say if Ground asks your status?

**Your second task**

The marshaller gives a signal you do not recognize and you are close to equipment. What do you do and say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: I stop immediately on the stop signal. If asked: ANAC123, holding position on the stand under marshalling. Second task: I stop, keep the aircraft safe, and say ANAC123, holding position, unclear marshalling signal — or coordinate on the apron frequency as applicable. I would not continue toward equipment on a doubtful signal.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Marshalling finishes what taxi clearances start.

Know the standard signals. Stop means stop. Unclear means stop.

In the H130, rotor clearance overrides a bad path — even if the wands keep moving.

Confirm the marshaller is yours. Park with discipline. Move again only with clearance.

That is apron professionalism in a visual language everyone on the ramp should share.
