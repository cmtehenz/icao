# Oil Temperature High

**Catalog ID:** 0060  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers high oil temperature — a warning that your engine is overheating.

Oil Temperature High means engine oil temperature has exceeded safe operating limits — lubrication efficiency drops and engine damage becomes likely.

In the H130, the turboshaft engine generates significant heat during high-power operations.

Sustained high oil temperature can lead to engine failure if not managed promptly.

You must aviate first, reduce power, then communicate with the correct urgency call.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Oil Temperature High is an indication that engine oil temperature has risen above safe operating limits, threatening engine damage or failure.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Oil Temperature High occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- reduce power to lower engine load and temperature
- monitor oil temperature and pressure together
- declare Mayday if engine failure is imminent
- declare Pan Pan if temperature is high but responding to power reduction

Oil Temperature High reporting commonly includes:

- high oil temperature or oil temperature warning
- reducing power, monitoring engine parameters
- request vectors to nearest suitable airport
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested

**Important:** Rapidly rising oil temperature with falling pressure is distress — Mayday. High but stable temperature responding to power reduction may warrant Pan Pan. Aviate first — then communicate clearly with standard phraseology.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Oil Temperature High declarations to:

- clear traffic from the aircraft's route
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable airport
- coordinate priority landing and emergency services
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- prepare for possible engine failure on approach

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in a hover-taxi operation near Congonhas helipad in your H130 on a hot afternoon.

Oil temperature rises into the caution range and the warning activates.

You transition to forward flight, reduce power, and gain airspeed for cooling.

When stable, you transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan, São Paulo Tower, ANAC123, high oil temperature, reducing power, request landing Congonhas helipad.

Tower clears the area and approves immediate landing.

Oil temperature decreases during the approach.

You land safely and shut down for inspection.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say oil temperature and souls on board.
2. Tower: ANAC123, cleared direct helipad, all traffic cleared from your approach.
3. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading two seven zero, descend at pilot's discretion.
4. Tower: ANAC123, wind calm, cleared to land helipad.
5. Departure: ANAC123, say nature of engine problem and intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

High oil temperature in a helicopter often comes from sustained high power.

Hovering on a hot day is a classic trigger.

The sequence every professional pilot learns:

Aviate. Cool. Communicate.

Fly the aircraft first.

Reduce power and gain airspeed if possible.

Airflow cools the engine.

Watch oil pressure alongside temperature.

Then declare your urgency.

Rapid rise with falling pressure — Mayday.

High but responding to reduction — Pan Pan.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "óleo quente" or "temperatura alta" on frequency.

Use high oil temperature or oil temperature warning — ICAO standard terms.

Do not hover indefinitely with a rising oil temperature gauge.

Land and investigate.

Train the reflex to transition to forward flight and call ATC early.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do if oil temperature became high during a hover operation?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain aircraft control, reduce power, transition to forward flight to improve engine cooling, and monitor oil temperature and pressure. I would declare Pan Pan Pan Pan or Mayday depending on severity, report high oil temperature, and request landing at the nearest suitable location. I would upgrade to Mayday if temperature continues rising or engine parameters deteriorate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing to hover with rising oil temperature.  
  ✔ Transition to forward flight or land to reduce engine load.

- ❌ Saying "óleo quente demais" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use high oil temperature or oil temperature warning.

- ❌ Ignoring oil temperature because pressure looks normal.  
  ✔ Monitor both — high temperature with normal pressure can still damage the engine.

- ❌ Delaying the call until engine failure.  
  ✔ Declare early while the engine still responds to power reduction.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are hovering near a construction site at five hundred feet near Joinville when oil temperature enters the warning range in your H130.

**Your first task**

After transitioning to forward flight and reducing power, transmit your urgency declaration.

**Your second task**

Answer this question:

Tower asks whether oil temperature is decreasing. What do you report?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report clearly: high oil temperature, ANAC123. I would state that I have reduced power and transitioned to forward flight, and describe whether temperature is decreasing, stable, or still rising. I would request landing at the nearest suitable helipad or airport.
