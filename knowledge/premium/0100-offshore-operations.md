# Offshore Operations

**Catalog ID:** 0100  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers offshore operations — helicopter flights over open water to and from oil platforms, vessels, or offshore installations.

Offshore Operations introduce unique risks: no forced landing area, long distances over water, rapidly changing weather, and often reduced radar coverage.

In the H130, offshore missions from Brazilian coastal airports such as Florianópolis and Navegantes are operationally significant for oil and gas support.

You must understand offshore communication procedures, fuel planning, position reporting, and emergency protocols over water.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2, Part 3, and Part 4 communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Offshore Operations describe helicopter flights conducted over water to service oil platforms, vessels, or other maritime installations, requiring specific communication, navigation, and emergency procedures due to the absence of suitable forced landing areas.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Operações offshore (em mar aberto).

Voos de helicóptero realizados sobre água para atender plataformas de petróleo, embarcações ou instalações marítimas, exigindo procedimentos específicos de comunicação, navegação e emergência devido à ausência de locais adequados para pouso forçado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Offshore Operations are conducted, the pilot should:

- file a flight plan and confirm search and rescue coverage
- brief passengers on ditching procedures and survival equipment
- maintain continuous position awareness and regular ATC reports
- monitor fuel state closely — no alternate below on the water
- declare emergency immediately if engine performance degrades

Offshore Operations reporting commonly includes:

- position report over water
- declaring emergency over open water
- fuel state critical, request priority
- diverting to nearest land point
- mayday or pan-pan over water

**Important:** Over water, there is no forced landing area. Engine failure means ditching. Plan fuel with divert reserve. Know the nearest landing point at all times.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC supports Offshore Operations by:

- maintaining regular position reporting intervals
- providing radar surveillance when in range
- coordinating search and rescue when contact is lost
- relaying weather updates for offshore areas
- approving priority and direct routing for emergencies

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are forty miles offshore from Florianópolis in your H130, routing to an oil platform.

Weather ahead is deteriorating and fuel reserve is being recalculated.

You transmit:

Florianópolis Control, ANAC123, position four zero miles east, descending to five hundred feet due cloud base, fuel state two hours, advise weather at platform.

Control provides updated weather and recommends a direct routing.

You confirm and proceed on the most fuel-efficient track to the platform.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, radar contact forty miles east, say fuel state and intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, weather at platform, cloud base one thousand feet, visibility five kilometers.
3. Control: ANAC123, no radar coverage beyond sixty miles, maintain position reports every fifteen minutes.
4. Control: ANAC123, roger mayday, rescue coordination center notified.
5. Control: ANAC123, cleared direct to platform, descend pilot's discretion.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Over water — there is nothing below you.

No field. No road. No beach. Just ocean.

The sequence:

File the plan. Brief the passengers. Check survival gear. Know the nearest land. Report position. Monitor fuel.

Brazilian offshore operations from Florianópolis and Santos are serious, high-demand missions.

Never skip the passenger ditching brief because it feels unnecessary.

Know your nearest divert at all times.

Fuel is not a comfort reserve over water — it is survival margin.

At the first sign of trouble, declare early. Do not wait until the tank is empty.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do if you suffered an engine malfunction over open water during an offshore mission?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare a mayday immediately, providing my position, altitude, and nature of the emergency. I would attempt to reach the nearest land or platform if within autorotation range, or prepare for a controlled ditching. I would give ATC my last known position and heading, activate all emergency equipment, and ensure passengers are briefed for ditching procedures.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Not briefing passengers on ditching procedures before offshore departure.  
  ✔ Always brief ditching, life jacket, and survival procedures — required by regulation.

- ❌ Delaying the mayday declaration until completely unable to manage the emergency.  
  ✔ Declare early — rescue resources take time to reach open water.

- ❌ Saying "tô com problema" on frequency instead of a standard emergency call.  
  ✔ Use mayday, mayday, mayday followed by position and nature of emergency.

- ❌ Not monitoring fuel reserve against the nearest landing point.  
  ✔ Continuously update your nearest-land calculation when offshore.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are thirty-five miles offshore from Florianópolis in your H130. The engine begins running rough and power is reducing.

**Your first task**

What is your immediate radio call?

**Your second task**

ATC asks for your intentions. What do you tell them?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

My immediate call would be: Mayday, mayday, mayday, ANAC123, engine malfunction, position three five miles east of Florianópolis, altitude five hundred feet, two persons on board. I would advise ATC of my intentions to either return to shore if power permits, or prepare for controlled ditching at my current position, and activate the emergency locator transmitter.
