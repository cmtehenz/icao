# Structural Icing

**Catalog ID:** 0367  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Structural Icing — ice accretion on the airframe, rotor system, and external surfaces that changes aerodynamics, adds weight, and can critically degrade helicopter performance.

Structural icing is a performance emergency in progress, not a weather footnote. Ice on main and tail rotor blades, fuselage, and antennas reduces lift, increases drag, and can lead to vibration, control issues, and inability to maintain altitude.

For H130 operations in Brazil — IFR over the Serra, winter arrivals into Curitiba and Porto Alegre, coastal cloud decks — structural icing demands immediate exit, clear ATC communication, and readiness to declare urgency if the aircraft cannot climb or maintain level flight.

Understanding how to report structural icing and request priority handling in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Structural Icing is the accumulation of ice on the aircraft structure — including wings or rotor blades, fuselage, empennage, antennas, and probes — caused by flight in supercooled visible moisture, resulting in increased weight and drag, reduced lift, and possible degradation of control and systems performance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gelo estrutural / Structural Icing.

Acúmulo de gelo na estrutura da aeronave — incluindo pás do rotor, fuselagem, estabilizadores, antenas e sondas — causado pelo voo em umidade visível super-resfriada, resultando em aumento de peso e arrasto, redução de sustentação e possível degradação de controle e desempenho de sistemas.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When structural icing is encountered or suspected, the crew should:

- exit icing conditions immediately — change altitude, reverse course, or divert
- report icing intensity, type if known, altitude, and temperature to ATC
- monitor collective position, power available, airspeed, and unusual vibration
- avoid further climb into colder cloud if descent to warmer air is available
- declare PAN PAN or MAYDAY if unable to maintain altitude or control
- plan diversion to VMC or an airport with better conditions

Structural icing reporting commonly includes:

- light, moderate, or severe structural icing
- request priority descent or climb to exit
- unable to maintain altitude due icing
- diverting due structural ice

**Important:** Many light helicopters, including typical H130 configurations, are not certified for flight into known icing. Structural ice on rotors is especially dangerous because blade section changes reduce lift efficiency. Do not delay the exit or the radio call.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use structural icing reports to:

- provide immediate altitude or routing changes to exit icing
- alert other aircraft with urgent PIREPs
- coordinate priority handling and diversions
- clear lower or higher levels that may be warmer or drier
- prepare emergency services if a forced landing becomes likely

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR at eight thousand feet toward São Paulo. Ice is building on the windshield, skids, and visible blade roots. Collective is rising to hold altitude. Vibration is increasing.

You transmit:

São Paulo Approach, ANAC123, severe structural icing at eight thousand, unable to maintain altitude, request immediate descent and priority handling.

Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain four thousand, priority approved, say souls on board when able.

You descend. Ice stops accreting in warmer air. You report clear of icing and request vectors to the nearest suitable airport.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger severe icing, descend immediately four thousand, priority approved.
2. Center: ANAC123, say icing intensity and souls on board.
3. Approach: ANAC123, diversion approved, vectors to Congonhas, advise fuel remaining.
4. Approach: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported severe structural icing at seven thousand.
5. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able to maintain altitude.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Structural icing is where weather becomes aircraft performance.

You are not collecting ice for a report — you are losing the shape of the machine that keeps you flying.

In the H130, watch collective and power. If you need more and more collective to hold level flight in cloud near freezing, assume structural ice and leave the layer.

Brazilian IFR winters give long stratiform exposures. That is how light icing becomes moderate, then severe, while crews hope it will stop.

Hope is not a procedure.

Exit first. Report with intensity. If you cannot hold altitude, say unable to maintain altitude and request priority. That language gets ATC moving.

If the situation is critical — vibration, rapid power loss, inability to climb — use urgency or distress phraseology. Structural icing kills options faster than most system failures.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Ice is forming on the airframe and you need increasing power to maintain altitude in cloud. How do you describe the situation and what do you request?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would treat this as structural icing affecting performance. I would request an immediate altitude change to exit icing and report severe or moderate structural icing with altitude and temperature if known. I would say I am unable to maintain altitude due to icing and request priority descent or diversion. I would monitor rotor performance and vibration, avoid remaining in the icing layer, and declare urgency if control or climb capability is threatened. In the H130 I would not continue into known icing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Softening the report: "a little ice" when performance is already degraded.  
  ✔ Use intensity: moderate or severe structural icing, unable to maintain altitude.

- ❌ Waiting for ATC to offer a level change.  
  ✔ Request immediate descent or climb; state the operational need.

- ❌ Continuing toward destination through the icing layer.  
  ✔ Divert or reverse course if exit by altitude change is not available.

- ❌ Omitting "unable to maintain" when altitude is slipping.  
  ✔ That phrase triggers priority handling — use it when true.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Severe Structural Icing, Request Immediate Descent

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, seh-VEER STRUK-cher-ul ICE-ing, reh-KWEST ih-MEE-dee-it deh-SENT

**Word Stress**

- Severe → seh-VEER (stress on second)
- Structural → STRUK-cher-ul (stress on first)
- Icing → ICE-ing
- Immediate → ih-MEE-dee-it (stress on second)

Practice:

Severe structural icing... request immediate descent.

Together:

ANAC123, severe structural icing at eight thousand, unable to maintain altitude, request immediate descent.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**ICE-OUT** — **I**dentify structural accretion, **C**ollective rising is your cue, **E**xit the layer now, **O**wn the radio call with intensity, **U**nable to maintain if altitude slips, **T**urn or divert if needed.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are at six thousand feet IFR near Curitiba. Structural ice is building. You cannot hold altitude even with high collective.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

ATC clears you to three thousand and asks souls on board. Ice stops. What do you say next?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, severe structural icing at six thousand, unable to maintain altitude, request immediate descent and priority handling. Second task: ANAC123, descending three thousand, three souls on board; then when stable: ANAC123, clear of icing at three thousand, request vectors to nearest suitable airport. Performance recovery does not cancel the need for a safe landing plan after severe icing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Structural icing attacks the aircraft you are flying — not the chart, not the forecast.

In the H130, leave known icing. Report intensity. Say unable when altitude is gone.

Brazilian winter IFR will test this. The crews who survive it are the ones who exit early and speak clearly.

Ice on structure is a critical weather event. Treat it that way on the frequency and in the cockpit.
