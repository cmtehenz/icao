# Automation Complacency

**Catalog ID:** 0469  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Automation Complacency — the tendency to over-trust automated systems and reduce monitoring, so that mode errors, path deviations, and system faults are detected late or not at all.

Complacency is quiet. The magenta line looks good. The autopilot is on. Scanning slows. When automation fails or does the unexpected, the crew is behind the aircraft.

For H130 operations with autopilot, flight directors, and GPS coupling, automation complacency is a real Part 1 human-factors topic with Part 2 consequences when deviations must be reported, and Part 4 crew monitoring discipline. Advise ATC when automation issues affect the clearance.

## 📖 Meaning

Automation Complacency is a human-factors condition in which pilots place excessive trust in automated systems and reduce active monitoring and cross-checking, resulting in delayed detection of automation failures, mode errors, or deviations from the intended flight path.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Complacência com a automação / Automation Complacency.

Condição de fatores humanos na qual os pilotos depositam confiança excessiva nos sistemas automatizados e reduzem o monitoramento e a checagem cruzada ativos, resultando em detecção tardia de falhas de automação, erros de modo ou desvios da trajetória de voo pretendida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When guarding against automation complacency, the crew should:

- monitor automation as if it will fail — because sometimes it will
- call out mode changes and verify targets against the clearance
- cross-check raw data, charts, and independent instruments
- hand-fly periodically to maintain skill and engagement
- tell ATC if automation degradation affects ability to comply

Automation complacency countermeasures include:

- PF/PM monitoring roles with explicit mode awareness
- briefed automation levels for departure and approach
- immediate challenge when path or mode looks wrong
- willingness to disconnect and fly manually

**Important:** Autopilot engaged is not the same as crew engaged. If you cannot state the active modes and targets, you are already complacent.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers see automation complacency outcomes when aircraft:

- deviate from assigned altitude or heading
- fail to capture an approach path as expected
- request delay after late recognition of a mode error
- report autopilot or navigation problems mid-clearance
- need radar vectors after losing RNAV/GPS coupling

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Coupled approach in the H130. Both pilots watch the magenta line. Neither notices the altitude select is still at three thousand while cleared to two thousand.

Approach: ANAC123, confirm descending two thousand, you appear level.

You disconnect complacency:

ANAC123, correcting, descending two thousand.

Then you reset monitoring: modes called, targets verified, raw data checked.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, confirm descending two thousand, you appear level.
2. Approach: ANAC123, you are left of course, confirm established.
3. Radar: ANAC123, resume own navigation, advise if RNAV problem.
4. Approach: ANAC123, fly heading two seven zero, vectors due navigation deviation.
5. Tower: ANAC123, go around, not aligned with runway.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Automation is a crew member that never gets tired and never explains itself. You must supervise it.

Complacency grows on quiet flights. The H130 can lull you on a smooth GPS arrival until a mode silently fails to arm.

Call the modes. Point to the targets. Ask: what is it doing, what do I want, what will it do next?

If monitoring has faded, reconnect: raw data, chart, clearance. Disconnect automation if the path is wrong. Then tell ATC if you need vectors.

Part 4 CRM means challenging a quiet cockpit: "Are we cleared two thousand? Why are we level?" That question is anti-complacency.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC says you appear level above your cleared altitude. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately verify mode and altitude select, correct the flight path, and transmit: ANAC123, correcting, descending two thousand. I would then increase monitoring and cross-checks. If automation is unreliable, I would fly manually and request vectors if navigation is in doubt.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Assuming the autopilot captured because it usually does.  
  ✔ Verify capture with raw data and mode annunciations.

- ❌ Both pilots heads-down on tablets while coupled.  
  ✔ Keep one pilot monitoring flight path continuously.

- ❌ Fixing a deviation silently without telling ATC when asked.  
  ✔ Report correcting when ATC queries the deviation.

- ❌ Leaving wrong targets set "for later."  
  ✔ Set and verify targets when the clearance is received.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On a coupled H130 arrival, Approach says you are left of course and still high. You had been trusting the GPS path without raw-data checks.

**Your first task**

What do you do immediately?

**Your second task**

What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Correct path and altitude — disconnect automation if needed — restore raw-data monitoring. Second task: ANAC123, correcting, request vectors if not established. Automation complacency ends when the crew re-engages and communicates the correction.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Automation complacency is trust without supervision.

Modes. Targets. Raw data. Challenge.

In the H130, coupled approaches into Brazilian terminals still need a human in the loop. Watch it. Correct early. Tell ATC when you deviate.

That is how automation remains a tool — not the pilot in command.
