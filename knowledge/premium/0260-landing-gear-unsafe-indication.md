# Landing Gear Unsafe Indication

**Catalog ID:** 0260  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Landing Gear Unsafe Indication — a cockpit warning or disagreement showing that the landing gear is not confirmed down and locked (or not in the expected position), requiring verification procedures, possible tower visual check, and clear ATC coordination before landing.

An unsafe gear indication is not automatically a collapsed gear. It is a disagreement between commanded position and confirmed locked status. The crew must treat it seriously, run the checklist, and use ATC — including a possible low approach for visual inspection — before committing to landing.

For H130 helicopter ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, many H130s use skids and will not present classic gear unsafe lights. The ICAO exam still expects this phraseology for wheeled aircraft. Learn it precisely; relate skid-equipped operations honestly when the concept does not apply to your airframe.

Understanding unsafe gear indication English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Landing Gear Unsafe Indication is a cockpit warning or status disagreement indicating that one or more landing gear legs are not confirmed in the safe down-and-locked (or expected) position, requiring abnormal procedures and ATC coordination before a normal landing is assumed.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Indicação insegura do trem de pouso / Landing Gear Unsafe Indication.

Aviso ou discordância no cockpit indicando que uma ou mais pernas do trem de pouso não estão confirmadas na posição segura baixada e travada (ou na posição esperada), exigindo procedimentos anormais e coordenação com o ATC antes de assumir um pouso normal.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a landing gear unsafe indication appears, the crew should:

- maintain control and apply the AFM gear unsafe checklist
- recycle gear only if the procedure permits
- attempt alternate extension if required
- advise ATC of unsafe gear indication
- request a low approach or tower visual check if useful and safe
- request priority landing and RFF if the gear cannot be confirmed down and locked

Unsafe indication scenarios commonly include:

- red unsafe light or missing green down-and-locked lights
- disagreement between gear handle position and indicator
- one gear unsafe while others show locked
- unsafe indication after extension on approach
- H130 note: skid aircraft may not have this system — still learn exam English

**Important:** Do not land assuming the gear is down because you selected it. Confirm down and locked — by indication, checklist, or visual check. If still unsafe, plan an abnormal landing with firefighters.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to unsafe gear indications to:

- authorize low approaches for visual gear inspection
- provide tower or ground reports of gear appearance
- alert RFF when confirmation fails
- sequence priority landing and clear the runway
- obtain souls, fuel, and landing intentions

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Wheeled-aircraft exam scenario: on approach to Guarulhos, you select gear down. Two greens. One unsafe light remains. Checklist complete. You request a low pass.

You transmit:

ANAC123, landing gear unsafe indication, request low approach for visual gear check.

Tower: ANAC123, cleared low approach runway two seven L, report gear appearance will be advised.

After the pass, Tower reports the right main appears down. You still treat the indication as unresolved until checklist logic says otherwise — then you land with RFF ready if required.

In the skid-equipped H130, you listen and learn; you may be the traffic held while a jet resolves an unsafe gear light.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger unsafe gear indication, cleared low approach, will advise visual.
2. Tower: ANAC123, gear appears down, advise intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm requesting firefighters for full-stop landing.
4. Tower: ANAC123, runway cleared, RFF in position, cleared to land.
5. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, landing gear unsafe indication, requesting low approach for visual check.
2. ANAC123, gear appears down copied, requesting full stop with firefighters.
3. ANAC123, affirmative, requesting firefighters for full-stop landing.
4. ANAC123, runway cleared, RFF copied, cleared to land.
5. ANAC123, souls four, fuel one hour.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Unsafe means unconfirmed — not automatically destroyed.

Your job is verification. Checklist. Possible recycle if allowed. Alternate extension if required. Visual check if it adds information. Then a decision: normal landing if confirmed, or abnormal landing with RFF if not.

On the ICAO exam, "landing gear unsafe indication" plus "request low approach for visual check" is high-value language. Use it.

In the H130 with skids, do not force this malfunction into daily flying. Master it for the test and for the day you fly a wheeled type — or share a frequency with one in trouble at Congonhas.

Never argue with an unsafe light by hoping. Confirm or prepare for the abnormal landing.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You have a landing gear unsafe indication after extension. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would apply the gear unsafe checklist, avoid assuming the gear is locked, and advise ATC of the unsafe indication. I would request a low approach for visual inspection if appropriate, then either confirm down and locked or plan an abnormal landing with priority and firefighters. I would not land as a normal landing while the indication remains unresolved.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Landing immediately with an unsafe light because "it usually works."  
  ✔ Complete verification procedures before treating the landing as normal.

- ❌ Not requesting a visual check when it could resolve doubt safely.  
  ✔ Request low approach for tower visual gear check when useful.

- ❌ Failing to alert firefighters when confirmation is impossible.  
  ✔ If still unsafe, request RFF and brief an abnormal landing.

- ❌ Using unsafe-gear radio calls in a skid H130 as if wheels existed.  
  ✔ Keep real operations accurate; keep exam English ready separately.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Landing Gear Unsafe Indication, Request Low Approach

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, LAN-ding GEER un-SAFE in-dih-KAY-shun, reh-KWEST LOH uh-PROCH

**Word Stress**

- Unsafe → un-SAFE (stress on SAFE)
- Indication → in-dih-KAY-shun (stress on KAY)
- Low → LOH
- Approach → uh-PROCH

Practice:

Landing gear unsafe indication... request low approach for visual check.

Together:

ANAC123, landing gear unsafe indication, request low approach for visual gear check.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**UNSAFE** — **U**se the checklist, **N**ever assume locked, **S**eek visual check, **A**dvise ATC early, **F**irefighters if unresolved, **E**xecute the decided landing type.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Gear selected down. One unsafe indication remains after checklist. You want a visual check, then a full-stop with RFF if still unresolved.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Tower says the gear appears down. The unsafe light remains. What do you say?

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Unsafe indication is a verification problem until proven otherwise.

Checklist. Visual check if useful. Honest report. Then land normal — or land abnormal with firefighters.

For ICAO exams, this phrase is core wheeled-aircraft English. For many H130s on skids, it is exam knowledge and shared-airport awareness.

Do not hope the light away. Confirm or prepare.

That is professional gear discipline.
