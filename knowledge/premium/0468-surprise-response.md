# Surprise Response

**Catalog ID:** 0468  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Surprise Response — the cognitive reaction when reality does not match expectation, causing delayed understanding, incorrect mental models, and sometimes inappropriate actions even without a loud startle stimulus.

Surprise is quieter than startle and often more dangerous. The automation does something unexpected. The runway environment looks wrong. The clearance does not match the FMS. For seconds or longer, the crew asks "what is it doing?" instead of flying.

For H130 crews, surprise appears with mode confusion, unexpected ATC instructions, and sudden weather. Part 1 HF language, Part 2 clarification calls, and Part 3 abnormal reporting all apply. Clarify or go around when the picture breaks.

## 📖 Meaning

Surprise Response is the cognitive and behavioral reaction that occurs when an unexpected event or mismatch between expectation and reality disrupts the pilot's mental model, often causing delayed comprehension, fixation, or incorrect actions until the situation is reassessed and a new plan is formed.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Resposta à surpresa / Surprise Response.

Reação cognitiva e comportamental que ocorre quando um evento inesperado ou um descompasso entre expectativa e realidade rompe o modelo mental do piloto, frequentemente causando compreensão atrasada, fixação ou ações incorretas até que a situação seja reavaliada e um novo plano seja formado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When surprise occurs, the crew should:

- recognize the mismatch: "this is not what I expected"
- return to basic flight path control — attitude, altitude, airspeed
- verbalize the surprise to share the problem: "mode is wrong," "wrong runway"
- use raw data and charts to rebuild the mental model
- tell ATC: confirm, unable, go around, or request delay as needed

Surprise management includes:

- briefing expected modes and ATC plans to reduce mismatch
- cross-checking automation against raw data
- refusing to continue when the picture does not make sense
- training for unexpected go-arounds and clearance changes

**Important:** Surprise without startle still freezes crews. If you do not understand the aircraft state, do not descend. Climb, hold, or go around — then analyze.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers help resolve surprise when crews:

- request confirmation of unexpected clearances
- go around after runway or approach confusion
- report automation or navigation problems
- ask for delay vectors to rebuild the plan
- declare unable until the situation is understood

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You expect vectors for ILS one six. Approach clears you for a sudden circle-to-land on runway three four. The mental model breaks.

You do not force the approach.

Approach, ANAC123, unable circle-to-land, request vectors for ILS one six or delay.

Approach: ANAC123, roger, turn left heading one eight zero, climb three thousand, expect ILS one six.

Surprise acknowledged. Plan rebuilt. Aircraft protected.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, cleared circle-to-land runway three four, report runway in sight.
2. Approach: ANAC123, roger unable, turn left heading one eight zero, maintain three thousand.
3. Tower: ANAC123, go around, expect new approach, contact approach.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you are established on the localizer.
5. Control: ANAC123, unexpected routing due traffic, advise ready to copy.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, unable circle-to-land, request ILS one six.
2. ANAC123, left heading one eight zero, maintain three thousand.
3. ANAC123, going around, request vectors, need time to rebrief.
4. ANAC123, not established, request vectors to intercept.
5. ANAC123, ready to copy new routing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Surprise is a broken story. Your job is to notice the break.

"What is it doing?" is an honest question — but it must be followed by "I fly wings level and altitude" while you answer it.

In the H130, automation may be simpler than a jet, but surprise still comes from weather, ATC changes, and navigation mismatches near terrain. If the story breaks on final, go around. Rebrief. Return.

Brief expectations before top of descent: expected runway, mode, minima, missed approach. The better the expectation, the smaller the surprise — and the faster the recovery.

Use Part 2 English to buy time: unable, request delay, confirm clearance. That is surprise management on frequency.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Automation or ATC presents something you did not expect on final. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not continue into confusion. I would secure the flight path — go around if necessary — and transmit: ANAC123, going around, request vectors, or ANAC123, confirm clearance. I would rebuild the mental model with the other pilot using charts and raw data before accepting another approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Forcing the original plan after reality changed.  
  ✔ Accept the mismatch; build a new plan or go around.

- ❌ Staring at the FMS while the aircraft drifts.  
  ✔ Fly attitude and altitude first; then diagnose.

- ❌ Saying nothing because you are embarrassed.  
  ✔ Verbalize: "I am surprised, confirm mode/clearance."

- ❌ Accepting complex new clearances while confused.  
  ✔ Request delay or simplified instructions.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On final in the H130, you suddenly realize the armed approach is for the wrong runway. Tower has cleared you to land.

**Your first task**

What do you do?

**Your second task**

What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Do not continue — go around and climb to a safe altitude while the crew rebuilds the plan. Second task: ANAC123, going around, request vectors, wrong approach loaded — or simply ANAC123, going around, request vectors for correct approach. Surprise response means rejecting confusion, not landing through it.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Surprise response is about noticing when the story breaks.

Name the mismatch. Fly basics. Rebuild. Communicate.

In the H130, Brazilian ATC and weather will change the plan. Brief hard. Verify harder. When surprised, buy time with unable and go around.

That is how crews convert confusion back into control.
