# Affirm

**Catalog ID:** 0132  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Affirm — the correct ICAO word for yes in aviation radio communication.

In ICAO standard phraseology, the word "yes" is never used. Affirm replaces it.

The reason is clarity: in high-noise radio environments, "yes" is easily confused with similar sounds. Affirm is unambiguous, internationally recognizable, and standardized.

In the H130, flying in and out of Brazilian airports like Florianópolis, Navegantes, and Joinville, you will be asked yes/no questions by ATC regularly. Knowing when to say Affirm — and understanding how it differs from Roger, Wilco, and Confirm — is core ICAO Level 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Affirm is the ICAO-standard word meaning yes. It is used to provide a positive answer to a question. It replaces the word "yes" in all aviation radio communications. It confirms that the stated condition, information, or action is correct or that the answer to a yes/no question is positive.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Afirmativo / Sim.

Palavra ICAO padrão que significa "sim." É usada para fornecer uma resposta positiva a uma pergunta. Substitui a palavra "yes" (sim) em todas as comunicações de rádio aeronáutico. Confirma que a condição, informação ou ação declarada é correta, ou que a resposta a uma pergunta é positiva.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When to use Affirm:

- to answer yes to a direct question from ATC
- to confirm a condition is correct when asked
- to confirm you have received a specific piece of information such as ATIS
- to confirm equipment status when asked

When NOT to use Affirm:

- in place of a required readback of specific values
- to indicate compliance — use Wilco
- to confirm receipt of a general message — use Roger
- to provide detailed information — state the information

Affirm operations commonly include:

- confirming you have ATIS information
- confirming you have the field in sight
- answering whether you are IMC or VMC
- confirming equipment capability (ILS capable, RNAV equipped)
- answering whether you are ready for departure

**Important:** Affirm and "affirmative" are different. Affirm is the correct ICAO word. Affirmative is longer and found in some non-ICAO environments. ICAO and DECEA both use Affirm. Do not use "affirmativo" on English-language frequencies.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers ask yes/no questions to verify:

- pilot has current ATIS information before issuing clearances
- aircraft equipment matches the assigned approach procedure
- pilot confirms traffic in sight before continuing
- pilot is ready for a specific action or clearance
- crew is aware of a specific condition or restriction

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Florianópolis in your H130.

Approach asks:

ANAC123, do you have information delta?

You have the ATIS. You reply:

Affirm, information delta, ANAC123.

Approach then asks:

ANAC123, runway two eight, cleared for visual approach. Traffic in sight?

You have the traffic. You reply:

Affirm, traffic in sight, ANAC123.

Both uses of Affirm are precise, professional, and compliant with ICAO standards.

If you had said "Yes, I have it" or "Sim, tenho" on the English frequency, you would sound unprofessional and potentially unclear on a noisy frequency.

Affirm is the standard. Use it every time.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you have ATIS information bravo?
2. Tower: ANAC123, runway in sight?
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you are RNAV equipped?
4. Ground: ANAC123, confirm you are ready for push and start?
5. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you can accept the visual approach?

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Affirm is a precision tool.

It answers yes — cleanly, clearly, and in ICAO-standard form.

Brazilian pilots often mix Affirm with Roger, or use "affirmativo" on English frequencies, or simply say "sim" when excited.

None of these are ICAO standard English.

Affirm is two syllables. Clear. Unmistakable. Internationally standardized.

Train yourself: whenever you would naturally say "yes" on the radio, say Affirm.

And notice the distinction:

ATC: Do you have the field in sight?
You: Affirm. (yes)

ATC: Descend to altitude three thousand.
You: Descend altitude three thousand, ANAC123. (readback)

ATC: Report when ready.
You: Wilco, ANAC123. (compliance)

ATC: Traffic information, Cessna to your north.
You: Roger, ANAC123. (receipt)

Four different situations. Four different words.

Affirm answers yes/no questions.

Roger, Wilco, and readbacks serve their own distinct roles.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Why does ICAO use Affirm instead of Yes? What is the difference between Affirm and Roger?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

ICAO uses Affirm because "yes" can sound similar to other words in radio communication, particularly with noise, accents, and frequency distortion. Affirm is standardized and internationally recognized, reducing the chance of confusion. Affirm and Roger have different meanings: Affirm is a positive answer to a yes/no question. Roger confirms receipt of a general message. If ATC asks "Do you have the weather?" I say Affirm. If ATC gives me a weather report and no action is required, I say Roger.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "affirmativo" or "sim" on English-language frequencies.  
  ✔ Use Affirm — the standard ICAO English word.

- ❌ Using Affirm to indicate compliance with an instruction.  
  ✔ Affirm answers a yes/no question. Use Wilco to indicate you will comply.

- ❌ Using Affirm when a readback is required.  
  ✔ If ATC asks "Confirm cleared to land runway two eight?" respond with the readback: Cleared to land runway two eight, ANAC123.

- ❌ Confusing Affirm with Roger after receiving an instruction that required no yes/no answer.  
  ✔ Roger for general receipt. Affirm for positive yes/no answers only.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Navegantes Approach asks: "ANAC123, confirm you have information alpha and you are ready for the RNAV approach runway two nine?"

**Your first task**

You have information alpha and you are ready. Give a complete professional response.

**Your second task**

Approach then asks: "ANAC123, confirm you can accept a circling approach to runway one one if required?" You can accept it. Respond.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First response: Affirm, information alpha, ready RNAV runway two nine, ANAC123. I confirm both elements and keep it concise. Second response: Affirm, circling runway one one acceptable, ANAC123. Affirm closes the yes/no question and I add the specific details for clarity. I do not say Wilco here because ATC has not yet issued the instruction — they are asking if I can accept it. Affirm signals readiness to accept; Wilco would follow the actual clearance.
