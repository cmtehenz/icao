# Fenestron Blockage

**Catalog ID:** 0436  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Fenestron Blockage — restriction or contamination of the fenestron (shrouded tail rotor) duct that reduces anti-torque authority and may lead to loss of yaw control.

Fenestron blockage awareness is high priority for H130 crews operating from dusty pads, vegetated confined areas, and debris-prone Brazilian sites.

You must describe recognition, go-around or emergency actions, and clear radio calls in ICAO English for Parts 2 and 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Fenestron Blockage is a condition in which the shrouded tail rotor duct or fenestron is partially or fully obstructed by foreign objects, ice, vegetation, or debris, reducing anti-torque thrust and potentially causing uncommanded yaw or inability to maintain heading in a hover or during power changes.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Bloqueio do fenestron / fenestron blockage.

Condição em que o duto do rotor de cauda carenado (fenestron) está parcial ou totalmente obstruído por objetos estranhos, gelo, vegetação ou detritos, reduzindo o empuxo antitorque e podendo causar guinada não comandada ou incapacidade de manter a proa no pairamento ou durante mudanças de potência.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When fenestron blockage is suspected, the pilot should:

- recognize reduced yaw authority, unusual pedal position, or uncommanded yaw
- avoid high-power hover in debris, tall grass, or loose material when possible
- inspect the fenestron during preflight and after operations in contaminated areas
- go around or land as soon as practical if yaw control degrades
- declare emergency if heading control cannot be maintained

Fenestron blockage reporting commonly includes:

- experiencing yaw control difficulty, going around
- possible fenestron blockage, request priority landing
- declaring emergency, loss of anti-torque authority
- landing clear, request inspection assistance

**Important:** The H130 fenestron is protected compared with an open tail rotor, but it is not immune. Dust, plastic bags, vegetation, and ice can still degrade performance. Preflight the duct. Choose clean surfaces for hover.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support fenestron-related emergencies by:

- clearing traffic for an immediate landing or go-around
- providing priority handling and long final if requested
- dispatching emergency services
- keeping the landing area clear of vehicles and people
- coordinating after-landing assistance and inspection

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You establish a hover over a dry, debris-strewn pad in your H130. Pedal authority feels reduced. The nose wants to yaw.

You go around to a clear area:

Yaw control difficulty, possible fenestron blockage, going around, request priority landing on clear surface.

You land with minimum hover time, shut down, and inspect the fenestron duct.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger going around, say intentions.
2. Tower: ANAC123, priority approved, cleared to land runway / pad as requested.
3. Tower: ANAC123, roger emergency, fire services rolling, say souls and fuel.
4. Tower: ANAC123, area cleared, land at your discretion, advise on ground.
5. Ground: ANAC123, assistance available, report shutdown.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around, yaw control difficulty, will advise intentions.
2. ANAC123, priority landing, possible fenestron blockage.
3. ANAC123, declaring emergency, loss of yaw control, souls and fuel when able.
4. ANAC123, landing at discretion, will advise on ground.
5. ANAC123, on ground, shutting down, request inspection assistance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fenestron blockage is anti-torque theft.

You ask for pedal. The aircraft does not give it. Leave the dirty hover. Land where you can inspect.

In the H130, preflight the shroud and duct every time — especially after grassy confined areas and dusty Brazilian dry-season pads. Part 3 loves: reduced pedal authority in hover — what do you do? Answer: go around or land ASAP on a clear surface, report yaw control difficulty, possible fenestron blockage, request priority.

Do not fight a blocked fenestron in a prolonged hover over debris. You will lose the argument.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

In a hover you lose yaw authority over a debris-covered pad. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would exit the hover with a go-around to a clear area if able, minimizing further debris ingestion, and transmit: yaw control difficulty, possible fenestron blockage, going around, request priority landing. If heading cannot be controlled, I would declare an emergency and land as soon as practical on a suitable surface. After landing I would shut down and inspect the fenestron.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Prolonged hover over loose plastic and dust.  
  ✔ Minimize hover time on contaminated surfaces.

- ❌ Skipping fenestron duct inspection on preflight.  
  ✔ Inspect shroud and duct every flight.

- ❌ Vague radio: " Pedal problem."  
  ✔ Say yaw control difficulty, possible fenestron blockage.

- ❌ Continuing the mission after degraded yaw feel.  
  ✔ Land and inspect before further flight.
