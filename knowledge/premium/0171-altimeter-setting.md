# Altimeter Setting

**Catalog ID:** 0171  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Altimeter Setting — the ATC-issued pressure value that pilots set on their altimeter to ensure their indicated altitude reflects actual height above sea level or above the airport, depending on the setting type.

An incorrect altimeter setting is not a minor error. It is a safety hazard.

A 1 hPa error produces approximately 30 feet of altitude error. A 10 hPa error — the kind that can occur when pilots neglect to update their altimeter after a significant pressure change — results in nearly 300 feet of deviation from intended altitude.

In mountainous Brazilian airspace between Joinville, Florianópolis, and Curitiba, terrain clearance margins are narrow. An incorrect altimeter setting can close that margin dangerously.

Altimeter settings are issued regularly — on ATIS, during clearance delivery, during approach briefings, and whenever ATC detects a significant pressure change.

Pilots must know when to set QNH versus QFE, how to request a setting, and how to read back the value correctly.

## 📖 Meaning

Altimeter Setting is the atmospheric pressure value, expressed in hectopascals (hPa) or inches of mercury (inHg), set on the aircraft altimeter subscale to calibrate the instrument so that it indicates the correct altitude — QNH for altitude above mean sea level, QFE for height above the aerodrome.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Ajuste do altímetro.

Valor de pressão atmosférica, expresso em hectopascais (hPa) ou polegadas de mercúrio (inHg), ajustado na escala do altímetro da aeronave para calibrar o instrumento de modo que indique a altitude correta — QNH para altitude acima do nível médio do mar, QFE para altura acima do aeródromo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ATC issues an altimeter setting, the pilot should:

- set the value on the altimeter subscale immediately
- confirm the indicated altitude agrees with known field elevation (if on the ground)
- read back the value as part of the clearance acknowledgment
- request a new altimeter setting if significant time has passed or pressure appears to have changed

Key altimeter settings:

- **QNH** — set to read altitude above mean sea level; used in most controlled airspace below transition level
- **QFE** — set to read height above the aerodrome elevation; reads zero on the ground at the reference point
- **Standard (1013.25 hPa / 29.92 inHg)** — set above transition altitude; altimeter reads flight level, not altitude

Brazil uses hPa (hectopascals) as the standard unit. Many Brazilian airports are at lower elevations, but high-altitude terrain between São Paulo and Curitiba makes correct QNH essential.

**Important:** The transition altitude in Brazil is typically 18,000 feet — well above most domestic routes. Below transition altitude, maintain QNH. Above, set standard.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue altimeter settings to:

- ensure all aircraft in the same airspace are using the same pressure reference
- support vertical separation on QNH
- alert pilots to significant pressure changes not yet reflected in their current setting
- provide the current QNH at initial contact, during approach, and before landing
- support safe terrain clearance in mountainous areas

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are descending toward Joinville in your H130 after transiting at flight level eight five.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, descend to two thousand feet, QNH one zero one three.

You set QNH 1013 on the altimeter subscale.

Your indicated altitude adjusts from flight level eight five — now reading altitude above sea level.

You respond:

Descend two thousand feet QNH one zero one three, ANAC123.

Passing through the high terrain segment south of Joinville, your altitude is referenced correctly to sea level.

Terrain clearance is maintained.

On the approach:

ANAC123, QNH revised, one zero zero eight.

You update the subscale immediately. The indicated altitude adjusts slightly downward — the pressure has dropped.

ANAC123, QNH one zero zero eight, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The altimeter subscale is not a set-and-forget control.

It changes. Pressure changes. The setting changes.

Every new QNH from ATC is an action item — not just information.

Set it. Confirm the indicated altitude makes sense. Read it back.

Brazilian pilots sometimes neglect to update the QNH mid-flight because the change seems small.

A 5 hPa drop is 150 feet of altitude error.

In mountainous terrain between Joinville and Florianópolis, 150 feet is not trivial.

And remember the transition level: below it, fly altitude on QNH. Above it, fly flight level on standard. Know where your transition altitude is for the route.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Why is it important to update the altimeter setting when ATC issues a revised QNH?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Atmospheric pressure changes with weather systems and with movement between different pressure areas. If a pilot does not update the altimeter when ATC issues a revised QNH, the indicated altitude will differ from the actual altitude above sea level. This creates a risk of terrain collision in areas with high ground, and a risk of altitude deviations and collision with other traffic in controlled airspace. Each hectopascal of QNH error produces approximately thirty feet of altitude error, so even a five hectopascal change means one hundred fifty feet of potential deviation — a significant margin in areas of high terrain or dense traffic.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Setting the altimeter at departure and not updating it throughout the flight.  
  ✔ Update the QNH every time ATC issues a revised setting.

- ❌ Reading back the QNH without confirming the indicated altitude on the ground.  
  ✔ On the ground, verify the indicated altitude against known field elevation after setting QNH.

- ❌ Confusing QNH and QFE — setting QFE below the transition altitude in controlled airspace.  
  ✔ In Brazilian controlled airspace, QNH is the standard below transition altitude.

- ❌ Omitting the QNH from the readback.  
  ✔ Always include QNH in the readback: descend two thousand QNH one zero one three, ANAC123.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Approach transmits: ANAC123, descend to two thousand feet, QNH one zero zero five.

Your altimeter currently shows two thousand three hundred feet with QNH one zero one zero set.

**Your first task**

Read back the clearance and state what action you take immediately.

**Your second task**

After setting the new QNH, your altimeter now reads one thousand eight hundred feet instead of the expected two thousand feet. Is this correct? Explain the altitude change and what it means operationally.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Readback: Descend two thousand feet, QNH one zero zero five, ANAC123. Immediate action: set one zero zero five on the altimeter subscale. The indicated altitude dropped from approximately two thousand three hundred to one thousand eight hundred after the setting change. This is expected: the pressure dropped by five hectopascals from one zero one zero to one zero zero five, which corresponds to approximately one hundred fifty feet of altitude difference. My actual altitude above sea level has not changed — only the reference has been updated. I am now at one thousand eight hundred feet above sea level, not two thousand, so I continue descending to two thousand feet on the new QNH setting.
