# Descend Via STAR

**Catalog ID:** 0156  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Descend Via STAR — the ATC clearance that authorizes an arriving aircraft to follow both the lateral routing and the published altitude restrictions of a Standard Terminal Arrival Route simultaneously.

Descend Via STAR is the arrival equivalent of Climb Via SID — and the same distinction applies.

A standard STAR clearance gives you the lateral routing. ATC still controls your altitude separately through sequential descent instructions.

Descend Via STAR gives you both: fly the STAR route and descend through the STAR's published altitude restrictions without requiring individual altitude clearances for each step.

Understanding the precise difference between STAR Clearance and Descend Via STAR is critical ICAO knowledge. Many pilots confuse the two — and that confusion leads to altitude deviations.

## 📖 Meaning

Descend Via STAR is an ATC clearance authorizing the arriving aircraft to follow the lateral routing of the Standard Terminal Arrival Route and to comply with all published altitude restrictions of that procedure, descending through the STAR altitude profile without requiring individual altitude instructions from ATC for each step.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorização do controle de tráfego aéreo para a aeronave em chegada seguir o roteamento lateral da chegada padrão para área terminal e cumprir todas as restrições de altitude publicadas desse procedimento, descendo pelo perfil de altitude do STAR sem necessitar de instruções individuais de altitude do controle para cada etapa.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives Descend Via STAR, they should:

- confirm the STAR name is loaded and active in the FMS
- comply with every published altitude restriction on the STAR from the point of clearance
- comply with all published speed constraints on the STAR
- do not stop at intermediate altitudes unless a published restriction or an ATC amendment requires it
- descend continuously through the STAR altitude profile to the final STAR altitude or the bottom altitude assigned in the clearance
- advise ATC of any inability to comply with a restriction before or immediately upon encountering it

Descend Via STAR operations commonly include:

- an assigned bottom altitude that limits the descent: "descend via STAR except maintain five thousand"
- complex STARs with step-down altitude restrictions at multiple fixes
- speed restrictions published at specific waypoints for sequencing or noise abatement
- transition from STAR guidance to approach vectoring or initial approach fix

**Important:** The key distinction — Descend Via STAR authorizes the crew to descend through published altitude restrictions. A standard STAR clearance does not. If ATC says "cleared [STAR name] arrival" without "descend via," you maintain your assigned altitude and wait for ATC descent instructions. If ATC says "descend via STAR," the STAR altitude profile is your authorized descent profile.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Descend Via STAR to:

- reduce radio communication during busy arrival flows by using published profiles
- allow the crew to manage their own altitude within the coded STAR profile
- optimize arrival sequencing through complex terminal airspace
- coordinate arrival traffic with approach sectors using pre-defined altitude profiles
- streamline clearances when the STAR altitude profile is consistent with the separation plan

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at flight level one two zero inbound to Florianópolis in your H130.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, descend via STAR, IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, except maintain four thousand.

You load the IDAMO ONE STAR in the GPS.

You brief with your co-pilot: the STAR routing, and every published altitude restriction — flight level nine zero at IDAMO, seven thousand at LUMEN, five thousand at ARCOS. Bottom altitude: four thousand.

You respond:

Descend via STAR, IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, except maintain four thousand, ANAC123.

You begin descent through the STAR altitude profile — flight level nine zero at IDAMO, seven thousand at LUMEN, five thousand at ARCOS, leveling at four thousand.

No intermediate altitude calls to ATC. The STAR profile was your clearance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Descend Via STAR is the complete arrival package.

Route: the STAR.

Altitude: the STAR restrictions, plus the assigned bottom altitude.

Speed: the STAR constraints.

All three. One clearance.

When you hear "descend via STAR" — every altitude restriction on that STAR plate is now an active clearance.

You do not need to call ATC for each step. You descend through them.

The trap that catches pilots: receiving "descend via STAR" and still waiting for individual descent clearances at each fix.

Or worse: receiving a standard STAR clearance and descending through published restrictions as if Descend Via was issued.

In Brazilian airspace, controllers at Florianópolis, Curitiba, and São Paulo increasingly use Descend Via STAR to streamline busy arrival flows. Know the phrase. Know the responsibility it carries.

In the H130, brief every altitude restriction on the STAR before top of descent. With Descend Via clearance active, they are your authorized altitude targets.

If you cannot comply with any restriction — advise ATC immediately.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Explain what Descend Via STAR authorizes and how it differs from a standard STAR clearance.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A standard STAR clearance authorizes the lateral routing of the Standard Terminal Arrival Route. The crew follows the STAR route but maintains the ATC-assigned altitude and waits for sequential descent instructions. The published altitude restrictions on the STAR are not active unless separately cleared. Descend Via STAR is a combined clearance authorizing both the lateral routing and the published altitude profile simultaneously. The crew descends through the STAR's altitude restrictions at each fix without requiring individual altitude clearances. Often a bottom altitude is specified: for example, descend via STAR except maintain four thousand means the crew follows the STAR altitude profile down to four thousand and levels off there. The crew is responsible for complying with every published restriction between top of descent and that bottom altitude.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Descending through STAR altitude restrictions after receiving a standard STAR clearance.  
  ✔ Standard STAR clearance: follow lateral routing and maintain ATC-assigned altitude only. Descend Via is required for the profile.

- ❌ Waiting for individual altitude clearances after receiving Descend Via STAR.  
  ✔ Descend Via STAR authorizes the entire published altitude profile. Descend through it.

- ❌ Not reading back the bottom altitude exception in the Descend Via clearance.  
  ✔ Always read back the exception: descend via STAR except maintain four thousand.

- ❌ Not briefing all STAR altitude restrictions before top of descent.  
  ✔ With Descend Via active, every published restriction is a target. Brief them all before beginning descent.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Descend Via STAR Except Maintain Four Thousand

**Pronunciation:** deh-SEND VY-uh STAR eks-EPT men-TAYN for THOW-zund

**Word Stress**

- Descend → deh-SEND (stress on second syllable)
- Via → VY-uh (two syllables)
- STAR → STAR (one word, not spelled out)
- Except → eks-EPT
- Maintain → men-TAYN
- Four Thousand → for THOW-zund

Practice:

Descend via STAR... except maintain... four thousand.

Together:

Descend via STAR except maintain four thousand, IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, ANAC123.

STAR is spoken as one word — not S-T-A-R — in operational phraseology.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Florianópolis Approach transmits: "ANAC123, descend via STAR, IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, except maintain four thousand."

The IDAMO ONE STAR has altitude restrictions: flight level nine zero at IDAMO, seven thousand at LUMEN, five thousand at ARCOS.

**Your first task**

Read back the complete clearance.

**Your second task**

Passing through seven thousand approaching LUMEN, strong headwinds reduce your descent rate significantly. You calculate you will cross ARCOS above five thousand. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would respond: Descend via STAR, IDAMO ONE arrival, runway one four, except maintain four thousand, ANAC123. If I could not meet the five thousand restriction at ARCOS due to reduced descent performance, I would advise ATC as soon as I recognized the issue: ANAC123, unable to meet ARCOS restriction, estimating crossing at six thousand, request amended clearance. I must not continue descending via STAR while knowingly unable to comply with a published restriction. ATC must be informed so separation with other traffic can be maintained.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Descend Via STAR gives you both the road and the altitude profile in one clearance.

That is more authority — and more responsibility.

Brief every restriction before top of descent. Know each target altitude at each fix.

Descend through the profile without waiting for individual clearances.

But if you cannot meet a restriction — say so. Immediately.

That honest communication is what keeps separation intact.

One clearance. Full responsibility. Professional execution.
