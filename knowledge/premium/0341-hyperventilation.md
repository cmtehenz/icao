# Hyperventilation

**Catalog ID:** 0341  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Hyperventilation — rapid or deep breathing that reduces carbon dioxide in the blood, often triggered by stress, anxiety, or fear in flight, and easily confused with hypoxia.

ICAO human-factors English expects you to distinguish hyperventilation from hypoxia, manage the crew or passenger, and communicate only when the situation affects safety or requires assistance.

For H130 ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, hyperventilation may appear in anxious passengers, first-time helicopter travelers, or a stressed crew member in a high-workload event. Mislabeling it as hypoxia can drive the wrong checklist. Correct recognition keeps the flight safe and the radio accurate.

Master the concept, the differential, and the calm operational language.

## 📖 Meaning

Hyperventilation is breathing at an abnormally rapid or deep rate, usually from anxiety or stress, causing dizziness, tingling, and a feeling of breathlessness that can mimic hypoxia but is managed primarily by calming breathing rather than emergency oxygen descent alone.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Hiperventilação / Hyperventilation.

Respiração anormalmente rápida ou profunda, geralmente por ansiedade ou estresse, causando tontura, formigamento e sensação de falta de ar que pode imitar hipóxia, mas é tratada principalmente acalmando a respiração — não apenas com descida de emergência por oxigênio.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When hyperventilation is suspected, the crew should:

- ensure aircraft control and a safe flight path first
- distinguish from hypoxia: if altitude/cabin pressure risk exists, treat as hypoxia until proven otherwise
- coach slow breathing; calm the passenger or crew member
- use oxygen only as procedure and situation require — do not skip hypoxia protection at altitude
- declare urgency only if the person becomes incapacitated or the flight is affected

Hyperventilation reporting commonly includes:

- passenger hyperventilating / anxiety attack
- crew member recovering, flight continuing
- request medical assistance if unresolved or passenger requests care on landing
- no emergency if fully resolved and safety unaffected

**Important:** At altitudes where hypoxia is possible, never assume "only hyperventilation." Protect oxygen and altitude first, then reassess.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers may become involved when hyperventilation reports to:

- understand whether an emergency descent or diversion is required
- distinguish a medical urgency from a resolved passenger anxiety event
- coordinate medical assistance if requested on landing
- avoid unnecessary traffic disruption when the flight can continue safely
- obtain clear status: continuing / diverting / assistance required

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

A passenger on a coastal H130 charter becomes anxious in turbulence, breathes rapidly, and reports tingling fingers.

You confirm you are at a safe low altitude with no cabin altitude threat. Cabin coaches slow breathing. Symptoms ease.

No urgency call is required. You continue. If symptoms persist or the passenger requests care, you may advise ATC you will need medical assistance on arrival — without declaring distress.

If the same symptoms appeared after a depressurization warning at high altitude, you would treat hypoxia first.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger, confirm if you are declaring an emergency.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say if medical assistance required on landing.
3. Tower: ANAC123, ambulances can be arranged, advise when ready.
4. Control: ANAC123, report if diverting or continuing to destination.
5. Tower: ANAC123, roger, no emergency, medical assistance noted for arrival.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, passenger hyperventilating, situation under control, continuing, no emergency.
2. ANAC123, passenger anxiety, request medical assistance on landing, not declaring emergency.
3. Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, passenger hyperventilation unresolved, request priority landing and medical assistance.
4. ANAC123, crew member hyperventilation resolved, flight normal, continuing.
5. ANAC123, negative emergency, medical assistance only on arrival, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Hyperventilation and hypoxia can feel similar. The altitude context decides your first move.

High cabin altitude or depressurization cues: oxygen and descend. Treat hypoxia.

Low-altitude anxiety with rapid breathing and tingling: slow the breathing, calm the person, reassess.

Brazilian pilots may say "crise de ansiedade" or "respirando demais." Prefer hyperventilation or passenger hyperventilating for ICAO English.

Do not declare Mayday for a resolved anxiety episode. Do declare urgency if the person becomes incapacitated or you must divert.

In the H130, a panicked passenger can distract the crew. Assign cabin management. You fly. Speak to ATC only with a clear status: continuing, diverting, or medical assistance on arrival.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How do you distinguish hyperventilation from hypoxia, and what might you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If there is any risk of hypoxia from altitude or depressurization, I would treat it as hypoxia with oxygen and descent first. If we are at a safe low altitude and symptoms fit anxiety-related hyperventilation, I would calm breathing and reassess. I would tell ATC only if we need medical assistance or a diversion — for example, passenger hyperventilating, request medical assistance on landing, no emergency, or Pan Pan if the situation is unresolved and urgent.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Assuming every tingling sensation is only anxiety at high altitude.  
  ✔ Treat possible hypoxia first when altitude risk exists.

- ❌ Declaring Mayday for a brief, resolved passenger anxiety episode.  
  ✔ Use no emergency / medical assistance on arrival when appropriate.

- ❌ Using only "ansiedade" without clear operational status.  
  ✔ Say hyperventilation and whether you are continuing or diverting.

- ❌ Ignoring a hyperventilating crew member who cannot perform duties.  
  ✔ If incapacitation occurs, handle as a crew medical/incapacitation event.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

At low altitude a passenger hyperventilates after turbulence. Symptoms begin to improve with coaching. The passenger still wants a doctor on arrival.

**Your first task**

What do you tell Approach if you are not declaring an emergency?

**Your second task**

If the passenger later becomes unresponsive, how does your call change?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, passenger hyperventilating, situation improving, continuing, request medical assistance on landing, no emergency. Second task: Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical emergency, passenger unconscious after hyperventilation, request priority landing and medical assistance. I would escalate when consciousness is lost.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Hyperventilation is a human-factors problem that demands differential thinking.

Protect against hypoxia when altitude requires it. Calm breathing when anxiety is the cause. Speak to ATC with accurate status — not drama, not silence when help is needed.

In Brazilian H130 operations, anxious passengers are common. Professional crews manage the cabin and keep the frequency clean.

Recognize. Differentiate. Communicate only what operations require.
