# Effective Translational Lift

**Catalog ID:** 0423  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Effective Translational Lift — ETL — the speed range, typically near sixteen to twenty-four knots, where the rotor fully outruns its vortices and lift efficiency increases markedly, often felt as a buffet and a clear performance improvement.

ETL is the practical milestone inside translational lift. Pilots use it to judge when climb can begin safely after takeoff and when approach deceleration will lose that efficiency again.

For H130 operations in Brazil — short pads, obstacle-rich city departures, and training circuits — you must communicate departure and approach plans that respect ETL, especially when power limited.

Understanding how to discuss Effective Translational Lift in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Effective Translational Lift is the flight condition at which the helicopter has accelerated sufficiently for the rotor system to leave its wingtip vortices and operate in cleaner horizontal airflow, producing a significant increase in lift efficiency, commonly occurring around sixteen to twenty-four knots depending on type and conditions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Sustentação translacional efetiva (ETL).

Condição de voo em que o helicóptero acelerou o suficiente para o sistema de rotor deixar seus vórtices de ponta e operar em fluxo de ar horizontal mais limpo, produzindo aumento significativo da eficiência de sustentação, comumente por volta de dezesseis a vinte e quatro nós, conforme o tipo e as condições.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing ETL, the pilot should:

- accelerate to ETL before committing to a steep climb on power-limited departures
- anticipate increased power demand as speed decreases below ETL on approach
- use wind to reach ETL earlier on departure
- explain slow initial climb or need for runway distance when ETL is delayed
- avoid obstacle climb profiles that assume ETL before it exists

ETL-related reporting commonly includes:

- accelerating, then climbing
- request into-wind departure
- unable immediate climb gradient
- requesting more departure distance

**Important:** You do not need to say "ETL" to ATC. You need to request the profile that lets you reach it safely — into wind, accelerate then climb, or runway departure.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support ETL-aware operations by:

- approving into-wind takeoffs
- allowing level acceleration segments when traffic permits
- providing obstacle and traffic information on the departure path
- accepting runway use for helicopters that cannot climb vertically
- not rushing a climb clearance that the aircraft cannot meet yet

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Departure from Santos Dumont in your H130, warm day, toward the bay. Obstacles and traffic demand a clean climb — but only after ETL.

Tower, request departure over the water, will accelerate to translational lift then climb.

You feel the buffet, performance improves, then you climb. The call was about the profile; the aerodynamics did the rest.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, departure over the water approved, wind one four zero at one zero, cleared for takeoff.
2. Tower: ANAC123, report airborne, climb when able.
3. Approach: ANAC123, maintain present altitude until advised, traffic ahead.
4. Tower: ANAC123, runway departure approved, air taxi as required.
5. Tower: ANAC123, caution obstacles north of the pad, advise intentions.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, over the water, cleared for takeoff, accelerate then climb.
2. ANAC123, airborne, climbing when able.
3. ANAC123, maintaining altitude, looking for traffic.
4. ANAC123, runway departure, air taxiing.
5. ANAC123, wilco, departing south to avoid obstacles, then climbing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

ETL is a speed you feel and a profile you negotiate.

The buffet and the sudden "more aircraft" sensation mark the efficiency gain. Until then, treat climb demands with suspicion on hot, heavy days.

In the H130 around Rio and São Paulo, water and runway options often beat a rooftop vertical. Ask for them. Part 4 English is calm negotiation: accelerate then climb, unable immediate gradient.

On approach, remember the reverse: below ETL, power required rises. Do not decelerate early over the city and discover you have no margin left for the last hundred feet.

Train students to name ETL in the briefing — and to translate it into simple ATC requests in the air.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Why might you tell ATC you will accelerate before climbing after takeoff?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Because I need to reach effective translational lift for better rotor efficiency before a steep climb. I would say: Will accelerate then climb, or request into-wind departure. That keeps the departure within power margins, especially when heavy or hot.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Climbing immediately at high pitch before ETL on a heavy departure.  
  ✔ Accelerate to ETL, then climb when performance appears.

- ❌ Saying nothing when you cannot meet an immediate climb clearance.  
  ✔ State unable, request accelerate-then-climb or alternate path.

- ❌ Decelerating below ETL too early on a steep city approach.  
  ✔ Keep speed until the landing site is assured and power margin exists.

- ❌ Using only Portuguese training slang on a mixed frequency.  
  ✔ Use clear ICAO English for the departure request.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Will Accelerate Then Climb

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, wil ak-SEL-uh-rayt then KLYM

**Word Stress**

- Accelerate → ak-SEL-uh-rayt (stress on second)
- Climb → KLYM

Practice:

Will accelerate... then climb.

Together:

Request into wind, will accelerate then climb.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**ETL** — **E**accelerate first, **T**hen climb, **L**imit steep attitudes before the buffet/gain.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Warm day at Santos Dumont. Heavy H130. Tower wants an immediate steep climb for traffic.

**Your first task**

What departure request do you make first?

**Your second task**

Tower insists on immediate climb. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, request departure over the water into wind, will accelerate then climb. Second task: ANAC123, unable immediate steep climb, power limited until translational lift, request short delay or alternate path — never accept a climb gradient you cannot meet before ETL.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Effective Translational Lift is the speed where the helicopter starts working with you.

Brief it. Feel it. Request the profile that reaches it safely.

In the H130 on Brazilian coastal and city pads, accelerate-then-climb is often the professional answer.

Do not climb on hope. Climb on ETL — and say so in plain ATC English.
