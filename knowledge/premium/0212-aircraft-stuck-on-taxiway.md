# Aircraft Stuck on Taxiway

**Catalog ID:** 0212  
**Category:** Ground Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2, Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Aircraft Stuck on Taxiway — the abnormal ground situation where an aircraft cannot continue taxi due to nose-gear issues, soft ground, brake problems, tug failure, or other immobilization on the movement area.

A stuck aircraft blocks routes, delays departures and arrivals, and may require maintenance, towing, or runway changes. Pilots must report the situation clearly, keep passengers and crew safe, and help ATC build a new surface plan.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, becoming "stuck" on a paved taxiway is less common than for airplanes, but soft shoulders, muddy helipad edges, or a technical inability to hover taxi still create equivalent blockages. ICAO exams test the English. Use H130 context notes while learning the standard phraseology.

Understanding how to declare, describe, and resolve an aircraft stuck on the taxiway supports Part 2 radiotelephony and Part 3 speaking scenarios.

## 📖 Meaning

Aircraft stuck on taxiway describes an aircraft that is immobilized on a taxiway or intersection and unable to continue under its own power or normal taxi procedures, requiring assistance, towing, or an alternate routing for other traffic.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aeronave parada / imobilizada na taxiway / aircraft stuck on taxiway.

Aeronave imobilizada em uma taxiway ou interseção, incapaz de continuar com potência própria ou procedimentos normais de táxi, exigindo assistência, reboque ou roteamento alternativo para o demais tráfego.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an aircraft becomes stuck on a taxiway, the crew should:

- stop further attempts that could damage gear or surface
- advise ATC immediately: position, inability to taxi, and any hazard
- set brakes as appropriate and secure the aircraft
- request tug, maintenance, or passenger assistance as needed
- follow ATC instructions for lights, evacuation, or stay-on-board
- monitor frequency for revised clearances to other aircraft

Stuck-on-taxiway handling commonly includes:

- reporting unable to taxi, requesting assistance
- ATC closing or restricting the taxiway
- towing the disabled aircraft clear
- rerouting other traffic via alternate taxiways
- delaying departures or changing runway in use

**Important:** Do not hide the problem hoping you can "rock free." Early notification lets Ground protect the runway sequence. In the H130, if you cannot lift to hover taxi due to a technical fault while on a taxiway, say unable to hover taxi, aircraft immobilized, request assistance — same operational idea as stuck.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers manage stuck aircraft to:

- protect other traffic from a blocked taxiway
- organize tug and maintenance response
- re-route departures and arrivals quickly
- decide whether runway operations can continue
- coordinate fire service if the immobilization involves risk

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

A light twin ahead of you on taxiway Bravo at Congonhas reports unable to taxi — nose wheel steering failure. The aircraft is stuck on the taxiway short of the holding point.

Ground stops your H130 hover taxi, tells you to hold on Alpha, and advises that Bravo is blocked by a disabled aircraft.

Maintenance and a tug are dispatched. Other departures are rerouted via Charlie. Arrivals continue on the runway because the stuck aircraft is clear of the strip.

You hold, manage fuel and rotor time, and wait for a new clearance. When Bravo is cleared, Ground reopens the route.

If it had been your H130 unable to move from a taxi position, you would have made the same style of call: position, nature of problem, request assistance — without blocking the frequency with panic.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, hold position, taxiway Bravo blocked, aircraft stuck, expect reroute via Charlie.
2. Ground: ANAC123, confirm able to taxi via Charlie, Bravo closed disabled aircraft.
3. Tower: ANAC123, expect departure delay, aircraft immobilized on taxiway Alpha.
4. Ground: ANAC123, tug proceeding to the stuck aircraft, remain clear of Bravo.
5. Ground: ANAC123, Bravo reopened, continue taxi via Bravo to holding point.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, holding position, expect reroute via Charlie.
2. ANAC123, able to taxi via Charlie, Bravo closed.
3. ANAC123, roger, departure delay, aircraft immobilized on Alpha.
4. ANAC123, remaining clear of Bravo, tug in sight.
5. ANAC123, Bravo reopened, taxi via Bravo to holding point.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Stuck is a status report, not a shame report.

Say where you are. Say you cannot move. Say what you need. Then stop making it worse.

Part 3 speaking questions often ask what you would do if an aircraft is stuck ahead or if you become unable to taxi. Structure the answer: safety, communication, assistance, alternate plan.

In the H130, immobilization may look different — unable to rise to hover, hydraulic caution, or soft ground at a pad edge. The English pattern is the same: unable to continue, position, request assistance.

While you wait as the other aircraft, manage time: fuel, rotor RPM policy, passenger briefing, and readiness for a sudden reroute clearance.

Never enter a taxiway ATC has closed for a disabled aircraft just because it "looks empty."

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

An aircraft ahead of you becomes stuck on the taxiway and blocks your route. Describe your actions and what you expect from ATC.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would hold position as instructed and keep clear of the blocked taxiway. I would expect Ground to advise that the taxiway is closed, to send assistance to the immobilized aircraft, and to issue an alternate taxi route if available. I would read back any reroute clearance carefully and continue only on the new route. If a long delay is expected, I would manage fuel and, if necessary, request return to parking. I would not attempt to pass the stuck aircraft without clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Delaying the call hoping the aircraft will free itself.  
  ✔ Report unable to taxi immediately with position and assistance needed.

- ❌ Using only Portuguese on the frequency during the emergency ground stop.  
  ✔ Use clear ICAO English: unable to taxi, immobilized, request tug.

- ❌ Following the original route into a closed taxiway.  
  ✔ Wait for reroute clearance; closed means closed.

- ❌ Shutting down communications while stuck.  
  ✔ Stay on frequency for tug coordination and further instructions.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Unable to Taxi, Request Assistance on Bravo

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, un-AY-bul too TAK-see, reh-KWEST uh-SIS-tans on BRAH-voh

**Word Stress**

- Unable → un-AY-bul (stress on second)
- Taxi → TAK-see
- Assistance → uh-SIS-tans (stress on second)
- Bravo → BRAH-voh

Practice:

Unable to taxi... request assistance on Bravo.

Together:

ANAC123, on taxiway Bravo short of holding point, unable to taxi, request assistance.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**STUCK** — **S**top damaging attempts, **T**ell ATC position and problem, **U**se lights and secure aircraft, **C**all for tug or maintenance, **K**eep frequency open for reroutes.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are hover taxiing behind a trainer on Bravo at Florianópolis. The trainer reports stuck with a flat nose wheel tire. Ground holds you on Alpha.

**Your first task**

Ground says: "ANAC123, hold on Alpha, Bravo closed, aircraft stuck, expect taxi via Charlie." What do you reply?

**Your second task**

In a Part 3 style answer, explain in plain English what you would do during a long delay while the stuck aircraft is removed.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, holding on Alpha, expect taxi via Charlie. Second task: I would hold clear, monitor Ground for the reroute, manage fuel and rotor time according to SOP, brief any passengers or students about the delay, and if the delay becomes excessive I would request return to parking. I would not enter Bravo until it is reopened.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Aircraft stuck on taxiway turns one problem into a surface puzzle for everyone.

If it is you — speak early, stop digging, request help. If it is them — hold clear, accept the reroute, manage the wait.

H130 crews learn the English even when pavement stuck events are rare. Soft ground and technical immobilizations still block pads and routes.

Clear status. Clear position. Clear request.

That is how Brazilian aerodromes recover flow after a taxiway blockage — and how you score clean Part 2 and Part 3 answers.
