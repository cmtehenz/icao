# Rudder Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0272  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Rudder Failure — loss or restriction of rudder control that degrades yaw authority, affecting directional control in flight and on the runway, requiring abnormal procedures and clear urgency communication with ATC.

The rudder controls yaw. Failure or jam can mean limited ability to coordinate turns, counter engine asymmetry on multi-engine types, or keep the nose straight at touchdown and during rollout. Crosswind landings become especially demanding.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, yaw is primarily anti-torque pedals, not a conventional rudder surface. That analogy helps: degraded pedal authority equals degraded yaw control. Do not invent a fixed-wing rudder on the H130 — teach honest systems and strong ICAO English for exams.

Part 2 and Part 3: rudder failure, limited yaw control, request priority and a runway aligned with the wind when possible.

## 📖 Meaning

Rudder Failure is a malfunction in which the rudder jams, disconnects, or otherwise fails to provide normal yaw control, reducing directional control in flight and during landing rollout, and requiring abnormal procedures and ATC notification for priority handling and the most suitable runway and wind conditions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the rudder fails, the crew should:

- maintain directional control with remaining methods per AFM — aileron, differential thrust, nosewheel steering as applicable
- apply the rudder failure / jam checklist
- avoid strong crosswind runways when a better-aligned runway exists
- advise ATC of rudder failure and limited yaw or directional control
- request priority landing and the runway with the most favorable wind
- H130 analogy: treat as degraded anti-torque pedal authority for communication logic only

Rudder failure scenarios commonly include:

- rudder jammed neutral — limited yaw, careful coordination
- rudder jammed deflected — continuous yaw requiring opposite control
- crosswind landing with no rudder authority
- multi-engine asymmetry without rudder to counter
- exam call: rudder failure, request runway into wind

**Important:** Do not accept a significant crosswind landing if rudder authority is gone and another runway or airport offers better alignment. Tell ATC you require a runway into wind or minimum crosswind.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use rudder failure reports to:

- assign the runway with the least crosswind component
- provide priority and a long straight-in
- avoid late sidestep or circling that demands yaw changes
- alert emergency services for a possible directional-control problem on rollout
- coordinate ground assistance after landing if the aircraft cannot taxi normally

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in a fixed-wing training scenario approaching Congonhas. Rudder feel goes dead; yaw response is almost nil. Wind favors runway one seven, but two seven Left has a strong crosswind.

You call:

ANAC123, rudder failure, limited yaw control, request priority landing runway one seven, minimum crosswind.

Approach: ANAC123, roger rudder failure, expect runway one seven, priority approved, emergency services advised.

On short final:

ANAC123, runway one seven, rudder failure, request assistance after landing if unable to vacate.

You choose the wind. You protect the rollout.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Rudder failure is a yaw and directional-control problem — in the air and on the ground.

Pick the runway that reduces crosswind. A perfect ILS into a strong crosswind with no rudder is a poor trade.

On frequency, rudder failure, limited yaw control, request runway into wind is clearer than "steering problem."

In the H130, pedals fight torque and control yaw. If pedal authority were degraded, you would use the same honesty: limited yaw control, priority, suitable landing site. Keep the hardware story truthful for the helicopter; keep the English strong for the exam.

After landing, say if you need a tow. Do not force a high-speed taxi with no directional control.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Rudder is jammed and crosswind on the active runway is strong. What do you request?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would advise ATC of rudder failure with limited yaw control and request priority landing on the runway with the minimum crosswind, or diversion if no suitable runway is available. I would plan a straight-in approach, avoid maneuvers that demand large yaw inputs, and advise after landing if I need a tow or cannot vacate normally.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting a strong crosswind runway with failed rudder.  
  ✔ Request runway into wind or minimum crosswind.

- ❌ Saying only "leme" without English on an ICAO test frequency.  
  ✔ Use rudder failure / limited yaw control.

- ❌ Planning a circling sidestep that needs aggressive yaw.  
  ✔ Request straight-in to the favorable runway.

- ❌ Forcing taxi after landing with no directional control.  
  ✔ Stop straight ahead if needed; request tow.
