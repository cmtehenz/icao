# Search and Rescue

**Catalog ID:** 0405  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Search and Rescue — SAR helicopter operations to locate and assist aircraft, vessels, or persons in distress, often in coordination with a Rescue Coordination Centre.

SAR is highly relevant H130 work in Brazil: coastal searches off Florianópolis and Santos, jungle and highland searches, and support to distressed vessels or overdue aircraft.

You must manage position reporting, search patterns, urgency communications, and coordination with ATC and RCC in ICAO-standard English for Parts 2, 3, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Search and Rescue operations are coordinated missions to locate and assist persons in distress, using aircraft — frequently helicopters — to search defined areas, communicate findings, and support recovery or medical evacuation under RCC and ATC coordination.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Busca e salvamento (SAR).

Operações coordenadas para localizar e prestar assistência a pessoas em perigo, utilizando aeronaves — frequentemente helicópteros — para vasculhar áreas definidas, comunicar achados e apoiar recuperação ou evacuação médica sob coordenação do RCC e do ATC.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting SAR operations, the pilot should:

- check in with ATC and state SAR mission status
- maintain scheduled position reports, especially offshore or remote
- report sightings immediately with position, description, and intentions
- coordinate pattern altitude and area with ATC to deconflict traffic
- declare or relay distress updates as the situation develops

SAR reporting commonly includes:

- SAR mission, on station
- commencing search pattern
- sighting, position, descending to investigate
- survivor located, requesting further assets

**Important:** Accurate position is the product. A vague sighting wastes the next asset. Use lat/long, radial/DME, or clear geographic reference.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support SAR by:

- reserving altitude blocks and search areas
- relaying communications with RCC
- providing radar assistance when coverage exists
- keeping unrelated traffic clear of the search box
- coordinating multiple SAR aircraft on frequency

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are fifty miles offshore from Florianópolis in your H130 on a SAR task for a overdue fishing vessel.

You transmit:

Florianópolis Control, ANAC123, SAR mission, on station five zero miles east, commencing expanding square search at five hundred feet, fuel state two hours three zero.

Later you sight debris:

ANAC123, sighting debris three two miles east of Florianópolis, descending to investigate, request traffic cleared.

Control notifies RCC and clears the area.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger SAR, report on station and fuel state.
2. Control: ANAC123, search block approved five hundred to one thousand, remain clear of airway.
3. Control: ANAC123, RCC requests your present position and search progress.
4. Control: ANAC123, roger sighting, rescue vessel advised, say exact position.
5. Control: ANAC123, additional SAR helicopter inbound, advise operating altitude.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, SAR on station, fuel two hours three zero, commencing search.
2. ANAC123, search five hundred to one thousand, remaining clear of airway.
3. ANAC123, position five zero miles east, search fifty percent complete.
4. ANAC123, sighting confirmed, position three two miles east, investigating.
5. ANAC123, operating five hundred feet, advise inbound traffic.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

SAR English is precision under fatigue.

You will be low, slow, and staring at water or canopy for hours. The radio still needs crisp position and status.

On station. Fuel state. Pattern. Sighting. Those four updates keep RCC effective.

In the H130, endurance and hover capability make you the recovery platform — but only if you leave fuel to return. Report fuel early. Do not let heroics create a second SAR case.

Offshore Brazil: no radar, no forced landing area. Position reports are your lifeline. Miss a report and ATC starts a search for you.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are assigned a coastal SAR search. What information do you give ATC when on station?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report mission status, position, search altitude or pattern, and fuel state: ANAC123, SAR mission, on station five zero miles east of Florianópolis, commencing expanding square at five hundred feet, fuel state two hours three zero. When I have a sighting, I would give an exact position and intentions to investigate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting "we see something" without a position.  
  ✔ Give position first, then description.

- ❌ Forgetting fuel state during long searches.  
  ✔ Include fuel in on-station and progress calls.

- ❌ Changing search altitude without advising ATC.  
  ✔ Coordinate the block so other assets stay clear.

- ❌ Going off frequency without telling Control.  
  ✔ Maintain the assigned frequency or arrange a relay.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Offshore SAR east of Florianópolis. You find a life raft with survivors.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit on first sighting?

**Your second task**

RCC asks if you can remain on scene. Fuel is one hour. Nearest land is forty minutes. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, sighting life raft, position three two miles east Florianópolis, survivors visible, descending to investigate, request traffic cleared. Second task: ANAC123, able to remain approximately two zero minutes, fuel one hour, then must return for reserve — request relief asset. Never create a second emergency.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

SAR succeeds on accurate positions and honest fuel.

Check in as SAR. Report on station. Call sightings with coordinates. Leave enough fuel to get home.

In the H130 off the Brazilian coast, you are often the best sensor in the system — and the most exposed.

Search smart. Speak precise. Save the mission and yourself.
