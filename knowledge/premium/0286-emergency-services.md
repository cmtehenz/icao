# Emergency Services

**Catalog ID:** 0286  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Emergency Services — the airport rescue and fire fighting (RFF), medical, and support units that ATC alerts when an aircraft declares an emergency, requests assistance, or reports a condition that may require ground response on landing. For H130 helicopter operations in Brazil, emergency services may meet you at a helipad, airport apron, or off-airport landing site coordinated through ATC and local responders.

Emergency services is a High Part 2 and Part 3 topic. Examiners expect you to request the right support clearly: firefighters, ambulance, or both — and to update ATC when the situation changes.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training, master the phraseology that gets RFF and medical assets positioned before you land, then transfer the same clarity to helicopter ops over cities, offshore, and remote terrain.

Understanding how to discuss emergency services in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Emergency Services are the rescue, fire fighting, medical, and related ground response units alerted by ATC or aerodrome authorities when an aircraft declares an emergency or requests assistance, positioned to support landing, evacuation, fire suppression, and medical care as required by the nature of the emergency.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Serviços de emergência / Emergency Services.

Unidades de resgate, combate a incêndio, atendimento médico e apoio em solo alertadas pelo ATC ou pela autoridade do aeródromo quando uma aeronave declara emergência ou solicita assistência, posicionadas para apoiar o pouso, a evacuação, a extinção de fogo e o atendimento médico conforme a natureza da emergência.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When emergency services are required, the crew should:

- declare the appropriate urgency (Mayday or Pan Pan) with nature of the problem
- request emergency services explicitly: firefighters, ambulance, or both
- state souls on board and fuel remaining when asked
- advise landing intentions and expected runway, helipad, or diversion airport
- update ATC if fire, smoke, injuries, or evacuation status changes after landing
- for H130: request services for helipad or off-airport sites when coordinated through ATC

Emergency services reporting commonly includes:

- request firefighters / ambulance / emergency services
- nature: fire, smoke, medical, hard landing, ditching, forced landing
- souls and fuel
- evacuation in progress / no evacuation required
- remaining on runway / taxiing clear / landed at helipad

**Important:** Do not assume ATC will alert RFF from a vague "we have a problem." Name the service you need. Firefighters and ambulance are different assets. Say what you need early so they are in position when you land.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers coordinate emergency services to:

- alert RFF and medical units at the correct category and location
- position vehicles before the aircraft lands
- keep runways, taxiways, and helipads clear for response
- pass souls, fuel, and nature of emergency to the airport authority
- coordinate with approach, tower, and ground during the arrival

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Congonhas with a cabin smoke smell after an electrical anomaly. You declare and request priority.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Approach, ANAC123, smoke in cabin, requesting priority landing and emergency services, firefighters and ambulance.

Approach confirms RFF alerted and vectors you for a short final. Tower advises emergency services are standing by on the runway.

After landing you stop, shut down as required, and report:

ANAC123, landed, smoke decreasing, no fire visible, remaining on runway, request ambulance for passenger check.

For H130 ops to a city helipad, the same structure applies — nature, request services, souls and fuel, update after landing.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Mayday, emergency services alerted, expect runway one seven left.
2. Tower: ANAC123, firefighters standing by, wind calm, cleared to land runway one seven left.
3. Tower: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
4. Ground: ANAC123, remain on runway, emergency services approaching from the left.
5. Tower: ANAC123, ambulance standing by at the helipad, report when shut down.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, runway one seven left, emergency services alerted, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, cleared to land one seven left, firefighters standing by.
3. ANAC123, souls on board eight, fuel remaining one hour.
4. ANAC123, remaining on runway, emergency services approaching.
5. ANAC123, wilco, will report when shut down, ambulance standing by.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Emergency services are not automatic just because you said Mayday.

Mayday gets priority. Naming firefighters, ambulance, or both gets the right trucks moving.

Be specific. Smoke without fire still often needs RFF. A medical passenger needs an ambulance. A hard landing may need both.

Give souls and fuel when asked — RFF plans foam and response around that information.

After landing, update. "No fire, remaining on runway" or "evacuating" changes what the trucks do next.

In the H130, you may land at a helipad or a field. Still request emergency services through ATC when you need them. Local responders still need a clear picture.

Request early. Update honestly. Do not leave the ground guessing.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You have smoke in the cabin and will land shortly. What do you request from ATC regarding ground support?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare the emergency, state the nature of the problem, and request priority landing with emergency services — specifically firefighters and an ambulance if passengers may need medical check. I would provide souls on board and fuel remaining when asked, and after landing I would update ATC whether there is fire, whether we are evacuating, and whether we remain on the runway or helipad.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "I need help on ground" without naming firefighters or ambulance.  
  ✔ Request emergency services and specify firefighters and/or ambulance.

- ❌ Assuming Mayday automatically positions RFF without stating the need.  
  ✔ Declare and request emergency services explicitly.

- ❌ Not updating after landing when smoke stops or evacuation starts.  
  ✔ Report status: no fire, evacuating, or remaining on runway.

- ❌ Forgetting souls and fuel when ATC asks.  
  ✔ Answer immediately with numbers.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**SERVICES** — **S**tate the emergency, **E**xplicitly name firefighters/ambulance, **R**eport souls and fuel, **V**ector for priority landing, **I**nform after touchdown, **C**onfirm fire or no fire, **E**vacuate if required, **S**tay on frequency until released.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Cabin smoke on approach to Congonhas. You need RFF and medical standing by.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit on first contact with Approach?

**Your second task**

After landing there is no fire. One passenger feels unwell. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday Mayday Mayday, Approach, ANAC123, smoke in cabin, requesting priority landing and emergency services, firefighters and ambulance. Second task: ANAC123, landed, no fire visible, remaining on runway, request ambulance for one passenger. I would keep the frequency clear and follow checklist for shutdown and possible evacuation if conditions change.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Emergency services turn a declared emergency into a prepared arrival.

Name what you need. Firefighters. Ambulance. Both.

Give souls and fuel. Update after landing.

In Brazilian H130 operations — airport, helipad, or coordinated off-airport — the English is the same: clear request, clear status, clear next action.

That is how emergency services get to you in time — because you asked for them in ICAO English that ATC can act on.
