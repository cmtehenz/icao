# Heart Attack

**Catalog ID:** 0329  
**Category:** Passenger  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Heart Attack — a critical in-flight medical emergency in which a passenger shows signs of a possible cardiac event and requires immediate assistance and expedited landing.

Heart Attack is Critical priority. Time matters. ICAO exams expect decisive English: medical emergency, possible heart attack, priority landing, medical assistance, and Pan Pan or Mayday as appropriate to severity.

For H130 operations with few or no cabin crew, the pilot must protect aircraft control while coordinating passenger help and ATC. Hospital helipad diversions are common in Brazilian helicopter work — know how to request them clearly.

Understanding how to communicate a heart attack scenario in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Heart Attack, in aviation communication, refers to a suspected cardiac medical emergency on board in which a passenger experiences severe chest pain or related symptoms requiring urgent medical assistance and usually priority landing or diversion.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Ataque cardíaco / Heart Attack.

Emergência médica cardíaca suspeita a bordo, em que um passageiro apresenta dor intensa no peito ou sintomas relacionados, exigindo assistência médica urgente e geralmente pouso prioritário ou desvio.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a suspected heart attack occurs, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control and stable flight path
- provide or direct first aid within training and available equipment
- declare urgency or distress as appropriate
- request priority landing or diversion to the nearest suitable airport or hospital helipad
- request medical assistance and give souls on board when asked

Heart Attack communication commonly includes:

- medical emergency on board, possible heart attack
- passenger with severe chest pain
- request priority landing and ambulance
- diversion to nearest suitable airport

**Important:** Pilots are not expected to confirm a clinical diagnosis on frequency. "Possible heart attack" or "passenger with severe chest pain" is professional. On the H130, prefer a hospital-capable landing site when range and weather allow. Do not delay the radio call while searching for perfect medical wording.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to cardiac medical emergencies to:

- clear traffic for priority approach and landing
- coordinate the nearest suitable airport or hospital helipad
- alert ambulance and emergency medical services
- request nature of emergency and souls on board
- provide vectors and short routing to minimize time to landing

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are twenty-five minutes from Congonhas in the H130 with four passengers and no cabin crew.

A passenger clutches his chest, pale and in severe pain. Another passenger says it may be a heart attack.

You keep the helicopter stable and transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical emergency on board, possible heart attack, request priority landing and medical assistance.

Approach clears traffic and vectors you for the shortest safe approach. Ambulance meets you on the apron. Minutes mattered; clear English bought those minutes.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
2. Approach: ANAC123, priority vectors, turn left heading three four zero, nearest suitable is Congonhas.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway one seven right cleared to land, medical services standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm possible heart attack, ambulance alerted.
5. Control: ANAC123, hospital helipad available if required, say intentions.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical emergency, possible heart attack, request priority landing.
2. ANAC123, passenger with severe chest pain, request medical assistance and priority approach.
3. Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, medical emergency, passenger possible heart attack, require immediate landing.
4. ANAC123, diverting to Congonhas, souls on board five, medical emergency.
5. ANAC123, request hospital helipad if available, possible heart attack on board.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Cardiac events are among the most time-critical passenger emergencies in the exam and in real life.

Use urgency language early. Pan Pan is the normal start for a serious medical emergency still under control. Escalate to Mayday if the passenger becomes unresponsive and you need immediate absolute priority.

Say possible heart attack or severe chest pain. Do not freeze because you are not a doctor.

In the H130, think landing site: airport with ambulance versus hospital helipad. Ask ATC what is available. Brazilian controllers can coordinate both.

Fly the machine. Shorten the flight. Speak with precision.

That is Critical-priority communication.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What should you say to ATC if a passenger may be having a heart attack?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare Pan Pan, state that there is a medical emergency on board with a possible heart attack or severe chest pain, and request priority landing and medical assistance. I would give souls on board when asked and consider Mayday if the passenger becomes critical and I require immediate landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Delaying the call while trying to confirm a medical diagnosis.  
  ✔ Report possible heart attack or severe chest pain and request help.

- ❌ Using only "passenger sick" for a critical cardiac event.  
  ✔ Use medical emergency and request priority landing.

- ❌ Forgetting to ask for medical services on the ground.  
  ✔ Always request ambulance or medical assistance on arrival.

- ❌ Leaving the controls unattended in single-pilot H130 ops.  
  ✔ Aviate first; direct another passenger to assist if available.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

A passenger has severe chest pain. He is conscious but worsening. Nearest suitable is fifteen minutes away.

**Your first task**

Transmit your urgency call.

**Your second task**

ATC offers Congonhas or a hospital helipad five minutes farther. What do you consider?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical emergency on board, possible heart attack, request priority landing and medical assistance. Second task: I choose the option that gets definitive medical care soonest with a safe approach — often the hospital helipad if weather and performance allow; otherwise the nearest airport with ambulance standing by. I state my intention clearly to ATC.
