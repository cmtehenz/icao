# SID Clearance

**Catalog ID:** 0153  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers SID Clearance — the ATC instruction authorizing a departing aircraft to fly a Standard Instrument Departure route from the departure runway.

The SID is the departure equivalent of the STAR. It codes the route from the runway into the en-route structure, providing lateral routing and, in some cases, altitude restrictions.

Brazilian airports including Congonhas, São Paulo, Curitiba, and Florianópolis have published SIDs that pilots must brief, load, and fly in IFR conditions.

Critically: receiving a SID clearance authorizes the lateral routing but does not automatically authorize the crew to climb to the SID's published altitude restrictions. That requires a separate altitude clearance — or a Climb Via SID instruction.

Understanding the distinction between SID Clearance and Climb Via SID is essential ICAO knowledge.

## 📖 Meaning

A SID Clearance is an ATC authorization for the departing aircraft to fly the Standard Instrument Departure procedure, following the published lateral routing from the runway to the en-route structure, subject to any altitude and speed instructions issued separately by ATC.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives a SID clearance, they should:

- confirm the SID name and transition in the clearance delivery readback
- load the SID into the FMS or GPS before departure
- brief the departure procedure: initial heading, turn points, altitude restrictions, and transition to en-route
- note the initial climb altitude assigned by ATC — this is the climb target, not the SID altitude restrictions
- after takeoff, follow the SID lateral routing
- climb to the ATC-assigned altitude, not the SID published altitudes, unless Climb Via SID has been issued

SID clearance operations commonly include:

- departure clearance received at the gate from Clearance Delivery
- SID name with runway and transition specified
- initial climb altitude assigned separately from the SID
- routing to the first en-route waypoint after the SID ends

**Important:** A SID clearance authorizes the lateral routing. It does not authorize climbing to SID published altitude restrictions. Only a Climb Via SID clearance provides authorization to follow both the lateral routing and the altitude restrictions simultaneously.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers assign SIDs to:

- standardize and simplify departure routing from complex terminal areas
- reduce clearance delivery radio time with coded routing designators
- coordinate departure traffic with terminal and en-route sectors
- separate departing traffic from arrivals using defined corridors
- optimize noise abatement and terrain clearance routes

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are departing Congonhas in your H130 bound for Curitiba on an IFR flight plan.

Clearance Delivery transmits:

ANAC123, cleared to Curitiba, BRASA THREE departure, runway one seven, squawk zero two three five, climb and maintain three thousand, expect flight level eight zero ten minutes after departure.

You brief the BRASA THREE SID with your co-pilot: the initial turn, the routing waypoints, the transition to the airway.

You respond:

Cleared to Curitiba, BRASA THREE departure, runway one seven, squawk zero two three five, climb and maintain three thousand, ANAC123.

You note: climb target is three thousand. Not the published SID altitudes. Three thousand.

After takeoff, you follow the BRASA THREE lateral routing. You climb to three thousand and level off awaiting further clearance.

ATC later clears you higher. The SID routing continues as filed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

There are two ways to depart on a SID.

The standard way: you fly the SID lateral routing and climb to your ATC-assigned altitude.

The Climb Via way: you fly the SID lateral routing AND comply with the published altitude restrictions.

Most pilots in Brazil encounter the standard SID clearance daily.

The SID gives you the road out of the terminal area.

ATC gives you the altitude ceiling for now.

That ceiling may be lower than any SID published altitude restriction — and that is intentional. ATC is managing separation below you.

Never climb above your assigned altitude based on the SID chart.

In the H130, loading the SID into the GPS means you have lateral guidance. But your climb target is always the ATC-assigned altitude until you are cleared higher.

Brief the SID fully: the initial turn direction, the first waypoint, any mandatory turn altitudes, and where the SID ends and the en-route begins.

A well-briefed SID departure is invisible — smooth, professional, efficient.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the difference between a SID Clearance and a Climb Via SID clearance?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A SID Clearance authorizes the crew to fly the Standard Instrument Departure lateral routing. The crew climbs to the altitude assigned by ATC in the clearance, not to the SID published altitude restrictions, unless specifically told otherwise. A Climb Via SID clearance authorizes the crew to follow both the lateral routing and the altitude restrictions as published on the SID chart simultaneously. It is a combined clearance for both routing and altitude profile. Without the Climb Via instruction, the crew must comply with ATC altitude assignments and may not use SID altitude restrictions as climb targets.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Climbing to SID published altitudes after receiving a standard SID clearance.  
  ✔ Climb to the ATC-assigned altitude only. Published SID altitudes require Climb Via.

- ❌ Not reading back the SID name and runway in the departure clearance.  
  ✔ Always include the SID designation: BRASA THREE departure, runway one seven.

- ❌ Forgetting to set the squawk code received in the departure clearance.  
  ✔ Read back and set the squawk code before departure: squawk zero two three five.

- ❌ Not loading the SID before takeoff.  
  ✔ Load and brief the SID during the gate phase — not after becoming airborne.

- BRASA → BRAH-za (two syllables, stress on first; Brazilian fix name)
- THREE → THREE
- Departure → deh-PAR-chur (stress on second syllable)

Practice:

BRASA... THREE... Departure.

Together:

Cleared to Curitiba, BRASA THREE departure, runway one seven, ANAC123.

Speak the SID name and number clearly — both elements must be unambiguous.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Congonhas Clearance transmits: "ANAC123, cleared to Curitiba via BRASA THREE departure, runway one seven, squawk zero two three five, climb and maintain three thousand, expect flight level eight zero ten minutes after departure."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete departure clearance.

**Your second task**

After takeoff, you notice the BRASA THREE SID chart shows an altitude restriction of five thousand at the first waypoint. May you climb to five thousand? Why or why not?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would respond: Cleared to Curitiba via BRASA THREE departure, runway one seven, squawk zero two three five, climb and maintain three thousand, ANAC123. After takeoff I may not climb to five thousand based on the SID restriction. My ATC-assigned altitude is three thousand. The published restriction on the SID applies only if ATC has issued a Climb Via SID clearance. Without that explicit instruction, I climb to three thousand and level off until ATC clears me higher.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

A SID clearance is your road out of the terminal area.

The lateral routing is yours to fly.

The altitude is what ATC gives you.

Those are two separate things.

Know the SID. Brief the SID. Load the SID.

But climb to ATC's altitude — not the chart's restrictions.

That precision is the difference between a professional IFR departure and a non-conformance report.
