# ILS Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0193  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers ILS Failure — loss of usable Instrument Landing System guidance, whether from airborne receiver failure, ground facility unserviceability, or flag indications on localizer or glideslope, requiring missed approach or transition to an alternate approach.

ILS failure on final is time-critical. In IMC, a flagged localizer or glideslope means you no longer have the precision path you were cleared to fly. The professional response is decisive: discontinue the approach if required, declare the failure, and accept an alternate.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations into Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Santos Dumont, ILS remains a primary precision approach option where published. Crews must communicate ILS failure clearly and manage the missed approach without hesitation.

Understanding ILS failure phraseology and ICAO speaking explanations supports Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

ILS Failure is the loss of valid localizer and/or glideslope guidance required for an Instrument Landing System approach, caused by airborne equipment fault, ground transmitter unserviceability, interference, or warning flags, such that the approach can no longer be continued using ILS guidance and an alternate course of action is required.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de ILS / ILS Failure.

Perda de orientação válida de localizador e/ou glide slope exigida para uma aproximação por Instrument Landing System, causada por falha do equipamento de bordo, inoperância do transmissor no solo, interferência ou bandeiras de aviso, de modo que a aproximação não pode mais continuar com orientação ILS e uma ação alternativa é necessária.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ILS failure occurs, the crew should:

- recognize localizer or glideslope flags, loss of ident, or unreliable raw data
- if in IMC and not visual, execute missed approach immediately unless another approved approach can be continued from a safe position
- advise ATC: missed approach, ILS failure (specify localizer or glideslope if useful)
- request an alternate approach: another ILS if available, VOR, RNAV, or visual
- comply with published missed approach track and altitude
- brief the next approach fully before accepting the subsequent clearance

ILS failure scenarios commonly include:

- glideslope failure with localizer still usable (may require change to localizer-only if published and authorized)
- localizer failure requiring full missed approach
- ground ILS NOTAMed unserviceable before approach clearance
- flight inspection or interference causing unreliable signals
- airborne receiver failure affecting one or both ILS channels

**Important:** Do not continue a precision approach in IMC on a flagged glideslope by "estimating" the path. In the H130, execute the missed approach, talk to ATC, and set up the alternate. If you become visual and company/regulatory rules allow a visual continuation from a safe position, that is a separate decision — never a silent improvisation on dead ILS guidance in cloud.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with ILS failure reports to:

- protect the missed approach path and climbing aircraft
- remove the aircraft from an unusable ILS and offer alternatives
- advise when the ground ILS is unserviceable
- resequence traffic for another runway or approach type
- coordinate go-arounds caused by airborne or ground ILS problems

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on ILS final into Guarulhos in IMC. At approximately one thousand feet AGL, the glideslope flags. Localizer remains, but you are not authorized for a localizer-only procedure on this approach today.

You call missed approach, ILS glideslope failure, and fly the published missed approach.

Approach: ANAC123, roger, climb via missed approach, fly heading three four zero, expect vectors for RNAV approach.

You climb, clean up, brief the RNAV, and come back for a stable second approach.

No attempt to dive for the runway on a dead glideslope. Clear declaration. Clean missed. Alternate plan.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger missed approach ILS failure, climb via missed approach, heading three four zero.
2. Tower: ANAC123, ILS unserviceable, expect RNAV or visual approach.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm glideslope failure or localizer failure.
4. Radar: ANAC123, radar vectors for ILS runway one zero Right, other ILS available.
5. Approach: ANAC123, unable ILS, cleared RNAV approach runway two seven Left.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

ILS failure on final is a go-around decision more often than a troubleshooting exercise.

Flags mean the precision path is gone. In IMC, missed approach is the default. Say why: ILS failure — glideslope or localizer if you can specify quickly.

Do not troubleshoot buttons while descending below a safe altitude on an invalid path. Fly the missed approach first. Talk second. Diagnose on the downwind or vectors.

If ATC already knows the ground ILS is out, do not accept an ILS clearance hoping it will work. Request the alternate from the start.

In Brazilian busy terminals — Guarulhos, Congonhas — a clean missed approach call helps ATC protect everyone. Be concise. Be early. Be decisive.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

On ILS final in IMC, the glideslope warning flag appears. Describe your actions.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would discontinue the precision approach and execute the missed approach immediately if I am not visual and cannot legally continue by another approved method. I would advise ATC that I am going missed due to ILS glideslope failure. I would fly the published missed approach track and altitude, then request an alternate approach such as another ILS, RNAV, or visual if conditions permit. I would fully brief the next approach before accepting the subsequent clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing below decision altitude on a flagged glideslope "to see the runway."  
  ✔ Go missed in IMC without valid guidance; do not push for a sighting.

- ❌ Staying silent during the missed approach.  
  ✔ Declare missed approach and ILS failure as soon as workload allows.

- ❌ Accepting the same ILS again without confirming the failure mode.  
  ✔ Confirm whether airborne or ground; request a different approach if the ILS is unreliable.

- ❌ Troubleshooting the receiver instead of flying the missed approach.  
  ✔ Aircraft control and missed approach path come first; diagnose after.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit while executing the missed approach?

**Your second task**

Approach offers vectors for the RNAV approach. What do you read back?

First task: ANAC123, missed approach, ILS localizer failure, climbing via missed approach. ## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

ILS failure demands decisiveness.

Flag in IMC means missed approach. Declare it. Fly the published path. Ask for the alternate.

Do not negotiate with a dead glideslope. Do not troubleshoot through decision altitude.

In H130 operations into Brazil's busiest airports, a clean go-around is professional — not a failure of skill.

See the flag. Go missed. Say ILS failure. Brief the next plan.

That is how precision approach contingency keeps you safe.
