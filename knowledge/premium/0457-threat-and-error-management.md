# Threat and Error Management

**Catalog ID:** 0457  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Threat and Error Management — the CRM framework for identifying threats, trapping errors, and managing undesired aircraft states before they become incidents.

TEM is not paperwork after the flight. It is how professional crews brief, fly, and communicate in real time. Weather, terrain, fatigue, ATC pressure, and automation are threats. Misheard clearances, skipped checks, and mode confusion are errors. An unstable approach is an undesired aircraft state.

For H130 operations — IFR arrivals, offshore legs, mountain weather — TEM language must appear in briefings and on the radio: unable, request delay, go around, divert. ICAO English supports Part 1 TEM vocabulary, Part 2 clearances under threat, and Part 4 crew interaction.

## 📖 Meaning

Threat and Error Management is a safety framework in which crews identify external threats and internal errors, apply countermeasures to trap or mitigate them, and recover from undesired aircraft states so that threats and errors do not escalate into incidents or accidents.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gerenciamento de Ameaças e Erros / Threat and Error Management (TEM).

Estrutura de segurança na qual as tripulações identificam ameaças externas e erros internos, aplicam contramedidas para capturá-los ou mitigá-los, e recuperam estados indesejados da aeronave para que ameaças e erros não se transformem em incidentes ou acidentes.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When applying TEM, the crew should:

- brief expected threats before departure and before approach
- assign countermeasures: who watches weather, fuel, terrain, ATC
- trap errors with cross-checks, readbacks, and challenge-and-response
- recognize undesired states early — unstable approach, wrong mode, fuel critical
- communicate decisions: go around, divert, request delay, declare if required

TEM elements include:

- threats — weather, terrain, traffic, fatigue, time pressure, system failures
- errors — incorrect readback, wrong altitude set, checklist interruption
- undesired aircraft states — low energy, high and fast, loss of separation
- countermeasures — briefings, SOPs, monitoring, assertive communication

**Important:** A go-around is a TEM success, not a failure. Trapping an error on final is the system working. Silence while the approach destabilizes is TEM failure.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers interact with TEM outcomes when crews:

- request delay or hold to manage weather or fuel threats
- go around from an unstable approach
- report unable due to performance, icing, or terrain
- divert and need new clearances and sequencing
- declare urgency or emergency after threats escalate

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You brief the H130 arrival into Florianópolis: coastal weather, possible windshear, short final, fatigue after a long duty day.

On final, airspeed is high and the approach is unstable. That is an undesired aircraft state.

You call:

Tower, ANAC123, going around, unstable approach.

Tower: ANAC123, roger go around, climb and maintain two thousand, fly runway heading.

You trapped the error chain. Threat identified in the brief. Error — late configuration — trapped by go-around. Undesired state ended.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, wind three two zero at one eight gust two eight, runway three two cleared to land.
2. Approach: ANAC123, expect holding for weather, advise fuel remaining.
3. Tower: ANAC123, go around, acknowledge, climb and maintain two thousand.
4. Approach: ANAC123, divert approved, cleared to Congonhas via flight planned route.
5. Radar: ANAC123, unable that routing due traffic, advise intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

TEM is how you think before the radio call and after it.

Threats are outside you — weather, terrain, ATC complexity, fatigue. Name them in the brief.

Errors are inside the crew — wrong number, missed check, late call. Trap them with SOPs and monitoring.

Undesired aircraft states are the red zone — continue only if you can recover immediately. Otherwise go around.

In the H130, threats stack quickly: low altitude, changing weather, single-pilot-like workload even with two crew. Brief the stack. Assign who owns each threat.

When you transmit going around or diverting, you are executing TEM in ICAO English. That language must be automatic under pressure.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You identify windshear and an unstable approach on short final. How does TEM guide your action and radio call?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Windshear is a threat. An unstable approach is an undesired aircraft state. The countermeasure is go around, not hoping it improves. I would apply go-around power and transmit: ANAC123, going around, windshear, climbing two thousand. Then I would reassess fuel and weather before accepting another approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating go-around as personal failure.  
  ✔ Treat go-around as successful TEM countermeasure.

- ❌ Briefing threats without assigning countermeasures.  
  ✔ Name who monitors weather, fuel, and approach gates.

- ❌ Continuing an unstable approach to "save the landing."  
  ✔ Go around early and communicate clearly.

- ❌ Not reporting fuel when requesting hold for weather.  
  ✔ Include fuel remaining so ATC can manage the threat with you.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**TRAP** — **T**hreats named in the brief, **R**ecognize errors early, **A**bandon undesired states with go-around, **P**hrase the decision clearly to ATC.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are on final to Santos Dumont in the H130. Gusty wind was briefed as a threat. At two hundred feet you are high and fast.

**Your first task**

What TEM decision do you make and what do you say?

**Your second task**

Tower asks if you can accept another immediate approach. Fuel is adequate but weather is worsening. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. Second task: ANAC123, request delay or vectors to assess weather, fuel remaining one hour two zero — or, if conditions are unacceptable: ANAC123, diverting to alternate, request clearance. TEM means trapping the undesired state first, then managing the next threat with a clear plan.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Threat and Error Management is CRM with a spine.

Name the threats. Trap the errors. Exit undesired states.

A go-around on the radio is not drama — it is countermeasure language.

In the H130, Brazilian terminals will give you weather, terrain, and traffic threats on the same approach. Brief them. Own them. Speak the recovery early.

That is how TEM keeps a normal flight normal — and turns a bad approach into a safe one.
