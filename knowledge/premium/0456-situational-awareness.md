# Situational Awareness

**Catalog ID:** 0456  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Situational Awareness — the continuous mental picture of where the aircraft is, what it is doing, what the environment is doing, and what will happen next.

Situational awareness is not a feeling of being "in the loop." It is an active product of perception, comprehension, and projection. When it degrades, crews miss altitude constraints, traffic conflicts, fuel states, and weather trends until the problem is already urgent.

For H130 helicopter operations in Brazilian IFR and VFR environments — coastal routes, mountainous approaches, offshore legs — situational awareness includes terrain, weather, fuel, ATC clearance state, and automation mode. ICAO English must support clear reports when awareness reveals a conflict: unable, request, advise, go around.

Understanding how to discuss and apply situational awareness in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 vocabulary, Part 2 radio discipline, and Part 4 interaction.

## 📖 Meaning

Situational Awareness is the accurate, up-to-date understanding of the aircraft's state, position, and trajectory relative to terrain, traffic, weather, fuel, systems, and ATC clearances, including the ability to project what will happen next and act before the situation becomes critical.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Consciência situacional / Situational Awareness (SA).

Compreensão precisa e atualizada do estado, posição e trajetória da aeronave em relação ao terreno, tráfego, meteorologia, combustível, sistemas e autorizações do controle, incluindo a capacidade de projetar o que acontecerá a seguir e agir antes que a situação se torne crítica.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When building and protecting situational awareness, the crew should:

- scan instruments, outside references, and ATC clearances continuously
- cross-check FMS, GPS, and raw data against the clearance and the chart
- verbalize changes: altitude, heading, weather, fuel, traffic, mode
- project ahead — next waypoint, next altitude, next frequency, next threat
- speak up early when the picture does not match: unable, request clarification, go around

Situational awareness includes:

- geographic awareness — where am I relative to terrain and airspace
- system awareness — what mode is the automation in
- temporal awareness — fuel, weather trend, ATC sequencing
- shared awareness — both pilots hold the same mental model

**Important:** Loss of situational awareness often feels like "everything is fine" until it is not. If you cannot state your altitude, clearance, and next threat in one sentence, rebuild the picture before continuing the approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers depend on crew situational awareness to:

- receive timely unable and request calls when clearances conflict with capability
- get accurate position and altitude reports when radar or ADS-B is limited
- coordinate go-arounds and diversions before the aircraft is committed
- manage traffic when a crew reports weather, fuel, or terrain concerns early
- maintain shared understanding of clearance state across frequency changes

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are flying the H130 on an IFR arrival into Confins. Weather is deteriorating. Approach clears you to descend to three thousand and join the localizer.

Your first officer notes the MSA ahead is three thousand five hundred. The picture does not match.

You call:

Approach, ANAC123, unable three thousand, minimum safe altitude three thousand five hundred, request higher or vectors.

Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand five hundred, turn left heading three six zero.

You rebuild the shared picture: altitude, heading, next threat — terrain — and continue with awareness restored.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain three thousand, join localizer runway one six.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able three thousand, terrain in your sector.
3. Radar: ANAC123, say altitude and position, radar contact intermittent.
4. Approach: ANAC123, go around, climb and maintain four thousand, fly runway heading.
5. Control: ANAC123, advise intentions, weather deteriorating at destination.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, unable three thousand, request three thousand five hundred for terrain.
2. ANAC123, climbing three thousand five hundred, left heading three six zero.
3. ANAC123, altitude three thousand two hundred, ten miles south of Confins.
4. ANAC123, going around, climbing four thousand, runway heading.
5. ANAC123, diverting to alternate, request weather and vectors.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Situational awareness is the difference between managing the flight and being managed by it.

Perceive — what do the instruments, the window, and ATC actually say?

Comprehend — what does that mean for this clearance, this terrain, this fuel state?

Project — what happens in two minutes if nothing changes?

In the H130, you often fly closer to terrain and weather than jet crews. That makes projection mandatory. If the next ridge, the next cloud base, or the next fuel check is unclear, stop the descent or go around and rebuild.

Shared awareness matters as much as personal awareness. If only one pilot knows the clearance, the crew does not have situational awareness — it has a secret.

Speak the picture out loud. Correct mismatches early. That is CRM in English and in action.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC clears you below the published MSA on vectors in IMC. How do you protect situational awareness and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would compare the clearance with the charted MSA and terrain. If the clearance conflicts, I would not descend. I would transmit: ANAC123, unable that altitude, minimum safe altitude is three thousand five hundred, request higher or vectors clear of terrain. I would keep both pilots aligned on altitude, heading, and next action until the picture matches the clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Descending on a clearance without checking MSA because "ATC knows."  
  ✔ Cross-check every altitude against terrain and published minima.

- ❌ Staying silent when the mental picture is incomplete.  
  ✔ Say: ANAC123, confirm clearance, or request clarification.

- ❌ Focusing only on the FMS magenta line.  
  ✔ Cross-check raw data, charts, and outside references.

- ❌ Not projecting fuel and weather until diversion is urgent.  
  ✔ Update fuel and weather awareness continuously and report early.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Unable That Altitude, Request Higher for Terrain

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, un-AY-bul THAT AL-ti-tood, reh-KWEST HY-er for tuh-RAYN

**Word Stress**

- Unable → un-AY-bul (stress on second)
- Altitude → AL-ti-tood (stress on first)
- Request → reh-KWEST (stress on second)
- Terrain → tuh-RAYN (stress on second)

Practice:

Unable that altitude... request higher for terrain.

Together:

ANAC123, unable three thousand, request three thousand five hundred for terrain.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**AWARE** — **A**ssess instruments and clearance, **W**atch terrain and weather, **A**lign both pilots' mental model, **R**eport unable early, **E**xpect the next threat two minutes ahead.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are on vectors to Confins in IMC in the H130. Approach clears you to two thousand five hundred. Your chart shows MSA three thousand. Weather is at minima.

**Your first task**

What do you say to protect situational awareness?

**Your second task**

Approach replies: "ANAC123, that altitude is available, traffic requires it." You still see terrain conflict on the chart. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, unable two thousand five hundred, minimum safe altitude three thousand, request three thousand or vectors. Second task: ANAC123, still unable two thousand five hundred due terrain, request alternate vectors or hold. I would not trade situational awareness for traffic convenience. Clearance does not remove terrain. Communicate the conflict clearly and keep the aircraft safe.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Situational awareness is the foundation of every CRM skill that follows.

Without it, decision making is guessing. Without it, workload management is firefighting. Without it, radio calls arrive too late.

Build the picture. Share the picture. Project the next threat.

In the H130, Brazilian terrain and weather will punish a late picture. Speak unable early. Rebuild before you continue.

That is how professional crews stay ahead of the aircraft — not behind it.
