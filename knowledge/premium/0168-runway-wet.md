# Runway Wet

**Catalog ID:** 0168  
**Category:** Airport Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Runway Wet — the ATC and ATIS advisory indicating that the runway surface is covered with a film of water that reduces tire-to-pavement friction without meeting the criteria for runway contamination.

Runway Wet and Runway Contaminated are not the same thing.

Runway Wet means the surface is visibly moist or has a thin layer of water — the pavement is still the primary braking surface. Performance is reduced, but the runway is usable with corrections.

Runway Contaminated means a larger portion of the runway is covered with standing water, slush, snow, or ice — the pavement itself is no longer the primary contact surface.

Brazilian coastal airports — Navegantes, Florianópolis, Congonhas — frequently report Runway Wet during and after the regular tropical rainfall. This is one of the most common surface condition advisories in Brazilian airspace.

Knowing the exact operational implications of Runway Wet keeps you safe and keeps your ICAO communication precise.

## 📖 Meaning

Runway Wet is an aerodrome surface condition advisory indicating that the runway pavement is covered with a water film sufficient to reduce braking effectiveness below dry pavement levels, but not meeting the criteria for runway contamination.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pista molhada.

Condição de superfície da pista indicando que o pavimento está coberto com uma película de água suficiente para reduzir a eficiência de frenagem abaixo dos níveis de pista seca, mas sem atingir os critérios de pista contaminada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Runway Wet is reported, the pilot should:

- apply the wet runway landing distance correction from the aircraft performance manual
- reduce approach speed if required by the wet runway procedure
- avoid braking aggressively early in the landing roll
- report braking action after landing if it differs materially from expected
- not assume wet means contaminated — the criteria are different

The distinction between Wet and Contaminated is precise:

- **Runway Wet:** entire surface covered by water film, depth typically less than 3mm, pavement still the dominant friction surface
- **Runway Contaminated:** 25% or more of the runway surface area (within the relevant zone) covered with 3mm or more of standing water, slush, compacted snow, or ice

**Important:** A wet runway with rubber deposits from previous landings — particularly in the touchdown zone — can be significantly more slippery than a clean wet surface. This condition, sometimes called "slippery when wet," can approach contaminated braking performance without meeting the depth threshold.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers advise Runway Wet to:

- alert pilots to reduced braking effectiveness
- comply with mandatory surface condition reporting procedures
- prompt pilots to apply appropriate landing distance corrections
- support safe operations without closing the runway unnecessarily
- distinguish from the more severe Runway Contaminated advisory

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on approach to Navegantes in your H130 after a heavy shower passed the airport fifteen minutes ago.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, runway two eight, runway wet, wind two five zero at one two knots, cleared to land.

You apply the 15% wet runway landing distance correction from your H130 performance supplement.

Your corrected distance fits comfortably within the available 1,800 meters.

You respond:

Cleared to land runway two eight, ANAC123.

You land, decelerate firmly, and clear the runway at the normal exit.

You transmit:

ANAC123, braking action Good, runway two eight.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Runway Wet is the most frequent surface advisory in Brazilian operations.

It rains almost every afternoon in coastal Brazil.

If you wait for a dry runway, you will wait forever.

Understand the distinction: Wet is manageable. Contaminated is serious.

Wet means: apply the performance correction. Land. Report.

The performance supplement in your H130 shows you exactly how much extra distance wet pavement requires.

Use it. Every time.

The trap: treating a wet runway with heavy rubber deposits as a normal wet runway. The rubber absorbs water and acts as a lubricant. Braking in the touchdown zone on a rubber-contaminated wet surface can be dramatically worse than on a clean wet surface.

Request braking action from the previous aircraft. Use it as additional data.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the operational difference between a wet runway and a contaminated runway?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A wet runway is one where the pavement is covered with a water film, typically less than three millimeters deep, which reduces braking friction below dry levels but still allows the pavement itself to provide the primary braking surface. A contaminated runway occurs when twenty-five percent or more of the runway surface in the relevant zone is covered with three millimeters or more of standing water, slush, compacted snow, or ice. On a contaminated runway, the contaminant becomes the primary surface the tires contact, which dramatically increases stopping distance and requires more conservative performance corrections. The key distinction is depth and coverage — wet is manageable with standard corrections, while contaminated demands a significantly higher level of caution and may require diversion.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using no landing distance correction for a wet runway.  
  ✔ Wet runway always requires applying the performance correction from the aircraft manual.

- ❌ Treating every rain-affected runway as contaminated.  
  ✔ Wet and Contaminated have specific definitions. Apply the correct correction for each.

- ❌ Forgetting to report braking action after landing on a wet runway.  
  ✔ Always transmit a braking action report to help the crew behind you.

- ❌ Ignoring the rubber deposit warning in the ATC advisory.  
  ✔ Rubber deposits on a wet surface significantly reduce braking — adjust your technique accordingly.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Tower advises: ANAC123, runway wet, braking action report by previous aircraft, Medium, runway two eight, Navegantes. Wind two five zero at one four knots.

Your H130 performance supplement shows: wet runway correction +15%, Medium braking action correction +20%. You must apply the more conservative of the two.

Available landing distance: runway two eight, 1,800 meters. Your normal landing distance: 1,200 meters.

**Your first task**

Calculate your corrected landing distance and determine whether you can accept the runway.

**Your second task**

If you land and experience Good braking despite the Medium report, what do you transmit?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Applying the Medium braking action correction of 20%: 1,200 multiplied by 1.20 equals 1,440 meters. The available 1,800 meters is sufficient, so I can accept the runway. After landing with Good braking, I would transmit: ANAC123, braking action report runway two eight, Good, surface wet but braking normal. This updates ATC and helps the next arriving crew.
