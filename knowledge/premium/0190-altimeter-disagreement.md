# Altimeter Disagreement

**Catalog ID:** 0190  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Altimeter Disagreement — a condition in which two or more altitude indications on the aircraft do not agree within acceptable limits, creating uncertainty about true altitude and requiring cross-check, troubleshooting, and clear communication with ATC when altitude integrity is in doubt.

Altimeter disagreement is not a minor instrument annoyance. Altitude is the primary vertical separation tool in IFR airspace. If you cannot trust the altitude you are flying, you must resolve the discrepancy or declare the limitation to ATC.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations — IFR cruise between Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, and Florianópolis, and approaches into Santos Dumont — crews rely on primary and standby altimetry. Understanding how to identify disagreement, apply the correct altimeter setting, and report altitude problems in ICAO English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Altimeter Disagreement is a discrepancy between altitude indications from independent altimeter systems or between the altimeter and another altitude reference, exceeding normal tolerance, which may result from incorrect altimeter setting, instrument failure, static system fault, or environmental factors, and which requires crew cross-check and, when necessary, ATC notification.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Discordância de Altímetros / Altimeter Disagreement.

Discrepância entre indicações de altitude de sistemas de altímetro independentes ou entre o altímetro e outra referência de altitude, excedendo a tolerância normal, que pode resultar de ajuste incorreto do altímetro, falha de instrumento, falha do sistema estático ou fatores ambientais, e que exige verificação cruzada pela tripulação e, quando necessário, notificação ao ATC.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When altimeter disagreement is detected, the crew should:

- cross-check primary, standby, and any available GPS or radio altitude references as applicable
- verify the correct local QNH or standard pressure setting for the phase of flight
- identify which instrument is most likely correct using consistency checks
- apply AFM abnormal procedures for altimeter or static system faults
- advise ATC if altitude indication is unreliable or if you must deviate from assigned altitude
- request an altitude confirmation or radar altitude check if available and appropriate

Altimeter disagreement scenarios commonly include:

- incorrect QNH set on one altimeter after level change or approach clearance
- standby altimeter disagreeing with primary after static blockage or instrument fault
- large split appearing during climb or descent through a pressure change
- crew uncertainty about which altitude to report to ATC
- need to continue with one reliable source and declare the limitation

**Important:** Always verify altimeter setting first — many "failures" are setting errors. In Brazilian ops, transition between QNH and standard pressure must be disciplined. In the H130, follow the AFM for instrument failure. If you cannot determine a reliable altitude, treat it as a safety issue: advise ATC, request assistance, and avoid terrain-critical maneuvers until resolved.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with altimeter-related reports to:

- maintain vertical separation based on reported altitudes
- provide QNH updates and confirm the correct setting
- assist aircraft with unreliable altitude indication using radar or preferred routing
- keep terrain-critical approaches from continuing with unsafe altimetry
- coordinate priority handling when a flight declares instrument problems

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are descending into Congonhas. Approach issues the local QNH. You set both altimeters, but one reads two hundred feet higher than the other in level flight.

You cross-check the setting — both show the same QNH. The split remains. You identify the standby as the outlier using consistency with previous readings and AFM guidance, and you advise Approach that you have an altimeter disagreement and are using the primary altimeter.

Approach confirms QNH again and continues the approach clearance. You brief the limitation, monitor closely, and complete a stable approach with heightened cross-check.

That is how altimeter disagreement is handled — verify setting, identify the reliable source, tell ATC, continue only if safe.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, QNH one zero one niner, report level at four thousand.
2. Radar: ANAC123, confirm altimeter setting, QNH one zero two one.
3. Approach: ANAC123, roger altimeter disagreement, continue approach, report any further problems.
4. Control: ANAC123, radar indicates you are level, confirm altitude.
5. Approach: ANAC123, if unable reliable altitude, advise intentions, vectors available.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Altitude trust is non-negotiable.

When two altimeters disagree, your first action is not panic — it is method. Check the setting. Check both instruments. Check against known references.

Most disagreements in training are QNH errors. Fix the setting and the problem disappears. If the split remains, decide which source is reliable using the AFM and consistency. Then tell ATC what you are using.

Do not silently fly an approach with unresolved altimetry near Congonhas or Santos Dumont terrain and traffic. Say the problem. Request confirmation of QNH. Request vectors if you need time.

Part 3 speaking should describe the problem clearly: what disagreed, what you checked, what you decided, and what you told ATC. Keep it factual.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

During descent, your primary and standby altimeters disagree by more than the normal tolerance. Describe your actions.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately cross-check both altimeter settings and confirm the correct QNH or standard pressure for the phase of flight. If the settings are correct and the disagreement remains, I would identify the most reliable altitude source using AFM procedures and consistency checks. I would advise ATC of the altimeter disagreement and state which altimeter I am using. If altitude becomes unreliable, I would request vectors or a diversion and avoid continuing a terrain-critical approach until I have a trustworthy altitude reference.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Assuming disagreement is always instrument failure without checking QNH.  
  ✔ Verify altimeter setting first on both instruments.

- ❌ Continuing an approach without telling ATC about unreliable altitude.  
  ✔ Advise ATC early and request assistance if needed.

- ❌ Averaging two disagreeing altimeters as a "compromise" altitude.  
  ✔ Select the reliable source per procedure; do not invent a midpoint.

- ❌ Reporting altitude from the failed instrument out of habit.  
  ✔ Brief which instrument is primary for the remainder of the flight and stick to it.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Altimeter disagreement is a trust problem before it is a systems problem.

Check the setting. Cross-check the instruments. Apply the AFM. Choose one reliable source.

Tell ATC what you have and what you are using. Near Brazilian coastal and city approaches, silent uncertainty about altitude is unacceptable.

If you cannot trust altitude, do not continue into terrain or traffic on hope. Request help. Divert if required.

Settings first. Source second. Radio third. Approach only if safe.

That is how you keep vertical navigation honest in the H130.
