# Holdover Time

**Catalog ID:** 0237  
**Category:** Ground Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Holdover Time — the estimated time of protection provided by anti-icing fluid, measured from the start of the final anti-icing application until the fluid is no longer effective, after which takeoff on contaminated or unprotected surfaces is not acceptable.

Holdover time is not a suggestion. It is the operational window between treatment and takeoff. When it expires, the fluid has failed or is assumed to have failed. Continuing to depart without re-treatment is a contamination risk and a decision error.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations in Brazil — especially Curitiba, Guarulhos, and São Paulo winter mornings — crews may face freezing drizzle, frost, or light snow with limited pad capacity and busy ground frequencies. Knowing how to obtain holdover information, brief it, and communicate expiration or urgency to ATC is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

Holdover time links de-icing, anti-icing, and the takeoff decision into one clear English concept.

## 📖 Meaning

Holdover Time is the estimated period of protection against ice, frost, or snow accretion provided by anti-icing fluid, beginning at the start of the final anti-icing fluid application and ending when the fluid is no longer effective under the prevailing precipitation and temperature conditions, as published in holdover time guidelines for the fluid type and intensity.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Tempo de proteção / Holdover Time (HOT).

Período estimado de proteção contra acúmulo de gelo, geada ou neve proporcionado pelo fluido anti-gelo, começando no início da aplicação final do fluido anti-gelo e terminando quando o fluido deixa de ser eficaz nas condições de precipitação e temperatura prevalecentes, conforme tabelas de holdover para o tipo de fluido e a intensidade.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When holdover time applies, the crew should:

- note fluid type, start of final anti-icing application, and precipitation conditions
- determine holdover from approved tables for temperature and precipitation intensity
- brief the latest acceptable takeoff time before taxi
- advise ATC if holdover is short and departure priority is needed
- return for re-treatment if holdover expires or becomes uncertain before takeoff
- never take off after expired holdover based on visual "guessing" that fluid still works

Holdover time decision-making includes:

- start time of final anti-icing application as the clock start
- fluid type (commonly Type II, III, or IV for anti-icing protection)
- precipitation type and intensity changes that can shorten protection
- taxi delays, sequencing, and runway changes that consume holdover
- H130 ops: even without airline-scale programs, the concept and English remain exam-critical

**Important:** Holdover time ends when the fluid fails — or when you can no longer be confident it is effective. If precipitation intensifies, if you lose track of start time, or if delay exceeds the table value, treat holdover as expired. Request re-treatment. Do not depart and hope.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use holdover-related information to:

- sequence departures for aircraft with short remaining protection
- understand why a crew cannot accept further delay
- coordinate return to the pad for re-treatment
- manage ground congestion during winter operations
- prioritize safety over rigid first-come sequencing when holdover is expiring

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You complete anti-icing at Guarulhos. Ground gives Type IV, start of application time, and light snow. Your holdover from the table is twenty-five minutes.

You brief: takeoff no later than that time. You call for taxi with margin.

During taxi, Ground advises a ten-minute delay for sequencing.

You check the clock. Only eight minutes of holdover would remain at the new takeoff time — too tight if snow intensifies.

You transmit:

ANAC123, holdover expiring, request priority departure or return to pad for re-treatment.

Ground: ANAC123, expect immediate departure, continue to holding point runway one zero Left.

You depart inside holdover. Clean decision. Clear English. No gamble on expired fluid.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, advise holdover time remaining.
2. Tower: ANAC123, expect departure delay one zero minutes, say if holdover still valid.
3. Ground: ANAC123, taxi via Bravo, hold short runway two seven Right, report holdover status.
4. Tower: ANAC123, unable immediate departure, say intentions.
5. Ground: ANAC123, cleared back to de-icing pad via Alpha for re-treatment.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Holdover time is a takeoff decision tool, not a paperwork detail.

The clock starts at the beginning of the final anti-icing application — not when you leave the pad, not when you start engines, not when you feel ready. Know the start. Know the table. Know the weather.

If ATC offers a delay that burns your holdover, you have two professional options: request priority departure, or return for re-treatment. Sitting in line past expiration is not a third option.

Brazilian winter ops can compress everything: short pads, busy Ground, changing precipitation. Your English must be calm and specific: holdover remaining, holdover expiring, request priority, request re-treatment.

In the H130 academy, you may not always see formal holdover paperwork like a major airline. The concept still owns the decision. Contaminated or unprotected departure after failed fluid is unacceptable — in operations and in the ICAO exam answer.

Protect. Time. Decide. Depart or return. Never invent protection that the fluid no longer provides.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Anti-icing is complete. Holdover is twenty minutes. ATC advises a twenty-five-minute departure delay. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not accept a delay that exceeds holdover time. I would advise ATC that holdover would expire before departure and request either immediate or priority departure within the remaining protection, or clearance to return to the pad for re-treatment. I would not take off after holdover expiration. If precipitation intensity increased, I would treat the original holdover as no longer valid and return for treatment even earlier.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Starting the holdover clock when leaving the pad instead of at final application start.  
  ✔ Start holdover at the beginning of the final anti-icing application.

- ❌ Accepting unlimited taxi delay because "fluid still looks wet."  
  ✔ Use published holdover guidance; return if expired or uncertain.

- ❌ Saying only "we need to go now" without explaining holdover.  
  ✔ State: holdover expiring, request priority departure or re-treatment.

- ❌ Continuing takeoff after holdover expires to avoid another pad visit.  
  ✔ Expired holdover means re-treat — departure is not optional convenience.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are holding short at Curitiba. Holdover expires in four minutes. Tower says expect five minutes delay.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Tower cannot give immediate departure. What do you request next?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, holdover expiring in four minutes, unable five-minute delay, request immediate departure. Second task: ANAC123, request return to de-icing pad for re-treatment. I would not depart after holdover expiration, and I would not stay in sequence hoping the fluid is "probably fine."

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Holdover time is the bridge between anti-icing and a legal, safe takeoff.

Know when the clock started. Know when protection ends. Speak clearly when the window is closing.

ATC can help with priority — but only if you explain holdover, not if you only sound impatient.

In Brazilian cold ops, delays happen. Re-treatment happens. Taking off after failed fluid must not happen.

Time it. Brief it. Depart inside it — or go back to the pad.

That is how holdover time keeps winter departures professional in the H130 world and in ICAO English.
