# Crew Oxygen On

**Catalog ID:** 0248  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Crew Oxygen On — confirmation that flight crew oxygen masks are donned and the crew oxygen system is supplying oxygen, restoring protected breathing and interphone communication during cabin altitude, smoke, or other oxygen-related emergencies. This concept is highly relevant to ICAO exams and remains operationally meaningful for H130 crews whenever smoke, fumes, or hypoxia risk requires oxygen and clear crew coordination.

Crew oxygen on is both a checklist item and a radio-status concept. Without crew oxygen and communication, the rest of the emergency procedure collapses.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master the phraseology for reporting crew oxygen status, then apply the same discipline to smoke, decompression, and any event where the crew must protect themselves first.

Understanding how to discuss crew oxygen in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Crew Oxygen On is the confirmation that flight crew members have donned oxygen masks and are receiving oxygen from the crew system, enabling protected breathing and crew communication while managing cabin altitude, smoke, fumes, or related emergencies, and supporting clear ATC notification of status and intentions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Oxigênio da tripulação ligado / Crew Oxygen On.

Confirmação de que os membros da tripulação de voo colocaram as máscaras de oxigênio e estão recebendo oxigênio do sistema da tripulação, permitindo respiração protegida e comunicação entre a tripulação ao gerenciar altitude de cabine, fumaça, vapores ou emergências relacionadas, e apoiando notificação clara ao ATC sobre estado e intenções.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When crew oxygen is required, the crew should:

- don crew oxygen masks immediately — protect the crew first
- select crew oxygen on / 100% as required by procedure
- establish crew communication through the mask interphone
- continue the applicable emergency checklist (cabin altitude, smoke, decompression)
- advise ATC of the emergency nature, crew oxygen status if relevant, and intentions
- for H130: apply the same protect-communicate-declare sequence for smoke or fumes

Crew oxygen reporting commonly includes:

- crew on oxygen / crew oxygen on
- smoke or cabin altitude context
- emergency descent if required
- request priority landing / diversion
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** Crew oxygen comes before long radio calls. A hypoxic or smoke-impaired crew cannot communicate professionally. Don masks, establish communication, then transmit. On ICAO exams, "crew oxygen on" shows you understand priority of actions.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use crew oxygen related reports to:

- understand that the flight crew is protected and still able to communicate
- prioritize handling for cabin altitude, smoke, or decompression emergencies
- anticipate possible communication difficulties if masks affect speech clarity
- coordinate priority landing and emergency services
- obtain souls and fuel for arrival planning

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: cabin altitude warning with smoke in the cockpit. You don crew masks, select oxygen, and establish communication.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, smoke in the cockpit, crew oxygen on, requesting priority vectors for immediate landing.

ATC acknowledges. You fly the procedure, update when smoke is decreasing, and continue for priority landing with emergency services standing by.

For H130 operations, the same sequence applies to cabin or avionics smoke: oxygen if available and required, communication, declaration, landing plan.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday, confirm crew on oxygen and say intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, priority handling approved, descend flight level one two zero, report able to continue.
3. Approach: ANAC123, expect straight-in runway one one, emergency services alerted.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Tower: ANAC123, fire services standing by, advise if evacuation required.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, smoke in cockpit, crew oxygen on, request immediate landing.
2. ANAC123, crew oxygen on, descending flight level one two zero, continuing.
3. ANAC123, runway one one straight-in, crew oxygen on, smoke decreasing.
4. ANAC123, souls four, fuel one hour twenty minutes.
5. ANAC123, request emergency services, no evacuation planned at this time.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Crew oxygen on means the pilots are still in the fight.

In smoke or high cabin altitude, the first professional act is selfish in the right way: protect your brain. Then talk to each other. Then talk to ATC.

If you transmit before you can think, you transmit garbage. If you delay oxygen to finish a perfect sentence, you may not finish the sentence.

Say the emergency. Say crew oxygen on when it helps ATC understand you are protected and communicating. Say what you need: priority landing, vectors, descent.

In the H130, smoke in a small cabin reaches you fast. The principle does not change. Protect. Communicate. Declare. Land.

Brazilian examiners love this logic. Show it in English without drama and without delay.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

There is smoke in the cockpit and cabin altitude is rising. What do you do first, and what do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would don the crew oxygen mask immediately, select oxygen as required, and establish crew communication. Then I would declare the emergency — typically Mayday for cockpit smoke — stating the nature of the problem, that crew oxygen is on, and requesting priority vectors for immediate landing. I would continue the applicable checklist, update smoke and cabin status, and provide souls and fuel when asked. The same protect-communicate-declare sequence applies in helicopter operations.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Calling ATC before donning oxygen in smoke or high cabin altitude.  
  ✔ Protect the crew first, then transmit clearly.

- ❌ Saying "we put the mask" instead of "crew oxygen on."  
  ✔ Use ICAO-standard "crew oxygen on" / "crew on oxygen."

- ❌ Forgetting that mask microphone discipline affects readbacks.  
  ✔ Speak slowly and clearly; confirm ATC has understood critical items.

- ❌ Treating crew oxygen as airline-only knowledge.  
  ✔ Transfer the priority sequence to H130 smoke and fumes scenarios.

**Word Stress**

- Crew → KROO
- Oxygen → OK-si-jen

- Landing → LAND-ing

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Crew oxygen on is the bridge between survival and communication.

Protect the crew. Restore the interphone. Declare the emergency. Fly the landing plan.

Learn the phrase for ICAO. Live the sequence for the H130 — especially with smoke in a small cabin.

Mask on. Talk. Declare. Land.
