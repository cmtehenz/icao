# Captain

**Catalog ID:** 0346  
**Category:** Crew  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Captain — the pilot-in-command, legally responsible for the operation and safety of the aircraft, final authority on board, and the standard ICAO title for the crew member in command.

Part 1 vocabulary and Part 2 operational English require precise use of captain, pilot-in-command, and command authority — especially in briefings, incapacitation scenarios, and decision announcements.

For H130 ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, the captain role often equals the single pilot-in-command. In two-crew operations, the captain remains PIC even when the first officer is pilot flying.

Master: captain, pilot-in-command, final authority, command decision.

## 📖 Meaning

Captain is the pilot-in-command of the aircraft, responsible for the safe conduct of the flight, crew coordination, and final operational decisions in accordance with regulations and company procedures.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Comandante / Captain / Pilot-in-Command.

Piloto em comando da aeronave, responsável pela condução segura do voo, pela coordenação da tripulação e pelas decisões operacionais finais de acordo com os regulamentos e os procedimentos da empresa.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When acting as captain, the pilot should:

- exercise final authority for safety of flight
- brief crew on roles, threats, and plans
- ensure compliance with ATC clearances or timely inability reports
- declare urgency/distress when required
- transfer control only with clear verbal confirmation

Captain-related communications commonly include:

- captain's decision to divert / reject / go around
- captain incapacitated (reported by the other pilot)
- request priority as PIC judgment requires
- unable to comply, captain's discretion alternatives when applicable

**Important:** Captain is a responsibility title. On frequency you still use the aircraft callsign. Authority is shown by decisions and clarity, not by saying "I am the captain" on every transmission.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers rely on captain/PIC authority when:

- a diversion or emergency decision is declared
- inability to comply must be resolved with an alternative clearance
- incapacitation reports identify who remains in command
- examiners test correct command vocabulary in English
- priority handling follows a clear PIC request

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are captain of an H130 flight from Congonhas toward the coast. Weather ahead deteriorates.

You decide to divert. You tell ATC you are diverting due to weather and request vectors.

The decision is yours as PIC. The radio call is standard and calm.

In two-crew airline scenarios, you may be pilot monitoring while still remaining captain. Role of flying and role of command are not always the same seat task — command remains with the captain.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger diversion, say intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, unable that level, say alternative request.
3. Tower: ANAC123, confirm you are declaring an emergency.
4. Approach: ANAC123, priority approved as requested.
5. Control: ANAC123, roger, advise when ready for further clearance.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, diverting Florianópolis due to weather, request vectors.
2. ANAC123, unable to comply, request alternative heading.
3. Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, declaring emergency, request priority landing.
4. ANAC123, going around, request vectors for another approach.
5. ANAC123, request priority landing, medical emergency on board.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Captain means you own the outcome.

CRM does not remove command — it improves it. Listen to the first officer. Then decide.

Brazilian Portuguese: comandante. ICAO English: captain or pilot-in-command.

Do not confuse captain with check pilot or relief pilot. Those are different roles that may sit in the flight deck without replacing PIC authority unless formally transferred under procedure.

In single-pilot H130 Brazilian operations, you are the captain every flight. Speak decisions clearly to ATC: diverting, unable, declaring emergency, going around.

Authority without clarity creates confusion. Clarity without authority creates drift. Use both.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the captain responsible for, and how might that appear in ATC communication?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The captain is the pilot-in-command, responsible for the safety of the flight and final operational decisions. That appears in ATC communication as clear intentions: diverting, unable to comply with a request for alternative clearance, declaring Mayday or Pan Pan, or requesting priority landing when required.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using only "comandante" in an English exam answer.  
  ✔ Prefer captain or pilot-in-command.

- ❌ Delaying a safety decision to avoid "bothering" ATC.  
  ✔ Decide, then communicate intentions promptly.

- ❌ Saying personal authority phrases instead of operational intentions.  
  ✔ Transmit diverting / unable / emergency — not speeches about rank.

- ❌ Confusing pilot flying with pilot-in-command.  
  ✔ The first officer may fly; the captain may still be PIC.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Weather at destination goes below minima. You are captain in the H130 with fuel for the alternate.

**Your first task**

What do you tell Approach?

**Your second task**

Approach offers holding. You decide the alternate is safer now. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, destination weather below minima, diverting to alternate, request vectors. Second task: ANAC123, negative holding, diverting alternate now, request routing. As captain I would not accept a hold that compromises the safe fuel or weather plan.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Captain is legal responsibility wrapped in operational English.

Brief. Decide. Communicate intentions. Accept CRM input without surrendering PIC duty.

In Brazilian H130 flying, you often wear every hat. Still use the standard titles when describing multi-crew roles.

Command is quiet when it is clear — and clear when it is spoken in time.
