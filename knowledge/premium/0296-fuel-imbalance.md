# Fuel Imbalance

**Catalog ID:** 0296  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Fuel Imbalance — an unequal fuel quantity between tanks that can affect lateral balance, range planning, and system management, requiring monitoring, possible transfer or crossfeed procedures, and clear ATC communication if performance or diversion becomes necessary. Multi-tank imbalance language is common on ICAO exams; for H130 crews, map the same English to fuel-quantity asymmetry, unusable fuel, and range or center-of-gravity concerns you may actually face.

Fuel imbalance is High priority because it can degrade handling, reduce usable fuel, and force an early return if transfer is unavailable.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master ICAO phraseology for fuel imbalance, then transfer the reporting structure to helicopter fuel-system anomalies under DECEA operations.

Understanding how to discuss fuel imbalance in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Fuel Imbalance is a significant difference in fuel quantity between tanks or sides of the aircraft that may affect lateral balance, usable fuel, and range, requiring crew monitoring, published transfer or balancing procedures when available, and prompt notification to ATC if the imbalance limits performance, requires diversion, or becomes an urgency situation.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Desbalanceamento de combustível / Fuel Imbalance.

Diferença significativa de quantidade de combustível entre tanques ou lados da aeronave que pode afetar o equilíbrio lateral, o combustível utilizável e o alcance, exigindo monitoramento pela tripulação, procedimentos publicados de transferência ou balanceamento quando disponíveis, e notificação rápida ao ATC se o desbalanceamento limitar o desempenho, exigir desvio ou se tornar uma situação de urgência.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When fuel imbalance is detected, the crew should:

- confirm quantities on gauges and compare with expected burn
- apply AFM/QRH fuel imbalance or transfer procedures if published
- assess effect on lateral control, range, and landing weight planning
- declare Pan Pan if imbalance threatens safe continuation or forces diversion
- report fuel imbalance, intentions (continue, return, divert), and request priority if needed
- for H130: use the same structure for asymmetric tank quantities or unusable fuel affecting endurance

Fuel imbalance reporting commonly includes:

- fuel imbalance / fuel quantity imbalance left and right
- attempting fuel transfer / transfer not available
- request return / diversion / priority landing
- souls and fuel remaining when asked
- advise if lateral control or range is affected

**Important:** On the ICAO speaking test, use "fuel imbalance" clearly. In H130 line flying, describe tank quantities and endurance honestly even if your AFM uses different warning names — retain exam vocabulary and transfer the logic.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to fuel imbalance reports to:

- understand possible handling or range limitations
- offer return, diversion, or priority landing
- keep traffic clear if the aircraft needs a shorter route
- plan runway and emergency services if landing with system degradation
- coordinate fuel-related urgency without assuming engine failure

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: en route toward Florianópolis, left tank reads significantly lower than right. Lateral trim feels off. Transfer is attempted per procedure.

You assess that continuation to destination is no longer prudent and transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Control, ANAC123, fuel imbalance, requesting return to land.

If usable fuel becomes critically low because of the imbalance:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, fuel imbalance, low usable fuel, request immediate priority landing.

For H130 reality, replace twin-tank jet language with your actual tank layout — keep the urgency logic identical: name the problem, state the effect, ask for what you need.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fuel imbalance is a systems problem that becomes a handling and range problem if you ignore it.

Confirm the gauges. Apply the published balancing or transfer steps. Then decide: continue, return, or divert. Do not wait until the aircraft feels wrong in cruise to tell ATC.

Say "fuel imbalance" — not only "fuel problem." Controllers and examiners need the precise picture.

In the H130, your fuel system may be simpler than a transport jet, but asymmetric quantity and unusable fuel still change your plan. Honest English buys you options.

Brazilian examiners want procedure plus vocabulary. Give them both.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You notice a significant fuel imbalance during cruise. What do you do, and what do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would confirm the fuel quantities, apply the fuel imbalance or transfer procedure from the checklist, and assess whether I can continue safely. If the imbalance affects handling or usable fuel, I would declare Pan Pan, report fuel imbalance, and request return or diversion as required. If usable fuel becomes critically low, I would upgrade to Mayday and request immediate priority landing. I would provide souls and fuel when asked. On a helicopter, I would use the same structure for asymmetric tank quantities or unusable fuel affecting endurance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "problem with fuel" without naming imbalance.  
  ✔ Prefer "fuel imbalance" plus effect on range or control.

- ❌ Continuing to destination without telling ATC when usable fuel is reduced.  
  ✔ Request return or diversion early if the plan is no longer safe.

- ❌ Confusing fuel imbalance with fuel leak without clarifying.  
  ✔ If quantity drops abnormally, say imbalance and suspected leak if applicable.

- ❌ Ignoring the topic because H130 tank layout differs from jets.  
  ✔ Learn exam English; transfer quantity and endurance reporting to the helicopter.
