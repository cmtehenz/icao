# Contact Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0162  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Contact Approach — a pilot-initiated approach conducted below the clouds using visual reference to the ground, separate from both an instrument approach and a visual approach.

Contact Approach is different from a visual approach.

A visual approach is ATC-initiated: the controller asks if you have the airport in sight and can proceed visually. It requires visual meteorological conditions or the airport in sight.

A contact approach is pilot-initiated: the crew requests it. It can be conducted in conditions below the instrument approach minima, as long as the crew can maintain clear of clouds, see one mile, and remain clear of terrain.

At Brazilian airports including Navegantes and smaller regional fields, contact approaches are used by pilots familiar with the local terrain who can maintain visual reference despite marginal weather.

## 📖 Meaning

A Contact Approach is a pilot-initiated approach in which a crew requests authorization from ATC to proceed to the destination airport by visual reference to the surface, clear of clouds, with at least one mile flight visibility, when the full instrument approach procedure or published minima are not required.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a crew requests a contact approach, they should:

- explicitly request the contact approach from ATC — it is pilot-initiated, never ATC-initiated
- confirm they can maintain clear of clouds throughout the approach
- confirm they have at least one mile flight visibility
- confirm they are familiar with the terrain and obstacles between their position and the airport
- accept that ATC obstacle clearance service is limited — the crew assumes increased terrain responsibility
- remain clear of the instrument approach protected area if other IFR aircraft are executing instrument approaches

Contact approach operations commonly include:

- request contact approach when partial instrument conditions exist but visual reference to the ground is possible
- crew initiates by transmitting: request contact approach
- ATC evaluates traffic conflicts and approves or denies
- ATC may still issue traffic information and sequence the aircraft

**Important:** A contact approach is not a shortcut through cloud. The crew must have clear-of-cloud visual reference to the ground and one mile visibility. ATC does not provide full obstacle clearance service during a contact approach — the crew is responsible for terrain and obstacle avoidance. If in doubt, fly the full instrument approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC approves contact approaches when:

- the crew has requested it and conditions permit
- it reduces workload in non-radar environments
- it allows the aircraft to proceed to the airport without consuming instrument approach sequencing resources
- traffic allows the aircraft to leave the normal IFR approach flow
- current visibility meets the minimum requirement for the approval

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Navegantes in your H130 on an IFR flight plan.

The weather is broken cloud at one thousand five hundred feet with three miles visibility. The ILS approach is available.

Below the cloud layer you can see the ground clearly and you are familiar with the terrain approaching Navegantes from the north.

You transmit:

ANAC123, request contact approach runway one five.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, contact approach runway one five approved, report field in sight, traffic is a citation five miles final.

You respond:

Contact approach runway one five, traffic in sight, ANAC123.

You maintain visual reference to the ground, clear of cloud, and proceed to the airport.

Approaching the field:

ANAC123, field in sight.

Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land runway one five.

The contact approach brought you in efficiently without requiring the full ILS procedure.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Contact approach is a pilot's request — not a controller's offer.

You decide if conditions allow it. You take the responsibility.

ATC checks traffic and approves or denies. But the judgment about weather and terrain is yours.

The key legal conditions: clear of clouds, one mile visibility, and you know where the terrain is.

If any of those fail — execute the instrument approach.

The trap: pilots request contact approaches in marginal weather to save time, then encounter deteriorating conditions during the approach. The contact approach becomes a visual descent into reduced visibility with no instrument guidance and terrain below.

When in doubt, fly the ILS.

In the H130, contact approaches into familiar airports like Navegantes can be efficient and safe — when conditions genuinely support them.

Contact approach is not for unfamiliar terrain in marginal conditions. It requires local knowledge, clear air below the cloud, and honest self-assessment.

The difference between visual approach and contact approach: visual approach requires the airport in sight. Contact approach requires only ground reference and one mile visibility — not necessarily the airport.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the difference between a contact approach and a visual approach, and who initiates each?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A visual approach is ATC-initiated. The controller asks the crew if they have the airport in sight and can proceed visually. A visual approach requires the crew to have the airport or the preceding traffic in sight and maintain visual separation and terrain avoidance. A contact approach is pilot-initiated. The crew requests it when they can maintain visual reference to the ground and one mile visibility, even if the full instrument approach minima are not met. ATC approves or denies based on traffic. The contact approach places more terrain-avoidance responsibility on the crew, since ATC obstacle clearance service is reduced. The crew must be familiar with the terrain and must maintain clear of cloud and visual reference throughout.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Expecting ATC to offer a contact approach — waiting for ATC to suggest it.  
  ✔ Contact approach is always pilot-initiated. Request it explicitly: request contact approach runway one five.

- ❌ Requesting a contact approach with cloud base below one mile visibility.  
  ✔ Contact approach requires at least one mile flight visibility and clear of clouds at all times.

- ❌ Confusing contact approach with visual approach in readbacks and radio calls.  
  ✔ Use the correct phrase: contact approach or visual approach — they are different procedures.

- ❌ Not reporting the field in sight when requested by ATC after a contact approach approval.  
  ✔ Report field in sight when required: ANAC123, field in sight.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Request Contact Approach Runway One Five

**Pronunciation:** reh-KWEST KON-takt uh-PROCH RUN-way WUN fyv

**Word Stress**

- Request → reh-KWEST (stress on second syllable)
- Contact → KON-takt (stress on first syllable)
- Approach → uh-PROCH
- Runway One Five → RUN-way WUN fyv

Practice:

Request contact approach... runway one five.

Together:

Request contact approach runway one five, ANAC123.

"Contact" in this phrase is the approach type — not a frequency-change instruction. Context makes this clear.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Navegantes Approach transmits: "ANAC123, contact approach approved, runway one five. Maintain clear of cloud and visual reference to the ground. Report field in sight."

**Your first task**

Read back the clearance.

**Your second task**

Three miles from the airport the visibility reduces significantly. You estimate you now have less than one mile. You are still technically clear of cloud. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Contact approach approved, runway one five, ANAC123. If visibility reduced below one mile during the contact approach, I would immediately abandon the contact approach and advise ATC: ANAC123, unable to maintain contact approach, visibility reduced below one mile, request ILS runway one five. The contact approach requires one mile visibility at all times. With less than one mile, I am no longer legally or safely on a contact approach. I must transition to the instrument approach immediately, not attempt to continue visually in reduced conditions with terrain in the vicinity.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Contact approach gives you the efficiency of a visual approach in conditions that are not quite visual.

But it gives you that efficiency by asking you to accept more responsibility.

You own the terrain avoidance. You own the obstacle clearance. You own the weather assessment.

Request it only when conditions genuinely support it.

Monitor visibility and cloud throughout. If conditions deteriorate — advise ATC immediately and fly the instrument approach.

The ILS is always there. Use it when in doubt.
