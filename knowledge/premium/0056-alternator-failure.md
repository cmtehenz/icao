# Alternator Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0056  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers a common electrical systems problem in flight.

Alternator Failure means the alternator has stopped producing electrical power — the aircraft must rely on battery power until the alternator is restored or the flight ends.

In the H130, the alternator charges the battery and powers avionics, instruments, and radios during normal operation.

A failed alternator does not always mean immediate catastrophe — but battery time is limited.

You must aviate first, monitor electrical load, then communicate with the correct urgency call.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Alternator Failure is the loss of alternator output, requiring the aircraft to operate on battery power with reduced electrical endurance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do alternador.

Perda da produção de energia pelo alternador, exigindo operação com bateria e tempo elétrico limitado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Alternator Failure occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- identify the alternator failure annunciation or warning
- reduce non-essential electrical load if possible
- monitor battery voltage and remaining endurance
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday based on severity and battery state

Alternator Failure reporting commonly includes:

- alternator failure or alternator out
- operating on battery power
- request vectors to nearest suitable airport
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested

**Important:** A failed alternator with adequate battery for a safe landing may warrant Pan Pan. Total electrical loss or critically low battery affecting safe flight may require Mayday. Aviate first — then communicate clearly with standard phraseology.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Alternator Failure declarations to:

- clear traffic from the aircraft's route
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable airport
- coordinate priority landing if battery endurance is critical
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- alert airport authorities if distress is declared

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at two thousand feet in your H130 on a charter from Joinville to Navegantes.

The alternator failure warning illuminates and battery voltage begins to drop.

You shed non-essential electrical load and maintain stable flight.

When stable, you transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan, Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, alternator failure, operating on battery power, request vectors to Navegantes.

Approach clears traffic and provides direct routing.

You complete a precautionary landing before battery endurance becomes critical.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say battery endurance and souls on board.
2. Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading zero nine zero, descend at your discretion, Navegantes one five miles.
3. Tower: ANAC123, all traffic cleared from your approach, runway two nine cleared to land.
4. Tower: ANAC123, wind two seven zero at one zero knots, report final.
5. Departure: ANAC123, say nature of electrical problem and intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Alternator failure is often manageable — if you act early.

The sequence every professional pilot learns:

Aviate. Shed. Communicate.

Fly the aircraft first.

Reduce electrical load — radios and essential instruments only.

Monitor battery voltage.

Then declare your urgency.

A healthy battery with a nearby airport — Pan Pan.

Critically low battery or total electrical loss — Mayday.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "alternador queimou" or "caiu o alternador" on frequency.

Use alternator failure or alternator out — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130, know your battery endurance from the flight manual.

Do not wait until the radios fail to call ATC.

Train the reflex so your radio call comes from a stable aircraft with a plan, not a surprised voice.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if your alternator failed during cruise?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After securing the aircraft, I would declare Pan Pan Pan Pan, state my callsign, report alternator failure and that I am operating on battery power, state my position, and request vectors to the nearest suitable airport. I would upgrade to Mayday if battery endurance becomes critical. I would provide souls on board and fuel remaining when ATC requests.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Waiting until the battery is dead before calling ATC.  
  ✔ Declare early — battery endurance is limited.

- ❌ Saying "alternador parou" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use alternator failure or alternator out.

- ❌ Not reducing non-essential electrical load.  
  ✔ Shed load to extend battery endurance.

- ❌ Forgetting to state operating on battery power.  
  ✔ Tell ATC you are on battery — it explains urgency.
