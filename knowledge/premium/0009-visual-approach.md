# Visual Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0009  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers one of the most common clearances issued in good weather.

Many pilots believe a Visual Approach means they are flying under VFR.

That is not correct.

A Visual Approach is still an ATC-authorized approach, and the pilot must continue to comply with all ATC instructions.

Understanding this difference is essential for both the ICAO test and real-world operations.

## 📖 Meaning

A Visual Approach is an ATC-authorized approach conducted with visual reference to the airport or the preceding aircraft while remaining under ATC control.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aproximação visual.

Uma aproximação autorizada pelo controle utilizando referências visuais para prosseguir até o pouso.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

A Visual Approach allows the pilot to complete the approach using visual references instead of flying every segment of the published instrument procedure.

However:

- ATC control continues.
- Separation responsibilities remain as assigned.
- Landing is not authorized until Cleared to Land is issued.

The pilot may be instructed to maintain visual separation from another aircraft or report the airport in sight before receiving the clearance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue a Visual Approach to:

- reduce approach time
- improve airport capacity
- simplify traffic sequencing
- avoid unnecessary instrument procedures
- reduce pilot and controller workload
- maintain efficient traffic flow in good weather

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You're arriving at Florianópolis on a clear afternoon.

Visibility is excellent.

The airport is already in sight more than ten miles away.

Approach says:

"ANAC123, report the airport in sight."

You reply:

"Airport in sight, ANAC123."

A few moments later:

"ANAC123, cleared visual approach runway one four."

You continue visually toward the runway.

Even though you're navigating visually, ATC still controls your arrival.

Only later does Tower transmit:

"ANAC123, runway one four, cleared to land."

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, report airport in sight.
2. Approach: ANAC123, cleared visual approach runway one four.
3. Approach: ANAC123, maintain visual separation from preceding traffic.
4. Tower: ANAC123, continue visual approach.
5. Tower: ANAC123, runway one four, cleared to land.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Airport in sight, ANAC123.
2. Cleared visual approach runway one four, ANAC123.
3. Maintaining visual separation, ANAC123.
4. Continuing visual approach, ANAC123.
5. Cleared to land runway one four, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Imagine you're driving to a friend's house.

Normally, you follow your GPS turn by turn.

But when you finally see the house at the end of the street, you don't need every remaining instruction from the GPS.

You simply continue visually until you arrive.

A Visual Approach works in a similar way.

Instead of following every published instrument segment, you're allowed to continue using visual references.

But here's the important difference:

You're still under ATC control.

The controller is still sequencing traffic.

The controller is still protecting separation.

And you still need a separate landing clearance.

Many ICAO candidates incorrectly think:

"Visual Approach means I can land whenever I want."

That's wrong.

The approach becomes visual.

The rules do not disappear.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When may ATC clear an aircraft for a Visual Approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

ATC may clear an aircraft for a visual approach when weather conditions permit visual navigation and the pilot has the airport or the preceding aircraft in sight. Even during a visual approach, the pilot must continue following ATC instructions and wait for landing clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Confusing Visual Approach with VFR flight.  
  A Visual Approach may occur during an IFR flight.

- ❌ Thinking a Visual Approach includes landing clearance.  
  Landing clearance is issued separately.

- ❌ Forgetting to report: Airport in sight.  
  When ATC requests it, this report is essential.

- ❌ Believing ATC no longer controls the flight.  
  During a Visual Approach, ATC continues providing separation and sequencing unless responsibilities are specifically transferred.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Visual Approach

**Pronunciation:** VIZH-oo-ul uh-PROACH

**Word Stress**

- Visual → stress the first syllable
- Approach → stress the second syllable

Practice:

Visual... Approach...

Now together:

Visual Approach.

Then practice in a full transmission:

Cleared visual approach runway one four, ANAC123.

Keep the rhythm natural and avoid separating the two words excessively.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

Imagine you're hiking with a guide.

At first, you follow the map carefully.

Then you finally see the cabin where you're going.

You stop looking at the map every few seconds because you can already see the destination.

The guide is still with you.

The journey simply became visual.

That's exactly what a Visual Approach is.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

ATC says:

Approach: ANAC123, report the airport in sight.

Pilot: Airport in sight, ANAC123.

Approach: ANAC123, cleared visual approach runway one four.

**Your first task**

Read back the second transmission exactly as a professional pilot.

**Your second task**

Answer this question:

Does a Visual Approach authorize you to land? Explain why.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

No. A Visual Approach only authorizes me to continue the approach using visual references. I must still receive a separate Cleared to Land clearance from Tower before landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Today's lesson teaches one of the most common misconceptions in aviation.

A Visual Approach changes how you navigate the final part of the approach.

It does not change who is controlling the operation.

Remember these three ideas:

- Visual Approach is still an ATC clearance.
- You remain under ATC control.
- You still need Cleared to Land before touching down.

Professional pilots don't confuse seeing the runway with owning the runway. That's one of the habits that keeps every approach safe.
