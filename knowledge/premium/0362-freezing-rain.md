# Freezing Rain

**Catalog ID:** 0362  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Freezing Rain — liquid rain that freezes on contact with cold aircraft surfaces, producing rapid clear ice accumulation that can quickly exceed anti-icing capability and destroy lift and control.

Freezing rain is Critical. The correct operational response is exit and diversion — not prolonged exposure while "monitoring." ICAO English must state freezing rain, request immediate climb, descent, or diversion, and report ice accumulation.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, freezing rain is less common than in high-latitude winters but appears in cold-front events in the South and on ICAO exams. Rotorcraft icing tolerance is limited — treat freezing rain as escape-now weather.

Understanding freezing rain phraseology is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Freezing Rain is rain that falls as liquid through a warm layer and freezes upon contact with surfaces at or below freezing, causing rapid ice accretion on the airframe and rotors, requiring immediate exit from the conditions and usually diversion to warmer or clear air.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Chuva congelante / Freezing Rain.

Chuva que cai como líquido através de uma camada quente e congela ao contato com superfícies em temperatura igual ou inferior a zero, causando acúmulo rápido de gelo na célula e nos rotores, exigindo saída imediata das condições e geralmente desvio para ar mais quente ou limpo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When freezing rain is encountered or reported, the crew should:

- treat the condition as Critical icing — exit immediately
- request climb or descent to find above-freezing or dry air as appropriate
- activate ice protection systems if installed and within limits
- divert to an airport clear of freezing precipitation
- report freezing rain and ice accretion to ATC
- do not hold in freezing rain to "see how it develops"

Freezing rain reporting commonly includes:

- freezing rain at altitude
- moderate or severe icing in freezing rain
- request immediate climb / descent / diversion
- unable to continue present altitude

**Important:** Freezing rain can overwhelm even equipped aircraft. Helicopters are especially vulnerable. The radio call must sound urgent and clear — this is not routine weather chatter.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to freezing rain reports to:

- warn other aircraft with PIREPs and altitude advice
- approve immediate altitude changes and diversions
- keep holding stacks out of freezing precipitation
- coordinate runway contamination and braking action on the ground
- update approach sequencing when icing forces multiple diversions

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Descending through four thousand feet toward Porto Alegre in the H130, you enter rain and ice begins forming rapidly on the windscreen and airframe. OAT is below freezing.

You report freezing rain and severe icing, request immediate descent to warmer air or diversion. Approach clears a priority divert to a field reporting above-freezing surface temperatures. You exit the freezing rain layer and land with ice still visible — but controllable.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, freezing rain reported at four thousand, advise.
2. Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain six thousand, warmer air reported.
3. Center: ANAC123, divert approved, say souls on board if declaring urgency.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say icing intensity and altitude.
5. Tower: ANAC123, runway contaminated, braking action reported medium.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, freezing rain at four thousand, request immediate descent.
2. ANAC123, severe icing in freezing rain, request diversion.
3. Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, freezing rain, severe icing, request priority landing.
4. ANAC123, exiting freezing rain, ice on airframe, continuing divert.
5. ANAC123, unable hold, freezing rain, request vectors clear of precipitation.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Freezing rain is a clock, not a debate.

Ice builds in minutes. Leave the altitude. Leave the area.

Brazilian pilots may say "chuva gelada" — use freezing rain, the METAR/ICAO term.

In the H130, you may have little or no certified flight-into-known-icing capability. Freezing rain is not a place to prove toughness. Escape and speak.

Critical priority on the catalog matches Critical priority in the cockpit.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You encounter freezing rain and ice forms quickly. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report freezing rain and the icing intensity at my altitude, request an immediate climb or descent out of the conditions, and request diversion if needed. If control or performance becomes threatened I would declare urgency or distress and request priority landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Staying in freezing rain to complete the approach.  
  ✔ Exit immediately — divert if necessary.

- ❌ Saying only "gelo" without freezing rain.  
  ✔ Name freezing rain so ATC understands the mechanism.

- ❌ Accepting a hold in known freezing precipitation.  
  ✔ Unable hold — request vectors clear of freezing rain.

- ❌ Soft language when ice is rapid.  
  ✔ Use immediate and, if needed, Pan Pan or Mayday.
