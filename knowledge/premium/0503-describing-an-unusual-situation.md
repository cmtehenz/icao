# Describing an Unusual Situation

**Catalog ID:** 0503  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Describing an Unusual Situation — the ICAO oral exam task in which you explain an abnormal, unexpected, or non-routine aviation event in clear plain English at Level 4 or above.

Unusual situation description is not panic speech. It is organized reporting.

In Part 1, the examiner may show a picture or describe a scenario — smoke, a bird strike, a passenger medical event, an open panel, weather that forces a change — and ask you to say what is happening, why it matters, and what you would do. Structure and calm vocabulary are the scoring targets.

For H130 ICAO Delta crews, this task mirrors real cockpit communication: state the problem, assess impact, state the plan.

Understanding how to describe an unusual situation in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Describing an Unusual Situation is the ICAO speaking task type in which the candidate explains a non-routine aviation event — identifying what is wrong or unexpected, describing observable effects, and outlining immediate actions — so that clarity, vocabulary, and problem-oriented speech can be assessed at Operational Level 4 or higher.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When describing an unusual situation, the candidate should:

- name the problem in the first sentence
- describe what you see, hear, or smell — concrete evidence
- state the operational impact: safety, performance, passengers, continuation
- outline immediate actions in order
- use plain English before rare technical terms
- for H130: include helicopter-specific effects when relevant — vibration, rotor, confined area
- avoid dramatizing; stay factual and sequenced

A strong unusual-situation description usually follows:

- what happened / what is unusual
- where and when in the flight phase
- effects on the aircraft or crew
- immediate actions already taken or planned
- what you would tell ATC or the examiner next

**Important:** Examiners score clarity under pressure. A calm three-part answer — problem, impact, action — outperforms a long emotional narrative with no structure.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Unusual-situation speaking tasks help examiners and controllers to:

- assess whether a pilot can report abnormalities clearly
- elicit problem-solving language without a full emergency script
- test prioritization: what matters first
- prepare candidates for real radiotelephony of non-normal events
- distinguish Level 4 operational English from memorized Mayday lines alone

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

The examiner describes: During cruise in an H130, you smell electrical smoke in the cabin. No fire warning light yet. One passenger is coughing.

You organize: The unusual situation is a possible electrical smoke smell in the cabin during cruise. The impact is passenger discomfort and a potential fire risk. Immediate actions: don oxygen if required by procedure, locate the source, run the smoke checklist, prepare to land as soon as practical, and advise ATC.

You speak in short sentences. You do not invent a full Mayday unless the scenario escalates. The examiner hears control, not chaos.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, report the nature of your problem.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm smoke in the cabin, say intentions.
3. Tower: ANAC123, emergency services are available, advise souls on board.
4. Examiner: ANAC123, describe the unusual situation and your first actions.
5. Radar: ANAC123, squawk 7700 if required, say again your request.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, possible electrical smoke in cabin, requesting priority landing.
2. ANAC123, confirming smoke smell, intentions are divert to nearest suitable.
3. ANAC123, souls on board four, requesting emergency services on arrival.
4. ANAC123, unusual situation is cabin smoke smell, running checklist, requesting vectors.
5. ANAC123, squawking 7700, request immediate landing Congonhas.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Unusual situation description is checklist thinking in English.

First: name it. Second: say what it does to the flight. Third: say what you will do. That order is Level 4 gold.

Candidates often start in the middle — "we need to land" — without saying why. Examiners then ask again. You lose fluency points to repair.

Use connectors that show control: first, then, because, so. Not poetry — sequence.

If the picture is ambiguous, say what is clear and what is uncertain: "There appears to be smoke near the panel, but I cannot confirm fire." That honesty is professional.

In the H130 you already practice non-normals with callouts. Translate those callouts into full sentences for the exam without losing the priority order.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You notice an unusual vibration after takeoff in the H130. How do you describe the situation at Level 4?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The unusual situation is abnormal vibration after takeoff. It may indicate a rotor, engine, or airframe issue. The impact is that continued climb may be unsafe if vibration increases. My first actions would be to check engine parameters, reduce power if required by procedure, avoid abrupt maneuvers, and prepare for a return or precautionary landing. I would advise ATC with a clear problem statement and intentions, then follow the checklist.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Jumping to solutions without naming the problem.  
  ✔ State the unusual condition first, then actions.

- ❌ Using only dramatic words: disaster, catastrophe.  
  ✔ Use precise operational language: abnormal, smoke, vibration.

- ❌ Mixing past and present randomly with no timeline.  
  ✔ Keep a clear sequence: what is happening now, then next steps.

- ❌ Forgetting impact on safety or continuation.  
  ✔ Always add why the situation matters operationally.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

The examiner shows a picture: oil streak on the H130 cowling after shutdown, ground crew pointing, no fire visible.

**Your first task**

Describe the unusual situation in three sentences: problem, evidence, impact.

**Your second task**

State what you would tell ATC if this were discovered just after landing. Give the call.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: The unusual situation is a possible oil leak on the engine cowling. Ground crew are pointing at an oil streak, and there is no visible fire. The impact is that the aircraft may be unserviceable until maintenance inspects it. Second task: ANAC123, possible oil leak observed after landing, requesting maintenance assistance, remaining on the pad. Clear, calm, complete.

Name the problem. Show the evidence. State the impact. Give the actions.
