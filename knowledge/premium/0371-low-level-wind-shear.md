# Low-Level Wind Shear

**Catalog ID:** 0371  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Low-Level Wind Shear — a sudden change in wind speed or direction near the ground that can critically affect takeoff, approach, and hover performance.

Low-level wind shear is one of the most dangerous weather threats to helicopters. A rapid loss of headwind or a downdraft on short final can drive the H130 below the approach path faster than collective can recover, especially near maximum power.

For Brazilian operations — thunderstorm outflows at Congonhas, dry-season gust fronts, coastal sea-breeze boundaries, and microburst-prone afternoons — crews must recognize shear cues, go around early, and report clearly to ATC.

Understanding low-level wind shear phraseology is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Low-Level Wind Shear is a sudden variation in wind velocity — speed and/or direction — within a few hundred feet of the surface, often associated with thunderstorms, frontal boundaries, or terrain, capable of causing abrupt airspeed and flight-path deviations during takeoff, approach, or landing.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Cisalhamento do vento em baixo nível / Low-Level Wind Shear.

Variação súbita da velocidade do vento — intensidade e/ou direção — a poucas centenas de pés da superfície, frequentemente associada a tempestades, frentes ou terreno, capaz de causar desvios abruptos de velocidade e trajetória durante decolagem, aproximação ou pouso.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When low-level wind shear is suspected or encountered, the crew should:

- avoid approach and departure through known shear, microbursts, or thunderstorm outflows
- go around at the first sign of uncontrolled airspeed or sink rate near the ground
- apply maximum power and climb attitude if caught in a downdraft, per training
- delay takeoff or landing until the threat has passed
- report wind shear to ATC with location and severity if able
- do not attempt another approach into the same cell or gust front

Low-level wind shear reporting commonly includes:

- wind shear on final / on departure
- airspeed loss or gain of a stated amount
- going around due wind shear
- request delay

**Important:** Avoidance is the primary defense. The H130 cannot out-climb a strong microburst. If Tower reports low-level wind shear or you see virga and blowing dust under a cell — hold, divert, or delay.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use low-level wind shear reports to:

- warn arriving and departing aircraft in real time
- suspend departures and approaches when shear is reported
- sequence aircraft away from thunderstorm outflows
- relay PIREPs of airspeed loss or gain on final
- coordinate runway changes when wind shifts violently

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on short final to Congonhas runway one seven Right. A cell is west of the field. Airspeed drops fifteen knots; sink rate increases despite collective.

You transmit:

Congonhas Tower, ANAC123, going around, low-level wind shear on final, airspeed loss one five knots.

Tower: ANAC123, roger wind shear, climb runway heading, wind shear alert all aircraft.

You climb away and hold until the outflow passes — or divert.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, wind shear alert, loss of two zero knots reported on final runway one seven.
2. Tower: ANAC123, roger go-around, climb runway heading three thousand.
3. Approach: ANAC123, hold as published, low-level wind shear reported at Congonhas.
4. Tower: ANAC123, departure delay, wind shear on runway, advise ready when conditions improve.
5. Tower: ANAC123, say wind shear intensity and altitude if able.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Low-level wind shear does not negotiate with helicopter power margins.

On short final you are slow, low, and committed to a path. A sudden headwind loss removes lift. A downdraft removes altitude. Together they remove options.

Brazilian summer convection around São Paulo and Brasília produces classic outflow shear. See the cell. See the dust. Hear the alert. Do not continue.

Go around at the first uncontrolled deviation. Report airspeed loss if you can — that number saves the next crew.

Never take off into a reported low-level wind shear alert "to see." The departure path is just as lethal as the arrival.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

On final your airspeed drops suddenly and you cannot maintain the glide path. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would execute an immediate go-around, apply maximum power as required, and climb away from the shear. I would report low-level wind shear to ATC with the airspeed loss if known, and I would not attempt another approach until conditions improve or I divert. I would treat thunderstorm outflow and microburst cues as reasons to delay or hold. In the H130 near the ground, avoidance and early go-around are safer than trying to fight a strong shear.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing the approach after a significant airspeed loss "to recover."  
  ✔ Go around immediately; report wind shear.

- ❌ Saying only "unstable" without wind shear.  
  ✔ Use: low-level wind shear, airspeed loss [number] knots.

- ❌ Taking off under a wind shear alert to save time.  
  ✔ Delay departure until the alert is cancelled or conditions improve.

- ❌ Returning for another approach into the same cell.  
  ✔ Hold or divert; wait for the outflow to clear.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Low-level wind shear is critical weather near the ground.

Avoid. Go around. Report. Delay.

In the H130, power available is finite. Shear can demand more than you have.

Brazilian convective afternoons will keep testing this. The professional answer is always the same: do not fight the outflow on short final — leave, tell ATC, and wait for safe air.
