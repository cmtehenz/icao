# Vestibular Illusion

**Catalog ID:** 0478  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Vestibular Illusion — false sensations of motion or attitude generated by the inner ear's vestibular system, especially when visual references are absent or degraded.

Vestibular illusions are the physiological engine behind many spatial disorientation events. The semicircular canals and otolith organs sense acceleration, not absolute attitude. In IMC or darkness, that limitation becomes a trap.

In H130 flight — hovering transitions, accelerating departures, and turning in cloud — vestibular illusions can create false banks, false climbs, and false levels. ICAO English must support clear instrument-recovery communication for Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

A Vestibular Illusion is a misleading perception of aircraft motion or attitude caused by vestibular system responses to angular or linear acceleration, occurring particularly when outside visual references are insufficient to correct the false sensation.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Ilusão vestibular / Vestibular Illusion.

Percepção enganosa de movimento ou atitude da aeronave causada por respostas do sistema vestibular a acelerações angulares ou lineares, ocorrendo especialmente quando as referências visuais externas são insuficientes para corrigir a sensação falsa.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a vestibular illusion is recognized, the crew should:

- cross-check attitude instruments immediately
- avoid making control inputs based solely on bodily sensation
- reduce unnecessary head movements during IMC recovery
- use autopilot or attitude-hold functions if available and appropriate
- report to ATC if a temporary deviation or climb is needed to regain a stable instrument scan

Vestibular risk management includes:

- briefing illusion threats before night or IMC sectors
- limiting aggressive acceleration and steep turns without instrument cross-check
- maintaining a disciplined instrument scan during prolonged turns
- recognizing common illusions: leans, somatogravic, coriolis

**Important:** Closing your eyes makes vestibular illusions worse, not better. Open the instrument scan. The cure is visual truth from the panel, not from the windshield when the windshield shows nothing reliable.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers assist crews affected by vestibular illusions by:

- issuing simple attitude-stabilizing clearances — climb, maintain, steady heading
- providing radar vectors to VMC when available
- avoiding rapid successive heading changes during recovery
- granting priority if the crew declares disorientation
- confirming altitude when an unexpected profile change appears

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

In IMC near São Paulo, you complete a prolonged right turn. When you roll out, your body still feels left-wing-down. You start to correct right — into a real bank.

Your first officer stops you: "Wings level on instruments."

You hold the attitude indicator and call:

ANAC123, vestibular illusion, maintaining present altitude and heading, request straight ahead for one minute.

Radar: ANAC123, maintain heading and altitude, report when ready for turn.

You respond:

ANAC123, maintaining, will report ready.

You wait until the sensation fades and the scan is stable.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, maintain present heading and altitude.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm wings level, you appear in a turn.
3. Radar: ANAC123, fly heading three six zero, gentle turn.
4. Control: ANAC123, climb and maintain four thousand, report established straight and level.
5. Approach: ANAC123, vectors to VMC available, say intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The vestibular system is a motion detector, not an attitude instrument.

After a turn, it may still scream that you are banked when you are level. After acceleration, it may scream climb when you are not.

In the H130, teach your hands to follow the attitude indicator even when your stomach argues. That argument is the illusion.

Straight and level on instruments is the reset button.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

After a long IMC turn you feel banked while instruments show wings level. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would trust the attitude indicator, hold wings level, and avoid "correcting" into a real bank. I would advise ATC if I need time: ANAC123, vestibular illusion, maintaining present heading and altitude, request straight ahead. I would resume turns only when the instrument scan is stable.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Correcting a false bank sensation after rollout.  
  ✔ Hold instrument wings-level until the sensation fades.

- ❌ Making rapid head movements while recovering in IMC.  
  ✔ Keep the head steady and the scan disciplined.

- ❌ Explaining the physiology at length on frequency.  
  ✔ State the need simply: maintaining, request straight ahead.

- ❌ Re-entering aggressive maneuvering immediately after the illusion.  
  ✔ Stabilize first; accept gentle vectors only when ready.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Vestibular illusions are predictable physiology, not personal failure.

Anticipate them in IMC. Defeat them with instruments. Buy time from ATC when the scan needs a reset.

In the H130, level on the panel beats level in your head — every time.
