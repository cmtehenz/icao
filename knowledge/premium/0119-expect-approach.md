# Expect Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0119  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Expect Approach — an ATC advisory that tells you which approach type to anticipate before you receive the final clearance.

Expect Approach is planning information.

It is not yet a clearance.

But it is essential for briefing, configuring, and managing fuel and time.

In the H130, flying into Curitiba or Florianópolis in reduced visibility, knowing which approach to expect allows you to brief the procedure before you reach the IAF.

This lesson prepares you to receive, read back, and use Expect Approach instructions effectively.

## 📖 Meaning

Expect Approach is an ATC advisory that informs a pilot of the approach type anticipated for the destination, used for flight planning and crew briefing purposes before the final approach clearance is issued.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Espera aproximação (tipo).

Informação do controle de tráfego aéreo que antecipa o tipo de aproximação previsto para o destino, usada para planejamento e briefing da tripulação antes da autorização de aproximação final.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a pilot receives an Expect Approach advisory, they should:

- note the approach type and runway
- brief the approach procedure immediately
- set navigation aids or load the procedure in the FMS
- review minima and required visibility
- advise ATC if the expected approach is unacceptable

Expect Approach advisories commonly include:

- approach type (ILS, RNAV, VOR, visual)
- runway number
- timing indicator such as expect in ten minutes

**Important:** Expect Approach is advisory, not a clearance. Do not begin the approach until you receive a formal approach clearance. However, brief and configure as if the expected approach will be assigned.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Expect Approach advisories to:

- allow flight crews to brief the approach in advance
- support planning when holds or delays are possible
- improve efficiency during busy arrival sequences
- ensure crew readiness before issuing the clearance
- coordinate approach type across multiple inbound aircraft

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are seventy miles from Curitiba, descending from cruise altitude in your H130.

Weather has deteriorated.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, expect ILS approach runway one five, expect approach in fifteen minutes.

You immediately brief the ILS runway one five procedure:

You review the chart, set the localizer frequency, confirm the glide slope, and review the missed approach.

You respond:

Expect ILS approach runway one five, ANAC123.

Fifteen minutes later, Approach clears you for the ILS.

You are fully briefed.

No rush. No surprise. Just professionalism.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, expect ILS approach runway one five, expect approach in fifteen minutes.
2. Approach: ANAC123, expect visual approach runway one five, advise ready.
3. Approach: ANAC123, expect RNAV approach runway two seven, report reaching flight level one zero zero.
4. Approach: ANAC123, expect approach, stand by for type confirmation.
5. Approach: ANAC123, expect ILS approach, hold as published, expect approach in ten minutes.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Expect ILS approach runway one five, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, expect visual approach runway one five, copied.
3. Expect RNAV approach runway two seven, ANAC123.
4. ANAC123, copied, expect approach in fifteen minutes.
5. ANAC123, expect ILS, holding as published, wilco.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Expect Approach gives you a head start.

The approach clearance may come in five minutes, or twenty.

But the moment you hear "expect ILS runway one five," the briefing clock starts.

Pull the chart. Review the procedure. Set the frequencies. Confirm the minima. Brief the missed approach.

When ATC says "cleared ILS runway one five," your only job is to fly it — because you already briefed it.

Brazilian pilots sometimes wait for the clearance before opening the chart.

That is too late. Brief on the advisory.

In the H130, the avionics are straightforward. But the discipline of early briefing applies to every aircraft, every approach.

If the expected approach changes — and it sometimes does — you have time to re-brief.

But you can only re-brief what you already briefed.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do after receiving an Expect Approach advisory from ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After receiving the Expect Approach advisory, I would read it back to ATC and immediately brief the expected approach procedure. I would set navigation aids, review the minima and missed approach procedure, and ensure the aircraft was configured and ready before the clearance was issued. The advisory gives me the opportunity to prepare before reaching the initial approach fix.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating Expect Approach as an approach clearance and beginning the procedure.  
  ✔ Expect Approach is advisory. Wait for the formal clearance before beginning.

- ❌ Waiting to brief the approach until after the clearance is received.  
  ✔ Brief immediately upon receiving the Expect Approach advisory.

- ❌ Not reading back the runway number.  
  ✔ Include the runway in the readback: Expect ILS approach runway one five, ANAC123.

- ❌ Forgetting to advise ATC if the expected approach is not acceptable.  
  ✔ If unable to accept the expected approach type, advise ATC immediately.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Expect Approach

**Pronunciation:** eks-PEKT uh-PROHCH

**Word Stress**

- Expect → eks-PEKT (stress on second syllable)
- Approach → uh-PROHCH (stress on second syllable)

Practice:

Expect... Approach... Expect Approach.

Together:

Expect ILS approach runway one five, ANAC123.

Avoid: *expec aproche* — pronounce both syllables of each word fully.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**BRIEF** — **B**rief on the advisory, **R**eview chart and minima, **I**nstruments set before clearance, **E**xpect may change, **F**ly when cleared.
