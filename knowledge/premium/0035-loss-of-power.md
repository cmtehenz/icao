# Loss of Power

**Catalog ID:** 0035  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers a critical distinction in emergency phraseology.

Loss of Power is not always the same as complete engine failure.

It may mean partial power loss — the engine is still running, but not producing enough thrust to maintain altitude or complete the flight as planned.

Reporting loss of power accurately helps ATC understand your situation and provide appropriate assistance.

This is essential for ICAO Part 2 vocabulary and Part 3 emergency communication — especially in helicopter operations where power margins are narrow.

## 📖 Meaning

Loss of Power is a reduction or partial loss of engine thrust output, which may or may not result in complete engine failure.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Perda de potência.

Redução da potência do motor — parcial ou completa — afetando a capacidade de manter altitude ou voo planejado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Loss of Power occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control
- assess whether altitude can be maintained
- identify which engine is affected if multi-engine
- declare Pan Pan for urgency or Mayday for distress as appropriate
- inform ATC of loss of power, ability to maintain altitude, and intentions

Loss of Power reporting commonly includes:

- we have a loss of power on engine two
- unable to maintain flight level, request lower
- Pan Pan Pan, loss of power, maintaining altitude
- MAYDAY for complete loss of power when safe flight is in doubt

**Important:** Distinguish partial loss of power from complete engine failure. Tell ATC whether you can maintain altitude — this changes the urgency level.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Loss of Power reports to:

- assign lower altitudes or vectors as needed
- clear traffic from the aircraft's route
- coordinate priority landing when required
- request which engine and ability to maintain altitude
- alert emergency services if the situation deteriorates

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at two thousand feet in your H130 near Florianópolis when engine power drops unexpectedly.

The engine is still running, but you cannot maintain altitude.

You declare urgency:

Pan Pan Pan, Florianópolis Approach, ANAC123, loss of power, unable to maintain altitude, descending.

Approach: ANAC123, say which engine and ability to maintain altitude.

You reply:

ANAC123, single engine helicopter, loss of power, unable to maintain two thousand feet, descending to nearest suitable area, request vectors.

Approach provides vectors and priority handling toward the airport.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, say which engine and ability to maintain altitude.
2. Approach: ANAC123, descend at pilot's discretion.
3. Approach: ANAC123, vectors to the nearest suitable airport.
4. Tower: ANAC123, all traffic cleared from your route.
5. Tower: ANAC123, runway one four cleared to land, wind calm.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Imagine your car engine sputtering on a highway hill.

It is still running — but you are slowing down.

That is loss of power.

Complete engine failure is the engine stopping entirely.

The distinction matters to ATC.

If you can maintain altitude, Pan Pan may be appropriate.

If you cannot, the situation may escalate to Mayday.

In the H130, even partial power loss can become critical quickly.

Always tell ATC: able or unable to maintain altitude.

Brazilian pilots sometimes confuse loss of power with electrical failure or say "no power."

Use loss of power — and quantify your situation.

Partial or complete. Able or unable. Which engine if applicable.

Precision helps controllers help you.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How would you describe loss of power versus complete engine failure?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Loss of power means reduced engine thrust — the engine may still be running but not producing enough power. Engine failure means the engine has stopped producing usable thrust. I would report loss of power when thrust is reduced, and engine failure when the engine is not producing usable power. I also state whether I can maintain altitude.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "no power" instead of loss of power.  
  ✔ Use standard phraseology: loss of power.

- ❌ Not specifying ability to maintain altitude.  
  ✔ Report able or unable to maintain altitude clearly.

- ❌ Confusing loss of power with electrical power loss.  
  ✔ Loss of power refers to engine thrust, not electrical systems.

- ❌ Using loss of power and engine failure interchangeably.  
  ✔ Be precise — partial loss versus complete failure.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Word Stress**

- Loss → one syllable, clear S
- Power → POW-er
- Maintain → second syllable (main-TAIN)
- Altitude → AL-ti-tude
