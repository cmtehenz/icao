# Overweight Landing

**Catalog ID:** 0293  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Overweight Landing — landing above the maximum certified landing weight, usually after an early return, diversion, or emergency when fuel dumping or burn-off is impossible or incomplete. For H130 operations, strict airline-style overweight landings are less common than on large jets, but the ICAO English, ATC coordination, and inspection-after-landing logic remain High-priority exam and professional skills — and heavy returns with limited fuel burn still apply.

Overweight landing is a High Part 2 and Part 3 topic. Controllers need to know you will land heavy so RFF can stand by and the runway can be inspected after rollout.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master the phraseology, then transfer the same clear "landing overweight, request inspection" structure to helicopter heavy returns when relevant.

Understanding overweight landing in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Overweight Landing is a landing performed above the aircraft's maximum certified landing weight, typically after an emergency return or diversion when fuel cannot be reduced sufficiently, requiring ATC notification, possible emergency services standby, a careful landing technique, and post-landing inspection of the landing gear and structure as required by the AFM and operator procedures.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pouso com peso acima do máximo / Overweight Landing.

Pouso realizado acima do peso máximo certificado de pouso da aeronave, tipicamente após retorno de emergência ou desvio quando o combustível não pode ser reduzido o suficiente, exigindo notificação ao ATC, possível prontidão dos serviços de emergência, técnica de pouso cuidadosa e inspeção pós-pouso do trem de pouso e da estrutura conforme o AFM e os procedimentos do operador.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an overweight landing is required, the crew should:

- determine landing weight versus maximum landing weight
- declare the situation: overweight landing / landing above maximum landing weight
- request runway of suitable length and emergency services if required by procedure
- fly a stabilized approach with appropriate touchdown technique per AFM
- after landing: request runway inspection if required; do not taxi until cleared if structural check is needed
- for H130: apply exam vocabulary; transfer "heavy landing / inspection required" clarity when weight or hard landing risk exists

Overweight landing reporting commonly includes:

- overweight landing / landing overweight
- reason: emergency return / diversion / unable to dump fuel
- request firefighters standby if required
- request runway inspection after landing
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** Overweight landing is planned communication, not a surprise for Tower on short final. Advise early. After landing, assume inspection may be required before further taxi or flight.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use overweight landing reports to:

- select a suitable runway and keep it clear
- alert RFF to stand by for possible gear or brake issues
- plan runway inspection after the landing
- sequence traffic to avoid pressure on an abnormal approach
- coordinate with airport operations for weight-related inspections

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: you return shortly after departure with a systems problem. Landing weight is above maximum landing weight. No fuel dump system.

You transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, returning, overweight landing, request longest runway and firefighters standing by.

Approach sequences you. Tower confirms RFF ready. You fly a smooth touchdown.

After landing:

ANAC123, overweight landing complete, request runway inspection, remaining on runway.

For H130 reality, you may not quote jet landing weights — but you still report abnormal landing conditions and request inspection when the AFM or event requires it.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan overweight landing, expect runway two eight left, emergency services alerted.
2. Tower: ANAC123, firefighters standing by, wind three zero zero at eight, cleared to land.
3. Tower: ANAC123, after landing remain on the runway for inspection.
4. Tower: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Ground: ANAC123, runway inspection in progress, hold position.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Overweight landing is honesty about weight and risk.

If you will land heavy, say so early. Ask for the suitable runway. Ask for firefighters if procedure calls for it.

Fly smooth. Do not float forever chasing a perfect touchdown while eating runway.

After landing, request inspection when required. Do not taxi away from a possible gear problem because you are embarrassed about the weight.

For ICAO exams, speak the classic overweight return cleanly. For the H130, transfer the same discipline to any landing that needs RFF standby and post-landing check.

Weight is a number. Communication is the safety net.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You must return and land above maximum landing weight. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would advise ATC that I am returning and will make an overweight landing, request a suitable runway and firefighters standing by if required, then after landing request a runway or aircraft inspection as per procedures and remain on the runway until cleared to taxi.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Landing overweight without telling ATC until after touchdown.  
  ✔ Advise overweight landing on the approach frequency early.

- ❌ Taxiing clear immediately when inspection is required.  
  ✔ Remain on the runway until inspection is complete if so instructed.

- ❌ Flying an unstable approach because "we are heavy and in a hurry."  
  ✔ Heavy landings need the most stable approach you can fly.

- ❌ Treating the topic as irrelevant to helicopters and skipping the English.  
  ✔ Learn exam phraseology; transfer inspection logic to H130 abnormal landings.
