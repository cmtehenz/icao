# Immediate Takeoff

**Catalog ID:** 0116  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Immediate Takeoff — the operational concept behind urgency at the holding point.

Immediate Takeoff is not just a clearance phrase.

It is a state of readiness.

When ATC offers or expects an immediate departure, the pilot must be fully prepared to roll without delay — before entering the runway.

In the H130, operating from busy airports like Congonhas or Joinville, this concept can determine whether you make the gap in traffic or wait several minutes.

This lesson is distinct from Cleared for Immediate Takeoff (0115), which covers the specific clearance phrase. Here, you learn the urgency concept — what it means to be ready, what to do, and when to decline.

## 📖 Meaning

Immediate Takeoff is the operational concept requiring a pilot to enter the active runway and begin the takeoff roll without any delay upon receiving the corresponding clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

O conceito operacional que exige que o piloto ingresse na pista ativa e inicie a corrida de decolagem sem qualquer atraso ao receber a autorização correspondente.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Immediate Takeoff is expected or offered, the pilot must:

- have completed all before-takeoff checks before reaching the holding point
- be in position to enter the runway and roll without stopping
- confirm the aircraft is fully configured and ready for departure
- accept or decline before entering the runway, not after

Immediate Takeoff readiness commonly includes:

- engine at correct power setting or ready for application
- all checklists complete at the holding point
- crew briefed and ready
- no pending system issues

**Important:** If you are not ready for an immediate departure, you must say so before entering the runway. Accepting and then stopping on the runway creates a far greater hazard than simply declining.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers offer or require Immediate Takeoff to:

- use a gap in the traffic sequence
- maintain runway efficiency during peak traffic
- avoid holding a departing aircraft while an arrival is on short final
- sequence departures tightly when workload demands
- offer an opportunity that expires in seconds

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are at Joinville holding short of runway 15 in your H130.

An arrival is on a two-mile final.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, can you accept immediate departure?

You have completed your before-takeoff checks.

You are ready.

You respond:

Affirmative, ANAC123, ready immediate.

Tower clears you without hesitation, the departure is made in the gap, and the arrival lands normally behind you.

Had you hesitated — had the checks been incomplete — you would have said:

Negative, not ready for immediate, ANAC123.

One extra minute on the ground. No risk to anyone.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, can you accept immediate departure runway one five?
2. Tower: ANAC123, immediate departure available, advise ready.
3. Tower: ANAC123, if immediate not possible, hold position, traffic on two-mile final.
4. Tower: ANAC123, immediate departure approved, traffic on short final.
5. Tower: ANAC123, roger, unable immediate, expect departure in four minutes.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Affirmative, ready immediate, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, ready for immediate departure.
3. Negative, not ready for immediate, ANAC123.
4. ANAC123, unable immediate, request one minute.
5. Ready immediate, ANAC123, holding short runway one five.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Immediate Takeoff is a test of preparation, not boldness.

Confident pilots who say yes when they are ready — that is professionalism.

Equally confident pilots who say no when they are not ready — that is also professionalism.

The mistake is neither accepting nor declining.

The mistake is saying yes when you are not actually ready, then fumbling through a checklist while an arrival is on short final.

Prepare everything before you reach the holding point.

That way, when Tower says "can you accept immediate?" your answer is already known.

In the H130, the before-takeoff checks are short. If you complete them at the run-up area, you arrive at the holding point ready for anything.

Brazilian pilots sometimes feel social pressure to accept immediate to "help the flow."

Do not accept it to please the controller.

Accept it because you are ready.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How would you respond if ATC asked whether you could accept an immediate departure and you were not fully ready?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would tell the controller I was unable to accept an immediate departure and request additional time to complete my before-takeoff checks. Accepting immediate when not ready creates a hazard on the runway, so the professional answer is to decline clearly and give ATC an estimate of how long I would need.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting immediate without having completed the before-takeoff checklist.  
  ✔ All checks must be complete before the holding point when immediate is possible.

- ❌ Saying "not ready" after entering the runway.  
  ✔ Decline before entering. Once on the runway, you are committed.

- ❌ Confusing Immediate Takeoff (readiness concept) with Cleared for Immediate Takeoff (the clearance phrase).  
  ✔ This lesson is about the urgency concept and pilot readiness, not the clearance itself.

- ❌ Feeling embarrassed to say "unable immediate."  
  ✔ Declining is correct and professional when you are not ready.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Ready Immediate

**Pronunciation:** REH-dee ih-MEE-dee-ut

**Word Stress**

- Ready → REH-dee
- Immediate → ih-MEE-dee-ut (four syllables, stress on second)

Practice:

Ready... Immediate... Ready Immediate.

Together:

Affirmative, ready immediate, ANAC123.

Avoid: *ready imediatly* — that is not a word. Say immediate.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**PREP** — **P**repare before the hold, **R**eady means NOW, **E**ntrance is commitment, **P**olitely decline if not set.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Tower at Joinville asks: "ANAC123, can you accept immediate departure runway one five?"

**Your first task**

Your before-takeoff checklist is complete and you are ready. What do you say?

**Your second task**

You realize your transponder mode has not been set. You are not ready. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If ready, I would respond: Affirmative, ready immediate, ANAC123. If not ready, I would say: Negative, not ready for immediate, request one minute, ANAC123. The important principle is that readiness must be genuine — not assumed.

It is about preparation.

Complete your checks before the hold.

Know your answer before Tower asks.

Say yes when you are ready.

Say no when you are not.

That is what professional pilots do.
