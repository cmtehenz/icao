# Cleared to Deviate

**Catalog ID:** 0105  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers cleared to deviate — the ATC clearance authorizing an aircraft to depart from its assigned route or track to avoid weather, traffic, or other hazards.

Cleared to Deviate is distinct from cleared to divert — deviation is a lateral or vertical departure from the assigned route, not a change of destination.

In the H130, route deviations for weather avoidance are common between Florianópolis, Congonhas, and Curitiba when convective weather develops along the coast.

You must know how to request a deviation, state the direction and extent, and keep ATC informed of your intentions throughout.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 critical phraseology.

## 📖 Meaning

Cleared to Deviate is an ATC clearance authorizing the pilot to depart from the assigned route, altitude, or flight path to avoid weather, terrain, traffic, or other hazards, with the expectation of returning to the assigned route when the hazard is clear.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorizado desvio de rota (cleared to deviate).

Autorização do ATC para que o piloto se afaste da rota, altitude ou trajetória de voo atribuída para evitar condições meteorológicas, terreno, tráfego ou outros perigos, com expectativa de retorno à rota atribuída assim que o perigo estiver livre.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Cleared to Deviate is issued or requested, the pilot should:

- acknowledge with the direction and extent of deviation
- advise ATC when back on the assigned route
- maintain assigned altitude unless a climb or descent is also approved
- report the nature of the hazard being avoided
- request return to route when deviation is complete

Cleared to Deviate reporting commonly includes:

- request deviation left or right of track for weather
- advise extent of deviation required
- report returning to assigned route
- state nature of hazard — convective activity, turbulence
- request altitude change if deviation requires altitude adjustment

**Important:** Cleared to deviate is route flexibility, not a new clearance to anywhere. You are expected to return to your assigned route. Advise ATC when back on track.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC issues Cleared to Deviate to:

- allow weather avoidance without full re-routing
- maintain traffic separation while permitting deviation
- accommodate radar advisories around convective activity
- respond quickly to pilot requests for flexibility
- manage traffic flow while pilots avoid hazards

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are transiting south along the coast in your H130, en route from Florianópolis to Navegantes.

Convective weather has built ahead of your track with heavy rain and embedded thunderstorms.

You transmit:

Florianópolis Control, ANAC123, request deviation right of track, weather ahead, estimate ten miles right.

Control responds:

ANAC123, cleared to deviate right, no traffic, report back on track.

You deviate ten miles right, clear the weather, and return to track.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, cleared to deviate right of track, no conflicting traffic.
2. Control: ANAC123, cleared to deviate, say extent of deviation required.
3. Control: ANAC123, deviation approved, report when back on track.
4. Control: ANAC123, unable to approve left deviation, traffic, cleared right deviation.
5. Control: ANAC123, deviation approved, maintain assigned altitude.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Cleared to deviate right, ANAC123, wilco, will report back on track.
2. Florianópolis Control, ANAC123, request deviation right of track, convective activity ahead.
3. ANAC123, deviating right approximately ten miles, convective weather.
4. Control, ANAC123, back on track, deviation complete.
5. ANAC123, cleared to deviate right, maintaining four thousand, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Deviation is route flexibility. Diversion is destination change.

Left or right. Miles needed. Hazard avoided. Return to track.

The sequence:

Identify the hazard. Estimate the deviation needed. Request clearance. State direction and distance.

Brazilian pilots sometimes ask for deviation without specifying direction or extent.

ATC cannot approve a vague deviation. Be specific.

Right ten miles — clear and actionable.

At the coast between Florianópolis and Navegantes, convective weather in summer is common.

Request early — before you are forced into a sharp turn to avoid cells.

Report back on track when complete. ATC is counting on that call.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How would you request a route deviation for weather avoidance?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would identify the hazard and estimate the lateral deviation needed. I would transmit: Florianópolis Control, ANAC123, request deviation right of track approximately ten miles, weather ahead. After receiving clearance, I would complete the deviation, clear the hazard, and report: ANAC123, back on track, deviation complete.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Requesting deviation without stating direction or extent.  
  ✔ Always specify direction — left or right — and approximate miles needed.

- ❌ Confusing deviate with divert.  
  ✔ Deviate is a route change. Divert is a destination change. Two different clearances.

- ❌ Not reporting back on track after the deviation is complete.  
  ✔ ATC expects the call: back on track, deviation complete.

- ❌ Deviating without ATC approval in controlled airspace.  
  ✔ Request clearance before deviating in controlled airspace — unless avoiding immediate danger.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are flying south along the coast in your H130 from Florianópolis. Ahead, convective cells with heavy rain are blocking your track. You estimate a right deviation of fifteen miles will clear the weather.

**Your first task**

What is your radio call to ATC?

**Your second task**

After clearing the weather, you are back on your assigned track. What do you transmit?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

My request would be: Florianópolis Control, ANAC123, request deviation right of track, approximately fifteen miles, convective weather blocking route. After clearing the weather and returning to track, I would transmit: ANAC123, back on track, deviation complete. I would maintain my assigned altitude throughout unless ATC approved a change.
