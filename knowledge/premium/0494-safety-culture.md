# Safety Culture

**Catalog ID:** 0494  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Safety Culture — the shared values, attitudes, and behaviors that determine how seriously an organization and its crews treat safety in daily decisions, reporting, and peer norms.

Safety Culture is what people do when nobody is checking the form. It shows in whether a tired pilot feels able to say stop, and whether a near miss becomes a report or a secret.

For H130 ICAO Delta training, Safety Culture links Part 1 conceptual English with Part 4 operational discussion — how crews talk about pressure, reporting, and standards.

Understanding Safety Culture in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Safety Culture is the enduring shared commitment of an organization and its people to value safety above competing pressures, demonstrated through open reporting, just accountability, continuous learning, and everyday behaviors that refuse to normalize risk.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Cultura de segurança / Safety Culture.

Compromisso compartilhado e duradouro de uma organização e de suas pessoas de valorizar a segurança acima de pressões concorrentes, demonstrado por relato aberto, responsabilização justa, aprendizado contínuo e comportamentos cotidianos que recusam normalizar o risco.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When living a strong Safety Culture, the crew should:

- refuse to normalize shortcuts that erode margins
- support colleagues who stop an unsafe operation
- report hazards and near misses as normal professional work
- for H130: resist passenger or schedule pressure on marginal pads
- discuss safety in clear English during briefings and debriefs
- align personal standards with company SMS — not with “how we always do it”

Safety Culture commonly includes:

- reporting culture
- Just Culture
- learning culture
- flexible culture under pressure
- informed culture (knowing the real risks)

**Important:** A poster about safety is not Safety Culture. Behavior under schedule pressure is Safety Culture.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

ATS organizations share Safety Culture goals when they:

- welcome pilot reports about unclear clearances or hotspots
- treat honest readback errors as learning, not shame
- adapt procedures from aggregated safety data
- support helicopter crews who refuse unsafe clearances
- coordinate SMS learning across operators and ATS units

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Your H130 company is behind schedule. Passengers push for a landing on a pad with rising wind and a questionable escape.

A weak culture says get it done. A strong Safety Culture supports your decision to delay or divert. You tell Operations and, if already airborne with ATC, you state the change of intentions: unable to land, diverting.

Later you debrief and report the pressure as an organizational hazard. Culture is proven in that moment — not in the safety week presentation.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, advise intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, confirm diversion, say again destination.
3. Tower: ANAC123, report if unable to accept landing clearance.
4. Approach: ANAC123, hold for weather improvement, advise.
5. Ground: ANAC123, taxi parking, company requests you call operations.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, unable to land, diverting Congonhas.
2. ANAC123, diverting Congonhas, request routing.
3. ANAC123, unable to accept landing clearance, requesting delay.
4. ANAC123, holding, will advise when able to continue.
5. ANAC123, taxi parking, wilco call operations.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Safety Culture is peer pressure in the right direction.

If the line laughs at people who go around, culture is sick. If the line respects the go-around, culture is alive.

In Part 1 and Part 4 answers, connect Safety Culture to Just Culture, reporting, and leadership under pressure. Examiners want systems thinking, not slogans.

In the H130, culture shows at the pad: who wins — the schedule or the escape path? Your English must be able to explain that choice calmly.

Culture is also radio honesty: say unable, say going around, say the hazard. Hidden risk is cultural failure.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is Safety Culture and how does it affect day-to-day flight operations?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Safety Culture is the shared attitude that safety comes before schedule and ego, shown in open reporting, Just Culture, and everyday decisions. In practice it means a crew can delay, divert, or go around without fear, and that near misses are reported so the organization learns. It turns policy into normal behavior under pressure.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Describing Safety Culture only as company posters or manuals.  
  ✔ Describe real behaviors under pressure and reporting habits.

- ❌ Confusing Safety Culture with zero accidents by luck.  
  ✔ Strong culture manages risk before the accident statistics move.

- ❌ Punishing honest reporters while claiming a safety culture.  
  ✔ Just Culture is a required pillar of Safety Culture.

- ❌ Accepting “normal” shortcuts because seniors do them.  
  ✔ Challenge normalized deviance — that is cultural leadership.

Unable to land, diverting, will file a safety report after arrival.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Passengers and schedule pressure push for an H130 pad landing as wind increases and your escape margin shrinks. Approach asks you to advise intentions.

**Your first task**

What do you say on frequency if you reject the landing?

**Your second task**

In Part 4 English, how do you explain that decision as Safety Culture in action?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, unable to land, diverting Congonhas, request routing. Second task: Safety Culture means I refuse to normalize an unacceptable escape margin under schedule pressure; I divert, then report the organizational pressure and weather hazard so the system learns. The safe decision is the cultural product — not the on-time arrival.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Safety Culture is safety when it costs something.

Report. Support the stop call. Reject normalized shortcuts.

On the radio, unable and diverting are cultural statements as much as operational ones.

In the H130, the pad will always wait for another day. A broken culture will not. Choose the standard — every sector.
