# Governor Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0418  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Governor Failure — malfunction of the engine/rotor RPM governor that normally maintains rotor speed automatically, requiring manual RPM control and clear communication of the emergency and intentions to ATC.

Governor failure turns an automatic system into a manual workload spike. Rotor RPM must be managed while you still speak clearly on frequency.

For H130 operations in Brazil — HEMS, charter, and training flights — you must report governor failure, state if you are on manual control, and request priority landing when needed.

Understanding how to discuss governor failure in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Governor Failure is a malfunction of the system that automatically governs engine and rotor RPM, forcing the pilot to control rotor speed manually and often requiring an expedited landing under increased workload.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do governador (RPM).

Malfuncionamento do sistema que governa automaticamente a RPM do motor e do rotor, forçando o piloto a controlar a velocidade do rotor manualmente e frequentemente exigindo pouso expedito sob maior carga de trabalho.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When governor failure occurs, the crew should:

- take manual control of RPM per AFM procedures immediately
- maintain rotor RPM within limits while configuring for landing
- declare pan-pan or mayday depending on controllability and RPM stability
- request priority landing and avoid high-workload maneuvers
- advise ATC of governor failure and manual RPM control

Governor failure reporting commonly includes:

- pan-pan, governor failure, manual RPM
- requesting priority landing
- unable continue mission
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** Unstable or decaying rotor RPM is mayday territory. Stable manual control may be pan-pan with priority landing — judge by the aircraft, not by pride.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to governor failure reports to:

- grant priority sequencing for landing
- reduce frequency congestion and complex clearances
- provide a simple approach path and wind
- alert emergency services if the pilot declares distress
- avoid assigning holding or long vectors that increase workload

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

North of Florianópolis in your H130, the governor fails. You take manual RPM control. RPM is stable but workload is high. You transmit:

Pan-pan pan-pan pan-pan, Florianópolis Approach, governor failure, manual RPM control, requesting priority landing Florianópolis.

Approach clears you for a straight-in, keeps instructions short, and stands by. You land with RPM in limits and report on ground.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, pan-pan acknowledged, priority approved, cleared direct Florianópolis, descend pilot's discretion.
2. Approach: ANAC123, expect straight-in runway one four, wind one five zero at eight.
3. Approach: ANAC123, keep transmissions short, say if you require mayday upgrade.
4. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land runway one four, emergency services optional, say requirements.
5. Approach: ANAC123, souls on board and fuel remaining.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, pan-pan, governor failure, manual RPM, priority landing Florianópolis.
2. ANAC123, straight-in one four, descending.
3. ANAC123, wilco, will advise if upgrading to mayday.
4. ANAC123, cleared to land one four, negative emergency services at this time.
5. ANAC123, souls three, fuel one hour two zero.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Governor failure English is workload English.

Tell ATC you are on manual RPM so they stop giving complex clearances. Priority and a simple path are the products you need.

In the H130, rotor RPM is life. If RPM is wandering, upgrade to mayday without hesitation. If it is stable under manual control, pan-pan plus priority is professional — not weak.

Brazilian approach sectors can be busy. A clear pan-pan cuts through. Do not bury the failure inside a long story about the mission.

After landing, one status call is enough. The maintenance write-up can wait until the rotor is stopped and the checklist is done.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The governor fails but you can hold RPM manually. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare urgency: Pan-pan pan-pan pan-pan, governor failure, manual RPM control, requesting priority landing at the nearest suitable field. If RPM becomes unstable I would upgrade to mayday. I would ask for a simple approach and avoid unnecessary vectors or holds.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing the mission "porque ainda está voando."  
  ✔ Land with priority; manual RPM is not a reason to press on.

- ❌ Not saying "manual RPM" so ATC keeps giving complex clearances.  
  ✔ State manual RPM control and request simple routing.

- ❌ Using only "problema no motor" without governor failure.  
  ✔ Name the system: governor failure.

- ❌ Waiting too long to upgrade when RPM decays.  
  ✔ Unstable RPM = mayday immediately.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Governor failure north of Florianópolis. RPM currently stable on manual control.

**Your first task**

Compose the pan-pan call.

**Your second task**

RPM begins to fluctuate toward limits. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Pan-pan pan-pan pan-pan, Florianópolis Approach, ANAC123, governor failure, manual RPM control, requesting priority landing Florianópolis. Second task: Mayday mayday mayday, ANAC123, governor failure, unstable RPM, making emergency landing — upgrade immediately when RPM is no longer reliably controllable.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Governor failure raises cockpit workload. Your radio must lower ATC workload.

Name the failure. Say manual RPM. Ask for priority and a simple path.

In the H130, protect rotor speed first. Speak second. Land third.

Stable manual control is pan-pan. Unstable RPM is mayday. Know the difference — and say it.
