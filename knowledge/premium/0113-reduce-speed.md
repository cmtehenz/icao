# Reduce Speed

**Catalog ID:** 0113  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Reduce Speed — an ATC instruction directing the pilot to decrease airspeed to a specified value or to a general reduction for separation and sequencing purposes.

Reduce Speed is one of the most frequently issued instructions during arrival sequencing at Brazilian airports.

In the H130, speed reductions are routine on approach to Congonhas, Florianópolis, Curitiba, and Navegantes as ATC sequences faster jets and slower helicopters together.

You must read back the assigned speed, comply promptly, and advise ATC if the instruction exceeds your operational limits.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and speed management in controlled airspace.

## 📖 Meaning

Reduce Speed is an ATC instruction requiring the pilot to decrease airspeed to a specified value, maintaining that speed until further advised or until a later speed restriction applies.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Reduza a velocidade.

Instrução do controle para o piloto diminuir a velocidade para um valor especificado, mantendo essa velocidade até nova instrução.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Reduce Speed is received, the pilot should:

- read back the assigned speed
- reduce airspeed promptly to the assigned value
- maintain the assigned speed until further instruction
- advise ATC if the speed is below minimum safe or structural limits
- comply with speed restrictions associated with SIDs, STARs, and approach procedures

Reduce Speed operations commonly include:

- sequencing deceleration during arrival
- speed reduction to maintain separation from preceding traffic
- approach configuration speed management
- final approach speed assignment

**Important:** If the assigned speed is below your minimum safe speed or published operating limits, advise ATC immediately: Unable, minimum speed is [X] knots, ANAC123. Do not accept an unsafe speed assignment.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC issues Reduce Speed to:

- create spacing between successive arrivals
- manage separation when faster aircraft are catching up
- allow preceding aircraft to clear the runway before the next arrival
- optimize runway occupancy during high traffic periods
- maintain safe approach sequencing at mixed-traffic airports

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on a twelve-mile final to Florianópolis in your H130 behind an Embraer 195.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, reduce speed one four zero knots, traffic ahead.

You check your limits and respond:

Speed one four zero knots, ANAC123.

At eight miles Approach clears the speed:

ANAC123, cancel speed restriction, resume own speed.

You respond:

Cancel speed restriction, ANAC123.

The Embraer has landed and you have the separation needed.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, reduce speed one four zero knots.
2. Approach: ANAC123, reduce speed, traffic ahead.
3. Approach: ANAC123, maintain one six zero knots until five mile final.
4. Approach: ANAC123, cancel speed restriction, resume own speed.
5. Approach: ANAC123, your speed two two zero, reduce now.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Speed one four zero, ANAC123.
2. Reducing speed, ANAC123.
3. Maintain one six zero until five miles, ANAC123.
4. Cancel speed restriction, ANAC123.
5. Reducing, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Speed management on approach is aircraft management.

You need to reduce, configure, and still remain stable.

The sequence:

Hear reduce speed. Check against limits. Read back. Reduce promptly. Maintain until released.

Brazilian pilots sometimes reduce speed so aggressively they overshoot configuration, or so slowly they fail to meet the instruction.

Smooth reductions with aircraft configuration changes together — gear, flaps, power.

In the H130, reduction to one four zero or less may require early configuration change. Plan ahead.

If the speed assigned is below your minimum safe speed: advise immediately. ATC will adjust.

Never accept a speed that puts your aircraft outside safe limits.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What do you say if ATC assigns a speed below your minimum safe speed?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If ATC assigns a speed below my minimum safe speed I would immediately advise: Unable, minimum speed is one zero zero knots, ANAC123. I would not attempt to comply with an unsafe speed assignment. ATC would then adjust the instruction and I would comply with the revised assignment within my limits.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Not reading back the assigned speed.  
  ✔ Always read back: Speed one four zero, ANAC123.

- ❌ Accepting a speed below minimum safe limits without advising ATC.  
  ✔ Immediately advise: Unable, minimum speed is [X] knots, ANAC123.

- ❌ Reducing speed so abruptly the aircraft becomes destabilized.  
  ✔ Reduce with coordinated configuration changes for a stable profile.

- ❌ Resuming cruise speed before ATC releases the restriction.  
  ✔ Maintain assigned speed until ATC says cancel speed restriction.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Speed One Four Zero, ANAC123

**Pronunciation:** SPEED WUN FOR ZEE-ro, AY-EN-AY-CEE WUN TOO THREE

**Word Stress**

- Speed → SPEED (clear and direct)
- One Four Zero → each digit separate

Practice:

Speed... one four zero...

Together:

Speed one four zero, ANAC123.

Say digits clearly — one four zero, not one hundred forty.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**SLOW** — **S**ay the speed back, **L**ower promptly and configured, **O**bey until released, **W**arn ATC if below your limit.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On a ten-mile final to Florianópolis, Approach transmits: ANAC123, reduce speed one two zero knots, traffic four miles ahead.

**Your first task**

What do you read back?

**Your second task**

Your minimum safe speed is one one zero knots but the instruction is within limits. At six miles Approach has not released the speed. Do you maintain or ask?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Speed one two zero, ANAC123. I would reduce promptly and maintain one two zero until released. If at six miles I had not received a release and my energy was too low for configuration, I would advise: ANAC123, requesting speed increase, approaching minimum configuration speed. I would not wait until I was in an unsafe condition to speak up.

Read it back. Reduce promptly.

Configuration and speed work together.

Speak up before the problem — not after.

Manage energy. Fly like a professional.
