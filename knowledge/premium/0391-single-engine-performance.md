# Single-Engine Performance

**Catalog ID:** 0391  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Single-Engine Performance — the aircraft's ability to continue flight, climb, or land safely after loss of one engine on a multi-engine aircraft, and the related performance planning language used in ICAO exams and ATC communication.

Critical note for H130 pilots: the Airbus H130 is a single-engine helicopter. It does not have multi-engine OEI (one-engine-inoperative) climb performance. This lesson teaches the ICAO English and exam concepts for single-engine / OEI performance as used in fixed-wing and multi-engine operations, while clarifying what applies to H130: engine failure means autorotation and forced landing planning — not continued OEI flight.

Understanding the vocabulary — and stating your actual aircraft capability to ATC — is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Single-Engine Performance (often discussed as OEI performance on multi-engine aircraft) is the certified or calculated ability to maintain control and meet required climb or drift-down profiles after failure of one engine, at a given weight and environmental condition.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Desempenho com um motor / Single-Engine Performance (OEI).

Capacidade certificada ou calculada de manter o controle e cumprir os perfis de subida ou descida controlada após a falha de um motor, em determinado peso e condição ambiental — conceito de aeronaves multimotoras; no H130 monomotor, a falha de motor implica autorrotação e pouso forçado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When discussing single-engine performance, the crew should:

- know whether the aircraft is single-engine or multi-engine
- for multi-engine: calculate OEI climb capability and obstacle clearance
- for H130: plan engine-failure forced landing options along the route
- communicate actual capability to ATC after engine failure
- never imply OEI continued flight capability the aircraft does not have

Single-Engine Performance context includes:

- multi-engine OEI net takeoff flight path and climb gradients
- drift-down after en-route engine failure (multi-engine)
- H130: autorotation, suitable landing areas, Mayday
- weight reduction to meet OEI requirements on multi-engine types
- clear radio language matching the aircraft type

**Important:** In ICAO exams you must explain OEI concepts. In the H130, after engine failure you aviate (autorotation), then declare Mayday — you do not report "continuing on one engine."

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use single-engine / engine-failure performance information to:

- prioritize aircraft with engine failure
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable airport
- alert emergency services for forced landing or OEI approach
- assign altitudes multi-engine aircraft can hold on one engine
- avoid assuming helicopter OEI capability that does not exist

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Training scenario — multi-engine exam language versus H130 reality.

If you were multi-engine and lost an engine after takeoff with OEI climb capability:

ANAC123, engine failure, continuing, request return runway one seven.

In the H130, the same event is different:

ANAC123, Mayday Mayday Mayday, engine failure, autorotating for landing, position north of the field.

ATC must hear the truth of your aircraft. Single-engine performance vocabulary is exam-critical; H130 application is emergency landing.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, confirm engine failure, say intentions.
2. Approach: ANAC123, roger Mayday, emergency services alerted, say souls on board.
3. Radar: ANAC123, nearest suitable airport is twelve o'clock, one five miles.
4. Tower: ANAC123, runway cleared, land any runway.
5. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able to maintain altitude.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, Mayday, engine failure, autorotating, landing ahead.
2. ANAC123, souls on board four, fuel remaining three zero minutes.
3. ANAC123, requesting vectors nearest airport — (multi-engine OEI continuation).
4. ANAC123, unable to maintain altitude, descending for forced landing.
5. ANAC123, runway in sight, landing any runway.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Separate exam English from aircraft reality.

ICAO Part 2 and Part 3 expect you to discuss single-engine performance, OEI climb, and continued takeoff concepts used on multi-engine aeroplanes. Learn that language.

Then put on the H130 hat: one engine. Failure means autorotation. Performance after failure is about rotor RPM, airspeed, and landing site — not OEI climb gradient.

When you speak to ATC in the H130, never borrow multi-engine phraseology that implies you can continue the flight. Say engine failure, Mayday, intentions for landing.

Know both dialects. Use the one that matches your machine.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Explain single-engine performance. How does it apply differently to a multi-engine aeroplane versus the H130?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

On a multi-engine aeroplane, single-engine or OEI performance is the ability to climb or continue after one engine fails, within certified weight and obstacle limits. On the H130, a single-engine helicopter, there is no OEI continued flight: engine failure requires autorotation and a forced landing. I would declare Mayday and state landing intentions. Exam vocabulary covers OEI; operational radio must match the aircraft.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using "continuing on one engine" phraseology in the H130.  
  ✔ Declare Mayday, engine failure, state autorotation / forced landing intentions.

- ❌ Ignoring OEI concepts in exams because you fly a helicopter.  
  ✔ Learn ICAO exam English for OEI; apply H130 procedures in real ops.

- ❌ Delaying the Mayday to "assess performance."  
  ✔ Aviate first, then Mayday promptly when engine has failed.

- ❌ Assuming ATC knows your aircraft is single-engine.  
  ✔ State engine failure and inability to maintain flight clearly.
