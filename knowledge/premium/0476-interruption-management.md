# Interruption Management

**Catalog ID:** 0476  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Interruption Management — the disciplined process of handling breaks in a checklist, briefing, or procedure so that omitted steps are detected and completed.

Interruptions are inevitable. ATC calls during a checklist. A caution light during a brief. A traffic alert during approach setup. The hazard is not the interruption — it is resuming in the wrong place.

In H130 multi-crew and single-pilot-with-support operations, interruption management is a core CRM skill. Crews that restart "somewhere in the middle" leave landing gear, fuel transfers, or clearance items unfinished.

Understanding how to manage interruptions in ICAO-standard English — including hold requests, say-again calls, and status reports — supports Part 1 and Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Interruption Management is the set of crew techniques used to pause, mark, and correctly resume a procedural sequence after an external or internal interruption, preventing omitted steps and conflicting priorities.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gerenciamento de interrupções / Interruption Management.

Conjunto de técnicas de tripulação usadas para pausar, marcar e retomar corretamente uma sequência procedural após uma interrupção externa ou interna, evitando etapas omitidas e prioridades conflitantes.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an interruption occurs, the crew should:

- acknowledge the interruption and identify who will handle it
- mark the last completed checklist or briefing item explicitly
- fly the aircraft and answer ATC before returning to the paper or screen
- resume from the marked item, not from memory of "about where we were"
- re-verify critical configuration after any interrupted before-landing flow

Interruption technique includes:

- saying "hold the checklist at item three" out loud
- using a finger, cursor, or checklist hold function as a physical place-mark
- delaying non-urgent ATC administrative requests until the flow is safe to pause
- restarting the entire critical checklist if place-mark confidence is low

**Important:** If you are unsure where you stopped, do not guess. Restart the checklist. A repeated item is cheap. A missed item is not.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers interact with interruption management when they:

- issue urgent instructions that correctly break a crew flow
- repeat clearances after a crew was mid-checklist
- accept "standby" when a crew must finish a critical item first
- keep transmissions concise during high-workload phases
- confirm configuration-related reports after a go-around or delay

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are midway through the before-landing checklist into Curitiba. Approach issues an immediate heading for traffic.

You say on interphone: "Hold checklist at flaps."

Then:

ANAC123, turning left heading two seven zero.

Approach: ANAC123, traffic no factor, resume own navigation, report checklist complete when able.

You respond:

ANAC123, own navigation, will report ready.

You return to the checklist at flaps, complete the flow, and verify gear down before continuing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Interruption management is place-marking under pressure.

The amateurs resume from a feeling. The professionals resume from a mark.

In the H130, say the hold point out loud. If the interruption was long or chaotic, restart. Ego is not a checklist aid.

ATC can wait one sentence. An omitted landing item may not wait at all.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC interrupts your before-landing checklist with an immediate turn. How do you manage it and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would mark the checklist item verbally, comply with the turn: ANAC123, turning left heading two seven zero, then resume from the marked item. Before declaring ready for approach I would confirm the full configuration. If I lost the place-mark, I would restart the checklist rather than guess.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Resuming a checklist from memory after a long ATC exchange.  
  ✔ Use an explicit hold point or restart the checklist.

- ❌ Ignoring an urgent ATC turn to finish a non-critical line.  
  ✔ Aviate and comply first, then return to the procedure.

- ❌ Saying "ready" before completing the interrupted flow.  
  ✔ Report ready only after configuration and checklist are verified.

- ❌ Both pilots answering ATC while nobody holds the place-mark.  
  ✔ One flies and answers; one owns the checklist mark.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Approach issues an immediate traffic turn while you are on the before-landing checklist at the flaps item.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC while handling the turn?

**Your second task**

After traffic is clear, what do you say when the checklist is truly finished?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, turning left heading two seven zero. Second task: ANAC123, ready for approach, checklist complete. Between those calls I would hold the checklist at flaps and resume from that mark — or restart if the interruption destroyed place-mark confidence.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Interruptions are normal. Omissions are optional.

In the H130, manage the pause as carefully as the procedure. Mark it. Comply. Resume. Verify.

If the place is uncertain, start again. That is interruption management — not checklist theatre.
