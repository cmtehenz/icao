# Fuel Reserve

**Catalog ID:** 0382  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Fuel Reserve — the planned fuel remaining after destination landing, diversion, and contingency, required so the flight can absorb delays, weather changes, and unexpected routing without becoming fuel-critical.

Fuel Reserve is not leftover fuel. It is a protected margin built into the release and defended in flight.

For H130 operations in Brazil — Congonhas delays, coastal weather holds, and short-range IFR legs — reserve fuel is often the difference between a routine diversion and a minimum-fuel declaration.

Understanding how to discuss fuel reserve, endurance, and fuel status in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Fuel Reserve is the quantity of fuel planned and retained beyond trip fuel, contingency, and alternate fuel, so that the aircraft can land with a regulatory or company minimum remaining after the most demanding planned scenario.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Reserva de combustível / Fuel Reserve.

Quantidade de combustível planejada e mantida além do combustível de viagem, contingência e alternado, para que a aeronave possa pousar com o mínimo regulamentar ou da empresa após o cenário planejado mais exigente.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing fuel reserve, the crew should:

- calculate trip, contingency, alternate, and final reserve before departure
- monitor actual burn against planned burn throughout the flight
- advise ATC early if delays threaten reserve fuel
- declare minimum fuel or Mayday fuel when criteria are met
- never commit to holding or diversion without knowing remaining endurance

Fuel Reserve management includes:

- final reserve as the last protected quantity
- contingency for wind, routing, and ATC delays
- alternate fuel when an alternate is required
- taxi and APU/ground consumption where applicable
- continuous comparison of fuel remaining versus fuel required

**Important:** Reserve fuel is not available for "just one more hold." When projected landing fuel drops to final reserve criteria, declare the appropriate fuel status to ATC immediately.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with fuel reserve information to:

- prioritize aircraft that report minimum fuel
- reduce holding and vectoring for fuel-critical flights
- coordinate expeditious routing to destination or alternate
- sequence arrivals when multiple aircraft report low fuel
- distinguish routine fuel queries from distress fuel situations

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are holding east of Congonhas in the H130. Planned landing fuel included final reserve. ATC extends the hold another ten minutes.

You recalculate: further delay will consume contingency and threaten final reserve.

You call:

ANAC123, minimum fuel, unable further delay, request approach or diversion.

Approach: ANAC123, roger minimum fuel, expect vectors for approach, turn left heading two seven zero.

You respond:

ANAC123, left heading two seven zero, minimum fuel.

You protected the reserve by speaking before it was gone.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, say fuel remaining.
2. Radar: ANAC123, expect further hold, advise if fuel critical.
3. Approach: ANAC123, roger minimum fuel, priority for approach.
4. Control: ANAC123, divert to alternate, confirm fuel endurance.
5. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, emergency services standing by if required.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fuel reserve is a line you defend with words as much as with numbers.

Calculate it on the ground. Protect it in the air. When ATC delay, weather, or a missed approach starts eating into it, speak early: unable further delay, minimum fuel, or — if safety is in doubt — Mayday fuel.

In Brazil, Congonhas and Santos Dumont can stack delays quickly. The H130 does not carry jet-sized margins. Know your numbers in hours and minutes, not vague "enough fuel."

Minimum fuel means you can accept no more delay to the planned aerodrome. It is not a Mayday by itself — but it must be clear and timely.

Never invent endurance. Never hide a fuel problem hoping for a shorter vector. ATC can only help if they know.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC asks you to hold for fifteen more minutes. Your calculation shows that would leave you at final reserve on landing. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would refuse further delay clearly: ANAC123, unable further hold, minimum fuel, request approach or diversion. I would state fuel remaining if asked. I would not accept a hold that consumes final reserve. If the situation becomes critical and landing fuel is in doubt, I would declare Mayday fuel. Early, precise fuel communication is part of professional ICAO English.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting indefinite holds without recalculating landing fuel.  
  ✔ Recalculate after every delay and advise ATC before reserve is threatened.

- ❌ Saying "low fuel" without standard status.  
  ✔ Use minimum fuel or Mayday fuel as appropriate — clear and standard.

- ❌ Reporting fuel in vague terms instead of time remaining.  
  ✔ Report endurance in hours and minutes when asked.

- ❌ Treating reserve as usable trip fuel.  
  ✔ Protect final reserve; declare status when it is at risk.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are holding for Congonhas. Approach offers another ten-minute hold. Your landing fuel would then equal final reserve only if the approach is immediate afterward.

**Your first task**

What do you say?

**Your second task**

Approach then says: "ANAC123, divert to Guarulhos if unable to hold." You have alternate fuel for Guarulhos. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, unable further hold, minimum fuel, request approach. Second task: ANAC123, diverting Guarulhos, fuel remaining five zero minutes — confirming I can complete the diversion with reserve protected. I would not accept a hold that leaves only final reserve with no margin for a missed approach. Clear fuel status lets ATC choose approach or diversion correctly.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Fuel reserve is planned protection — not optional extra.

Calculate it. Monitor burn. Recalculate when ATC changes the plan.

When delay threatens the reserve, say minimum fuel. When safety is in doubt, say Mayday fuel.

In the H130 over Brazilian terminals, short endurance means early words matter.

Know your numbers. Protect the reserve. Speak in time.

That is how fuel stays a performance item — not an emergency.
