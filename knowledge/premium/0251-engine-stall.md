# Engine Stall

**Catalog ID:** 0251  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Engine Stall — a disruption of smooth airflow through a turbine engine compressor, often felt as bangs, vibration, yaw, and thrust loss, requiring immediate thrust reduction or checklist actions and clear ATC communication. Propeller and compressor-stall vocabulary appears in ICAO exams; for H130 crews, treat this as essential exam English and map it to turbine engine anomalies, unusual noise, vibration, and thrust changes you may actually encounter.

Engine stall is Critical because thrust can decay quickly and asymmetric thrust can challenge control. Your English must state the engine problem, the effect on flight, and your intentions without delay.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master ICAO phraseology for engine stall, then transfer the same reporting structure to H130 engine anomalies and single-engine considerations.

Understanding engine stall in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Engine Stall is a breakdown of normal airflow in a turbine engine compressor (compressor stall), which may produce bangs, flame from the intake or exhaust, vibration, and loss of thrust, requiring immediate crew actions per procedure and prompt notification to ATC of the nature of the malfunction and intentions for continued flight or diversion.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an engine stall occurs, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control — pitch, roll, yaw, and airspeed/rotor as applicable
- reduce thrust / follow QRH engine stall or surge procedures
- identify affected engine and monitor parameters
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday based on thrust available and controllability
- report engine stall, intentions (level off, return, divert), request priority if needed
- for H130: apply same structure to turbine anomalies, unusual engine noise, or power loss

Engine stall reporting commonly includes:

- engine stall / compressor stall on number one / number two
- thrust reduced / engine parameters abnormal
- request level off / return / diversion / priority landing
- souls and fuel when asked
- request emergency services if landing with degraded engine

**Important:** In ICAO English, distinguish aerodynamic wing stall from engine/compressor stall when context requires clarity. On the test, "engine stall" means the powerplant event. In H130 ops, describe the symptoms accurately even if your AFM uses different warning names.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to engine stall reports to:

- understand possible thrust loss and reduced performance
- provide altitude, heading, and speed flexibility
- offer priority return or diversion
- keep conflicting traffic away from an aircraft with degraded climb capability
- alert emergency services for precautionary or emergency landing

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: during climb, a loud bang and yaw — engine stall indications on the right engine.

You control the aircraft, reduce thrust as required, and transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Departure, ANAC123, engine stall number two, request level off and return to land.

If thrust loss is severe or control is in doubt:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, engine stall, significant thrust loss, request immediate vectors to land.

For H130 reality, replace twin-jet language with your turbine symptoms — bang, vibration, power fluctuation — and keep the urgency logic identical.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Engine stall is a powerplant event — not a wing stall. Say "engine stall" clearly so ATC does not invent the wrong picture.

First fly. Then identify. Then declare with the correct urgency. A momentary stall that recovers may be Pan Pan and a precautionary return. Persistent thrust loss or fire risk may be Mayday.

Do not hide behind "we have a small engine vibration." If it was a stall event, say engine stall. If parameters remain abnormal, say so.

In the H130, your engine talks through N1, T4, torque, and your ears. Unusual bangs and power rolls deserve the same honest English.

Brazilian examiners want control of the aircraft and control of the vocabulary. Give them both.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

During climb you experience an engine stall. What do you do, and what do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control of the aircraft, apply the engine stall procedure — typically reducing thrust and following the checklist — and assess whether the engine recovers. I would advise ATC with Pan Pan if the situation is serious but controllable, or Mayday if thrust loss threatens safe flight, stating engine stall, the effect on performance, and requesting level off, return, or priority landing as required. I would provide souls and fuel when asked. On a helicopter, I would use the same structure for a serious turbine anomaly with bangs, vibration, or power loss.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Confusing "engine stall" with aerodynamic stall in the radio call.  
  ✔ Say "engine stall" or "compressor stall"; keep wing stall separate.

- ❌ Continuing climb without telling ATC about thrust loss.  
  ✔ Request level off or priority early if performance is degraded.

- ❌ Using only "problem in the engine" without the standard term.  
  ✔ Prefer ICAO-recognizable "engine stall" plus status.

- ❌ Ignoring the topic because H130 AFM wording differs.  
  ✔ Learn exam English; transfer symptom-based reporting to the helicopter.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**STALL** — **S**top climbing if thrust is weak, **T**ell ATC engine stall clearly, **A**pply QRH thrust actions, **L**evel or return as needed, **L**and with priority if unresolved.
