# Confirm

**Catalog ID:** 0134  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Confirm — an ICAO-standard phrase used to request verification of information already exchanged.

Confirm means: I want to verify that what I think I heard (or what you have) is correct.

It is a safety phrase. It is used to double-check critical information — altitudes, runways, squawk codes, clearances — before acting on them.

In the H130, operating into busy Brazilian airports like Congonhas, São Paulo, and Florianópolis, using Confirm correctly and confidently is an important ICAO Level 4 skill and a key tool for catching communication errors before they become operational errors.

## 📖 Meaning

Confirm is an ICAO-standard word used to request verification of a message, clearance, value, or condition. It asks the addressed station to verify and restate a specific piece of information. It is used when the requesting station needs to ensure that a value or instruction is correct before acting on it.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When to use Confirm:

- when you want to verify a specific clearance element before acting
- when you want ATC to verify information they have about you
- when you read back a clearance and want ATC to confirm the readback is correct
- when you are uncertain about a value and need explicit confirmation before proceeding

When NOT to use Confirm:

- as a substitute for a readback — readback is active; confirm is a verification request
- as a way to ask for information you have not yet received — use Request instead
- to check on general frequency status — use a different inquiry

Confirm operations commonly include:

- Confirm altitude clearance
- Confirm runway assignment
- Confirm squawk code
- Confirm QNH
- Confirm cleared for approach
- Confirm holding instructions

**Important:** Confirm is used when you already have some information and need it verified. If you have no information at all on a topic, you request — you do not confirm. For example: "Confirm cleared to land runway two eight" checks a clearance you believe you received. "Request landing clearance" is used when you have not received one.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers use Confirm to:

- verify the pilot's understanding of a specific clearance element
- ensure a pilot has the correct QNH or altitude before a critical phase
- double-check a readback contains the right values
- request verification of pilot-reported information (position, altitude)
- confirm the pilot's intentions match the issued clearance

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on approach to Florianópolis in your H130.

In workload, you believe you were cleared to altitude two thousand, but you want to verify before descending.

You transmit:

ANAC123, confirm cleared altitude two thousand?

Approach responds:

ANAC123, affirm, cleared altitude two thousand, QNH one zero one two.

You read back:

Altitude two thousand, QNH one zero one two, ANAC123.

Your confirmation request prevented you from descending on a clearance you were not fully certain about.

Later, ATC asks:

ANAC123, confirm squawk four four five two?

You check the transponder and reply:

Affirm, squawk four four five two, ANAC123.

Confirm works in both directions — pilot to ATC and ATC to pilot — to verify critical operational information.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Confirm is your verification tool.

Use it any time you are not fully certain about a critical value before acting.

This is not weakness. This is airmanship.

Brazilian pilots sometimes hesitate to use Confirm because they think it signals a mistake or uncertainty that might make them look unprofessional.

The opposite is true.

Asking Confirm before descending to an uncertain altitude is professional.

Descending without confirming, then busting an altitude restriction, is not professional — and it is potentially dangerous.

The sequence for Confirm is:

You received information. You are not fully certain. You ask Confirm. ATC restates the value. You read back the confirmed value. You act.

That sequence is thorough, safe, and ICAO-compliant.

In the H130, single-pilot in busy airspace, do not let uncertainty go unresolved.

If in doubt, Confirm.

If you missed a value, Say Again.

These two tools together eliminate most communication errors before they matter.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the difference between Confirm and Say Again?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Confirm is used when you have received information and want ATC to verify that it is correct. For example: Confirm altitude cleared two thousand. Say Again is used when you did not receive the transmission clearly and need a repetition. For example: Say Again — you were broken. Confirm checks a value already held. Say Again requests a fresh transmission of a message that was not properly received. Both are verification tools, but they serve different moments in the communication process.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using Confirm when you have no information on the topic — you need to Request.  
  ✔ Confirm checks existing information. For new information, use Request.

- ❌ Saying "confirm" casually mid-transmission without specifying what to confirm.  
  ✔ Always state exactly what you are confirming: Confirm altitude four thousand?

- ❌ Treating ATC's "Confirm" question as a readback request — responding with Wilco.  
  ✔ Answer a Confirm question with Affirm or Negative plus the relevant information.

- ❌ Not waiting for ATC's confirmation before acting on an uncertain value.  
  ✔ Ask Confirm, wait for ATC's affirm or correction, then read back and act.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**CONFIRM = Check Before Act** — **C**ritical value uncertain? **O**pen the radio, **N**ame the element, **F**ocus on the answer, **I**nclude the readback, **R**eceive ATC's affirmation, **M**ove only after.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You believe Joinville Approach cleared you to altitude three thousand, but you are not fully certain — it may have been two thousand five hundred.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit to verify before descending?

**Your second task**

Approach responds: "ANAC123, affirm, cleared altitude three thousand, QNH one zero one one." Provide the full professional readback.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would transmit: ANAC123, confirm cleared altitude three thousand? The question form makes it clear I am verifying a value I believe I received. If the answer is two thousand five hundred, I would know immediately and avoid an altitude bust. On ATC's affirmation, my readback would be: Altitude three thousand, QNH one zero one one, ANAC123. Using Confirm in a moment of uncertainty is one of the most professional things a pilot can do. It costs three seconds and prevents a potentially serious error.
