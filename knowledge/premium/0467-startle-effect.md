# Startle Effect

**Catalog ID:** 0467  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Startle Effect — the involuntary physiological and cognitive reaction to a sudden, intense, unexpected stimulus that can briefly impair perception, motor control, and decision making.

Startle is not fear alone. It is a nervous-system interrupt: a loud warning, a sudden buffet, a bird strike, a horn. For a few seconds, fine motor skills and clear thinking degrade. Crews who do not expect startle may make the wrong first move — or freeze.

For H130 operations, startle appears with GPWS, traffic alerts, engine noises, and abrupt weather. Part 1 vocabulary, Part 2 radio after recovery, and Part 3 emergency communication all matter. Stabilize first; then report.

## 📖 Meaning

Startle Effect is an automatic, short-duration physiological response to a sudden intense stimulus — such as an aural warning, impact, or abrupt maneuver — that can temporarily degrade cognitive processing, fine motor performance, and the quality of the pilot's initial actions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Efeito de sobressalto / Startle Effect.

Resposta fisiológica automática e de curta duração a um estímulo intenso e súbito — como um alerta sonoro, impacto ou manobra abrupta — que pode degradar temporariamente o processamento cognitivo, o desempenho motor fino e a qualidade das ações iniciais do piloto.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When startle occurs, the crew should:

- expect a brief performance dip after sudden warnings or impacts
- apply trained first responses — power, attitude, climb — not improvisation
- avoid immediate complex FMS or radio tasks during the startle window
- use the monitoring pilot to confirm aircraft state
- communicate with ATC only after the flight path is under control

Startle management includes:

- training for unexpected GPWS, TCAS, engine failure cues
- SOP memory items that survive startle
- deliberate pause before non-critical actions
- clear emergency or urgency calls after stabilization

**Important:** The first two to three seconds after startle are high-risk. Fly a trained response. Do not troubleshoot on the radio yet.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond after startle-driven events when crews:

- report GPWS, TCAS, or go-around after a sudden alert
- request immediate climb or vectors
- declare urgency following bird strike or system shock
- need simplified instructions while recovering
- report unable to continue the approach

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Descending in the H130 toward Belo Horizonte, a sudden GPWS "PULL UP" startles both pilots.

You apply the escape: climb attitude, power, wings level. No debate.

When climbing and the warning ceases:

Approach, ANAC123, GPWS warning, climbing, request four thousand.

Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain four thousand, turn left heading three six zero.

Startle hit. Trained response first. Radio second.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger GPWS, climb and maintain four thousand, turn left heading three six zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm climbing, say altitude.
3. Tower: ANAC123, roger go around, bird strike acknowledged, services available.
4. Approach: ANAC123, TCAS climb, approved as necessary, advise when clear.
5. Control: ANAC123, simplified vectors available, advise ready.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Startle steals your first seconds. Training buys them back.

If the response is in muscle memory — pitch up for GPWS, go around for windshear — startle cannot invent a worse idea as easily.

In the H130, sudden noise and vibration are part of the environment. Still, a master warning at night in IMC will startle anyone. Brief it. Expect it. Assign who flies through it.

Do not make the radio your first action. A perfect English call from an aircraft still descending into terrain is useless. Climb. Then call.

Part 3 English comes after the aircraft is climbing and under control.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

A sudden GPWS warning startles you on descent. What is your sequence?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately fly the escape maneuver — climb attitude and power — without troubleshooting. After the flight path is safe and the warning stops, I would transmit: ANAC123, GPWS warning, climbing, request higher. Startle management means trained action first, ATC second.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Freezing to analyze the warning before flying.  
  ✔ Apply the trained escape; analyze when climbing.

- ❌ Transmitting before the aircraft is under control.  
  ✔ Aviate first; then report.

- ❌ Both pilots heads-down reading the QRH during startle.  
  ✔ One flies the escape; then checklist when stable.

- ❌ Feeling embarrassed about startle and hiding the event.  
  ✔ Report what ATC needs: climbing, going around, requesting vectors.
