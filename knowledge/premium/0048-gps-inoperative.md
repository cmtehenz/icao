# GPS Inoperative

**Catalog ID:** 0048  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers a navigation systems failure every modern pilot must handle.

GPS Inoperative means the Global Positioning System receiver or navigation function is no longer providing reliable position data.

In the H130, GPS is often the primary navigation source for VFR and IFR operations in Brazil.

When GPS fails, you must revert to conventional navigation — VOR, NDB, pilotage, or dead reckoning.

You do not always need Mayday or Pan Pan — but you must inform ATC clearly.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and navigation communication.

## 📖 Meaning

GPS Inoperative is the loss of GPS navigation capability, requiring the pilot to resume conventional navigation methods.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

GPS inoperante.

Perda da capacidade de navegação por GPS, exigindo métodos de navegação convencionais.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When GPS Inoperative occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- revert to alternate navigation sources
- inform ATC that GPS is inoperative
- request radar vectors or conventional navigation clearance if needed
- resume own navigation using available navaids when able

GPS Inoperative reporting commonly includes:

- GPS inoperative or GPS navigation lost
- resuming conventional navigation
- request vectors to rejoin route or proceed to destination
- electronic navigation capability reduced

**Important:** GPS failure alone is usually not a distress call. Inform ATC promptly so they can provide vectors or issue conventional clearances. Declare urgency only if safe navigation is seriously compromised.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to GPS Inoperative reports to:

- provide radar vectors when available
- issue conventional navigation clearances
- coordinate with adjacent sectors for handoff
- advise of available navaids in the area
- monitor aircraft position when pilot navigation is degraded

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are en route from Porto Alegre to Florianópolis in your H130 at three thousand five hundred feet.

The GPS display goes blank and does not recover after reset.

You identify your position using VOR and pilotage.

You transmit:

Florianópolis Approach, ANAC123, GPS navigation lost, resuming conventional navigation, request vectors to Florianópolis.

Approach provides radar vectors while you rejoin the published route.

You complete the approach and land without incident.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger GPS inoperative, turn left heading two four zero, vectors to Florianópolis.
2. Approach: ANAC123, radar contact, say position and intentions.
3. Tower: ANAC123, GPS unavailable noted, continue approach runway three zero.
4. Departure: ANAC123, resume own navigation when able, direct POA.
5. Approach: ANAC123, electronic navigation capability reduced, say alternate navaids available.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Florianópolis Approach, ANAC123, GPS navigation lost, resuming conventional navigation.
2. ANAC123, GPS inoperative, request vectors to nearest suitable airport.
3. ANAC123, electronic nav systems inoperative, proceeding VFR using pilotage.
4. Approach, ANAC123, GPS unavailable, position ten miles south of Navegantes, request direct.
5. ANAC123, resuming conventional navigation, GPS unavailable, maintaining three thousand five hundred.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

GPS failure is common in the ICAO exam and in real operations.

The sequence every professional pilot learns:

Aviate. Navigate. Communicate.

Do not declare Mayday because the GPS screen went blank.

That is not distress — it is a navigation problem.

Inform ATC clearly.

Request vectors if you need them.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "GPS caiu" or "perdeu o GPS" on frequency.

Use GPS inoperative or GPS navigation lost — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130 over Brazilian terrain, know your VOR stations and carry current charts.

GPS is a tool — not your only skill.

Train conventional navigation so a blank screen does not blank your confidence.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if your GPS stopped working during en route flight?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would inform ATC clearly: GPS inoperative or GPS navigation lost, state my callsign and position, and report that I am resuming conventional navigation. I would request vectors if needed or state my intentions to proceed using alternate navaids. I would not declare Mayday unless safe navigation is seriously compromised.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "GPS caiu" or "perdeu sinal" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use GPS inoperative or GPS navigation lost.

- ❌ Declaring Mayday or Pan Pan for GPS failure alone.  
  ✔ Inform ATC normally — urgency only if navigation safety is seriously affected.

- ❌ Not informing ATC that navigation capability is reduced.  
  ✔ Report GPS inoperative promptly so ATC can assist.

- ❌ Continuing on assigned RNAV route without informing ATC.  
  ✔ Request conventional clearance or vectors when GPS is unavailable.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are fifteen miles north of Navegantes at four thousand feet when your GPS fails in your H130.

**Your first task**

After identifying your position using alternate means, inform Approach of the navigation failure.

**Your second task**

Answer this question:

Approach asks what alternate navigation you have available. What do you report?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report clearly: resuming conventional navigation, ANAC123. I would state available navaids such as VOR or pilotage, my current position, and request vectors to Navegantes if needed.
