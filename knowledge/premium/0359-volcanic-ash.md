# Volcanic Ash

**Catalog ID:** 0359  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Volcanic Ash — airborne ash from volcanic eruptions that can damage engines, windscreens, and systems, requiring avoidance, immediate exit from ash cloud if entered, and clear Mayday or urgency communication.

Volcanic ash is Critical because it can cause engine failure and loss of visibility. There is no safe penetration strategy for training or line flying — avoid, escape, land if required.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, ash events are less frequent than in some Andean corridors but remain an ICAO exam and international ops topic. Treat ash as a no-go weather hazard with precise English.

Understanding volcanic ash phraseology is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Volcanic Ash is fine pulverized rock and glass particles ejected by a volcano and carried by the wind, hazardous to aircraft engines and systems, requiring route avoidance, immediate escape turns and descents or climbs out of the cloud if inadvertently entered, and prompt reports to ATC.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Cinza vulcânica / Volcanic Ash.

Finas partículas de rocha e vidro pulverizados ejetadas por um vulcão e transportadas pelo vento, perigosas para motores e sistemas da aeronave, exigindo desvio de rota, escape imediato da nuvem se entrada inadvertida, e informes rápidos ao ATC.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When volcanic ash is a factor, the crew should:

- check SIGMETs, NOTAMs, and ASHTAMs before flight
- refuse routes through known ash; request re-route
- if ash is encountered: reduce power as published, exit the cloud, avoid abrupt thrust changes per procedure
- declare Mayday if engines are affected or visibility is lost
- report ash encounter position, altitude, and time to ATC
- land at the nearest suitable airport if damage or engine anomalies occur

Volcanic ash reporting commonly includes:

- volcanic ash cloud observed / encountered
- request weather deviation / re-route
- Mayday, engine failure / power loss in ash
- exiting ash cloud, request descent or climb

**Important:** Ash may look like normal cloud or haze. St. Elmo's fire, sulfur smell, and dust in the cockpit are classic cues — exit immediately and report.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to volcanic ash information to:

- keep aircraft clear of published ash clouds and SIGMET areas
- accept and relay PIREPs of ash encounters
- coordinate emergency descents and diversions
- alert downstream sectors and meteorological offices
- protect following traffic from the same cloud

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

On an IFR cross-country in the H130, a SIGMET warns of volcanic ash to the west of your route. Center offers a re-route east.

You accept the re-route and remain clear. Another aircraft that clipped the edge reports sulfur smell and requests immediate descent. Your early avoidance kept the helicopter out of Critical engine risk.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Center: ANAC123, volcanic ash SIGMET west of your route, advise intentions.
2. Center: ANAC123, re-route approved, turn left heading one two zero.
3. Center: ANAC123, say if ash encountered, altitude and position.
4. Approach: ANAC123, divert approved, expect priority landing.
5. Center: ANAC123, roger Mayday, engines affected, nearest airport is...

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Volcanic ash is not "weather to push through."

Avoid. Exit. Report. Divert.

Brazilian exam candidates sometimes underplay ash because local volcanoes are distant. ICAO English still requires the vocabulary and the decision: no penetration.

In the H130, a single engine makes ash-related power loss Critical. Treat SIGMETs as hard walls unless ATC and meteorology confirm clear corridors.

If you smell sulfur or see dust on the glare shield — you are already late. Escape and talk.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You inadvertently enter a volcanic ash cloud. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would exit the ash cloud immediately using published escape procedures, avoid unnecessary thrust changes, and report volcanic ash encountered with altitude and position. If engines are affected I would declare Mayday and request immediate diversion to the nearest suitable airport.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating ash like ordinary IMC cloud.  
  ✔ Ash is a Critical engine hazard — avoid or escape.

- ❌ Delaying the PIREP until after landing.  
  ✔ Report position and altitude immediately for other traffic.

- ❌ Climbing or descending randomly without telling ATC.  
  ✔ Escape, then report exiting ash and request altitude as needed.

- ❌ Using only "fumaça" for ash.  
  ✔ Say volcanic ash.

- Volcanic → vol-KAN-ik
- Ash → ASH
- Encountered → en-KOUN-terd
- Diversion → di-VER-zhun

Together:

ANAC123, volcanic ash encountered, exiting cloud, request diversion.
