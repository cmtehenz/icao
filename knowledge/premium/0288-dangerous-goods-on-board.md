# Dangerous Goods on Board

**Catalog ID:** 0288  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Dangerous Goods on Board — the presence of regulated dangerous goods (DG) carried as cargo or company material under ICAO Technical Instructions, and the need to declare that fact clearly to ATC when an emergency, diversion, or fire risk requires special ground handling. Related to hazmat, but taught here as a distinct exam phrase: say "dangerous goods on board" when that is the standard wording for the scenario.

Dangerous goods on board is a High Part 2 and Part 3 topic. Controllers and RFF need to know what is in the aircraft before they approach after a landing, leak, or fire.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training on the H130 in Brazil — including offshore and utility ops that may carry regulated items — master the phrase and the information ATC will ask for.

Understanding dangerous goods on board in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Dangerous Goods on Board means regulated dangerous goods are being carried on the aircraft in accordance with applicable regulations, and this information must be communicated to ATC and emergency services during an emergency, diversion, or abnormal situation so that fire fighting and handling procedures account for the hazard class and quantity.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Artigos perigosos a bordo / Dangerous Goods on Board.

Significa que artigos perigosos regulamentados estão sendo transportados na aeronave conforme a regulamentação aplicável, e essa informação deve ser comunicada ao ATC e aos serviços de emergência durante uma emergência, desvio ou situação anormal para que o combate a incêndio e o manuseio considerem a classe de risco e a quantidade.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When dangerous goods are relevant to an emergency, the crew should:

- declare the emergency or urgency as required by the primary problem
- state clearly: dangerous goods on board
- provide UN number, class, and quantity if known and requested
- request emergency services and advise of any leak, smoke, or fire near the DG
- for H130: apply the same report when carrying regulated company material or cargo under DG rules
- distinguish exam phrasing: "dangerous goods" vs "hazmat" as separate taught phrases

Dangerous goods reporting commonly includes:

- dangerous goods on board
- class / UN number / proper shipping name if known
- location in aircraft if known
- leak / no leak / fire / smoke
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** Dangerous goods and hazmat describe related hazards. In ICAO Delta lessons they are taught as distinct exam phrases. Use the wording the scenario and examiner expect. Do not invent details you do not have — say what you know and what you do not know.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers pass dangerous goods information to:

- alert RFF to special extinguishing and approach procedures
- protect ground personnel from toxic, flammable, or reactive materials
- coordinate isolation distances on the apron or runway
- inform the airport authority and operator
- prioritize landing and parking away from terminals when required

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You divert to Florianópolis with an electrical smell and known dangerous goods in the cargo compartment.

You transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, electrical smell, diverting Florianópolis, dangerous goods on board, request priority landing and firefighters.

Approach asks for details. You reply with class and UN number from the NOTOC if available.

After landing:

ANAC123, landed, no smoke visible, dangerous goods on board, request remain clear until firefighters inspect.

For H130 offshore or utility flights carrying regulated items, the same structure applies — primary problem first, then dangerous goods on board.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, confirm dangerous goods on board, say class if known.
2. Approach: ANAC123, priority approved, emergency services alerted for dangerous goods.
3. Tower: ANAC123, firefighters standing by, cleared to land, remain on runway after landing.
4. Tower: ANAC123, say UN number and quantity if available.
5. Ground: ANAC123, remain in position, RFF approaching, keep passengers on board until advised.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, affirmative, dangerous goods on board, class three flammable liquid.
2. ANAC123, priority, emergency services for dangerous goods, ANAC123.
3. ANAC123, cleared to land, will remain on runway.
4. ANAC123, UN one two zero three, quantity two hundred liters.
5. ANAC123, remaining in position, passengers on board until advised.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Dangerous goods on board is information that protects the people who run toward your aircraft.

Lead with the emergency. Then add the DG fact. Then the details you actually have.

If you have a NOTOC, use it. If you only know "dangerous goods on board, details unknown," say that. Guessing a UN number helps nobody.

Keep "dangerous goods" and "hazmat" as separate exam phrases in this academy. Related hazard. Distinct wording. Use the one the question asks for.

In Brazilian H130 ops, regulated cargo still exists. The radio rule is the same: clear, early, factual.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You divert with smoke and you are carrying dangerous goods. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare the appropriate urgency, state the nature of the problem such as smoke, report that I am diverting, and clearly advise that dangerous goods are on board. I would request priority landing and firefighters, provide class or UN number if known, and after landing update whether there is fire, leak, or smoke near the cargo.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Declaring the emergency but never mentioning dangerous goods.  
  ✔ Add "dangerous goods on board" as soon as practicable.

- ❌ Using "hazmat" and "dangerous goods" as if examiners treat them as identical answers.  
  ✔ Treat them as related but distinct exam phrases; use the required wording.

- ❌ Inventing UN numbers when the NOTOC is not available.  
  ✔ Say details unknown; provide what you can confirm.

- ❌ Letting passengers deplane into a DG fire zone without ATC/RFF guidance.  
  ✔ Follow checklist and remain clear instructions from ATC and RFF.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Electrical smell in cruise. You divert. Dangerous goods are in the cargo.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

ATC asks for the UN number. You have it on the NOTOC. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, Control, ANAC123, electrical smell, diverting to nearest suitable airport, dangerous goods on board, request priority and firefighters. Second task: ANAC123, UN number one two zero three, class three, quantity as per NOTOC — stating the actual figures from the document. I would not guess if the NOTOC were unavailable.
