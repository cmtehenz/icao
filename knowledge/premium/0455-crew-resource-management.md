# Crew Resource Management

**Catalog ID:** 0455  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Crew Resource Management — the effective use of all available resources — people, information, equipment, and procedures — to achieve safe flight operations.

CRM is not soft theory. It is how professional crews brief, challenge, decide, and communicate under pressure. It links directly to ICAO language proficiency: clear statements, assertive inquiry, and closed-loop communication.

For H130 helicopter operations, CRM applies to dual-pilot crews, pilot-plus-crewman operations, and single-pilot flights that still use ATC, maintenance, and dispatch as resources. Part 1 comprehension, Part 2 interaction, and Part 4 interactions all depend on CRM discipline.

Understanding how to practice and verbalize CRM in ICAO-standard English is essential for Parts 1, 2, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Crew Resource Management is the application of teamwork, communication, leadership, decision-making, and situational awareness to use all available human and technical resources for safe and efficient flight operations.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gerenciamento de recursos da tripulação (CRM).

Aplicação de trabalho em equipe, comunicação, liderança, tomada de decisão e consciência situacional para utilizar todos os recursos humanos e técnicos disponíveis em prol de operações de voo seguras e eficientes.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When applying CRM, the crew should:

- brief roles, threats, and contingency plans before departure
- use closed-loop communication: clear statement, readback, confirmation
- assert concerns early with specific observations, not vague discomfort
- decide with shared mental models — who flies, who talks, who checks
- review after the flight: what worked, what to improve

CRM communication commonly includes:

- my aircraft / your aircraft (positive exchange of control)
- confirm checklist complete
- I am concerned / I am uncomfortable / I assert we go around
- request confirmation of clearance or altitude

**Important:** CRM fails when someone sees a threat and stays silent — or speaks without a clear request. Say what you see, what you think, and what you want.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers interact with CRM-disciplined crews when:

- clear readbacks prevent altitude and runway conflicts
- crews state inability early instead of accepting unsafe clearances
- workload is shared so the flying pilot stays ahead of the aircraft
- emergency roles are declared: one flies, one communicates
- questions are specific, reducing frequency congestion

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are operating your H130 dual-pilot IFR into Congonhas in marginal weather.

Before top of descent you brief: threats, approach, missed approach, and who handles radios. On final, the monitoring pilot sees the approach becoming unstable and states: go around, not stable. The flying pilot executes the missed approach. You advise ATC of the missed approach and request vectors for a second approach.

After landing you debrief the callout, the decision, and the radio work. That is CRM — brief, assert, decide, review.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain four thousand, expect ILS runway one seven left.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able ILS one seven left in current weather.
3. Tower: ANAC123, go around, I say again go around, turn left heading one two zero.
4. Approach: ANAC123, radar contact, climb three thousand, vectors for second approach.
5. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions and fuel remaining.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

2. ANAC123, affirmative, able ILS one seven left.
3. ANAC123, going around, turning left heading one two zero.
4. ANAC123, climbing three thousand, request vectors second approach.
5. ANAC123, intentions second ILS one seven left, fuel remaining one hour.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

CRM is how adults refuse to kill each other with silence.

The best technical pilot still needs a second brain — a crewman, a copilot, or a disciplined self-brief when alone. Resources include ATC, checklists, AFM limits, and your own voice saying "unable."

On the H130, exchange of control must be unambiguous: "You have control" — "I have control." Checklists are closed-loop. Concerns are specific: "Airspeed trending low, go around" beats "I don't like this."

In emergencies, assign roles out loud: I fly, you declare Mayday and run the checklist. Two people doing the same job is not teamwork — it is collision.

Language proficiency and CRM meet in the same place: clear, assertive, standard English under stress.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

On final your monitoring pilot says the approach is not stable. What do you do and say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would execute the go-around immediately, then transmit: going around, request vectors for a second approach. Internally I would acknowledge the callout, confirm who has control, and re-brief the next approach. CRM means accepting the challenge and flying the missed approach without argument on short final.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Soft hints instead of clear assertiveness: "Maybe we are a bit high?"  
  ✔ State the threat and the required action: not stable, go around.

- ❌ No positive exchange of control.  
  ✔ Use clear handovers: you have control — I have control.

- ❌ Both pilots talking on frequency during a critical phase.  
  ✔ One communicates; one flies — roles stated aloud.

- ❌ Skipping the debrief because "it worked out."  
  ✔ Short debrief locks in learning for the next flight.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Going Around, Request Vectors Second Approach

**Pronunciation:** GO-ing uh-ROUND, reh-KWEST VEK-terz SEK-und uh-PROCH

**Word Stress**

- Going → GO-ing
- Around → uh-ROUND (stress on second)
- Vectors → VEK-terz
- Approach → uh-PROCH (stress on second)

Practice:

Going around... request vectors second approach.

Together:

Going around, not stable, request vectors for second approach.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are flying. Your crewman sees wires on short final that you have not called.

**Your first task**

What should the crewman say, and what do you do?

**Your second task**

After the go-around, what do you say to ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: The crewman asserts: wires ahead, go around — and I execute the go-around immediately without debate. Second task: ANAC123, going around, obstacle on final, request vectors for another approach. We re-brief the landing site threats before the next attempt.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

CRM is the operating system of a professional crew.

Brief the threats. Speak the concern. Close the loop. Decide out loud. Debrief briefly.

On the H130, your resources include every person and every frequency that can keep the rotor disk safe. Use them.

Silence is not politeness in the cockpit. Silence is how small problems become accidents.
