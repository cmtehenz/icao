# Propeller Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0258  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Propeller Failure — abnormal or failed propeller operation on propeller-driven aircraft, and the related ICAO exam English used for thrust or rotor-drive anomalies, including how H130 crews relate the concept to main rotor, fenestron, or propulsive system phraseology across types.

Propeller failure is high-stakes systems language on ICAO exams. Fixed-wing crews may face overspeed, feathering problems, or thrust loss. Helicopter crews must still master the English — and map it honestly to rotorcraft reality: main rotor anomalies, fenestron or anti-torque issues, and any propulsive propeller on other types you may fly later.

For H130 helicopter ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, teach the exam phraseology clearly, then relate it: the H130 is a turbine helicopter with main rotor and fenestron — not a classic fixed-wing propeller — but "propeller failure" English appears in tests and multi-type careers. Report the failure, the effect on thrust or control, and the intention to land.

Understanding this concept in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Propeller Failure is a malfunction of a propeller or equivalent propulsive rotating system that results in abnormal thrust, vibration, inability to control blade angle, or loss of effective propulsion, requiring abnormal or emergency procedures and clear ATC communication of the operational effect.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de hélice / Propeller Failure.

Malfuncionamento de uma hélice ou sistema rotativo propulsivo equivalente que resulta em empuxo anormal, vibração, incapacidade de controlar o ângulo das pás ou perda de propulsão efetiva, exigindo procedimentos anormais ou de emergência e comunicação clara ao ATC do efeito operacional.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When propeller failure — or the rotorcraft equivalent — occurs, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control and apply the AFM emergency or abnormal checklist
- identify the effect: thrust loss, overspeed, severe vibration, or control difficulty
- for fixed-wing exam English: consider feathering, securing the engine, and drift-down or forced landing as applicable
- for H130 context: treat main rotor or fenestron anomalies as critical — autorotation or emergency landing per AFM
- advise ATC of the failure and the operational effect
- request priority landing or declare Mayday if control or continued flight is threatened

Propeller failure scenarios commonly include:

- propeller overspeed or inability to control blade pitch (exam / multi-engine types)
- severe vibration requiring power reduction and immediate landing
- thrust loss on a propeller-driven aircraft
- H130 relation: main rotor system abnormality or fenestron / anti-torque issue — use precise AFM language
- need for forced landing or priority approach

**Important:** On the exam, use standard propeller-failure English. In the H130, do not invent a propeller you do not have — relate the concept: report the actual system (main rotor / fenestron / engine) and the effect. Precision protects you in both worlds.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to propeller or propulsion failure reports to:

- provide immediate priority landing or forced-landing coordination
- clear traffic and protect the approach path
- understand reduced performance or possible forced landing site
- launch RFF when a landing emergency is declared
- obtain souls on board and fuel remaining

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam scenario language — multi-engine propeller aircraft: a propeller overspeeds and will not feather normally. You secure per checklist and request priority.

You transmit:

ANAC123, propeller failure left engine, requesting priority landing Congonhas, Mayday if performance critical.

For H130 academy reality, a severe main rotor vibration or fenestron abnormality gets the same radio discipline:

ANAC123, main rotor vibration, requesting immediate landing, Mayday.

Different machine. Same communication structure: failure, effect, intention, request.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger propeller failure, say intentions and which engine.
2. Tower: ANAC123, runway one seven L cleared to land, firefighters alerted.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able to maintain altitude, say if forced landing required.
4. Center: ANAC123, priority approved, descend pilot's discretion, report field in sight.
5. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Propeller failure English is about thrust and control — not about pretending every aircraft has a propeller.

On the ICAO exam, expect fixed-wing language: overspeed, feather, secure the engine, priority landing. Learn it. Say it cleanly.

In the H130, your rotating systems are the main rotor and fenestron. If something is wrong there, use the real words from the AFM and the real emergency: control first, land immediately if required, Mayday when safety is threatened.

Brazilian academy pilots often freeze between "exam English" and "my helicopter." Do both: master the exam term, then map it to rotorcraft truth without mixing them into nonsense.

Failure. Effect. Intention. Request. That sequence works for a feathered prop and for a rotor anomaly alike.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You experience a propeller failure with severe vibration and reduced thrust. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control, apply the emergency checklist, and reduce the situation to a safe thrust and configuration as published. I would advise ATC of propeller failure, state the operational effect, and request priority landing or declare Mayday if continued flight or control was threatened. In a helicopter, I would describe the actual system affected — for example main rotor or fenestron — using the same clear structure.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using vague "engine problem" when the issue is propeller or rotor related.  
  ✔ Name the system and effect: propeller failure, unable to feather, severe vibration.

- ❌ Mixing H130 fenestron language into a fixed-wing exam answer incorrectly.  
  ✔ On exams, use propeller phraseology; in the H130, report the real system accurately.

- ❌ Delaying the Mayday when control or thrust is critically degraded.  
  ✔ If safety is threatened, declare Mayday and request immediate landing.

- ❌ Long technical explanations on the frequency during the emergency.  
  ✔ Short: failure, effect, intention, request.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Propeller Failure, Request Priority Landing

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, PROP-uh-ler FAYL-yer, reh-KWEST pry-OR-ih-tee LAN-ding

**Word Stress**

- Propeller → pruh-PEL-er (stress on PEL) — also heard as PROP-uh-ler in rapid radio speech
- Failure → FAYL-yer
- Priority → pry-OR-ih-tee
- Landing → LAN-ding

Practice:

Propeller failure... request priority landing.

Together:

ANAC123, propeller failure, severe vibration, request priority landing.
