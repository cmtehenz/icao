# Brownout

**Catalog ID:** 0073  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers brownout — one of the most dangerous visibility conditions in helicopter operations.

Brownout occurs when rotor downwash recirculates dust, sand, or snow, reducing visibility to zero during landing or takeoff.

In the H130, brownout is a serious risk at unpaved sites, construction areas, and dry Brazilian terrain.

You must use technique, reference points, and clear communication when operating in brownout conditions.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Brownout is a visibility condition caused by rotor downwash lifting dust or sand, obscuring visual references during landing or takeoff.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Brownout.

Condição de visibilidade em que a ressaca do rotor levanta poeira ou areia, obscurecendo referências visuais no pouso ou decolagem.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Brownout conditions exist, the pilot should:

- identify brownout risk before approach — dry loose surface material
- use a stabilized approach with defined hover reference points
- inform ATC or company frequency of brownout landing intentions
- maintain aircraft control using instruments and peripheral cues when visual references are lost
- declare urgency if disorientation or loss of control occurs

Brownout reporting commonly includes:

- brownout conditions on landing or departure
- position and landing site description
- request traffic cleared
- Pan Pan if loss of visual reference causes disorientation

**Important:** Brownout has caused numerous helicopter accidents. Train brownout technique before operating at unpaved sites.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Brownout reports to:

- clear traffic from the landing area
- maintain awareness of delayed landing or departure reports
- coordinate if aircraft becomes disoriented
- request position reports when visual contact is lost
- prepare for possible Pan Pan or emergency reports

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are landing at an unpaved construction site near Brasília in your H130.

The surface is dry red dust — classic brownout conditions.

Before approach, you transmit:

Brasília Approach, ANAC123, landing construction site one zero miles north, caution brownout conditions expected, will report safe on the ground.

On final, downwash engulfs the cockpit in dust at fifty feet.

You maintain hover reference using the pad markers and peripheral cues.

You touch down and report safe on the ground when dust settles.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger brownout caution, report safe on the ground.
2. Tower: ANAC123, no traffic in your area, cleared to land at your discretion.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say if you have visual reference.
4. Tower: ANAC123, report go-around if unable to land.
5. Approach: ANAC123, traffic advise when clear of the brownout area.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Landing construction site, caution brownout conditions, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, brownout on final, maintaining hover reference, will report touchdown.
3. Pan Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, brownout, disoriented, going around.
4. Tower, ANAC123, safe on the ground, brownout landing complete.
5. ANAC123, departing site, brownout expected, will report clear.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Brownout is when the ground disappears in dust.

You are not IFR — but you may have no outside visual reference for seconds.

The sequence:

Identify risk. Stabilized approach. Hover reference points. Instruments and periphery. Commit or go around.

Never let brownout surprise you — if the surface is dry and loose, expect it.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "poeira" or "perdi referência" on frequency.

Use brownout conditions or caution brownout — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130, train at a safe site with loose material before you need it at a real job.

Go around if disoriented — Pan Pan is appropriate.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is brownout and how do you manage it during landing?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Brownout is when rotor downwash lifts dust or sand and obscures visual references during landing or takeoff. I would identify the risk before approach, inform ATC of caution brownout conditions, use a stabilized approach with defined hover references, maintain control using instruments and peripheral cues, go around if disoriented, and report safe on the ground after landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Not anticipating brownout on dry unpaved surfaces.  
  ✔ Identify brownout risk before approach — inform ATC.

- ❌ Saying "não vejo nada" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use brownout conditions or caution brownout.

- ❌ Continuing descent when completely disoriented.  
  ✔ Go around and declare Pan Pan if visual reference is lost.

- ❌ Not using stabilized approach technique.  
  ✔ Stabilized approach with hover reference points reduces brownout risk.
