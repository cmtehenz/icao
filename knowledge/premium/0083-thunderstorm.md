# Thunderstorm

**Catalog ID:** 0083  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers thunderstorms — one of the most hazardous weather phenomena for helicopters.

A Thunderstorm produces lightning, severe turbulence, hail, heavy rain, and strong wind gusts.

In the H130, thunderstorms along the Brazilian coast and inland can develop rapidly in the afternoon.

You must avoid thunderstorms by a wide margin, request weather deviation from ATC, and never attempt to penetrate a cell.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 operational communication.

## 📖 Meaning

A Thunderstorm is a convective weather system producing lightning, thunder, and often severe turbulence, hail, and heavy precipitation.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Tempestade / trovoada.

Sistema convectivo que produz raios, trovões e frequentemente turbulência severa, granizo e precipitação intensa.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a Thunderstorm is present, the pilot should:

- avoid the cell by a wide margin — never penetrate
- request weather deviation from ATC early
- report thunderstorm activity to assist other aircraft
- divert or hold if the route is blocked
- expect wind shear and turbulence near the cell

Thunderstorm reporting commonly includes:

- request weather deviation left or right of course
- thunderstorm ahead, unable to continue present routing
- request vectors around weather
- diverting due thunderstorm

**Important:** Helicopters are especially vulnerable to thunderstorm turbulence and hail. Avoidance is the only safe strategy.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Thunderstorm reports to:

- approve weather deviations when traffic permits
- provide weather radar information when available
- coordinate alternate routing around cells
- sequence traffic clear of convective weather
- assist with diversion to suitable airports

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR from Curitiba to Navegantes in your H130.

A thunderstorm builds across your route.

You transmit early:

Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, thunderstorm ahead, request weather deviation twenty degrees left of course.

Approach approves the deviation and provides vectors around the cell.

You remain clear of the storm and continue to Navegantes.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, deviation approved, remain clear of weather, report when back on course.
2. Approach: ANAC123, weather radar shows cells ahead, advise intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading zero four five, vectors around weather.
4. Center: ANAC123, cleared weather deviation as requested, report clear of weather.
5. Approach: ANAC123, thunderstorm reported by previous aircraft, say if able to continue.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, thunderstorm ahead, request weather deviation left of course.
2. ANAC123, unable to continue present routing due thunderstorm, request vectors around weather.
3. ANAC123, weather deviation twenty degrees left, will report back on course.
4. Approach, ANAC123, clear of thunderstorm, resuming own navigation.
5. ANAC123, diverting to Florianópolis due thunderstorm on route.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Thunderstorms are not negotiable.

You do not go through. You go around.

The sequence:

See. Deviate early. Tell ATC. Remain clear.

Twenty miles of avoidance is better than twenty seconds of hail.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "tempestade na frente" or "cumulonimbus" on frequency.

Use thunderstorm ahead or request weather deviation — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130, you have less margin than a jet — respect the cell.

Train yourself to call the deviation before you are trapped between cells.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if a thunderstorm blocked your route?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would request weather deviation left or right of course, or request vectors around the weather. I would state thunderstorm ahead and that I am unable to continue present routing. If deviation was not possible, I would divert to a suitable alternate and inform ATC of my intentions.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Attempting to fly under or through a thunderstorm.  
  ✔ Avoid by a wide margin — request weather deviation early.

- ❌ Saying "tempestade" without clear intention.  
  ✔ Use thunderstorm ahead, request weather deviation.

- ❌ Waiting until inside the rain shaft to call ATC.  
  ✔ Deviate early while options still exist.

- ❌ Not reporting when clear of weather.  
  ✔ Report clear of weather and resume navigation as cleared.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Thunderstorm Ahead, Request Weather Deviation

**Pronunciation:** THUN-der-storm uh-HED, ree-KWEST WETH-er dee-vee-AY-shun

**Word Stress**

- Thunderstorm → THUN-der-storm
- Ahead → uh-HED
- Deviation → dee-vee-AY-shun

Practice:

Thunderstorm... ahead...

Together:

Thunderstorm ahead, request weather deviation left of course, ANAC123.

Speak clearly — Approach needs direction and reason.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**AVOID** — **A**sk for deviation early, **V**ector around the cell, **O**ut — never penetrate, **I**nform when clear, **D**ivert if trapped.
