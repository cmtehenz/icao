# Risk Assessment

**Catalog ID:** 0492  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Risk Assessment — the structured process of evaluating the likelihood and severity of hazards so the crew can accept, mitigate, or reject an operation before and during flight.

Risk assessment is TEM with teeth. You do not only see the threat — you decide what it means for this aircraft, this crew, and this fuel state.

For H130 operations into confined areas, decks, and weather margins, risk assessment is continuous. Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 English must express that judgment clearly to examiners and to ATC when intentions change.

Understanding risk assessment in ICAO-standard English is essential across Parts 1, 2, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Risk Assessment is the systematic evaluation of identified hazards by considering their probability and potential severity, then selecting controls — procedural, operational, or abort criteria — so that residual risk remains acceptable for the intended flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Avaliação de risco / Risk Assessment.

Avaliação sistemática de perigos identificados considerando probabilidade e severidade potencial, e então selecionando controles — procedimentais, operacionais ou critérios de aborto — para que o risco residual permaneça aceitável para o voo pretendido.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When assessing risk, the crew should:

- identify hazards, then estimate likelihood and severity
- apply mitigations: delay, divert, change profile, add fuel, add crew
- set hard abort triggers before entering the high-risk phase
- for H130: weigh wind, wires, weight, and escape path for every pad
- tell ATC early if risk drives a hold, diversion, or cancellation
- reassess when conditions change — risk is not a one-time form

Risk assessment commonly includes:

- go / no-go decision
- residual risk after mitigation
- weather and performance margins
- operational abort criteria
- fuel and alternate adequacy

**Important:** If mitigations cannot bring risk to an acceptable level, the professional answer is no-go — spoken clearly to the company and, when needed, to ATC.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers interact with crew risk decisions when:

- pilots request hold to reassess weather
- diversions are filed after residual risk becomes unacceptable
- helicopters refuse a pad or deck landing and reposition
- fuel emergencies follow earlier risk misjudgment
- operators cancel IFR legs and need routing changes

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You plan an H130 landing at a mountain helipad. Wind is gusting, density altitude is high, and the escape path is narrow.

You assess: severity high if a rejected landing goes wrong; likelihood elevated in this wind. Mitigations: reduce weight, brief a hard decision point, and request an orbit to assess.

Approach asks intentions. You request orbit, then either continue or divert. Risk assessment becomes a clear radio plan — not a silent hope.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, advise intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, hold as published, advise when ready.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm diversion airport.
4. Tower: ANAC123, report if unable to land, remaining in the pattern.
5. Control: ANAC123, request fuel endurance after diversion decision.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, requesting orbit to assess landing conditions.
2. ANAC123, holding as published, will advise.
3. ANAC123, diverting Congonhas, request routing.
4. ANAC123, unable to land, requesting vectors to alternate pad.
5. ANAC123, endurance one hour one five, diverting.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Risk assessment is judgment you can explain.

Examiners in Part 1 and Part 4 want likelihood, severity, mitigation, and decision — not vague “it was dangerous.”

Use plain structure: hazard, risk level, what we will do, what will make us stop.

In the H130, performance and obstacles make residual risk visible. If the escape is not real, the landing is not available — regardless of passenger pressure.

On frequency, translate risk into intentions: orbit, hold, divert, unable. ATC cannot manage a risk they never hear.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Explain how you assess risk before an H130 confined-area landing in gusty wind.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would identify hazards such as gusty wind, obstacles, and a limited escape path, then judge likelihood and severity for a rejected landing. I would mitigate by reducing weight if needed, briefing a hard decision point, and requesting an orbit to assess. If residual risk remains unacceptable, I would divert or delay and communicate that intention clearly.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Confusing hazard listing with actual risk assessment.  
  ✔ Assess likelihood and severity, then decide mitigations.

- ❌ Continuing because “we usually make it.”  
  ✔ Use abort criteria set before the approach, not hope.

- ❌ Failing to tell ATC when risk forces a plan change.  
  ✔ Translate the decision into hold, orbit, or divert calls.

- ❌ Assessing risk once at dispatch and never again.  
  ✔ Reassess when weather, weight, or ATC plan changes.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Gusty wind at a mountain helipad makes your H130 rejected-landing escape marginal. Approach asks you to advise intentions.

**Your first task**

What do you say while you still need time to assess?

**Your second task**

You decide residual risk is unacceptable. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, requesting orbit to assess landing conditions, will advise. Second task: ANAC123, diverting Congonhas, request routing. Risk assessment ends in a clear operational decision transmitted without apology.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Risk assessment turns hazards into decisions.

Judge likelihood and severity. Mitigate. Set abort lines. Reassess.

If residual risk stays high, say no — to the mission and on the frequency.

In the H130, the pad is optional. The escape is not. Assess until that truth is obvious — then act.
