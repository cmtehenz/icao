# Cleared RNAV Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0148  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Cleared RNAV Approach — the formal ATC authorization to commence and fly the Area Navigation approach to the assigned runway.

RNAV approaches are the most common instrument approach type at Brazilian regional airports, including Navegantes, Joinville, and Florianópolis.

Cleared RNAV Approach is authorization to fly. Expect RNAV Approach was preparation. The distinction is as important here as it is for any precision approach.

Flying an RNAV approach requires verified GPS integrity, a current navigation database, and a fully briefed crew — all completed before this clearance is issued.

This lesson links Part 2 phraseology with the Part 4 technical language needed to discuss RNAV system requirements at ICAO Level 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Cleared RNAV Approach is an ATC authorization for the pilot to commence the Area Navigation approach to the specified runway, following the FMS or GPS-computed lateral and vertical guidance to the applicable minima, where the crew must execute either a landing or a missed approach.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorização do controle de tráfego aéreo para o piloto iniciar a aproximação RNAV para a pista especificada, seguindo a guia lateral e vertical calculada pelo FMS ou GPS até os mínimos aplicáveis, onde a tripulação deve executar o pouso ou a arremetida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives Cleared RNAV Approach, they should:

- confirm the correct RNAV approach procedure is active in the FMS or GPS
- verify the approach mode has been selected and the system is sequencing correctly
- confirm GPS integrity and RAIM are available for the full approach
- cross-check the final approach course and the applicable minima line
- verify step-down altitudes or vertical path angle if VNAV guidance is available
- at the minima — land if visual reference is established, or execute the missed approach

Cleared RNAV Approach operations require:

- navigation database current and applicable
- RAIM prediction confirming integrity for the approach duration
- correct minima selected: LNAV, LNAV/VNAV, or LPV as system supports
- crew briefing completed before clearance receipt
- missed approach procedure fully briefed

**Important:** Cleared RNAV Approach is the authorization. Expect RNAV Approach was the advisory. Never commence the procedure before the cleared instruction. Never accept the clearance if GNSS is degraded or RAIM is unavailable — advise ATC and request an alternate approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Cleared RNAV Approach to:

- authorize the commencement of a non-precision or APV approach
- sequence the aircraft when no ILS is available on the assigned runway
- confirm the approach type at airports with multiple RNAV variants
- allow the crew to fly FMS-computed guidance to the applicable minima
- provide approach clearance after holding or vectoring is complete

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Joinville in your H130 on a day with low cloud and reduced visibility.

You briefed the RNAV approach runway one five earlier in the flight. GPS is normal. RAIM is available. The FMS shows the approach loaded and active.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, proceed direct JUINA, cleared RNAV approach runway one five, report final.

You are now authorized to fly the procedure.

You respond:

Direct JUINA, cleared RNAV approach runway one five, ANAC123.

You select approach mode. The FMS sequences through the intermediate fix and turns onto final.

The vertical path guidance shows on the attitude indicator. Step-down altitudes verified.

At minimums — runway in sight. Continue for landing.

Ready before the clearance. Precise during the approach. Safe at the end.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Two phrases. Same approach type. Completely different status.

Expect RNAV Approach: ATC is telling you to prepare. Load the procedure. Check the system. Brief the crew.

Cleared RNAV Approach: ATC is telling you to fly. The authorization is issued. You may commence.

That transition — from advisory to clearance — is the critical moment in every RNAV arrival.

In the H130, the RNAV approach relies entirely on GPS. There is no glideslope beam. No localizer antenna. The guidance comes from satellites.

So you must verify that guidance before you need it.

RAIM check: confirm it before the approach, not during.

Database currency: check it before departure, not at the destination.

Minima selection: know which minima your aircraft and avionics system qualify for.

LNAV only, or LNAV/VNAV, or LPV — the difference in decision altitude can be significant.

Know your system. Trust it when it is verified. Refuse it when it is not.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Explain the difference between Expect RNAV Approach and Cleared RNAV Approach and what action each requires.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Expect RNAV Approach is an advisory issued by ATC before the clearance, allowing the crew to load the approach into the FMS, verify GPS integrity and RAIM, select the correct minima, and brief the procedure. No approach maneuver is authorized by the advisory. Cleared RNAV Approach is the formal authorization to commence the procedure. When the crew receives the clearance, they may activate the approach mode and fly the FMS-computed path to the applicable minima. If GNSS or RAIM is unavailable when the clearance is issued, the crew must advise ATC and request an alternate approach rather than accepting a clearance they cannot safely execute.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Beginning the RNAV approach descent after Expect RNAV Approach.  
  ✔ Expect is advisory only. Wait for the formal cleared instruction.

- ❌ Accepting Cleared RNAV Approach when RAIM is unavailable.  
  ✔ Advise ATC: ANAC123, unable RNAV, GNSS not available, request alternate approach.

- ❌ Using LNAV minima when the system qualifies for LPV.  
  ✔ Always select the lowest applicable minima your avionics system supports.

- ❌ Not verifying navigation database currency before the approach.  
  ✔ Check the database expiry before departure — not after receiving the clearance.

**Pronunciation:** kleerd AR-nav uh-PROHCH RUN-way WUN FYVE

**Word Stress**

- Cleared → kleerd (strong, one syllable)
- RNAV → AR-nav (two syllables, stress on first)
- Approach → uh-PROHCH
- One Five → WUN FYVE

Practice:

Cleared... RNAV... Approach... Runway... One Five.

Together:

Cleared RNAV approach runway one five, ANAC123.

RNAV is two syllables in operational speech: AR-nav. Not spelled out as R-N-A-V.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**VALID** — **V**erify RAIM before the clearance, **A**ctivate the correct approach procedure, **L**oad and confirm the minima, **I**dentify step-down or vertical path, **D**ecide at MDA or DA — land or go around.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Joinville Approach transmits: "ANAC123, proceed direct JUINA, cleared RNAV approach runway one five, cross JUINA at or above two thousand."

**Your first task**

Read back the full clearance correctly.

**Your second task**

Your GPS then shows a RAIM fault warning before you reach JUINA. What do you say to ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would respond: Direct JUINA, cleared RNAV approach runway one five, cross JUINA at or above two thousand, ANAC123. If RAIM faulted before reaching JUINA I would immediately advise: ANAC123, unable RNAV approach, RAIM fault, request alternate approach. Advising early gives ATC time to assign an alternative and prevents commencing an approach with degraded navigation integrity.
