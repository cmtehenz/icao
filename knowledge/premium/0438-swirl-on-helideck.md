# Swirl on Helideck

**Catalog ID:** 0438  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Swirl on Helideck — turbulent, circulating airflow over an offshore helideck caused by platform structure, exhaust, and relative wind, which can produce sudden updrafts, downdrafts, and yaw disturbances on short final and in the hover.

Swirl is a high-threat H130 topic for Brazilian Campos and Santos Basin operations out of Macaé, Rio, and Vitória corridors.

You must describe deck-environment effects, go-around decisions, and coordination with deck crew and ATC in ICAO English for Parts 2, 3, and 4.

## 📖 Meaning

Swirl on Helideck is disturbed, rotating or recirculating airflow over and around an offshore helideck created by the installation structure, hot exhaust, and wind, resulting in unpredictable lift and yaw changes during approach, hover, and departure.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Turbilhonamento no helideck / swirl on helideck.

Fluxo de ar perturbado, rotativo ou recirculante sobre e ao redor de um helideck offshore criado pela estrutura da instalação, exaustão quente e vento, resultando em mudanças imprevisíveis de sustentação e guinada durante a aproximação, o pairamento e a decolagem.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When operating with possible helideck swirl, the pilot should:

- obtain relative wind, deck status, and hotspot or exhaust advisories before final
- brief a firm go-around gate for uncommanded sink, yaw, or turbulence
- avoid prolonged hover in known swirl or hot exhaust sectors
- wave off if control margin disappears on short final
- report swirl or turbulence to ATC and the next inbound crew when able

Swirl-related reporting commonly includes:

- going around, helideck swirl / turbulence
- request orbit, deck conditions unsuitable
- on deck, strong swirl on short final for information
- lifting, expect swirl on departure

**Important:** Green deck does not mean calm air. Structure-induced swirl can exist with a green light. If the aircraft is not controllable on the path, go around — status lights do not override physics.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers and offshore coordination use swirl reports to:

- warn following helicopters of deck turbulence
- support go-arounds and orbits clear of the platform
- coordinate diversions when deck environment is unworkable
- maintain traffic picture during multiple wave-offs
- assist emergency landings if control is lost near the deck

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on short final to an offshore helideck in your H130. Deck is green. Relative wind is over the structure.

Entering the last fifty feet, an uncommanded sink and yaw hit from swirl off the accommodation block.

You go around:

Going around, helideck swirl, uncommanded sink, remaining clear, request orbit.

You report the condition for the next aircraft and wait for a more favorable relative wind or revised approach sector.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, helideck green, wind three six zero at two zero, report on deck.
2. Control: ANAC123, roger going around, orbit east, advise when ready.
3. Control: ANAC123, next aircraft advised of swirl report, say intentions.
4. Control: ANAC123, platform recommends approach from south, swirl reduced, cleared approach.
5. Control: ANAC123, roger lifting, report coastal freecall.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Helideck swirl is invisible until it hits you.

Structure, exhaust, and wind direction write the story. Your job is a hard gate: if sink or yaw is uncommanded and outside tolerance — wave off.

In the H130, power margin on a high deck with swirl is the exam. Part 3 and Part 4 love: green deck, severe swirl on short final — what do you do? Answer: go around, state helideck swirl, orbit, wait for better relative wind or sector; do not force the landing.

Pass the report to the next crew. Brazilian basin ops improve when everyone shares the turbulence picture.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Deck is green but swirl causes uncommanded sink on short final. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would initiate an immediate go-around clear of the platform and transmit: going around, helideck swirl, uncommanded sink, remaining clear, request orbit. I would not continue to land through uncontrollable turbulence. When relative wind or approach sector improves, or if conditions remain poor, I would request another approach or divert.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing because "deck is green."  
  ✔ Green is status, not a turbulence guarantee. Go around if unstable.

- ❌ Not reporting swirl for the next helicopter.  
  ✔ Pass the information via ATC or company.

- ❌ Prolonged hover in the hotspot or swirl sector.  
  ✔ Land promptly or leave.

- ❌ Vague call: "going around, turbulence."  
  ✔ Say helideck swirl so the cause is clear.
