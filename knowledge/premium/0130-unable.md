# Unable

**Catalog ID:** 0130  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Unable — one of the most important and most underused words in aviation radio communication.

Unable means: I cannot comply with your instruction or clearance.

It is simple, direct, and required when a pilot genuinely cannot execute what ATC has assigned.

Brazilian pilots are often reluctant to say Unable. There is a cultural tendency to accept ATC instructions without question, even when compliance would compromise safety or exceed aircraft limitations.

In the H130, flying into Congonhas, Florianópolis, or Joinville, knowing when and how to say Unable — and what to offer in its place — is a critical ICAO Part 2 and Part 3 skill. It may be the most important word you ever transmit.

## 📖 Meaning

Unable is the ICAO-standard word used to inform ATC that the pilot cannot comply with an ATC clearance, instruction, or request. It is used when compliance is not possible due to aircraft performance, equipment limitations, weather, regulations, or safety concerns.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Impossível / Não consigo cumprir.

Palavra ICAO padrão utilizada para informar ao controle que o piloto não pode cumprir uma autorização, instrução ou solicitação do ATC. É usada quando o cumprimento não é possível devido ao desempenho da aeronave, limitações de equipamento, condições meteorológicas, regulamentos ou preocupações de segurança.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When to use Unable:

- when the assigned altitude exceeds aircraft performance capability
- when the assigned speed is outside aircraft limits
- when the assigned routing enters IMC the aircraft cannot handle
- when a procedure is incompatible with aircraft equipment
- when compliance would endanger the flight
- when a clearance conflicts with a regulatory or operator limitation

After transmitting Unable, the pilot should:

- state the reason briefly when possible
- offer an alternative when practical
- request a new clearance or instruction
- never attempt to comply with an instruction beyond safe limits

Unable operations commonly include:

- unable to maintain assigned altitude due performance
- unable to accept the speed restriction
- unable ILS — not equipped / equipment unserviceable
- unable immediate takeoff — not ready

**Important:** Unable is not rude. Unable is professional. It is your responsibility as Pilot in Command to declare Unable when compliance would compromise safety. ATC will offer an alternative. Saying nothing and attempting to comply with an unsafe instruction is the dangerous choice.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers respond to Unable by:

- issuing a revised clearance or instruction
- requesting the reason for inability
- coordinating an alternative routing or level
- accommodating the pilot's limitation in the sequence
- treating the response as safety-critical information

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are climbing out of Joinville in your H130.

Departure transmits:

ANAC123, climb to flight level one eight zero.

Your H130 performance in current conditions limits you to flight level one four zero. You transmit:

ANAC123, unable flight level one eight zero, H130 maximum today flight level one four zero, request flight level one four zero.

Departure responds immediately:

ANAC123, roger, climb flight level one four zero, report reaching.

You read back and climb:

Climb flight level one four zero, wilco, ANAC123.

You operated safely, professionally, and in full compliance with your aircraft limitations.

If you had said nothing and attempted to climb to flight level one eight zero, you would have accepted a clearance you could not safely execute. That is the scenario Unable prevents.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, unable to approve request, traffic.
2. Tower: ANAC123, unable immediate takeoff, hold position.
3. Approach: ANAC123, roger your Unable, fly heading zero nine zero, rejoin sequence.
4. Control: ANAC123, say reason for Unable, if able.
5. Approach: ANAC123, Unable accepted, cleared new routing via XINGU.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, unable flight level one eight zero, request flight level one four zero.
2. ANAC123, unable ILS — equipment unserviceable, request RNAV approach.
3. ANAC123, unable assigned speed, minimum clean speed is one six zero knots.
4. ANAC123, unable immediate takeoff — not ready, request two minutes.
5. ANAC123, unable to comply, request alternative routing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Unable is your most important safety word.

It is also the word Brazilian pilots are most reluctant to use.

The cultural bias in Brazil toward acceptance — toward not inconveniencing the controller or the sequence — sometimes overrides a pilot's professional judgment.

This is the wrong bias to carry in the flight deck.

ATC issues instructions based on available information. They do not always know your performance ceiling. They do not know your equipment status. They do not know your passenger situation.

You do.

When an instruction exceeds what you can safely execute, Unable is the professional response.

It is not disobedience. It is communication.

After Unable, offer what you can:

Unable flight level one eight zero, request flight level one four zero.

Unable ILS, request RNAV approach.

Unable immediate takeoff, ready in two minutes.

Give ATC something to work with.

In the H130, performance limitations and equipment specifics will regularly produce situations where ATC's default clearance does not fit. Train Unable until it feels as natural as Wilco.

The day you need it, it must come out immediately.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

A controller clears you to an altitude your aircraft cannot reach today due to performance. What do you say, and why is it important to respond immediately?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would transmit: ANAC123, unable [altitude], maximum today [my limit], request [my limit]. It is essential to respond immediately because ATC is planning separation and sequencing based on the assumption that I will reach the assigned altitude. If I say nothing and begin climbing while unable to comply, ATC may issue conflicting instructions to other traffic believing I will reach the assigned level. Immediate communication of Unable allows ATC to reroute or resequence safely.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting a clearance beyond aircraft limits to avoid inconveniencing ATC.  
  ✔ Unable is a professional obligation. Transmit it clearly and immediately.

- ❌ Saying only "Unable, ANAC123" without reason or alternative.  
  ✔ State the limitation and offer an alternative: Unable flight level one eight zero, request one four zero.

- ❌ Delaying the Unable transmission hoping the situation resolves.  
  ✔ Transmit Unable as soon as you determine compliance is not possible.

- ❌ Using "Unable" for preference rather than genuine limitation.  
  ✔ Unable is for genuine inability — not inconvenience or personal preference.

**Target Phrase:** Unable

**Pronunciation:** un-AY-bul

**Word Stress**

- Unable → un-AY-bul (stress on second syllable)

Practice:

Un... AY... bul... Unable...

Together:

Speak it firmly. Unable should sound decisive, not apologetic.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**UNABLE = Act Now** — **U**nexpected clearance beyond limits, **N**otify ATC immediately, **A**lways offer an alternative, **B**e specific about the limitation, **L**imit delay to zero, **E**xpect a revised clearance.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Curitiba Approach transmits: "ANAC123, maintain one eight zero knots to the marker." Your H130 minimum speed in current configuration is one six zero knots and maximum is one seven zero knots for approach.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit? Include your limitation and an alternative.

**Your second task**

Approach responds: "ANAC123, best speed to the marker." What does this mean and how do you reply?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would transmit: ANAC123, unable one eight zero knots, maximum approach speed one seven zero knots, request one seven zero knots. Best speed to the marker means ATC is accepting my maximum — I should confirm: ANAC123, best speed one seven zero knots. Best speed is an instruction to fly as fast as your configuration allows — not an order to exceed limits. Stating Unable with the specific limitation immediately allows ATC to manage the sequence around my actual capability.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Unable is not a failure.

It is the honest, professional declaration of a real operational constraint.

Controllers need to know your limitations to keep everyone safe.

Do not let cultural pressure push you past your aircraft's limits.

Do not let the desire to avoid inconvenience become a safety compromise.

Train Unable. Say it clearly. Offer an alternative.

The controller will always find a solution.

Your job is to communicate the constraint — immediately, professionally, and without hesitation.

Unable. It may be the most important word in your radio vocabulary.
