# Obstacle Clearance

**Catalog ID:** 0390  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Obstacle Clearance — the requirement to maintain a safe vertical and lateral margin from terrain and obstacles during takeoff, climb, approach, and missed approach.

Obstacle Clearance is why departure procedures, climb gradients, and approach minima exist. It is performance and procedure together.

For H130 Brazilian operations — city helipads, Congonhas rising terrain, coastal cliffs, and offshore platforms — obstacles define where you can go and how you must climb. Discussing inability to meet a climb gradient or requesting an alternate departure in ICAO English is Part 2, Part 3, and Part 4 material.

## 📖 Meaning

Obstacle Clearance is the maintenance of required minimum distances from terrain and obstacles, achieved through published procedures, climb performance, and crew adherence to altitude and tracking constraints.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Afastamento de obstáculos / Obstacle Clearance.

Manutenção das distâncias mínimas exigidas em relação ao terreno e aos obstáculos, obtida por procedimentos publicados, desempenho de subida e cumprimento pela tripulação das restrições de altitude e de trajetória.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ensuring obstacle clearance, the crew should:

- brief departure and approach obstacle environment
- verify climb gradient capability at actual takeoff weight
- follow SID tracks and altitude constraints precisely
- request an alternate departure if unable to meet gradient
- go around or divert rather than continue below safe margins

Obstacle Clearance planning includes:

- engine-out or reduced-performance climb capability where applicable
- published minimum climb gradients on SIDs
- MSA and approach segment obstacle clearance altitudes
- visual obstacles around helipads and confined areas
- night and IMC when obstacles are not visible

**Important:** If you cannot meet the published climb gradient at current weight, you are not cleared by performance — even if ATC clears the SID. Reduce weight, request a different departure, or delay.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers protect obstacle clearance by:

- assigning SIDs and altitudes that provide procedural separation from obstacles
- issuing climb-via and altitude constraints
- providing radar vectors with minimum vectoring altitudes
- approving alternate departures when performance-limited
- coordinating missed approach instructions that remain obstacle-safe

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Departure from a mountain-adjacent field in southern Brazil. The SID requires a steep climb gradient. Your H130 at current weight and density altitude cannot meet it.

You call before taxi:

ANAC123, unable SID climb gradient at current weight, request alternate departure or delay for weight reduction.

Clearance Delivery: ANAC123, roger, expect radar departure, maintain visual with terrain, climb as soon as practicable to five thousand.

You respond:

ANAC123, radar departure, climb five thousand, wilco.

Obstacle clearance protected by an honest performance call.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Clearance: ANAC123, cleared via SID, climb gradient required.
2. Departure: ANAC123, climb via SID, comply with restrictions.
3. Radar: ANAC123, radar departure approved, maintain terrain clearance visually.
4. Approach: ANAC123, missed approach as published, climb to four thousand.
5. Tower: ANAC123, advise able to meet climb gradient.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Obstacle clearance is non-negotiable.

Procedures assume you can climb and track as published. If performance says you cannot — change weight or change procedure before you take off.

In Brazilian terrain — Serra do Mar, coastal hills, city towers — the H130's flexibility in visual conditions does not cancel IFR gradient requirements. Visual terrain clearance when approved is a specific clearance, not a casual substitute.

On approach, minima and missed approach climbs exist for obstacles. Do not dive below minima hoping to "see and avoid" in IMC.

Brief obstacles. Meet the gradient. Or say unable.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The SID requires a climb gradient your aircraft cannot meet at current weight. ATC has already issued the SID clearance. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not accept the SID as usable: ANAC123, unable SID climb gradient at current weight, request alternate departure or delay for weight reduction. A clearance does not create performance. I would remain on the ground until an obstacle-safe plan exists. Clear inability language allows ATC to issue a radar departure or different SID.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting a SID without checking climb gradient capability.  
  ✔ Verify gradient vs performance before accepting the clearance.

- ❌ Relying on visual see-and-avoid in IMC for obstacles.  
  ✔ Fly published altitudes and procedures for obstacle clearance.

- ❌ Not advising ATC when unable to meet a restriction.  
  ✔ State unable climb gradient / unable altitude restriction early.

- ❌ Reducing margin on missed approach to "make the field."  
  ✔ Execute the published missed approach for obstacle protection.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Obstacle clearance is why procedures and performance charts exist.

Meet the gradient. Fly the track. Respect minima.

If you cannot — say unable, request another plan, reduce weight.

In the H130 among Brazilian terrain and city obstacles, honesty about climb capability is survival language.

Clear the obstacles. Or do not go.

That is obstacle clearance professionalism.
