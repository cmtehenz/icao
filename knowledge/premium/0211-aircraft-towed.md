# Aircraft Towed

**Catalog ID:** 0211  
**Category:** Ground Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Aircraft Towed — the status and phraseology used when an aircraft is being moved on the ground by a tug, without using its own engine power for taxi.

An aircraft under tow is traffic. It occupies taxiways, crosses aprons, and may have limited ability to stop or maneuver quickly. Pilots and controllers must treat towed movements with the same respect as powered taxi — often with more caution.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, you may rarely tow the H130 itself, but you will share Brazilian airports with towed fixed-wing trainers and airline aircraft. ICAO exam English also tests this concept. Learn it with H130 context: hold for the tow, give way, and never start into a tug path.

Understanding how to report, acknowledge, and sequence around an aircraft being towed is important for Part 2 proficiency and apron situational awareness.

## 📖 Meaning

Aircraft towed means an aircraft is being pushed or pulled on the ground by a tug or towing vehicle, normally with engines shut down, and is not taxiing under its own power.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aeronave rebocada / aircraft towed / under tow.

Aeronave sendo empurrada ou puxada no solo por um trator de reboque, normalmente com motores desligados, sem táxi sob potência própria.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an aircraft is towed, or when you operate near a tow, crews should:

- advise Ground before commencing a tow onto the movement area
- state route and destination stand or hangar
- display lights and follow marshaller or tug crew procedures
- give way to or hold for towed traffic when instructed
- never assume a towed aircraft can accelerate clear like a taxiing jet
- confirm when the tow is complete and the aircraft is parked

Aircraft towed operations commonly include:

- tow from hangar to stand
- tow from stand to hangar for maintenance
- pushback from stand onto the taxiway (related but distinct phraseology)
- crossing active taxi lanes under Ground control
- holding other traffic until the tow is clear

**Important:** "Aircraft towed" or "under tow" tells everyone the movement is slow, wide, and dependent on the tug. In the H130, if you are cleared to hover taxi near a tow, give extra margin for rotor wash — do not blast the tug crew or destabilize light towed aircraft.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers track towed aircraft to:

- separate slow tug movements from normal taxi speed traffic
- protect runway and taxiway crossings during tows
- advise other pilots that traffic ahead cannot expedite
- coordinate maintenance repositioning with active operations
- prevent startup or taxi clearances into an active tow path

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are ready to hover taxi the H130 from the academy ramp at Curitiba. Ground advises that an aircraft is being towed from the hangar across your planned route to stand six.

You hold on the ramp. You watch the tug, the tow bar, and the towed trainer crawl across the apron.

When Ground reports the towed aircraft clear of Alpha, you receive hover taxi clearance and proceed.

Later, if your own academy needs an H130 moved into the hangar with blades folded and a ground handling team, operations may coordinate a ground move. Even then, Ground must know when any aircraft occupies the movement area under tow or under handling.

The key English is simple: aircraft under tow, route, and clear when done.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, hold position, aircraft under tow crossing Alpha to stand six.
2. Ground: ANAC123, give way to aircraft being towed on Bravo.
3. Apron: ANAC123, towed aircraft clear of the ramp, cleared to start.
4. Ground: ANAC123, caution, aircraft towed from hangar to maintenance area.
5. Ground: ANAC123, after the towed traffic, taxi via Alpha to holding point Charlie.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, holding position, aircraft under tow in sight.
2. ANAC123, giving way to aircraft being towed on Bravo.
3. ANAC123, towed aircraft clear, cleared to start.
4. ANAC123, roger, caution towed aircraft hangar to maintenance.
5. ANAC123, after towed traffic, taxi via Alpha to holding point Charlie.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Under tow means under someone else's control — the tug crew — but still under ATC's surface plan.

You do not overtake a tow for convenience. You do not hover close to "sneak past." You hold until clear.

For ICAO speaking and listening exams, expect phrases like aircraft being towed, under tow, and tow complete. Answer with hold, give way, or continue after the tow.

In H130 operations, your threat is rotor wash on the tug and misjudged clearance at intersections. Slow traffic needs more space, not less.

If you are the one coordinating a tow for maintenance, make sure Ground knows before the tug enters the movement area. Surprise tows create surprise conflicts.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Ground advises that an aircraft is being towed across your taxi route. Explain what you do.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would hold position or give way as instructed, keep the towed aircraft in sight, and not proceed into the conflicting route until Ground confirms the tow is clear or I can see that it is safely past. I would read back the hold or give-way instruction. After the towed aircraft is clear, I would continue on my taxi or hover taxi clearance. I would allow extra space because a tug cannot expedite like a normal taxiing aircraft.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating a towed aircraft as "not real traffic."  
  ✔ Treat under tow as surface traffic with priority as assigned by ATC.

- ❌ Trying to pass closely in a hover to save time.  
  ✔ Hold clear. Rotor wash and wingtip clearance matter.

- ❌ Confusing pushback with a long hangar tow.  
  ✔ Both are ground moves without taxi power — follow the specific clearance given.

- ❌ Continuing after "hold for tow" without hearing clear.  
  ✔ Wait for clear confirmation or visual pass, then continue as cleared.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Holding Position, Aircraft Under Tow in Sight

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, HOHL-ding puh-ZISH-un, AIR-kraft UN-der TOH in SITE

**Word Stress**

- Holding → HOHL-ding
- Position → puh-ZISH-un (stress on second)
- Aircraft → AIR-kraft
- Tow → TOH

Practice:

Holding position... aircraft under tow in sight.

Together:

ANAC123, holding position, aircraft under tow crossing Alpha, traffic in sight.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**TOWED** — **T**ug is moving the aircraft, **O**wn engines normally off, **W**ait and give way when told, **E**xtra space for slow movement, **D**epart your hold only when clear.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are cleared to hover taxi via Alpha at Florianópolis. Before you move, Ground reports a twin under tow entering Alpha from the hangar.

**Your first task**

Ground says: "ANAC123, hold position, aircraft under tow on Alpha." What do you reply?

**Your second task**

Ground later says: "ANAC123, towed aircraft clear of Alpha, hover taxi to holding point Charlie via Alpha." What is your readback?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, holding position, aircraft under tow in sight. Second task: ANAC123, towed aircraft clear, hover taxi to holding point Charlie via Alpha. I would verify Alpha is visually clear before lifting into the hover taxi.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Aircraft towed is slow metal under tug power.

It still owns the pavement until it is clear. Your job is to hear the advisory, hold or give way, and continue only when the route is free.

H130 pilots may not tow every day. They share every busy Brazilian apron with aircraft that do.

Respect the tow. Protect the tug crew from rotor wash. Speak clear hold and continue readbacks.

That is ICAO ground English that prevents apron collisions.
