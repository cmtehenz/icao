# Fumes in the Cabin

**Catalog ID:** 0040  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers an invisible but dangerous cabin emergency.

Fumes in the Cabin means unidentified vapours or smells without visible smoke.

Oil fumes, electrical odours, and unknown smells can incapacitate the crew before you see any smoke.

This is different from smoke in the cabin — and your report must reflect that distinction.

Pan Pan is often appropriate initially, but strong fumes demand immediate action.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Fumes in the Cabin are unidentified vapours or odours in the cabin or cockpit without visible smoke.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Vapores na cabine.

Odores ou vapores não identificados na cabine ou cockpit, sem fumaça visível.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Fumes in the Cabin occurs, the pilot should:

- don oxygen immediately
- identify whether fumes are in cockpit or cabin
- investigate the source if safely possible
- declare Pan Pan for urgency or Mayday if crew is affected
- request descent and priority landing if fumes worsen

Fumes in the Cabin reporting commonly includes:

- Pan Pan, strong fumes in the cockpit
- fumes in the cabin, request descent
- fumes increasing, request immediate landing
- fumes dissipated, continuing with caution

**Important:** Fumes are not smoke. Report fumes specifically. Oil fumes can incapacitate crew — treat strong unknown fumes with the same urgency as visible smoke.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Fumes in the Cabin reports to:

- assign descent and priority vectors
- coordinate medical support on arrival if crew is affected
- request fumes status updates during descent
- clear traffic for priority approach
- alert ground services for possible incapacitation

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are at six thousand feet in your H130 on a corporate flight from Brasília to Goiânia.

A strong oily smell fills the cockpit. No visible smoke — but your eyes begin to water.

You don oxygen and transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Brasília Approach, ANAC123, strong fumes in the cockpit, request descent to four thousand feet.

Approach assigns descent and asks you to report fumes status.

The smell intensifies. You upgrade your request:

ANAC123, fumes increasing, request immediate landing at Brasília.

You land safely and request medical evaluation for the crew.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, descend to four thousand, report fumes status.
2. Approach: ANAC123, turn right direct Brasília.
3. Tower: ANAC123, medical advice available on arrival.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say number of crew affected.
5. Tower: ANAC123, cleared priority approach runway one one left.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fumes versus smoke — know the difference.

Smoke is visible. Fumes are smell and vapour without visible smoke.

Brazilian pilots say smell bad or cheiro estranho on frequency.

Use fumes in the cockpit or fumes in the cabin — standard ICAO words.

The Students material uses Pan Pan for strong fumes in the cockpit.

That is correct — but do not underestimate fumes.

Oil fumes from engine bleed air or electrical faults can incapacitate you in minutes.

Don oxygen first. Then report.

If fumes worsen, upgrade to immediate landing without waiting for visible smoke.

In the H130, hydraulic or engine oil fumes entering the cabin through ventilation are a documented risk.

Specify cockpit versus cabin — the location tells ATC how impaired the flying pilot may be.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How are fumes different from smoke in your report to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Smoke means visible smoke in the cabin or cockpit. Fumes mean smell or vapour without visible smoke. I report fumes in the cockpit or fumes in the cabin specifically. Fumes can be as dangerous as smoke because they can incapacitate the crew before smoke appears.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying smell bad instead of fumes or unknown odour.  
  ✔ Use fumes in the cockpit or fumes in the cabin.

- ❌ Underestimating fumes — treating them as minor discomfort.  
  ✔ Don oxygen and request descent — oil fumes can incapacitate crew.

- ❌ Not mentioning cockpit versus cabin location.  
  ✔ Specify where fumes are detected — cockpit or cabin.

- ❌ Waiting for visible smoke before declaring urgency.  
  ✔ Report strong fumes with Pan Pan and upgrade if worsening.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Strong Fumes in the Cockpit

**Pronunciation:** STRONG FYOOMZ in the COCK-pit

**Word Stress**

- Fumes → FYOOMZ (one syllable)
- Cockpit → COCK-pit
- Odour → OH-dur

Practice:

Pan Pan Pan... strong fumes in the cockpit...

Together:

Pan Pan Pan, Brasília Approach, ANAC123, strong fumes in the cockpit, request descent.

Speak clearly — fumes are invisible, so your words carry the urgency.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**FUMES** — **F**ind source if safe, **U**se oxygen, **M**onitor crew, **E**xpedite if worsening, **S**ay status to ATC.

No smoke does not mean no danger.

Don oxygen. Report fumes specifically. Distinguish from smoke.
