# Supercooled Large Droplets

**Catalog ID:** 0364  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Supercooled Large Droplets (SLD) — water droplets below freezing that are large enough to form ice aft of protected surfaces, rapidly creating severe icing beyond the design of many ice-protection systems.

SLD is Critical. Visual cues include ice aft of boots or heated areas, and unusually rapid accretion. The response is immediate exit and a clear report — not continued flight to destination for schedule.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, many helicopters are not certified for flight into known SLD. ICAO exams expect the term, the hazard, and escape phraseology.

Understanding supercooled large droplets in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Supercooled Large Droplets are water droplets that remain liquid below freezing temperature and are large enough to strike and freeze on unprotected surfaces behind normal ice-protection zones, producing severe icing that can quickly exceed aircraft certification and control margins.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gotas grandes super-resfriadas / Supercooled Large Droplets (SLD).

Gotículas de água que permanecem líquidas abaixo de zero e são grandes o bastante para atingir e congelar em superfícies desprotegidas atrás das zonas normais de proteção contra gelo, produzindo gelo severo que pode rapidamente exceder a certificação da aeronave e as margens de controle.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When SLD icing is suspected or encountered, the crew should:

- recognize cues: ice aft of protected surfaces, rapid accretion, freezing rain/drizzle environments
- exit the conditions immediately — climb, descend, or divert as required
- declare urgency if performance or control is affected
- report severe icing and suspected SLD or freezing precipitation type
- do not rely on normal anti-ice alone to "keep up"
- land as soon as practicable if ice remains hazardous after exit

SLD-related reporting commonly includes:

- severe icing, ice forming aft of protected surfaces
- suspected supercooled large droplets
- freezing rain / freezing drizzle with severe icing
- request immediate altitude change / diversion

**Important:** You may not say "SLD" on every frequency worldwide, but you must communicate severe icing and the need to exit. On the ICAO oral, know the full term supercooled large droplets.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to SLD and severe icing reports to:

- clear immediate altitude changes without delay
- warn other aircraft away from the same layer
- coordinate priority diversions
- keep holds out of the reported icing band
- relay special air reports to meteorology

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

In IMC near freezing level over southern Brazil in the H130, ice builds unusually fast and appears behind areas you would expect to stay relatively clear. Control feel changes.

You report severe icing, request immediate descent and diversion, and state freezing precipitation with rapid ice accretion. Approach treats it as priority. You exit the layer toward warmer air and land. Post-flight, ice residue confirms a serious encounter.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, say icing intensity and altitude.
2. Approach: ANAC123, immediate descent approved, maintain three thousand.
3. Center: ANAC123, divert approved, priority handling.
4. Approach: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported severe icing in freezing drizzle.
5. Center: ANAC123, roger severe icing, advise when clear of icing.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, severe icing at five thousand, ice aft of protected surfaces, request immediate descent.
2. Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, severe icing, suspected SLD, request diversion.
3. ANAC123, freezing rain, severe icing, unable to continue, request priority landing.
4. ANAC123, exiting icing conditions, descending three thousand.
5. ANAC123, clear of severe icing, diverting, request vectors.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

SLD is the icing chapter where systems lose the race.

If ice is forming where protection does not reach, you are already behind. Leave.

Brazilian exam answers should include the full phrase supercooled large droplets and the action: immediate exit.

In the H130, assume you are not equipped for SLD combat. Your tools are altitude change, diversion, and clear English.

Critical means minutes matter. Speak like it.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What are supercooled large droplets and what do you do if you encounter them?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Supercooled large droplets are below-freezing liquid droplets large enough to create ice behind protected surfaces and cause severe icing. I would exit the conditions immediately with a climb, descent, or diversion, report severe icing to ATC, and declare urgency if performance or control is affected.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing because anti-ice is selected on.  
  ✔ SLD can exceed protection — exit immediately.

- ❌ Reporting only "icing" without severity.  
  ✔ Say severe icing and request immediate change.

- ❌ Not knowing the English term on the oral exam.  
  ✔ Practice supercooled large droplets until fluent.

- ❌ Holding in the same freezing layer.  
  ✔ Unable hold — request clear of icing conditions.
