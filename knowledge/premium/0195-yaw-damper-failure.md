# Yaw Damper Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0195  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Yaw Damper Failure — loss of the yaw damper function that normally dampens Dutch roll or yaw oscillations, requiring manual yaw control compensation, possible speed or configuration limitations per the AFM, and ATC communication when the failure affects handling, comfort, or ability to comply with the clearance.

Yaw damper failure is often subtle compared with engine or hydraulic failures. The aircraft may develop uncomfortable yaw oscillation, especially in turbulence or at certain speed/altitude combinations. The crew must recognize the change, apply AFM limitations, and decide whether ATC needs to know — for example, if you need a longer final, reduced maneuvering, or priority to land.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, yaw stability augmentation (where fitted as part of the automatic flight control system) supports smoother IFR flight on routes serving Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Santos Dumont. Understanding how to describe the failure and any operational limitation in ICAO English supports Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Yaw Damper Failure is the malfunction or disengagement of the yaw damping system that normally reduces unwanted yaw oscillations, resulting in degraded directional stability characteristics, possible AFM operating limitations, and the need for increased pilot compensation and, when operationally relevant, notification to ATC.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do Amortecedor de Guinada / Yaw Damper Failure.

Mau funcionamento ou desconexão do sistema de amortecimento de guinada que normalmente reduz oscilações indesejadas de guinada, resultando em características degradadas de estabilidade direcional, possíveis limitações operacionais do AFM, e a necessidade de maior compensação pelo piloto e, quando operacionalmente relevante, notificação ao ATC.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When yaw damper failure occurs, the crew should:

- recognize increased yaw oscillation, disconnect indications, or system fault annunciations
- assume manual yaw control and damp oscillations with coordinated pedal inputs as appropriate
- consult the AFM for speed, altitude, or autopilot limitations with yaw damper inoperative
- disconnect or limit related automation if required by procedure
- advise ATC if handling qualities require a simpler approach, longer final, or priority
- avoid aggressive maneuvering that aggravates Dutch roll or yaw hunting

Yaw damper failure scenarios commonly include:

- system disconnect in cruise turbulence with noticeable yaw oscillation
- inability to re-engage yaw damper after fault
- AFM restriction on autopilot use without yaw damper
- request for extended final to reduce last-minute heading changes
- continuation to destination with limitation versus diversion if handling is unacceptable

**Important:** Fly the aircraft smoothly. Do not fight oscillations with large aggressive pedal inputs. In the H130, follow the specific AFM guidance for stability augmentation failures. Tell ATC about the failure when it changes what you can accept — not as a long technical story, but as a clear operational limitation: yaw damper inoperative, request extended final / vectors for a stable approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with yaw damper failure reports to:

- understand requests for extended finals or reduced maneuvering
- avoid last-second heading changes that increase crew workload
- provide priority or uncomplicated routing when handling is degraded
- coordinate a suitable runway or approach with fewer tight turns
- maintain awareness that the aircraft may be hand-flying with limitations

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are descending toward Santos Dumont. In light turbulence, the yaw damper disconnects. The helicopter develops a mild yaw oscillation that you control with smooth pedal work. The AFM allows continuation with the autopilot limited or disengaged as applicable.

You advise Approach: yaw damper inoperative, request extended final for a stable approach.

Approach gives you a longer vector to final and avoids late heading changes. You brief a stable manual approach and land without forcing tight S-turns on short final.

That is appropriate use of the radio after a yaw damper failure — operational need stated clearly, ATC helps with geometry.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger yaw damper failure, expect extended final, fly heading one four zero.
2. Radar: ANAC123, understand handling limitation, radar vectors for long final.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able to accept visual approach with normal maneuvering.
4. Control: ANAC123, roger continuing with yaw damper inoperative, advise if you require priority.
5. Approach: ANAC123, turn final now or request more track miles?

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Yaw damper failure is about smoothness and limitations — not drama.

Control the yaw. Read the AFM. Decide if the approach geometry needs to change.

If you can continue normally, you may only need a brief advisory. If turbulence and oscillation make a tight circling approach a bad idea, say what you need: extended final, vectors, or a more straightforward approach.

Do not give ATC a systems class on Dutch roll. Give them the operational request.

In H130 ops near Santos Dumont or Congonhas, a long final request is often the difference between a stable arrival and a high-workload mess. Ask early — before you are inside the turn to final.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your yaw damper fails in turbulence during arrival. Handling is manageable but you want to avoid tight maneuvering on short final. Describe what you do and say.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would control the aircraft smoothly, apply AFM limitations for yaw damper inoperative, and stabilize the flight path. I would advise ATC that the yaw damper is inoperative and request an extended final or radar vectors for a stable, low-maneuvering approach. I would avoid aggressive pedal inputs and last-minute tight turns. If handling became unacceptable, I would request priority or divert as required. The radio call should state the limitation and the operational request, not a detailed technical explanation.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Ignoring mild yaw oscillation until short final becomes unstable.  
  ✔ Recognize early, apply AFM limits, and request approach geometry that helps.

- ❌ Giving a long technical explanation of Dutch roll on frequency.  
  ✔ State: yaw damper inoperative, request extended final.

- ❌ Accepting tight S-turns to final when handling is degraded.  
  ✔ Request more track miles; do not accept geometry you cannot fly smoothly.

- ❌ Fighting oscillations with large pedal inputs.  
  ✔ Use smooth, measured corrections per training and AFM guidance.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are being vectored for a short approach to Santos Dumont. The yaw damper fails and yaw oscillation increases in light turbulence.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit to request a more suitable approach geometry?

**Your second task**

Approach asks if you need priority. Handling is manageable with a long final. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, yaw damper inoperative, request extended final, minimal maneuvering. Second task: ANAC123, negative priority required at this time, extended final is sufficient — or the equivalent concise reply Approach expects. I would not accept a tight short approach that aggravates handling, and I would escalate to priority only if control or safety margins became unacceptable.
