# Flap Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0267  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Flap Failure — a malfunction in which wing flaps fail to extend, retract, or move symmetrically, forcing abnormal approach speeds, possible flaps-up or asymmetric procedures, and clear ATC reporting of the system limitation.

Flap failure is not only a cockpit caption. It changes drag, pitch attitude, approach speed, and landing distance. Asymmetric flaps can also create a roll moment that must be controlled before the radio call becomes the priority.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, the H130 does not have conventional wing flaps. Learn this as fixed-wing ICAO English for exams and multi-type careers. You may analogize "configuration drag change" to collective and attitude management — without inventing H130 flap hardware.

Clear Part 2 and Part 3 language — flap failure, asymmetric flaps, flaps-up landing — keeps ATC from guessing your performance.

## 📖 Meaning

Flap Failure is a malfunction in which the flap system does not operate normally — including failure to extend, failure to retract, or asymmetric flap position — requiring abnormal procedures, revised approach and landing performance, and ATC notification when configuration or handling is limited.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de flaps / Flap Failure.

Malfuncionamento em que o sistema de flaps não opera normalmente — incluindo falha de extensão, falha de recolhimento ou posição assimétrica — exigindo procedimentos anormais, desempenho revisado de aproximação e pouso, e notificação ao ATC quando a configuração ou o controle estiver limitado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When flaps fail, the crew should:

- identify the failure mode: jammed up, jammed down, or asymmetric
- apply the AFM abnormal checklist before changing configuration further
- compute approach speed and landing distance for the actual flap setting
- control any roll from asymmetric flaps before continuing the approach
- advise ATC of flap failure and state intentions: continue, divert, or flaps-up landing
- request longest runway, priority, or delay vectors as required

Flap failure scenarios commonly include:

- flaps stuck up on approach — flaps-up landing
- flaps stuck partially extended — intermediate configuration landing
- asymmetric flaps with roll control difficulty
- inability to retract flaps after takeoff — climb and fuel planning impact
- exam radio: name the failure and the operational effect

**Important:** Do not keep cycling the flap lever hoping for a miracle while the approach becomes unstable. Secure the configuration per checklist, fly the matching speeds, and tell ATC what you can and cannot do.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use flap failure reports to:

- understand that approach speed and landing distance may change
- offer the longest runway or delay vectors for checklist completion
- provide priority when handling or distance margins are reduced
- avoid assigning speed reductions the aircraft cannot meet in the failed configuration
- coordinate emergency services if the crew requests them

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in a fixed-wing training scenario inbound to Congonhas. You select flaps for approach. Nothing moves. The indication stays up.

You level the wings, run the checklist, and call:

ANAC123, flap failure, flaps remain up, request vectors to complete checklist, then longest runway for flaps-up landing.

Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading one eight zero, maintain three thousand, advise when ready for approach.

When ready:

ANAC123, ready for approach, flaps-up landing, request priority runway two seven Left.

You own the system failure. You own the radio. You do not surprise the tower on short final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Flap failure is a performance problem first and a radio problem second — but both must be clean.

Identify jammed up, jammed down, or asymmetric. Fly the checklist speeds. Do not invent a hybrid configuration because it "looks closer to normal."

On frequency, flap failure plus the effect — flaps remain up, asymmetric flaps, requesting delay vectors — beats a vague "flap problem."

In the H130 you manage lift and drag with collective, cyclic, and airspeed, not flap settings. Still learn the exam English. Controllers and examiners expect the standard terms.

If asymmetric, control the aircraft before you craft the perfect sentence. Wings level. Then talk.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Flaps fail to extend on base leg. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control, apply the AFM flap failure checklist, and decide whether to continue with a flaps-up or partial-flap landing. I would advise ATC of the flap failure, request delay vectors if I need time for the checklist, then request the longest suitable runway and priority if required for the abnormal landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "flap no work" or mixing Portuguese on the frequency.  
  ✔ Use flap failure and state the configuration effect clearly.

- ❌ Continuing to short final while still troubleshooting the lever.  
  ✔ Request delay vectors; complete the checklist before committing.

- ❌ Not stating flaps-up or asymmetric when that is the case.  
  ✔ Name the failure mode so ATC understands speed and distance.

- ❌ Accepting a speed assignment incompatible with the failed setting.  
  ✔ Advise unable assigned speed if configuration limits performance.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On base, flaps will not extend. You need two minutes for the checklist. A short runway is active.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit first?

**Your second task**

Checklist complete; flaps remain up. What do you say next?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, flap failure, request delay vectors to complete checklist. Second task: ANAC123, ready for approach, flaps-up landing, request longest runway and priority. I would not rush an incomplete checklist onto a short runway.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Flap failure changes how the airplane flies. Your English must change with it.

Name the failure. Name the configuration. Ask for time, runway, or priority.

In helicopter academy life you may never move flaps — in ICAO English you must still own the concept.

Control first. Checklist second. Clear radio third. That order keeps flap failures from becoming unstable approaches.
