# Battery Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0058  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers battery failure — when your last electrical reserve is gone.

Battery Failure means the aircraft battery can no longer supply adequate electrical power — radios, instruments, and essential avionics may fail.

In the H130, the battery is the backup when the generator or alternator fails.

If the battery also fails, you may lose communication capability entirely.

You must aviate first, use remaining power wisely, then communicate before you go silent.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Battery Failure is the inability of the aircraft battery to supply adequate electrical power, threatening loss of communication and essential avionics.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Incapacidade da bateria de fornecer energia elétrica adequada, ameaçando perda de comunicação e aviônicos essenciais.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Battery Failure occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- use remaining electrical power for essential communication only
- declare Mayday if safe flight or communication is threatened
- squawk 7700 if transponder remains powered
- proceed to the nearest suitable airport immediately

Battery Failure reporting commonly includes:

- battery failure or battery critically low
- possible loss of radio contact
- request vectors to nearest suitable airport
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested

**Important:** Battery failure after generator or alternator failure is a distress situation — Mayday. Communicate while you still can. Aviate first — then speak clearly with standard phraseology.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Battery Failure declarations to:

- clear all traffic from the aircraft's route immediately
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable airport
- prepare for possible loss of radio contact
- coordinate emergency services on the ground
- monitor transponder if still operational

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are ten miles south of Congonhas at one thousand two hundred feet in your H130.

The generator failed twenty minutes ago. Now battery voltage collapses and warning lights flash.

You have seconds of radio power remaining.

You transmit immediately:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, São Paulo Approach, ANAC123, battery failure, generator previously failed, request immediate vectors to Congonhas.

Approach clears traffic and vectors you direct.

You land at Congonhas as the last radio transmission fades.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, Mayday acknowledged, turn left heading three six zero, Congonhas five miles, descend at pilot's discretion.
2. Approach: ANAC123, all traffic cleared, say souls on board.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway one seven cleared to land, emergency services standing by.
4. Tower: ANAC123, wind three five zero at zero eight knots, report final if able.
5. Approach: ANAC123, radar contact, if you read, squawk ident.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Battery failure is the end of your electrical runway.

If generator or alternator already failed, this is Mayday — not Pan Pan.

The sequence every professional pilot learns:

Aviate. Transmit. Proceed.

Fly the aircraft first.

Use every second of remaining radio power.

Declare Mayday immediately.

State souls on board.

Request immediate landing.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "bateria acabou" or "morreu a bateria" on frequency.

Use battery failure or battery critically low — ICAO standard terms.

If you lose radio before landing, squawk 7700 if the transponder still works.

ATC will see you and clear traffic.

Train the reflex to call Mayday before silence — not after.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do if your battery failed after a generator failure in flight?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain aircraft control, declare Mayday Mayday Mayday immediately, state battery failure and that generator previously failed, report souls on board, and request immediate vectors to the nearest suitable airport. I would squawk seven seven zero zero if possible. I would use remaining radio power to confirm intentions before possible loss of communication.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating battery failure as minor urgency after generator failure.  
  ✔ Battery failure after generator loss is distress — Mayday.

- ❌ Saying "bateria fraca" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use battery failure or battery critically low.

- ❌ Waiting to call ATC until the radio dies.  
  ✔ Transmit Mayday while communication still works.

- ❌ Forgetting to squawk 7700 when radio may fail.  
  ✔ Squawk seven seven zero zero — ATC tracks you without voice.

**Your first task**

**Your second task**

You fear losing radio contact before landing. What additional action do you take?
