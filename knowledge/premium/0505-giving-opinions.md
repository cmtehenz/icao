# Giving Opinions

**Catalog ID:** 0505  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Giving Opinions — the ICAO oral exam task in which you state a view on an aviation topic, support it with reasons, and discuss alternatives at Level 4 or above.

Giving opinions is not arguing. It is reasoned professional judgment in English.

In Part 1, the examiner may ask whether you prefer IFR or VFR for a mission, whether automation helps safety, or what you think about a procedure. You must take a position, justify it, and remain open to a follow-up challenge.

For H130 ICAO Delta candidates, opinion tasks test flexible vocabulary and discourse markers — the language of professional discussion beyond radio phraseology.

Understanding how to give opinions in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Giving Opinions is the ICAO speaking task type in which the candidate expresses a professional viewpoint on an aviation subject — stating a position, supporting it with reasons or examples, and responding to counterpoints — so that discourse management and vocabulary range can be assessed at Operational Level 4 or higher.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When giving an opinion, the candidate should:

- state the opinion clearly in the first sentence
- give at least one reason and one concrete example
- use opinion markers: I think, in my view, from my experience
- acknowledge another side briefly when appropriate
- avoid absolute claims you cannot defend
- for H130: draw on helicopter operations when the topic fits
- invite or accept examiner follow-up without changing topic randomly

Useful opinion structure:

- position: "In my opinion, ..."
- reason: "because ..."
- example: "for example, on an H130 flight to a confined area ..."
- balance: "however, ..."
- close: short restatement of the view

**Important:** Level 4 does not require a perfect debate. It requires a clear view, intelligible reasons, and the ability to continue when the examiner disagrees or asks "why?"

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Opinion tasks help examiners to:

- move beyond description into evaluative language
- test connectors and discourse markers
- assess whether candidates can justify decisions in English
- simulate professional briefing-room discussion
- probe depth after a short initial answer

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

The examiner asks: Do you think automation makes helicopter flying safer?

You answer: In my opinion, automation can improve safety when crews understand its limits. For example, autopilot reduces workload in IFR cruise, but in the H130 near a confined landing site the pilot must stay fully engaged visually. However, over-reliance on automation can reduce manual flying skill. So I support automation as a tool, not as a replacement for airmanship.

The examiner challenges you. You hold the position with one more example — and stop. Opinion delivered.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Examiner: ANAC123, in your opinion, should the flight continue or divert?
2. Approach: ANAC123, advise your preference — hold or proceed to alternate.
3. Radar: ANAC123, say intentions, weather ahead is deteriorating.
4. Examiner: ANAC123, do you agree that visual approaches increase risk at night?
5. Tower: ANAC123, confirm you prefer runway one four due wind.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Opinions need a spine: position, reason, example.

Without a reason, an opinion is just a preference. Examiners push "why?" until you provide one. Give it early.

Use soft markers when you are not certain: "I believe," "from my experience." Use stronger markers for safety positions you would actually fly: "I would not continue because..."

Do not fight the examiner. If challenged, acknowledge and restate with a clearer reason. Interaction skill and opinion skill travel together.

In H130 operations you give opinions in briefings every day — go/no-go, landing site suitability, fuel. Translate that briefing voice into exam English.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The examiner asks: "Is it better to divert early or wait and see when destination weather is marginal?" Give a Level 4 opinion.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

In my opinion, it is better to divert early when weather is clearly below minima or trending down and fuel for delay is limited. Waiting can consume reserves and increase pressure. For example, on an H130 IFR flight I would review fuel, alternate, and passenger needs, then decide with the crew. However, if weather is fluctuating around minima and fuel allows a short hold, a brief wait may be reasonable. My priority is landing with safe margins, not forcing the destination.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Stating "I think yes" with no reason.  
  ✔ Always add because + example.

- ❌ Changing opinion every time the examiner challenges.  
  ✔ Acknowledge the point, then restate your justified view.

- ❌ Using only absolute words: always, never, impossible.  
  ✔ Prefer usually, in most cases, depending on conditions.

- ❌ Giving a long lecture with no clear position.  
  ✔ Open with the opinion, then support it.

- Opinion → uh-PIN-yun

Practice:

In my opinion... it is safer to divert.

Together:

In my opinion it is safer to divert because fuel margins are limited.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**OPINION** — **O**pen with your position, **P**rove it with a reason, **I**llustrate with one example, **N**ote the other side briefly, **I**nvite follow-up by stopping cleanly, **O**mit absolute claims, **N**ever abandon safety logic under challenge.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

The examiner asks whether single-pilot IFR in helicopters is acceptable in busy terminal airspace.

**Your first task**

Give a clear Level 4 opinion with one reason and one example.

**Your second task**

The examiner disagrees. Respond without collapsing your position. If ATC asked your intention in related weather, what call might you make?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: In my opinion, single-pilot IFR in busy terminal airspace requires strong proficiency and careful workload management because radio, navigation, and monitoring compete for attention — for example, an H130 arrival into Congonhas in IMC. Second task: I understand the concern about workload; I still believe it is acceptable only with preparation and conservative minima. If diverting: ANAC123, requesting diversion due weather and workload management. Hold the reasoned view; do not invent a new speech.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Giving opinions is professional judgment in English.

Position. Reason. Example. Brief balance. Stop.

You already decide go/no-go in the H130. Say those decisions out loud with structure in the exam.

That is how opinion tasks show Level 4 — not by winning an argument, but by justifying a clear view.
