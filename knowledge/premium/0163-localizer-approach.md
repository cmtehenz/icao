# Localizer Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0163  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Localizer Approach — an instrument approach that uses only the localizer signal for lateral guidance, without the ILS glideslope for vertical guidance.

A Localizer approach is not the same as an ILS approach.

An ILS approach uses both the localizer for lateral guidance and the glideslope for vertical guidance — it is a precision approach with a Decision Altitude.

A Localizer approach uses only the localizer. There is no glideslope signal available or usable. It is a non-precision approach with a Minimum Descent Altitude — flown with step-down fixes or CDFA technique.

At Brazilian airports including Congonhas, Curitiba, and Florianópolis, localizer-only approaches are published as backups when the glideslope is unserviceable, and as the primary approach at some runways.

## 📖 Meaning

A Localizer Approach is an instrument approach procedure that uses only the localizer component of the ILS system for lateral course guidance, without glideslope vertical guidance, published with a Minimum Descent Altitude and flown as a non-precision approach.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aproximação por localizador.

Procedimento de aproximação por instrumentos que utiliza apenas o componente localizador do sistema ILS para orientação lateral de curso, sem a orientação vertical do glideslope, publicada com uma altitude mínima de descida e executada como uma aproximação de não precisão.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives a localizer approach clearance, they should:

- verify the glideslope is not to be used — fly LOC minima, not ILS minima
- tune and identify the localizer frequency and confirm the glideslope flag is showing unreliable or inactive
- review the LOC approach plate: step-down fixes, MDA, visibility minima
- brief the approach as a non-precision approach — MDA higher than ILS DA
- fly lateral guidance from the localizer precisely
- use step-down fixes for vertical guidance or fly CDFA technique for a stabilized profile
- at MDA, maintain MDA until in position to make a normal descent to the runway

Localizer approach differences from ILS:

- no glideslope — vertical guidance is step-down fixes or CDFA
- higher minima: typically two to three hundred feet higher MDA than ILS DA
- non-precision categorization — no decision altitude, uses minimum descent altitude
- missed approach point based on timing or DME, not altitude crossing

**Important:** If the glideslope needle is visible and moving during a LOC approach, do not use it for guidance — it may be unreliable. Following an unreliable glideslope during a localizer-only approach can lead to a descent below safe terrain clearance altitudes.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers clear localizer approaches when:

- the glideslope component of the ILS is unserviceable due to maintenance or equipment failure
- the airport publishes a LOC approach as the primary non-precision procedure
- weather minima are above LOC minima but below ILS minima, making the LOC approach viable
- an ILS approach becomes a LOC approach mid-sequence due to glideslope failure in flight

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on a vectored ILS approach to Congonhas runway one seven in your H130.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, glideslope unreliable, cleared localizer approach runway one seven.

You respond:

Cleared localizer approach runway one seven, ANAC123.

You pull up the LOC runway one seven plate. MDA is eight hundred and fifty feet. Visibility minima: one thousand two hundred meters.

You fly lateral guidance from the localizer — your cross-track deviation needle is your primary guidance.

At the published step-down fix, you check your altitude against the published minimum at that fix.

Passing the final approach fix, you descend to MDA: eight hundred and fifty feet.

At MDA, runway lights are in sight. You commence your normal descent.

Cleared to land runway one seven, Tower confirms.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

A localizer approach is disciplined non-precision flying.

Without glideslope, your vertical profile is your responsibility.

The tools you have: step-down fixes, DME, altimetry, and the CDFA technique.

Use them. Cross-check them. Do not improvise a descent profile.

The most dangerous technique on a non-precision approach: the dive-and-drive.

Descend steeply to MDA, then fly level at MDA hoping to see the runway.

The safer technique is CDFA — Continuous Descent Final Approach. Calculate the required descent rate from the final approach fix altitude to the runway threshold elevation, and fly a continuous stabilized descent. At MDA, if the runway is not in sight, execute the missed approach.

In the H130, a localizer approach on a dark night into Congonhas requires complete discipline. MDA is your floor. Do not go below it without the runway environment in sight.

Know your localizer: the LOC course is typically four degrees wide at the threshold. It narrows with distance from the antenna. In close to the threshold, a full-scale deflection represents very little actual displacement.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the difference between an ILS approach and a localizer approach, and what does a glideslope-unreliable notification mean for your approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

An ILS approach uses both the localizer for lateral guidance and the glideslope for vertical guidance. It is a precision approach with a decision altitude, and the crew follows the glideslope needle to a precisely defined descent profile. A localizer approach uses only the localizer. Without glideslope guidance, the crew uses published step-down fixes or the CDFA technique to manage vertical profile. The approach is non-precision, with a minimum descent altitude that is typically higher than the ILS decision altitude. When ATC advises glideslope unreliable, the crew should confirm they are flying localizer-only minima, disregard any glideslope needle indication even if it appears to move, and apply non-precision approach technique to the approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Flying ILS minima when cleared for a localizer approach.  
  ✔ LOC minima apply — not ILS minima. LOC MDA is higher. Do not descend to the ILS DA.

- ❌ Following the glideslope needle during a localizer-only approach.  
  ✔ The glideslope may appear to move during a LOC approach but is unreliable. Disregard it.

- ❌ Using dive-and-drive technique rather than CDFA on a localizer approach.  
  ✔ CDFA — Continuous Descent Final Approach — produces a more stable, safer descent profile.

- ❌ Not identifying the localizer and confirming glideslope is inactive before beginning the approach.  
  ✔ Always verify the localizer identification and confirm glideslope status before commencing.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Congonhas Approach transmits: "ANAC123, glideslope unserviceable, cleared localizer approach runway one seven."

**Your first task**

Read back the clearance and describe how your approach technique changes from an ILS to a localizer-only approach.

**Your second task**

Established on the localizer, the glideslope needle begins to move as if it is active. Your co-pilot suggests following it. What do you say and do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Cleared localizer approach runway one seven, ANAC123. I would change my approach technique: pull up the LOC runway one seven plate, note the MDA and step-down fixes, and use CDFA technique or step-down altitudes for vertical profile — not a decision altitude. I would set the MDA on the altitude alerter. Regarding the glideslope needle: I would advise my co-pilot that the glideslope is declared unserviceable and the needle is unreliable. Following an unreliable glideslope on a localizer approach could lead below safe terrain clearance. I would disregard the needle and fly the published non-precision profile.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

A localizer approach is a test of your non-precision instrument discipline.

Lateral guidance is precise — the localizer tells you exactly where the runway centerline is.

Vertical guidance is yours to manage — step-down fixes, timing, and CDFA technique.

Use MDA as your absolute floor. Never below it without the runway environment in sight.

If the glideslope needle appears during a LOC approach, ignore it. It is not trustworthy.

Trust the plate. Trust the step-down fixes. Trust your training.

That is professional non-precision instrument flying.
