# Procedure Turn

**Catalog ID:** 0160  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Procedure Turn — a course reversal maneuver used to align an aircraft with an instrument approach course when approaching from an unfavorable direction.

A Procedure Turn is a defined, published navigation maneuver — not just any turn.

It appears on approach plates as a specific pattern flown at a defined altitude and within a protected area, designed to reverse course and intercept the inbound approach track.

At Brazilian airports including Navegantes, Joinville, Florianópolis, and smaller regional airports, procedure turns are found on non-precision and ILS approaches when radar vectoring is not available.

Knowing when a procedure turn is required, when it is prohibited, and what "No PT" means is fundamental ICAO navigation knowledge.

## 📖 Meaning

A Procedure Turn is a published instrument flight procedure maneuver in which a course reversal is made to align the aircraft with an instrument approach from the correct direction, flown at a specified altitude and within the protected airspace shown on the approach chart.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Manobra de procedimento de voo por instrumentos publicada na qual uma inversão de rumo é realizada para alinhar a aeronave com uma aproximação por instrumentos na direção correta, executada em uma altitude especificada e dentro do espaço aéreo protegido indicado na carta de aproximação.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a procedure turn is required, the crew should:

- confirm the procedure turn is depicted on the approach chart and not marked "No PT"
- fly the outbound course from the procedure turn fix to the depicted reversal
- execute the turn pattern at or above the published procedure turn altitude
- remain within the protected area shown on the chart
- intercept the inbound approach course and track it inbound to the final approach fix
- not exceed the published procedure turn speed limitation (typically two hundred and fifty knots indicated airspeed)

Procedure turns are not required when:

- the approach chart is marked "No PT" (No Procedure Turn)
- ATC issues radar vectors to final
- the crew is cleared for a straight-in approach
- the crew is conducting a timed approach from a holding fix when directed

**Important:** Do not attempt a procedure turn when the chart shows "No PT." That notation means the procedure turn area has not been assessed for conflicting traffic or terrain, and the procedure turn is not authorized. If you arrive from a direction that would normally require a procedure turn on a "No PT" approach, advise ATC and request vectors or an alternative procedure.

## 🎯 Why Navigation Uses It

Procedure turns are published to:

- allow aircraft to reverse course and align with the final approach track from unfavorable arrival directions
- provide a safe, protected course reversal within defined airspace
- enable instrument approaches at airports without radar coverage
- standardize the course reversal maneuver across all aircraft types
- protect against conflicting traffic during the reversal

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Joinville in your H130 for the VOR/DME runway two seven approach.

The chart shows a procedure turn is required for aircraft arriving from the northeast sector.

You are arriving from the northeast — from the direction of Florianópolis.

ATC transmits:

ANAC123, cleared VOR/DME approach runway two seven, report procedure turn established.

You fly outbound on the published procedure turn course — runway heading two seven, then turn left to the depicted outbound heading.

You hold the procedure turn altitude: three thousand feet.

You fly the outbound leg, timing your turn at the appropriate distance.

You turn inbound, intercept the approach course, and track inbound to the final approach fix.

You report:

ANAC123, procedure turn established, inbound VOR runway two seven.

ATC acknowledges.

The procedure turn aligned you correctly for a stabilized approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The procedure turn exists to solve a navigation problem: you are approaching from the wrong direction for the final approach course.

Instead of making a random turn, the procedure turn gives you a precise, published solution.

Fly the exact depicted pattern. Maintain the procedure turn altitude until established inbound. Stay within the protected area.

The most common procedural error: flying a procedure turn when the chart says "No PT."

"No PT" means: this approach was designed for radar vectors or straight-in arrivals. If you execute an unauthorized procedure turn, you may enter airspace with conflicting traffic or terrain not assessed for the reversal.

If ATC gives you radar vectors to final, there is no procedure turn. You are vectored to the intercept.

If you receive a clearance for straight-in approach, there is no procedure turn.

In both cases, acknowledge and fly straight to the approach course intercept.

In the H130 operating into smaller Brazilian airports without radar coverage, procedure turns are the normal method of course reversal. Know them. Brief them. Fly them precisely.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When is a procedure turn required and when is it not required for an instrument approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A procedure turn is required when the approach chart depicts it and the aircraft is arriving from a direction that does not allow a straight-in alignment with the final approach course, and ATC has not issued radar vectors to final or a straight-in clearance. A procedure turn is not required when the chart is marked "No PT," when ATC provides radar vectors to the final approach course, when the crew receives a straight-in approach clearance, or when conducting a timed approach from a holding fix as directed by ATC. The published altitude must be maintained throughout the procedure turn, and the turn must be completed within the protected area shown on the chart.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Flying a procedure turn when the approach chart shows "No PT."  
  ✔ "No PT" is a prohibition. Never execute a procedure turn on a "No PT" approach.

- ❌ Not maintaining the published procedure turn altitude throughout the maneuver.  
  ✔ The procedure turn altitude protects you from terrain and conflicting traffic. Hold it precisely.

- ❌ Exceeding the protected area by flying the outbound leg too long.  
  ✔ Time the outbound leg according to the published timing or distance limit on the chart.

- ❌ Not reporting procedure turn established when required by ATC.  
  ✔ When ATC asks for a report, transmit: ANAC123, procedure turn established, inbound.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Joinville Approach transmits: "ANAC123, cleared VOR/DME approach runway two seven. Procedure turn authorized. Report established inbound."

You are arriving from the northeast. The chart shows a procedure turn is published with a protected altitude of three thousand feet.

**Your first task**

Read back the clearance and describe your procedure turn maneuver.

**Your second task**

On the outbound leg you realize you flew past the published distance limit for the procedure turn. You are now outside the protected area. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Cleared VOR/DME approach runway two seven, report established inbound, ANAC123. I would fly the published procedure turn: track outbound on the depicted heading, maintain three thousand feet, execute the published turn pattern within the protected area, and intercept the inbound course. If I recognized that I had flown beyond the protected area on the outbound leg, I would immediately advise ATC: ANAC123, procedure turn extended beyond protected area, request clearance to return or vectors. I must not continue an approach if I have departed the protected area — I am no longer on a defined, safe procedure.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

The procedure turn is precision flying in a defined space.

It exists because the approach was designed for aircraft arriving from a specific direction. It solves the course reversal problem safely and predictably.

Know when it is required. Know when it is prohibited.

Fly the published altitude. Stay in the protected area. Report established when asked.

At airports without radar in Brazil, the procedure turn may be your only way to align for the approach. Know it cold.
