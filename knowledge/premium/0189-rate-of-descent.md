# Rate of Descent

**Catalog ID:** 0189  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Rate of Descent — the vertical speed at which an aircraft loses altitude, typically expressed in feet per minute, and the ATC and operational language used to assign, report, or adjust that rate.

Rate of descent is not only an instrument reading. It is a control tool for meeting altitude constraints, sequencing traffic, and maintaining a stable path into the terminal area. Too steep creates passenger discomfort, high workload, and approach instability. Too shallow leaves you high and late.

For H130 helicopter operations, rate of descent is highly adjustable. That flexibility is an advantage in Brazilian IFR and VFR training routes into Congonhas, Santos Dumont, Curitiba, and Florianópolis — but only when the crew communicates capability and compliance clearly with ATC.

Understanding how to acknowledge expedite descent, report actual rate when asked, and advise unable when performance or comfort limits apply is essential Part 2 phraseology.

## 📖 Meaning

Rate of Descent is the vertical speed of an aircraft during descent, measured in feet per minute, representing how quickly altitude is being lost, and used by crews and ATC to plan profile compliance, traffic separation, and expedited or reduced descent as required by the traffic situation.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Taxa de Descida / Rate of Descent.

Velocidade vertical de uma aeronave durante a descida, medida em pés por minuto, representando a rapidez com que a altitude está sendo perdida, e usada por tripulações e ATC para planejar o cumprimento do perfil, a separação de tráfego e a descida acelerada ou reduzida conforme a situação de tráfego.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing rate of descent, the crew should:

- set and monitor vertical speed appropriate to the altitude to lose and distance available
- comply with ATC requests to expedite or reduce rate of descent when able
- advise ATC if unable to meet a requested rate due to aircraft limits, icing, or passenger considerations
- report actual rate of descent when ATC asks
- plan level-off early enough to avoid busting the assigned altitude
- adjust rate to meet STAR or approach crossing restrictions

Rate of descent operations commonly include:

- standard cruise descent planning (often linked to the three-to-one rule)
- ATC instruction to expedite descent through a flight level
- request to reduce rate for traffic below
- pilot report of current feet per minute when queried
- helicopter operational descents using higher rates than typical fixed-wing traffic

**Important:** In the H130, high rates of descent are available, but rotor RPM, airspeed limits, and passenger comfort still apply. If ATC says expedite descent, confirm you can comply and level off smoothly at the assigned altitude. Never chase an extreme rate that compromises control or altitude capture.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with rate of descent to:

- create vertical separation between climbing and descending traffic
- expedite an aircraft through an occupied altitude band
- slow a descent when traffic is below or a restriction is ahead
- sequence arrivals onto a stable profile for approach
- verify that an aircraft can meet a crossing restriction in time

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are descending into Florianópolis from flight level nine zero. Approach needs you through six thousand quickly for traffic above, then wants a reduced rate below four thousand for spacing.

Approach: ANAC123, expedite descent through six thousand.

You increase rate of descent, report leaving, and pass six thousand cleanly.

Later: ANAC123, reduce rate of descent, traffic below.

You shallow the descent, capture the next altitude smoothly, and continue the approach without a level bust.

That is rate-of-descent discipline — flexible in the H130, always communicated, always capped by a clean level-off.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, expedite descent through six thousand.
2. Radar: ANAC123, reduce rate of descent, traffic one thousand feet below.
3. Control: ANAC123, say rate of descent.
4. Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain three thousand, expedite until reaching.
5. Radar: ANAC123, able to increase rate of descent? Need you at four thousand in two minutes.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, expediting descent through six thousand.
2. ANAC123, reducing rate of descent, traffic below.
3. ANAC123, rate of descent one thousand five hundred feet per minute.
4. ANAC123, descending three thousand, expediting until reaching.
5. ANAC123, affirmative, able to increase rate of descent, will be at four thousand in two minutes.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Rate of descent is how you spend altitude against time and distance.

ATC uses it as a separation tool. You use it as a profile tool. When those needs meet, the radio call is short: expedite, reduce, or say your rate.

In the H130, you can often say affirmative to an expedite request that a jet might struggle with. That is useful around Congonhas and Guarulhos. Still protect the level-off. A fast descent that overshoots the altitude creates a new problem.

If you are asked for rate of descent, give a clear number in feet per minute. Do not answer with "normal" or "steep" when ATC wants a figure.

If you cannot comply — turbulence, icing, aircraft limitation — say unable and offer what you can do. Silence followed by a missed restriction is worse than an early unable.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC asks you to expedite descent through a flight level, then later asks you to reduce rate because of traffic below. Describe how you manage this.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would increase my rate of descent to pass through the requested flight level promptly, monitoring airspeed and rotor limits in the H130. After clearing that level, if ATC asks me to reduce rate due to traffic below, I would shallow the descent immediately and prepare for a smooth capture of the next assigned altitude. If I were unable to meet either request due to aircraft or weather limits, I would advise unable and state the rate I can maintain. The priority is compliance when able, clear communication when not, and no altitude bust at level-off.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "expediting" but not actually increasing vertical speed.  
  ✔ Change the rate promptly and monitor until through the requested level.

- ❌ Reporting rate of descent vaguely ("more or less one thousand").  
  ✔ Give a clear figure: one thousand five hundred feet per minute.

- ❌ Expediting so aggressively that you bust the level-off altitude.  
  ✔ Plan the capture early; expedite through, then reduce to level off cleanly.

- ❌ Saying nothing when unable to meet an expedite request.  
  ✔ Advise unable and state the maximum rate you can provide.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Expediting Descent Through Six Thousand

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, EK-spuh-dy-ting deh-SENT throo SIX THOW-zund

**Word Stress**

- Expediting → EK-spuh-dy-ting (stress on first)
- Descent → deh-SENT (stress on second)
- Through → throo
- Thousand → THOW-zund

Practice:

Expediting descent... through six thousand... reducing rate now.

Together:

ANAC123, expediting descent through six thousand, will reduce rate below.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**RATE** — **R**ead the altitude to lose and distance left, **A**djust feet per minute to the profile, **T**ell ATC your actual rate when asked, **E**xpedite or reduce on request — but never bust the level-off.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Rate of descent is vertical speed with a purpose.

Use it to meet the profile. Change it when ATC needs separation. Report it when asked.

In the H130 you have a wide usable range — that is an operational strength in Brazilian terminal airspace. Use it professionally: expedite when able, reduce when required, and always capture the assigned altitude cleanly.

Unable is an acceptable word. An altitude bust is not.

Know your rate. Fly your rate. Say your rate.

That is how descent stays a controlled maneuver — not a chase.
