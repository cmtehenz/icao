# Emergency Descent

**Catalog ID:** 0249  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Emergency Descent — a rapid, controlled descent to a safe altitude, most commonly after cabin pressurization failure or rapid decompression on pressurized aircraft, and also used when fire, smoke, or other critical failures require getting the aircraft down quickly. For H130 operations, full airline-style emergency descents from high flight levels are less common, but the ICAO English, ATC coordination, and decision logic remain essential exam and professional skills — and rapid descents for fire or systems failures still apply.

Emergency descent is a Critical Part 2 and Part 3 topic. Controllers must clear traffic below you. Your English must be immediate, precise, and continuously updated.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master Mayday emergency descent phraseology, then transfer the same clarity to any rapid descent required in helicopter operations.

Understanding emergency descent in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Emergency Descent is a rapid descent initiated by the flight crew to reach a safe altitude as quickly as practicable, typically due to loss of cabin pressurization, rapid decompression, fire, or another critical condition, requiring immediate ATC notification, traffic clearance below, and priority handling to a suitable landing airport when required.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Descida de emergência / Emergency Descent.

Descida rápida iniciada pela tripulação de voo para alcançar uma altitude segura o mais rápido possível, tipicamente devido à perda de pressurização da cabine, descompressão rápida, fogo ou outra condição crítica, exigindo notificação imediata ao ATC, liberação de tráfego abaixo e tratamento prioritário para um aeródromo adequado quando necessário.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an emergency descent is required, the crew should:

- don oxygen as required and establish crew communication
- initiate the emergency descent per QRH/AFM — thrust, speed brakes, attitude, target altitude
- declare Mayday and state commencing emergency descent
- squawk 7700 if required and able
- request ATC to clear traffic below and provide priority vectors
- for H130: apply rapid descent discipline for fire/smoke/systems failures with the same radio clarity

Emergency descent reporting commonly includes:

- commencing emergency descent
- reason: decompression / cabin altitude / fire / other
- leaving present flight level, target altitude
- request priority / diversion
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** Do not begin a blind emergency descent into unknown traffic without declaring. Transmit early so ATC can protect the airspace below. Fly the aircraft first, but do not stay silent.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to emergency descent calls to:

- immediately clear altitude blocks below the aircraft
- stop conflicting climb and cruise traffic
- provide the shortest safe routing to a suitable airport
- coordinate between sectors during a rapidly changing altitude profile
- alert emergency services for arrival

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: rapid decompression at flight level three five zero. Masks deploy. You begin emergency descent.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, rapid decompression, commencing emergency descent, request clear traffic below, diverting to nearest suitable airport.

Control clears the block and issues:

ANAC123, roger Mayday, descend unrestricted, traffic cleared, report passing flight level two zero zero.

You update levels as you descend and transition to approach for priority landing.

For H130 reality, a rapid descent for cabin smoke or engine fire uses the same declaration and traffic-protection logic.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday emergency descent, descend unrestricted, traffic cleared below.
2. Control: ANAC123, report leaving each ten thousand feet, say intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, priority handling approved, expect runway one five, emergency services alerted.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Control: ANAC123, stop descent at flight level one zero zero if able, terrain considerations, advise.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, commencing emergency descent, request traffic cleared below.
2. ANAC123, descending unrestricted, leaving flight level three five zero, will report passing two zero zero.
3. ANAC123, priority runway one five, continuing emergency descent.
4. ANAC123, souls one seven two, fuel two hours five minutes.
5. ANAC123, leveling flight level one zero zero, cabin altitude under control, request vectors for landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Emergency descent is where altitude becomes time.

Every second at an unsafe cabin altitude costs cognitive performance. Every second of radio silence costs ATC the chance to move traffic out of your way.

Fly the profile. Declare Mayday. Say commencing emergency descent. Ask for traffic cleared below. Then update.

Do not invent poetry. Do not explain the whole system failure on the first call. Nature, action, request.

In the H130 you may never leave flight level three five zero in airline fashion — but you will still need rapid descent English for fire and critical failures. Examiners know this. Show both the exam scenario and the transferable logic.

Smooth is fast. Clear is safe. Silent is dangerous.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You experience a rapid decompression in cruise. Describe your actions and what you say to ATC.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would don the oxygen mask, establish crew communication, and commence an emergency descent according to procedures. I would declare Mayday, state rapid decompression, report that I am commencing emergency descent, and request that traffic below be cleared and priority vectors to the nearest suitable airport. I would update ATC while descending, provide souls and fuel when asked, and advise when leveling at a safe altitude. In helicopter operations, I would use the same clear structure for any rapid descent required by fire or critical failure.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "I am going down fast" instead of "commencing emergency descent."  
  ✔ Use ICAO-standard "commencing emergency descent."

- ❌ Descending without declaring, hoping ATC will notice the altitude change.  
  ✔ Declare Mayday immediately so traffic below can be cleared.

- ❌ Giving a long technical explanation on the first call.  
  ✔ Nature, action, request — then details if asked.

- ❌ Treating emergency descent as fixed-wing only.  
  ✔ Keep exam scenario + transfer the radio logic to H130 rapid descents.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Commencing Emergency Descent, Request Traffic Cleared Below

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, kuh-MEN-sing ih-MUR-jen-see dee-SENT, reh-KWEST TRAF-ik KLEERD bih-LOH

**Word Stress**

- Commencing → kuh-MEN-sing (stress on MEN)
- Emergency → ih-MUR-jen-see
- Descent → deh-SENT
- Cleared → KLEERD

Practice:

Commencing emergency descent... request traffic cleared below.

Together:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, commencing emergency descent, request traffic cleared below.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Rapid decompression at high altitude. You start the emergency descent.

**Your first task**

What is your initial Mayday call?

**Your second task**

ATC says traffic is cleared and asks you to report passing flight level two zero zero. What do you reply, and what do you say when passing that level?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, rapid decompression, commencing emergency descent, request traffic cleared below and priority to nearest suitable airport. Second task: ANAC123, wilco, will report passing flight level two zero zero; then: ANAC123, passing flight level two zero zero, continuing emergency descent. I would keep updates short until leveling and approaching to land.
