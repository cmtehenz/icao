# Deck Landing

**Catalog ID:** 0408  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Deck Landing — helicopter landing on a ship or vessel deck, where motion, wind over deck, limited space, and obstacle clearance define the approach.

Deck landing is core H130 maritime work in Brazilian waters: support vessels, navy cooperation, pilot boats, and offshore logistics out of Santos, Rio, and Florianópolis.

You must communicate deck status, wave/pitch/roll when known, go-around intentions, and landing reports in ICAO Parts 2, 3, and 4 English.

## 📖 Meaning

Deck Landing is a helicopter landing conducted on a ship's or vessel's deck, requiring assessment of deck motion, relative wind, obstacles, and clear communication with the ship and ATC or maritime control regarding clearance to land and go-around options.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pouso em convés / deck landing.

Pouso de helicóptero realizado no convés de um navio ou embarcação, exigindo avaliação do movimento do convés, vento relativo, obstáculos e comunicação clara com o navio e o ATC ou controle marítimo sobre autorização de pouso e opções de arremetida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When performing a Deck Landing, the pilot should:

- obtain deck clearance and relative wind information before final
- brief go-around path clear of masts, cranes, and exhaust
- report short final and landing or go-around promptly
- do not attempt landing if deck motion exceeds limits
- advise ATC or company frequency of on-deck and lifting times when required

Deck Landing reporting commonly includes:

- request deck landing, say deck status
- short final deck, request land
- going around, deck not stable
- on deck / lifting, request departure

**Important:** A moving deck is a moving target. Limits are limits. Go around early and say why — deck motion, wind, or obstacles.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers and maritime coordinators support Deck Landing by:

- providing traffic separation near coastal and harbor areas
- relaying vessel position and deck readiness when able
- coordinating helicopter transit between shore and ship
- handling go-arounds back into controlled airspace
- assisting emergencies during deck operations

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are approaching a support vessel twenty miles off Santos in your H130.

You transmit on company/ship and keep coastal control informed:

Santos Approach, ANAC123, ten miles east, descending for deck landing on support vessel, will report on deck.

Ship reports deck green, wind over deck three two zero at two zero.

On short final, roll increases beyond limits. You go around:

ANAC123, going around, deck not stable, remaining offshore, will re-attempt when ready.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger deck landing, report on deck, no conflicting traffic.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions if go-around from deck.
3. Control: ANAC123, radar contact, advise when lifting from vessel.
4. Approach: ANAC123, cleared coastal transit, report pad or field in sight.
5. Control: ANAC123, roger going around from deck, maintain one thousand, say when ready.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, will report on deck, descending for vessel.
2. ANAC123, going around, deck not stable, maintaining one thousand offshore.
3. ANAC123, on deck, will advise lifting.
4. ANAC123, lifting from vessel, request coastal transit Santos.
5. ANAC123, field in sight, descending for landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Deck landing English separates ship talk from ATC talk — but both need clarity.

To the ship: deck status, wind over deck, land or wave-off. To ATC: position, deck landing intention, on deck, lifting, go-around.

In the H130, you feel every pitch and roll. If the deck is dancing, do not "try one more foot lower." Go around. Say deck not stable.

Brazilian offshore swell can change between brief and short final. Recheck. The go-around path must clear the mast — brief it like a one-way street.

Part 4 discussion loves limits and wave-off criteria. Answer with motion, wind, and obstacles — then the radio call.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Deck motion exceeds limits on short final. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would initiate a go-around on the briefed path clear of masts and obstacles. I would transmit: ANAC123, going around, deck not stable, remaining offshore at one thousand, will re-attempt when deck is green. I would not force a landing outside limits. When ready, I would request another approach to the deck.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Calling only "going around" without "deck not stable."  
  ✔ State the reason so ship and ATC understand.

- ❌ Forgetting to report on deck / lifting to coastal ATC.  
  ✔ Keep the shore unit in the loop for traffic and SAR awareness.

- ❌ Accepting a land signal when your limits are exceeded.  
  ✔ Pilot authority: unable deck landing, going around.

- ❌ Briefing no go-around path because "the deck is large."  
  ✔ Always brief escape clear of structure.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Short final to a vessel deck off Santos. Roll exceeds limits.

**Your first task**

Radio call as you go around.

**Your second task**

Ship later reports deck green. You are ready for a second approach. What do you say to Approach?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around, deck not stable, maintaining one thousand offshore. Second task: ANAC123, request another approach for deck landing, vessel reports deck green, will report on deck.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Deck landing is a moving confined area with a mast tax.

Brief wind and motion. Brief the wave-off. Land only inside limits.

In the H130 on Brazilian vessels, early go-around is professionalism.

Call deck not stable when it is. Call on deck when you are. Call lifting when you leave. That keeps ship and shore aligned.
