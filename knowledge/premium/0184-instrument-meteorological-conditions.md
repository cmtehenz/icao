# Instrument Meteorological Conditions

**Catalog ID:** 0184  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Instrument Meteorological Conditions — IMC — the weather conditions in which visibility, cloud clearance, or ceiling fall below the minimums required for Visual Meteorological Conditions, requiring the pilot to rely entirely on instruments for safe flight.

IMC is not just low cloud. It is any condition below VMC minimums — reduced visibility in haze, fog, rain, or night conditions where visual reference is insufficient for the applicable airspace.

This lesson pairs directly with Lesson 0183 — Visual Meteorological Conditions — to complete your understanding of the VMC/IMC distinction. Together they define the weather boundary that separates two completely different operating environments.

For H130 operations, IMC encounters are a genuine operational risk in Brazil. Coastal fog at Florianópolis and Navegantes, convective cloud buildups in São Paulo, morning mist in Joinville valley — any of these can place a VFR-cleared crew into IMC without warning. Knowing how to react and what to say to ATC is not optional knowledge. It is survival knowledge.

## 📖 Meaning

Instrument Meteorological Conditions are meteorological conditions expressed in terms of visibility, distance from cloud, and ceiling below the minimums specified for Visual Meteorological Conditions, requiring flight under Instrument Flight Rules where the pilot relies on instruments for navigation, attitude control, and separation rather than external visual reference.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Condições meteorológicas por instrumentos (CMI).

Condições meteorológicas expressas em termos de visibilidade, distância de nuvens e teto abaixo dos mínimos especificados para as condições meteorológicas visuais, exigindo voo sob as regras de voo por instrumentos, onde o piloto depende de instrumentos para navegação, controle de atitude e separação em vez de referência visual externa.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When encountering IMC during VFR flight, the crew must:

- immediately declare to ATC: ANAC123, IMC encountered, request IFR clearance
- establish instrument scan and transition to instrument flight attitude
- maintain current altitude while requesting clearance to avoid terrain and traffic conflicts
- comply with ATC IFR clearance immediately upon receipt
- do not attempt to maintain visual reference once instruments are required
- advise ATC of aircraft type and position for radar identification

When operating IFR in IMC:

- comply with all IFR clearances, altitude assignments, and approach procedures
- report position as required in non-radar airspace
- advise ATC of any deterioration in conditions below instrument approach minimums
- execute missed approach if visual reference for landing is not established at minimums

**Important:** VFR into IMC is one of the leading causes of fatal general aviation and helicopter accidents worldwide. A pilot without instrument qualification who enters IMC has an average of three minutes before spatial disorientation leads to loss of control. The only correct response to unexpected IMC is immediate IFR clearance request — not continued VMC flight, not pressing on, not hoping it clears. Declare and transition.

## 🎯 Why ATC Prioritizes IMC Calls

Controllers respond to IMC calls with priority because:

- a VFR aircraft in IMC is in immediate danger
- separation between VFR traffic and IFR traffic requires different standards
- the pilot may be experiencing spatial disorientation
- terrain clearance may be compromised if the aircraft is off the intended route
- rapid coordination with adjacent sectors may be necessary

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are flying VFR in your H130 from Joinville toward Navegantes at one thousand feet, following the valley route.

Visibility was fifteen kilometers at departure. Now, thirty minutes into the flight, valley fog has rolled in. Visibility drops to less than one kilometer. You can no longer maintain visual separation from terrain.

You are in IMC.

You declare immediately:

ANAC123, IMC encountered, request immediate IFR clearance, one thousand feet, H130, heading two five zero.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, emergency IFR clearance, climb and maintain three thousand feet, squawk seven seven zero zero, maintain heading two five zero, radar contact in two minutes.

You respond:

ANAC123, emergency IFR, climbing to three thousand feet, squawk seven seven zero zero, heading two five zero.

You establish instrument scan. Attitude indicator. VSI. Altimeter. Heading. You climb smoothly to three thousand feet and await radar contact.

Approach calls:

ANAC123, radar contact, identified twenty miles northeast of Joinville, clear of terrain at three thousand, continue heading two five zero.

3. Radar: ANAC123, radar contact, confirm IMC or visual.
4. Approach: ANAC123, advise minimum instrument approach altitude accepted.
5. Approach: ANAC123, ILS runway one four available in IMC, advise ready to copy.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

IMC is the boundary between flying by eye and flying by instruments.

When you cross that boundary — intentionally or not — the rules change. The technique changes. And the communication requirements change.

In IMC, your instruments are your world. Attitude indicator, altimeter, VSI, heading indicator. Outside the cockpit is grey. Nothing. Trust the instruments. Trust them completely.

But your instruments cannot tell ATC where you are. Your radio does that.

The moment you recognize IMC — declare. Do not wait to see if it clears in the next hundred meters. In a valley with fog, it does not clear. It thickens.

ANAC123, IMC encountered, request IFR clearance, altitude, heading.

Three seconds of radio work. It starts a chain of ATC responses that can save your life.

For H130 instrument-qualified crews — know your instrument approach limits for every destination. Know your alternate. Know the weather at both before departure.

IMC is not a surprise if you briefed the weather. And if it becomes a surprise anyway — declare. Immediately.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are flying VFR and encounter IMC unexpectedly. Describe your immediate actions and the communications required.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

My immediate action is to establish a stabilized instrument scan — attitude, altitude, heading — to prevent spatial disorientation. I would simultaneously declare to ATC: ANAC123, IMC encountered, request immediate IFR clearance, current altitude, heading. I would provide my aircraft type so ATC can identify performance and assign an appropriate clearance. I would not attempt to continue VFR or descend to look for visual reference. I would comply immediately with any IFR clearance issued. If I lacked instrument qualification or the aircraft was not instrument-equipped, I would declare an emergency: ANAC123, Mayday, VFR flight inadvertent IMC, request immediate assistance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing VFR hoping conditions improve after entering IMC.  
  ✔ Declare and request IFR clearance immediately. Do not press on.

- ❌ Descending to look for visual reference after entering IMC.  
  ✔ Maintain altitude or climb. Descending into unknown terrain in IMC is lethal.

- ❌ Not declaring the emergency when not instrument-rated and in IMC.  
  ✔ Non-instrument-rated pilot in IMC is an emergency. Declare Mayday. Request immediate vectors.

- ❌ Omitting altitude and heading from the IMC declaration call.  
  ✔ Include all information: ANAC123, IMC encountered, altitude one thousand, heading two five zero, request IFR.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, IMC Encountered, Request Immediate IFR Clearance

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, EYE-em-SEE en-KOWN-terd, reh-KWEST ih-MEE-dee-ut EYE-ef-AR KLEER-ans

**Word Stress**

- IMC → EYE-em-SEE (three letters, spoken individually)
- Encountered → en-KOWN-terd (three syllables, stress on second)
- Immediate → ih-MEE-dee-ut (four syllables, stress on second)
- IFR → EYE-ef-AR (three letters, spoken individually)
- Clearance → KLEER-ans

Practice:

IMC encountered... request immediate IFR clearance.

Together:

ANAC123, IMC encountered, request immediate IFR clearance, one thousand feet, heading two five zero.

Speak clearly. Do not rush. This is your most important radio call.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You depart Joinville VFR in your H130 heading toward Navegantes at eight hundred feet, following the valley. Visibility is ten kilometers. Twenty minutes later, dense fog fills the valley. Visibility drops to three hundred meters. You cannot see the terrain ahead.

**Your first task**

Compose the complete radio call to ATC.

**Your second task**

ATC assigns a squawk and instructs: climb and maintain three thousand feet, heading two five zero. While climbing through two thousand feet, you lose any remaining visual reference completely. You are entirely in cloud. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, IMC encountered, request immediate IFR clearance, eight hundred feet, heading two five zero, H130. Second task: I would continue the climb on instruments — attitude indicator, VSI, altimeter, heading indicator. Full instrument scan. Trust the instruments. I would not attempt to look outside. I would continue the climb to three thousand as cleared and maintain heading two five zero, then advise ATC: ANAC123, climbing through two thousand, complete IMC, instruments only.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

IMC is a hard boundary. On one side: visual reference, VMC, VFR operations. On the other: instruments only, IFR rules, ATC-managed separation.

The boundary can be crossed in seconds. A turn into a valley. Coastal fog at night. A convective cloud that builds faster than expected.

Your reaction must be faster than the boundary change.

Declare. Immediately. Every time.

Not because you are weak or unqualified. Because IMC changes everything — and ATC must know.

Maintain altitude. Trust your instruments. Work the radio.

Three disciplines. All three together. That is how you survive an unplanned IMC encounter.

Know the difference between VMC and IMC. Recognize the crossing. Declare immediately.

That sequence has saved lives. It will save yours too.
