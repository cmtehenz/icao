# Electronic Navigation Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0197  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Electronic Navigation Failure — the loss or serious degradation of electronic navigation sources used to determine position and track, including GPS, VOR, DME, or integrated electronic nav displays.

This is broader than a single GPS failure. Electronic navigation failure means the electronic means you were using to navigate are no longer reliable, and you must revert to remaining navaids, radar assistance, pilotage, or dead reckoning.

For H130 helicopter operations in Brazil — Congonhas, Guarulhos, Santos Dumont, Curitiba, Florianópolis — electronic navigation is often primary for IFR and for precise VFR routing in busy terminal areas. When it fails, ATC needs a clear report of capability and intentions.

This lesson prepares you for Part 2 phraseology: reporting the failure, requesting vectors, and accepting conventional clearances without cluttering the frequency.

## 📖 Meaning

Electronic Navigation Failure is the loss or significant degradation of one or more electronic navigation systems used for position fixing and course guidance, requiring the pilot to use alternate navigation methods and to advise ATC of reduced navigation capability.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de navegação eletrônica / Electronic Navigation Failure.

Perda ou degradação significativa de um ou mais sistemas eletrônicos de navegação usados para determinação de posição e orientação de curso, exigindo métodos alternativos de navegação e comunicação ao controle sobre a capacidade reduzida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When electronic navigation failure occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control and established heading/altitude
- identify which electronic sources failed and which remain usable
- switch to alternate navigation: remaining navaids, radar vectors, visual landmarks
- inform ATC of electronic navigation failure and remaining capability
- request radar vectors or a revised routing/approach as required
- avoid procedures that depend on the failed electronic source

Electronic navigation failure management includes:

- distinguishing total electronic nav loss from partial loss (for example GPS lost, VOR still available)
- using the magnetic compass and attitude instruments for basic orientation
- requesting progressive taxi or detailed routing if failure occurs on the ground or in the circuit
- planning diversion if destination approach capability is compromised

**Important:** Report capability, not just the word "failure." ATC needs to know whether you can still fly VOR, ILS, or only need vectors. A precise capability report speeds the correct clearance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use electronic navigation failure reports to:

- provide radar vectors and increased monitoring
- amend RNAV or electronic-nav-dependent clearances
- offer conventional approaches and navaid-based routing
- coordinate with adjacent sectors when the aircraft cannot self-navigate the route
- protect separation when pilot navigation accuracy may be reduced

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are en route from Santos Dumont to Curitiba in the H130 at four thousand feet.

Your primary electronic navigation display fails. GPS and CDI guidance go blank. The VOR receiver still powers up on a standby unit, but you are temporarily uncertain of exact track.

You hold heading by compass and attitude. You fix approximate position by coastline and known landmarks, then confirm with the standby VOR.

You transmit:

ANAC123, electronic navigation failure, primary nav display lost, able VOR, request radar vectors to Curitiba.

Approach provides headings. You rejoin the arrival with conventional guidance and land normally.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger electronic navigation failure, radar contact, turn left heading two four zero.
2. Control: ANAC123, confirm remaining navigation capability.
3. Approach: ANAC123, vectors for VOR runway one five, report established on final.
4. Radar: ANAC123, traffic information, maintain present heading, radar service.
5. Approach: ANAC123, cleared VOR approach runway one five, report field in sight if able.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, left heading two four zero, electronic navigation failure, ANAC123.

3. ANAC123, vectors VOR one five, will report established on final.
4. ANAC123, maintaining present heading, radar service, ANAC123.
5. ANAC123, cleared VOR approach runway one five, will report field in sight.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Electronic navigation failure is a capability problem. Speak in capabilities.

ATC does not need the serial number of the failed box. ATC needs: what you lost, what you still have, and what you want.

"Electronic navigation failure, request vectors" is the core message. Add "able VOR" or "unable RNAV" when it changes the clearance.

In Brazilian terminal airspace around Guarulhos or Congonhas, radar is often available. Use it. Do not invent a complex self-navigation plan while you are still diagnosing the failure.

In the H130, keep the scan simple: attitude, altitude, heading, then navigation. Electronic displays can fail; the helicopter still flies.

If you are VFR with good visual references, say so. If you are IFR with only partial navaids, say that too. Matching the report to the actual situation is professional radiotelephony.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your primary electronic navigation system fails while IFR. You still have VOR available and radar contact. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control of the aircraft and continue on the last assigned heading and altitude. I would identify remaining navigation sources and inform ATC of electronic navigation failure, stating that I am able to use VOR and that I request radar vectors. I would accept a conventional clearance or approach and avoid procedures that depend on the failed electronic system. I would keep communications short and update ATC if capability changes.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting "navigation problem" without saying what still works.  
  ✔ State remaining capability: able VOR, request vectors.

- ❌ Trying to continue an RNAV route after electronic nav required for RNAV is lost.  
  ✔ Request amended clearance; do not continue procedures you cannot fly.

- ❌ Overloading the frequency with troubleshooting details.  
  ✔ Report failure, capability, and request — then fly the clearance.

- ❌ Ignoring radar service when available after nav degradation.  
  ✔ Request and use radar vectors until navigation is stable again.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Electronic navigation failure is managed with clarity.

Fly the aircraft. Know what still works. Tell ATC in one clean transmission.

Ask for the help that matches your capability — usually radar vectors and a conventional procedure.

In Brazilian H130 operations, that discipline keeps a systems problem from becoming a navigation emergency.

Report capability. Accept the clearance you can fly. Finish the mission.
