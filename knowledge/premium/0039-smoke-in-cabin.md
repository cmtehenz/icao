# Smoke in the Cabin

**Catalog ID:** 0039  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers a visible and urgent cabin emergency.

Smoke in the Cabin means visible smoke is present in the passenger area or cockpit.

Smoke may indicate fire, electrical fault, or overheating — and it always demands immediate action.

Your urgency call depends on severity: Pan Pan for manageable smoke, Mayday when safe flight is in doubt.

In your H130, you are never far from the ground — but smoke can incapacitate the crew within minutes.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Smoke in the Cabin is visible smoke present in the passenger cabin or cockpit during flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Fumaça na cabine.

Presença de fumaça visível na cabine de passageiros ou no cockpit durante o voo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Smoke in the Cabin occurs, the pilot should:

- don oxygen immediately
- identify the smoke source if safely possible
- descend and navigate toward the nearest suitable airport
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday based on severity
- request immediate descent and vectors to land

Smoke in the Cabin reporting commonly includes:

- smoke in the cabin
- smoke in the cockpit
- request immediate descent
- smoke dissipating, request vectors to departure airport

**Important:** Specify cabin or cockpit. Request immediate descent when smoke is thick or source is unknown. Upgrade to Mayday if safe flight is in doubt.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Smoke in the Cabin reports to:

- assign immediate descent and priority vectors
- clear traffic from the aircraft's approach path
- alert fire and rescue services on the ground
- request souls on board and smoke status updates
- coordinate emergency landing at the nearest suitable airport

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at five thousand feet in your H130 on a sightseeing flight near Rio de Janeiro.

A passenger reports grey smoke rising from the floor near the rear seats.

You don oxygen, begin descent, and transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Rio Approach, ANAC123, smoke in the cabin, request immediate vectors to land on the nearest runway.

Approach clears traffic and vectors you toward Santos Dumont.

After checklist action, the smoke begins to dissipate. You update ATC:

ANAC123, smoke dissipating, request vectors to Santos Dumont for precautionary landing.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, descend immediately, turn left heading two seven zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, nearest airport one two o'clock, one five miles.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway cleared to land, fire services standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board.
5. Approach: ANAC123, report smoke dissipating or continuing.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Emergency, smoke in the cabin!
2. We have smoke in the cabin, request immediate descent.
3. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Approach, ANAC123, smoke in the cockpit, request immediate vectors to land on the nearest runway.
4. Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, smoke in the cabin, descending.
5. ANAC123, smoke dissipating, request vectors to departure airport.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Smoke in the cabin is not a wait-and-see event.

Don oxygen first. Then descend. Then speak.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say only smoke — controllers need cabin or cockpit.

If smoke is in the cockpit, say smoke in the cockpit. It changes the urgency picture.

Pan Pan works when smoke is light and you have control.

Mayday when smoke is thick, source unknown, or crew affected.

The ICAO Delta Part 3 simulado uses smoke in cockpit with Mayday and vectors — same urgency applies to cabin smoke.

Keep ATC updated: smoke continuing or smoke dissipating.

In the H130, electrical smoke from avionics or passenger equipment is a realistic scenario over Rio and Guanabara Bay.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When would smoke in the cabin require Mayday versus Pan Pan?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would use Pan Pan when smoke is light, source is identified, and I can maintain control. I would use Mayday when smoke is thick, source is unknown, crew is affected, or safe flight is in doubt. I always request immediate descent and vectors to the nearest runway.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only smoke without specifying cabin or cockpit.  
  ✔ State smoke in the cabin or smoke in the cockpit clearly.

- ❌ Requesting descent without declaring urgency level.  
  ✔ Declare Pan Pan or Mayday before requesting immediate descent.

- ❌ Using fumaça in English transmission.  
  ✔ Use smoke in the cabin — standard ICAO phraseology.

- ❌ Not updating ATC when smoke dissipates or worsens.  
  ✔ Report smoke dissipating or smoke continuing on every major change.

**Pronunciation:** SMOKE in the CAB-in, im-ME-di-ate de-SCENT

**Word Stress**

- Smoke → SMOKE (one syllable)
- Immediate → im-ME-di-ate
- Vectors → VEC-tors

Practice:

Smoke in the cabin... request immediate descent...

Together:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Rio Approach, ANAC123, smoke in the cabin, request immediate vectors to land.

Speak steadily — do not rush and lose clarity.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are at four thousand five hundred feet over Guanabara Bay in your H130 when grey smoke appears near the rear cabin floor.

**Your first task**

After donning oxygen and beginning descent, transmit your emergency declaration to Approach.

**Your second task**

After checklist action, smoke begins to clear. What do you report to ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report that smoke is dissipating, state my current altitude and position, and request vectors to Santos Dumont for precautionary landing and inspection. I would continue to monitor and report any change, ANAC123.
