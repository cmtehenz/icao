# Critical Engine Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0392  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Critical Engine Failure — the failure of the engine whose loss most adversely affects aircraft performance and controllability, a core multi-engine concept in ICAO performance English, and the related emergency communication after any engine failure.

In multi-engine aeroplanes, the critical engine is typically the one whose failure produces the greatest yaw and performance penalty (often related to P-factor and asymmetric thrust). Crews plan Vmc, V1, and OEI climb around that case.

For H130 pilots: there is only one engine — every engine failure is critical. This lesson teaches exam vocabulary for "critical engine" and the operational Mayday language you use in Brazilian H130 operations when the engine fails.

## 📖 Meaning

Critical Engine Failure is the failure of the engine that, when inoperative, has the most adverse effect on aircraft performance and handling; more broadly in operations, any engine failure that immediately threatens continued safe flight and requires emergency procedures and ATC notification.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do motor crítico / Critical Engine Failure.

Falha do motor que, quando inoperante, causa o efeito mais adverso no desempenho e na pilotagem da aeronave; em operações de helicóptero monomotor como o H130, qualquer falha de motor é crítica e exige autorrotação, Mayday e pouso forçado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When critical engine failure occurs or is discussed, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- identify the failed engine (multi-engine) and secure per checklist
- for H130: enter autorotation and select a landing area
- declare Mayday when safe flight is in doubt
- inform ATC of engine failure, position, intentions, souls, and fuel

Critical Engine Failure actions include:

- multi-engine: control yaw, maintain Vmc or above, follow OEI procedures
- takeoff phase: decide reject vs continue per V1 / company procedure (aeroplane)
- H130: collective reduction, autorotation profile, Mayday
- landing site selection and emergency services request
- clear, standard distress phraseology

**Important:** Do not delay the Mayday to discuss "critical engine" theory on frequency. Aviate, navigate, communicate. Use exam terms in the oral test; use distress language in the aircraft.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to critical engine failure by:

- acknowledging Mayday and providing priority handling
- clearing runways and alerting emergency services
- vectoring to the nearest suitable landing site
- requesting souls on board and fuel remaining
- keeping frequency clear for the distressed aircraft

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

H130 over the approach to Florianópolis. Engine fails. You lower collective, establish autorotation, pick a field east of the airport.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, engine failure, autorotating, forced landing east of Florianópolis, four souls on board.

Approach: ANAC123, Mayday copied, emergency services alerted, land as required.

You fly the aircraft first. The radio call is short and complete.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, Mayday copied, say position and intentions.
2. Tower: ANAC123, all runways available, emergency services rolling.
3. Radar: ANAC123, nearest landing site twelve o'clock, three miles.
4. Approach: ANAC123, souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Tower: ANAC123, wind calm, land any area, frequency will stand by.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

"Critical engine" is multi-engine textbook language. Memorize it for Part 2 and Part 3: the engine whose failure hurts most — asymmetric thrust, Vmc, OEI climb.

Then return to the H130: one engine. Failure is always critical. There is no non-critical engine to lose.

Your sequence never changes: aircraft, landing site, Mayday. The call is: Mayday, engine failure, intentions, souls, fuel when asked.

Do not clutter the frequency with technical essays. ATC needs position and intentions. Examiners need definitions. Know which audience you are talking to.

Brazilian coastal and city operations mean landing sites may be limited. Brief forced landing options before they are needed.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is a critical engine? What do you do if the engine fails in an H130?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The critical engine on a multi-engine aeroplane is the engine whose failure has the most adverse effect on performance and controllability. In the H130, the single engine is always critical. If it fails, I maintain rotor RPM in autorotation, select a landing area, and declare Mayday: engine failure, position, and landing intentions. I aviate first, then communicate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Delaying Mayday while troubleshooting on frequency.  
  ✔ Aviate, then Mayday with position and intentions.

- ❌ Confusing exam "critical engine" definition with H130 procedures.  
  ✔ Know both: definition for the test, autorotation for the aircraft.

- ❌ Omitting souls on board and fuel when ATC asks.  
  ✔ Answer promptly: souls and fuel remaining.

- ❌ Continuing toward a distant airport instead of a suitable forced landing site.  
  ✔ Land in the best available area within autorotation range.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Engine fails at 800 feet on approach to Congonhas in the H130. You establish autorotation toward a clear area short of the airport.

**Your first task**

What is your Mayday call?

**Your second task**

Approach asks souls and fuel. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, engine failure, autorotating, forced landing short of Congonhas, north of the approach. Second task: ANAC123, souls on board three, fuel remaining two five minutes. I would not attempt to stretch to the runway beyond autorotative range. Short, complete distress communication supports emergency response.
