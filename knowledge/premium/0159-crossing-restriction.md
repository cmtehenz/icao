# Crossing Restriction

**Catalog ID:** 0159  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Crossing Restriction — the ATC instruction requiring an aircraft to be at a specific altitude when it reaches a named fix or waypoint.

A crossing restriction ties an altitude to a specific geographic point.

Unlike a general altitude clearance — "descend and maintain five thousand" — a crossing restriction says: be at this altitude at this exact fix.

At Brazilian airports including Curitiba, São Paulo, Florianópolis, and Congonhas, crossing restrictions are issued during arrival sequencing, STAR operations, and terminal area coordination between sectors.

Knowing how to hear, read back, program, and comply with a crossing restriction is essential precision IFR flying.

## 📖 Meaning

A Crossing Restriction is an ATC instruction requiring the crew to be at a specified altitude — either at, at or above, or at or below — when the aircraft reaches a named fix or waypoint during flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Restrição de cruzamento.

Instrução do controle de tráfego aéreo que exige que a tripulação esteja em uma altitude especificada — exatamente em, em ou acima, ou em ou abaixo — quando a aeronave alcançar uma posição ou waypoint nomeado durante o voo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives a crossing restriction, they should:

- identify the named fix precisely in the FMS
- determine the altitude type: at, at or above, or at or below
- program the altitude constraint at that fix in the FMS
- calculate the required descent or climb rate to meet the restriction
- comply with the restriction at the fix — not before, not well after
- advise ATC immediately if unable to meet the restriction before reaching the fix

Crossing restriction operations commonly include:

- cross BRASA at flight level nine zero
- cross LUMEN at or below six thousand
- cross ARCOS at or above four thousand
- cross the outer marker at three thousand
- cross IDAMO at flight level eight zero, then descend and maintain four thousand

**Important:** A crossing restriction requires planning well in advance of the fix. You cannot wait until you are at the fix to decide whether you will meet it. Calculate the required descent rate from your current altitude and distance to the fix. If the math does not work — advise ATC before you reach the fix.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue crossing restrictions to:

- coordinate handoffs between sectors at defined altitudes
- ensure vertical separation from traffic on crossing routes
- protect approach segments and minimum vectoring altitudes
- manage arrival sequencing by assigning altitude targets at common fixes
- ensure obstacle and terrain clearance through complex terminal areas

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are at flight level one two zero, seventy miles from Curitiba in your H130.

Control transmits:

ANAC123, cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero.

You check the FMS: ARMIN is forty miles ahead.

You calculate: you need to descend four thousand feet in forty miles. At your current descent rate, that is achievable with a normal descent profile.

You program the FMS altitude constraint at ARMIN: at or below flight level eight zero.

You respond:

Cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

You initiate descent.

Passing flight level nine two approaching ARMIN, you are on profile.

You cross ARMIN at flight level seven eight — below eight zero, restriction met.

Control: ANAC123, contact Curitiba Approach one two two decimal five.

The crossing restriction was the altitude contract that enabled the handoff.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero.

3. Approach: ANAC123, cross BRASA at four thousand, continue descent two thousand five hundred.
4. Control: ANAC123, cross IDAMO at flight level one zero zero, then cleared direct destination.
5. Approach: ANAC123, cross the outer marker at three thousand, cleared ILS runway one four.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

A crossing restriction is a commitment.

When you read back "cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero," you are promising ATC you will be at that altitude at that fix.

ATC has made their traffic plan based on that promise.

Other aircraft, other sectors, approach sequencing — all depend on you keeping that commitment.

The technique: as soon as you receive a crossing restriction, calculate the math immediately.

Distance to fix. Altitude to lose. Required descent rate. Can your aircraft do it comfortably?

If yes — program it, monitor it, hit it.

If no — advise ATC now: ANAC123, unable to cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero, request lower clearance.

In the H130, crossing restrictions at mountain airports like Curitiba require extra attention. Terrain elevation affects your planning. Do not underestimate the required descent gradient.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC instructs you to cross a fix at flight level eight zero. You are at flight level one two zero, fifty miles from the fix. What is your planning process?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately calculate the required descent. I need to lose four thousand feet in fifty miles. A standard three degree descent covers roughly three hundred feet per nautical mile, so fifty miles gives me fifteen thousand feet of descent capacity — more than sufficient. I would program the altitude constraint in the FMS, set the target altitude, and begin descent. I would monitor my altitude at each ten-mile interval to confirm I am on profile for the crossing. If I detected I was high — for example, still at flight level nine zero with ten miles to go — I would either increase the descent rate or advise ATC that I could not meet the restriction. I would never arrive at the fix well above the restriction without warning ATC first.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Not programming the crossing restriction as an FMS altitude constraint.  
  ✔ Use FMS constraints to monitor compliance and get early warning if off-profile.

- ❌ Planning to meet the restriction only by checking altitude at the fix.  
  ✔ Monitor your descent profile continuously from the clearance point to the fix.

- ❌ Not reading back the fix name along with the altitude.  
  ✔ The fix is as important as the altitude: cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero.

- ❌ Advising ATC of non-compliance after passing the fix.  
  ✔ Advise ATC before reaching the fix if you cannot comply. After is too late for ATC to act.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Cross ARMIN at or Below Flight Level Eight Zero

**Pronunciation:** kros AR-min at or beh-LO flyt LEV-ul AYT ZE-ro

**Word Stress**

- Cross → kros
- ARMIN → AR-min (stress on first syllable; Brazilian fix name)
- At or Below → at or beh-LO
- Flight Level → flyt LEV-ul
- Eight Zero → AYT ZE-ro

Practice:

Cross ARMIN... at or below... flight level eight zero.

Together:

Cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero, ANAC123.

Speak the fix name clearly and without rushing — controllers need to confirm it matches their traffic picture.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**CROSS** — **C**alculate descent math immediately on receipt, **R**ead back fix name and altitude with restriction type, **O**pen FMS and set the altitude constraint, **S**tay on profile — monitor every ten miles, **S**peak up if unable, before reaching the fix.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Curitiba Control transmits: "ANAC123, cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero, then descend and maintain four thousand."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete instruction.

**Your second task**

Thirty miles from ARMIN you are still at flight level one zero zero. Winds have reduced your groundspeed significantly. You calculate you will cross ARMIN at flight level eight five. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero, then descend and maintain four thousand, ANAC123. Thirty miles out with a calculated crossing at flight level eight five, I would immediately advise ATC: ANAC123, unable to cross ARMIN at or below flight level eight zero, estimating flight level eight five due reduced groundspeed, request clearance. I must not wait until I reach the fix to report. ATC needs the information now to adjust separation with other traffic. They may issue a lower intermediate altitude or amend the restriction.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

A crossing restriction is a navigational commitment with real separation consequences.

Calculate immediately. Program the FMS. Monitor continuously.

Your job is to arrive at that fix at the contracted altitude — not approximately, not roughly.

Precisely.

If circumstances make it impossible — say so before you reach the fix.

That early communication is the difference between a manageable situation and a separation event.

Professional pilots plan the fix compliance from the moment the clearance is issued.
