# Flight Management System Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0196  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2, Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Flight Management System Failure — the loss or degradation of the FMS that manages navigation, performance, and flight-plan guidance.

An FMS failure is not the same as losing the aircraft. It is the loss of automated route and performance management. The pilot must revert to raw data, conventional navaids, and manual flight-path management while keeping ATC informed.

In H130 helicopter training academy operations, FMS capability may be limited compared with transport-category jets, but many IFR-equipped H130s use integrated GPS/FMS-style navigation for RNAV routes, approaches, and performance cues. When that system fails, the crew must fly the aircraft, navigate with remaining instruments, and communicate clearly.

Understanding how to report FMS failure, request vectors or conventional clearances, and describe the situation in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 radiotelephony and Part 3 speaking proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Flight Management System Failure is the loss or significant degradation of the aircraft's flight management computer or integrated navigation management function, requiring the crew to discontinue FMS-guided navigation and performance management and to continue the flight using alternate navigation sources, raw data, and manual procedures.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do sistema de gerenciamento de voo / Flight Management System (FMS) Failure.

Perda ou degradação significativa do computador de gerenciamento de voo ou da função integrada de navegação, exigindo que a tripulação abandone a navegação guiada pelo FMS e continue o voo com fontes alternativas de navegação, dados brutos e procedimentos manuais.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When FMS failure occurs, the crew should:

- fly the aircraft first — maintain attitude, altitude, and heading with primary flight instruments
- identify what remains available: GPS raw, VOR/NDB, radar vectors, visual references
- discontinue reliance on FMS lateral and vertical guidance
- inform ATC of FMS failure and current navigation capability
- request radar vectors, conventional approach, or revised clearance as needed
- rebrief the remainder of the flight using charts and remaining systems

FMS failure management includes:

- confirming whether the failure is total or partial (nav only, performance only, display)
- selecting an alternate navigation mode before the next waypoint or approach segment
- avoiding RNAV approaches that require FMS integrity if the system cannot support them
- coordinating with ATC early so sequencing and approach type can be adjusted

**Important:** FMS failure alone is usually not a Mayday. It is a capability reduction. Report it promptly so ATC can provide vectors or conventional clearances. Escalate to urgency only if safe navigation or approach capability is seriously compromised.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to FMS failure reports to:

- provide radar vectors when the aircraft can no longer self-navigate the cleared route
- amend clearances from RNAV to conventional procedures
- coordinate approach type changes with the destination unit
- increase monitoring of an aircraft with degraded navigation automation
- sequence traffic when the aircraft may need more spacing or a simpler procedure

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR from Congonhas to Florianópolis in the H130 at flight level nine zero.

Approaching the terminal area, the FMS freezes. Waypoints stop sequencing. Lateral guidance drops. Raw GPS position remains, but the managed flight plan is gone.

You maintain heading and altitude on primary instruments. You identify your approximate position relative to Florianópolis using remaining GPS and VOR.

You transmit:

ANAC123, flight management system failure, navigation capability reduced, request radar vectors to Florianópolis.

Approach: ANAC123, roger FMS failure, turn left heading one eight zero, vectors for ILS runway one four.

You read back the heading and prepare the conventional approach. The aircraft is still fully flyable. The automation is not.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger FMS failure, radar contact, say intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, unable RNAV, expect vectors for conventional approach.
3. Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading two seven zero, vectors to ILS runway one four.
4. Radar: ANAC123, confirm navigation capability, able VOR and ILS?
5. Approach: ANAC123, cleared ILS runway one four, report established.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

FMS failure is an automation problem, not an aircraft problem — until you treat it as one.

The first action is always the same: fly the helicopter. Attitude. Altitude. Heading. Then diagnose. Then talk.

Do not chase a frozen flight plan. If the FMS is not sequencing, it is not managing. You are.

In Brazilian IFR operations into Florianópolis, Curitiba, or Congonhas, ATC expects a clear capability statement: what failed, what you can still do, and what you need. "FMS failure, request vectors" is better than a long technical story.

If you were cleared for an RNAV approach and the FMS cannot support it, say so early. Do not continue hoping the box will recover at the final approach fix.

In the H130, you may have less FMS complexity than a jet — but the communication standard is the same. Report the failure. State remaining capability. Accept the simpler clearance.

Automation is a tool. When the tool fails, the pilot remains the system.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are cleared for an RNAV approach when your flight management system fails and lateral guidance is lost. Describe what you do and what you tell ATC.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain aircraft control using primary flight instruments and discontinue the RNAV approach if FMS guidance is required and no longer available. I would assess remaining navigation sources such as raw GPS, VOR, or ILS. I would inform ATC that the flight management system has failed, that RNAV capability is lost, and that I request radar vectors or a conventional approach. I would not continue an RNAV procedure that I can no longer fly to the required standard. After receiving a revised clearance, I would rebrief the approach and continue with the available systems.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "computer problem" without stating navigation impact.  
  ✔ Report: FMS failure, navigation capability reduced, request vectors.

- ❌ Continuing an RNAV approach after FMS lateral guidance is lost.  
  ✔ Discontinue RNAV if required guidance is unavailable; request conventional approach.

- ❌ Declaring Mayday for an FMS failure with remaining navaids and radar available.  
  ✔ Report the failure and request assistance; escalate only if safe navigation is compromised.

- ❌ Giving a long technical explanation on frequency.  
  ✔ Keep it short: what failed, what you can do, what you need.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are IFR from Curitiba to Congonhas. Cleared RNAV approach. Approaching the initial approach fix, the FMS freezes and stops providing lateral guidance. Raw GPS position remains. Radar is available.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit to Approach?

**Your second task**

Approach replies: "ANAC123, roger, turn left heading three six zero, vectors for ILS runway one seven Left." What is your full readback, and what do you do next in the cockpit?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, flight management system failure, unable RNAV, request radar vectors for conventional approach. Second task: ANAC123, left heading three six zero, vectors ILS runway one seven Left. Next I would discontinue RNAV guidance, set up the ILS, rebrief the approach, and continue flying primary instruments while following the assigned heading. I would not attempt to force the failed FMS to sequence the RNAV procedure.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

FMS failure removes automation, not airmanship.

Fly first. Navigate with what remains. Communicate early and clearly.

Tell ATC what failed and what you still can do. Ask for the clearance you can actually fly.

In the H130 academy environment, that usually means vectors and a conventional approach — not a long debate about computers.

When the flight management system fails, you become the flight management system.

That is the standard.
