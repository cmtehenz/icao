# Cleared for Immediate Takeoff

**Catalog ID:** 0115  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Cleared for Immediate Takeoff — one of the most time-critical clearances in aviation phraseology.

Cleared for Immediate Takeoff means ATC is authorizing departure now, with no delay. The pilot is expected to taxi onto the runway and begin the takeoff roll without stopping.

In the H130, this clearance is issued at busy Brazilian airports including Congonhas, Florianópolis, Joinville, and Navegantes when arriving traffic or sequencing demands immediate departure or a runway vacancy.

You must know when to accept it, when to decline it, and what hazards result from hesitation or misunderstanding.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and time-critical runway operations.

## 📖 Meaning

Cleared for Immediate Takeoff is an ATC clearance authorizing the pilot to enter the runway and begin the takeoff roll without delay, used when traffic or operational urgency requires departure with no pause.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorização do controle para o piloto entrar na pista e iniciar a corrida de decolagem sem atraso, utilizada quando o tráfego ou a urgência operacional exige a partida imediata.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Cleared for Immediate Takeoff is received, the pilot should:

- either accept and taxi onto the runway without delay, beginning the takeoff roll immediately
- or decline if not ready, using clear phraseology
- complete all pre-takeoff checks before reaching the holding point
- not line up, stop, complete checklists, and then depart — immediate means immediate
- be aware that declining is always acceptable and never penalized

Cleared for Immediate Takeoff operations commonly include:

- gap between a landing aircraft and the next arriving aircraft
- sequencing window for a departing aircraft between arrivals
- urgent operational requirement requiring priority departure
- traffic management at high-density airports

**Important:** If you are not ready to depart immediately — decline. Say: Unable immediate, request normal departure, ANAC123. Accepting when not ready and then stopping on the runway is far more dangerous than a polite decline.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC issues Cleared for Immediate Takeoff to:

- use a short gap between arriving aircraft for a departure
- maximize runway utilization at high-traffic airports
- expedite a priority departure such as medevac or VIP
- avoid a go-around for a landing aircraft behind
- clear the runway before the next arrival reaches final

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are holding short of runway one four at Florianópolis in your H130, pre-takeoff checks complete.

An Embraer 195 is on a six-mile final.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, cleared for immediate takeoff runway one four, traffic six miles on final.

You are ready and respond immediately:

Cleared immediate takeoff runway one four, ANAC123.

You taxi onto the runway without stopping and begin the takeoff roll.

You are airborne and climbing before the Embraer is at two miles.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, cleared for immediate takeoff runway one four, traffic six miles final.
2. Tower: ANAC123, line up and take off immediately.
3. Tower: ANAC123, traffic on final, immediate departure approved, runway one four.
4. Tower: ANAC123, if able, immediate takeoff runway one four.
5. Tower: ANAC123, cleared immediate takeoff, no delay.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Cleared immediate takeoff runway one four, ANAC123.
2. Lining up and taking off immediately, ANAC123.
3. Immediate departure, runway one four, ANAC123.
4. Unable immediate, request normal departure, ANAC123.
5. Immediate takeoff, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Immediate means now. Not in thirty seconds. Not after the checklist item you forgot.

Immediate means your feet are on the rudder pedals before you finish reading back.

The sequence:

Receive cleared immediate takeoff. Accept if ready. Enter runway. Begin roll without stopping. Climb.

Brazilian pilots sometimes accept immediate clearances when not fully ready — and then pause on the runway to complete preparations.

That pause is the worst possible outcome. It wastes the gap and creates a collision risk.

In the H130, if pre-takeoff checks are done and you are lined up, immediate is achievable.

If you are not ready — decline with confidence. ATC expects both answers. There is no shame in: Unable immediate, ANAC123.

Train the decision before you reach the holding point. Know whether you are ready.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When should you decline a cleared for immediate takeoff instruction?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I should decline a cleared for immediate takeoff instruction any time I am not fully ready to begin the takeoff roll without stopping — for example if pre-takeoff checks are incomplete, if there is a mechanical issue, if the crew is not ready, or if I have any doubt about the aircraft's readiness. I would transmit: Unable immediate, request normal departure, ANAC123. Stopping on the runway after accepting an immediate clearance is far more dangerous than a clear decline before entering the runway.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting immediate takeoff and then stopping on the runway.  
  ✔ Only accept when genuinely ready to roll without any pause.

- ❌ Not completing pre-takeoff checks before the holding point.  
  ✔ Finish all checks while taxiing — arrive at the hold ready.

- ❌ Saying "Roger" instead of reading back the runway and clearance.  
  ✔ Cleared immediate takeoff runway one four, ANAC123.

- ❌ Feeling pressured to accept when not ready.  
  ✔ Declining is safe, professional, and expected when you are not ready.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Holding short of runway one four at Florianópolis, pre-takeoff checks complete. Tower transmits: ANAC123, cleared for immediate takeoff, traffic five miles final.

**Your first task**

What do you read back and what action follows immediately?

**Your second task**

Now imagine your transponder warning light came on just as you reached the hold. You are not ready. What do you transmit?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If ready I would transmit: Cleared immediate takeoff runway one four, ANAC123 — and begin the takeoff roll the moment I enter the runway. If I had a technical issue and was not ready, I would transmit: Unable immediate, transponder issue, holding short, ANAC123. Stopping on the runway or accepting when not ready would create a risk far greater than a confident, clear decline.
