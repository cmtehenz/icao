# Rime Ice

**Catalog ID:** 0366  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Rime Ice — opaque, rough ice that forms when supercooled water droplets freeze on contact with the airframe, typically in stratiform cloud at temperatures well below freezing.

Rime ice is not a textbook curiosity. It changes rotor blade shape, adds weight, and can obscure antennas and the windshield. In Brazilian winter IFR — Curitiba, São Paulo plateau, Serra do Mar approaches — rime is the ice type crews most often report in layered cloud.

For H130 helicopter operations, the aircraft is generally not certified for flight into known icing. Recognizing rime early, exiting the layer, and reporting clearly to ATC are Part 2 and Part 3 skills that protect performance and traffic awareness.

Understanding how to describe rime ice in ICAO-standard English — intensity, altitude, and exit request — is essential for professional IFR communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Rime Ice is a milky, opaque, granular ice deposit formed when small supercooled droplets freeze almost instantly on impact with an aircraft surface, usually in stratiform cloud at sub-freezing temperatures, producing a rough texture that disrupts airflow and increases drag and weight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gelo rime / Rime Ice.

Depósito de gelo leitoso, opaco e granular formado quando pequenas gotículas super-resfriadas congelam quase instantaneamente ao impactar a superfície da aeronave, geralmente em nuvem estratiforme em temperaturas abaixo de zero, produzindo textura irregular que perturba o fluxo de ar e aumenta arrasto e peso.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When rime ice is encountered, the crew should:

- recognize opaque, rough ice on windshield, pitot, or visible airframe surfaces
- exit icing as soon as practicable — climb, descend, or divert to warmer or clearer air
- report icing type and intensity to ATC with altitude and temperature if able
- monitor power required, rotor RPM, and any vibration or performance loss
- avoid prolonged flight in known icing; the H130 is not approved for continuous icing exposure
- divert or declare urgency if performance becomes critically degraded

Rime ice reporting commonly includes:

- light, moderate, or severe rime icing
- altitude band and outside air temperature
- request climb or descent to exit the layer
- PIREP for following traffic

**Important:** Rime often forms in layered cloud between roughly minus ten and minus twenty Celsius, but can occur near freezing with small droplets. Do not wait for clear ice to appear before acting. Exit and report. Ice accretion on rotors can reduce lift margin quickly in a light helicopter.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use rime ice reports to:

- issue altitude changes that may exit the icing layer
- broadcast PIREPs to other aircraft in the same sector
- coordinate diversions to airports with better weather or warmer levels
- sequence traffic away from known icing altitudes
- provide priority handling when performance is degraded

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR at six thousand feet toward Curitiba in layered stratus. Outside air temperature is minus eight Celsius. Opaque ice begins building on the windshield wiper and pitot mast — rough, white, granular.

You transmit:

Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, moderate rime icing at six thousand, temperature minus eight, request descent to four thousand to exit icing.

Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain four thousand, report when clear of icing.

You leave six thousand. In warmer air below the layer, accretion stops. You report clear of icing and continue with caution.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger rime icing, descend and maintain four thousand, say intensity.
2. Center: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported moderate rime at five thousand.
3. Approach: ANAC123, advise when clear of icing.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say air temperature and icing type if able.
5. Approach: ANAC123, diversion approved, say intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Rime ice looks harmless because it is white and rough — not glossy like clear ice. Do not trust appearance.

Rime changes the aerodynamic shape of blades and airfoils. Drag rises. Power required rises. In the H130, you feel it as more collective for the same airspeed and altitude.

Brazilian winter IFR often puts you in stratiform layers over the plateau. That is classic rime territory: small droplets, cold air, long exposure if you stay in the layer.

Your job is simple: recognize, exit, report.

Say the type when you know it — rime — and the intensity. ATC turns that into a PIREP that protects the next crew.

Do not negotiate with the layer. Climb, descend, or turn. If you cannot exit and performance is falling, declare the situation early. Ice does not wait for a perfect radio call.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You enter cloud at five thousand feet with a temperature of minus six Celsius and see opaque, rough ice forming on the windshield. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would identify this as rime ice forming in freezing cloud. I would request an immediate altitude change to exit the icing layer and report the type and intensity to ATC. I would say I am experiencing moderate rime icing at five thousand, temperature minus six, and request descent or climb to warmer or clearer air. I would monitor power required and rotor performance while exiting, and I would divert if I cannot leave the icing conditions promptly. I would not continue in known icing in the H130.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "ice" without type or intensity.  
  ✔ Report: moderate rime icing at five thousand, request descent.

- ❌ Staying in the layer to "see if it gets worse."  
  ✔ Exit as soon as practicable; report and change altitude.

- ❌ Mixing Portuguese weather slang on the frequency.  
  ✔ Use ICAO English: rime icing, clear of icing, request diversion.

- ❌ Forgetting to report when clear of icing.  
  ✔ Advise ATC when accretion stops so the PIREP can be updated.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Moderate Rime Icing, Request Descent

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, MOD-er-it RYME ICE-ing, reh-KWEST deh-SENT

**Word Stress**

- Moderate → MOD-er-it (stress on first)
- Rime → RYME (one syllable, long i)
- Icing → ICE-ing (stress on first)
- Descent → deh-SENT (stress on second)

Practice:

Moderate rime icing... request descent to exit icing.

Together:

ANAC123, moderate rime icing at six thousand, request descent to four thousand.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are IFR at seven thousand feet toward Florianópolis. Opaque rough ice is building on the windshield. Temperature is minus ten. Approach has not offered an altitude change.

**Your first task**

What do you say to report the icing and request an exit?

**Your second task**

Approach clears you to five thousand. Accretion stops. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, moderate rime icing at seven thousand, temperature minus ten, request descent to exit icing. Second task: ANAC123, clear of icing at five thousand, temperature plus one. I would continue monitoring performance and avoid re-entering the cold layer without a plan. Clear, typed icing reports help ATC protect other traffic on the same route.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Rime ice is the quiet builder — white, rough, and easy to underestimate.

In the H130, underestimation costs power margin and options.

See it. Name it. Exit it. Report it.

Brazilian winter layers will give you rime again. The professional response does not change: leave the icing, tell ATC what you found, and do not accept prolonged exposure in an aircraft not certified for known icing.

That is how you keep the rotor system honest and the frequency useful.
