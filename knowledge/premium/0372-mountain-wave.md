# Mountain Wave

**Catalog ID:** 0372  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Mountain Wave — oscillating airflow downwind of mountains that produces strong updrafts, downdrafts, and turbulence, often marked by lenticular clouds when moisture is present.

Mountain wave is not only a jet-stream problem. In Brazil, strong flow over the Serra do Mar, Serra da Mantiqueira, and southern ranges can create wave activity that affects IFR altitudes and VFR ridge routes. The H130 can experience rapid altitude loss on the lee side and severe turbulence in rotor zones beneath the wave.

Crews must recognize forecasts and cloud signatures, avoid rotor areas, request altitude or routing changes, and report wave conditions to ATC in clear ICAO English.

## 📖 Meaning

Mountain Wave is a standing atmospheric wave formed when stable air flows over a mountain barrier, producing alternating ascending and descending currents downwind of the ridge, frequently accompanied by turbulence — including severe rotor turbulence beneath the wave — and possible lenticular cloud formations.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Onda orográfica / Mountain Wave.

Onda atmosférica estacionária formada quando ar estável flui sobre uma barreira montanhosa, produzindo correntes ascendentes e descendentes alternadas a sotavento da crista, frequentemente acompanhada de turbulência — incluindo turbulência severa de rotor sob a onda — e possíveis formações de nuvens lenticulares.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When mountain wave is forecast or encountered, the crew should:

- avoid flying through rotor zones beneath lenticulars on the lee side
- maintain terrain clearance with extra altitude margin on lee descents
- request altitude or route change if downdrafts prevent level flight
- reduce speed to turbulence penetration guidance if severe turbulence occurs
- report mountain wave, altitude deviations, and turbulence intensity to ATC
- divert or reverse course if unable to maintain safe altitude over terrain

Mountain wave reporting commonly includes:

- moderate or severe mountain wave
- altitude gain or loss of a stated amount
- request higher or deviation from route
- turbulence associated with wave / rotor

**Important:** Smooth air between wave crests can hide violent rotors below. Lenticular clouds are a warning, not scenery. On H130 VFR ridge work in strong wind, treat the lee side as hostile until proven otherwise.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use mountain wave reports to:

- warn aircraft on lee-side routes and airways
- approve altitude changes for ride quality and terrain clearance
- reroute traffic away from reported severe wave or rotor
- collect PIREPs for SIGMET / AIRMET updates
- understand large altitude deviations that are weather-driven, not pilot error

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR at nine thousand feet crossing the lee of the Mantiqueira with a strong northwest flow. The aircraft enters a sustained downdraft; you cannot hold altitude even with climb power.

You transmit:

Brasília Center, ANAC123, moderate mountain wave, altitude loss one thousand feet, request climb to one one thousand or deviation south.

Center: ANAC123, climb and maintain one one thousand, report when smooth.

You climb out of the strongest sink and report conditions for the next aircraft.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Center: ANAC123, roger mountain wave, climb and maintain one one thousand.
2. Center: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported severe mountain wave over the ridge.
3. Approach: ANAC123, advise able to maintain altitude, terrain in your area.
4. Center: ANAC123, deviation south approved, report clear of wave.
5. Center: ANAC123, say turbulence intensity with the wave if able.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, climbing one one thousand, moderate mountain wave, will advise.
2. ANAC123, roger severe wave PIREP, requesting higher.
3. ANAC123, unable maintain nine thousand due mountain wave, requesting climb.
4. ANAC123, deviation south approved, reporting clear of wave when able.
5. ANAC123, severe turbulence with mountain wave, requesting immediate climb.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Mountain wave is organized violence in the atmosphere.

The updraft feels like free performance. The downdraft that follows can outrun your climb rate — especially in a helicopter near max continuous power with a high density altitude.

Brazilian ranges are lower than the Andes, but strong stable flow still builds wave and rotor. Do not hug the lee slope in strong wind.

If you cannot hold altitude, say unable to maintain and request climb or deviation. That is professional language, not weakness.

Watch for lenticulars. Give rotors a wide berth. Report what you find — the next crew is flying your PIREP.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You encounter a strong downdraft on the lee side of a mountain ridge and cannot maintain altitude. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would apply climb power and turn away from terrain as required while requesting an immediate altitude or route change. I would report mountain wave with the altitude loss and turbulence intensity if known. I would say I am unable to maintain present altitude due to mountain wave and request climb or deviation. I would avoid the rotor zone beneath the wave and not descend on the lee side without adequate terrain clearance. In the H130 I treat sustained lee-side sink as a reason to leave the area promptly.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Fighting the downdraft toward rising terrain.  
  ✔ Turn toward lower terrain / request climb; do not sink into the ridge.

- ❌ Reporting only "turbulence" when wave is the cause.  
  ✔ Say mountain wave — it explains altitude loss and helps forecasts.

- ❌ Flying under lenticulars for a "smooth ride."  
  ✔ Rotors often hide beneath; avoid the lee rotor zone.

- ❌ Delayed PIREP after recovering.  
  ✔ Report while the information is still useful to ATC and traffic.

Strong northwest wind over the Serra. You lose eight hundred feet in a downdraft at MSA.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Center clears you higher. Conditions improve. What do you say?

First task: ANAC123, moderate mountain wave, altitude loss eight hundred feet, unable to maintain, request immediate climb or deviation. Second task: ANAC123, level [new altitude], mountain wave decreasing, light turbulence. Protect terrain first, then refine the PIREP so ATC can help others.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Mountain wave organizes updrafts and downdrafts downwind of ridges.

Respect the lee. Avoid the rotor. Report the wave.

In Brazilian mountain IFR and ridge VFR, altitude margin is life. When the atmosphere takes it away, ask for it back on the frequency — clearly and early.
