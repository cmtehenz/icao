# Mode Confusion

**Catalog ID:** 0471  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Mode Confusion — a human-factors condition in which the crew misunderstands the active automation mode, the expected aircraft behaviour, or the transition between modes, leading to unintended flight-path outcomes.

Mode confusion is not a software failure. It is a mismatch between what the crew believes the aircraft is doing and what the aircraft is actually doing.

In the H130, automation may be lighter than on a transport jet, but autopilot modes, flight-director cues, and coupled approaches still demand mode awareness. A crew that assumes altitude hold when the aircraft is in vertical speed, or expects a capture that never armed, can drift off profile quickly.

Understanding how to discuss mode confusion in ICAO-standard English — including status reports, intentions, and recovery calls to ATC — is essential for Part 1, Part 2, and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Mode Confusion is a human-factors state in which the flight crew incorrectly interprets the active or armed automation mode, resulting in unexpected aircraft behaviour, delayed recognition of the true flight path, and increased risk of altitude, heading, or approach-path deviations.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Confusão de modo / Mode Confusion.

Estado de fatores humanos em que a tripulação interpreta incorretamente o modo de automação ativo ou armado, resultando em comportamento inesperado da aeronave, reconhecimento tardio da trajetória real e risco aumentado de desvios de altitude, proa ou trajetória de aproximação.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When mode confusion is suspected, the crew should:

- cross-check the mode annunciator against the intended flight path
- verbalize the active mode and the expected capture or hold
- disconnect or reselect modes deliberately if the aircraft is not responding as briefed
- fly the aircraft first — pitch, power, and trim — then reconfigure automation
- advise ATC if a temporary deviation or go-around is required to regain the profile

Mode awareness includes:

- confirming armed versus active modes before each phase change
- briefing expected mode transitions on approach and departure
- monitoring for silent mode changes after ATC altitude or heading amendments
- rejecting automation that does not match the clearance

**Important:** If the aircraft is not doing what you expect, assume mode confusion until proven otherwise. Do not chase the flight director blindly. Take manual control, stabilize, then re-engage automation with a clear mode plan.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to mode-related deviations to:

- detect unexpected altitude or heading changes early
- issue traffic or terrain separation when a crew reports automation issues
- clear a climb, level-off, or go-around while the crew recovers
- coordinate with approach or departure when a coupled approach is abandoned
- keep the sequence stable when an aircraft leaves the expected profile

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on a coupled approach into Congonhas in the H130. You believe altitude hold is active at three thousand feet. The aircraft continues descending through two thousand eight hundred.

You scan the mode annunciator. Vertical speed is still engaged. Altitude was never armed.

You disconnect, level off, and call:

ANAC123, mode confusion, leveling two thousand eight hundred, request climb three thousand.

Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand, report established when ready.

You respond:

ANAC123, climbing three thousand, will report established.

You rebrief the modes, re-arm altitude, and continue only when the aircraft matches the clearance.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, confirm altitude, you appear below profile.
2. Radar: ANAC123, say intentions, unexpected descent observed.
3. Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand, report when ready for approach.
4. Control: ANAC123, discontinue approach, fly heading three six zero, climb four thousand.
5. Approach: ANAC123, go around, climb and maintain three thousand, report automation status when able.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Mode confusion kills quietly.

The aircraft looks calm. The needles look almost right. The crew keeps waiting for a capture that was never armed.

In the H130, do not treat light automation as optional awareness. Every mode you engage must be announced and monitored. Every clearance change is a mode-check trigger.

Fly the aircraft. Then manage the modes. Never the reverse.

If you cannot explain the active mode in one sentence, you do not own the automation.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

During a coupled approach, the aircraft descends through the cleared altitude. You discover vertical speed is still active. Describe what you say and do.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would take manual control, stop the unintended descent, and advise ATC immediately: ANAC123, mode confusion, leveling, request climb to cleared altitude. After stabilizing, I would rebrief the modes, confirm altitude is armed and active, and only then continue or request a new approach clearance. I would not remain on the unexpected path while troubleshooting silently.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Staying silent while trying to fix automation without telling ATC.  
  ✔ Report the deviation and intentions as soon as control is regained.

- ❌ Chasing the flight director without checking mode annunciators.  
  ✔ Verify active and armed modes before following guidance cues.

- ❌ Re-engaging automation immediately without a mode plan.  
  ✔ Stabilize manually, brief the mode sequence, then re-engage.

- ❌ Assuming the previous mode remains after an ATC amendment.  
  ✔ Recheck modes after every altitude or heading change.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are cleared to maintain three thousand on approach. The H130 continues descending. Vertical speed is active; altitude was never armed.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC as you take manual control?

**Your second task**

Approach replies: "ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand, report ready." What do you say after leveling?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, mode confusion, leveling, request climb three thousand. Second task: ANAC123, climbing three thousand, will report ready for approach. I would not continue descending or remain silent while sorting modes. Clear communication buys separation while I restore a correct automation state.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Mode confusion is a crew problem before it is an aircraft problem.

If the mode does not match the clearance, the clearance is not being flown.

In the H130, keep mode calls short, honest, and early. Stabilize first. Reconfigure second. Report third when the path is safe.

Know the mode. Own the path. Tell ATC when the two diverge.
