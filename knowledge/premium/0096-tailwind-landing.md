# Tailwind Landing

**Catalog ID:** 0096  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers tailwind landing — landing with wind from behind the aircraft, increasing groundspeed and extending stopping distance.

Tailwind Landing demands higher approach speeds, longer landing roll, and careful calculation of landing distance, especially on short runways.

In the H130, tailwind conditions are common when the preferred runway is active for traffic sequencing at airports like Congonhas and Joinville.

You must recognize tailwind limitations, request an into-wind runway when operationally feasible, and communicate clearly with ATC about your intentions.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 operational communication.

## 📖 Meaning

A Tailwind Landing is a landing performed with wind from behind the aircraft, resulting in increased groundspeed, extended landing roll, and reduced control authority near the ground.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pouso com vento em cauda (tailwind).

Pouso realizado com vento vindo de trás da aeronave, resultando em maior velocidade de solo, maior distância de pouso e menor autoridade de controle próximo ao solo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a Tailwind Landing is required, the pilot should:

- calculate the tailwind component against aircraft limitations
- verify landing distance available is sufficient with the tailwind
- request an into-wind runway if traffic permits
- increase approach speed and be prepared for longer rollout
- go around if landing performance is marginal

Tailwind Landing reporting commonly includes:

- request runway into wind
- tailwind component on final
- unable to accept tailwind, request other runway
- going around, tailwind exceeds limits

**Important:** A tailwind increases your groundspeed at touchdown. Know your limit — typically five knots for many light aircraft and helicopters. Exceeding it is a runway excursion risk.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC supports Tailwind Landings by:

- reporting wind direction and speed to arriving aircraft
- assigning into-wind runways when traffic permits
- approving runway changes requested for wind
- advising when tailwind limits are likely exceeded
- coordinating go-arounds when landing distance is a concern

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Joinville in your H130.

Tower reports wind one zero zero at twelve knots and assigns runway two seven — giving you a direct tailwind.

You transmit:

Joinville Tower, ANAC123, request runway zero nine, into wind, tailwind on two seven.

Tower checks traffic and approves runway zero nine.

You land with a clean headwind component and normal stopping distance.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, wind one zero zero at one two knots, runway two seven, cleared to land.
2. Tower: ANAC123, runway zero nine available, say able to accept.
3. Tower: ANAC123, wind check, one one zero at one five knots, gusting two zero.
4. Tower: ANAC123, roger request, runway zero nine, cleared to land.
5. Tower: ANAC123, be advised tailwind component on runway two seven, approximately one two knots.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Tailwind landing is all about numbers and distance.

Wind from behind. More speed at touchdown. More runway needed.

The sequence:

Check tailwind component. Compare to limits. Request into-wind runway. Or calculate landing distance carefully.

Brazilian pilots sometimes accept any assigned runway without checking the wind.

Know your limit. Five knots tailwind is a common certified limit for light aircraft.

At Joinville or Congonhas, the sequence can push you to an unfavorable runway.

Request the better runway. ATC usually cooperates when the reason is clear.

Train the request until it is automatic.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do if ATC assigned you a runway with a significant tailwind component?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would check the tailwind component against my aircraft landing limitations. If it exceeded the limit or made the landing distance insufficient, I would request a runway into wind. If no other runway was available, I would calculate whether the available runway was sufficient and either accept or go around and divert if it was not safe.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting any assigned runway without checking wind direction.  
  ✔ Always calculate or estimate the tailwind component on landing.

- ❌ Saying "vento em cauda" on frequency instead of a standard request.  
  ✔ Use request runway into wind or unable to accept tailwind.

- ❌ Underestimating the landing roll increase with tailwind.  
  ✔ Tailwind increases groundspeed — landing distance increases significantly.

- ❌ Not going around when tailwind exceeds limits.  
  ✔ Go around or divert when the tailwind component is outside limits.
