# High Hover

**Catalog ID:** 0425  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers High Hover — a sustained hover out of ground effect at a significant height above the surface, used for reconnaissance, external load, rescue, or ATC-requested positioning, and demanding careful power management and clear communication.

High hover is a performance and exposure problem: more power, less margin, and often nowhere to put the aircraft quickly if something fails.

For H130 operations in Brazil — urban observation, SAR, HEMS scene survey, and obstacle clearance — you must state high hover intentions, limitations, and emergencies in ICAO-standard English.

Understanding how to discuss and report high hover operations is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

High Hover is a helicopter hover maintained well above the surface, typically out of ground effect, requiring greater power than an in-ground-effect hover and exposing the aircraft to higher risk if engine or control problems occur, because a safe landing area may not be immediately beneath the aircraft.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting a high hover, the crew should:

- confirm OGE hover performance before committing
- brief escape routes and landing options beneath or nearby
- advise ATC of high hover altitude and purpose when relevant
- declare inability if power margin is insufficient
- declare emergency immediately if power or control is lost in the high hover

High hover reporting commonly includes:

- high hover at [altitude], for survey / rescue
- unable high hover, power limited
- leaving high hover, descending
- mayday / pan-pan from high hover if failure occurs

**Important:** A high hover is not a holding pattern. Minimize time. Keep an exit. Do not accept ATC high-hover requests that exceed OGE capability.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers may request or accommodate high hovers to:

- hold a helicopter clear of arriving/departing traffic
- allow observation of an accident scene
- separate the helicopter from ground vehicles or crowds
- coordinate rescue or external-load positioning
- keep low routes clear while the helicopter remains in one place

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

HEMS scene south of São Bernardo. Approach asks you to hold high for traffic before descending to the highway.

You check power: OGE hover available at this weight.

São Paulo Approach, high hover two thousand, scene in sight, ready to descend when cleared.

After traffic passes, you descend. Later, on a hotter heavier flight, the same request comes — and you refuse:

Unable high hover, power limited, request orbit or delay.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, hold high hover two thousand, traffic on approach, report scene in sight.
2. Approach: ANAC123, cleared to descend to the scene, report landing.
3. Approach: ANAC123, roger unable high hover, orbit south of the scene, expect delay.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel if declaring urgency.
5. Approach: ANAC123, traffic clear, descend at your discretion, remain clear of the highway until landing assured.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

High hover English is capability English first, mission English second.

If you cannot do OGE, say unable before you leave ground effect. If you can, say the altitude and the purpose so ATC can sequence traffic around you.

In the H130 over Brazilian highways and cities, a high hover looks calm on frequency and feels exposed in the cockpit. Engine failure options shrink with height and surrounding obstacles.

Keep the hover short. Keep an escape. If power flutters or Nr decays, leave the hover — descend or transition forward — and tell ATC you are leaving high hover.

Never let a controller's traffic plan push you into an OGE hover the AFM does not support. Unable is a complete sentence.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC asks you to high hover at two thousand feet over a scene. You are power limited for OGE. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would refuse: Unable high hover, power limited, request orbit or delay until I can descend. I would not climb into an out-of-ground-effect hover without performance margin. If already in a high hover and power becomes critical, I would leave the hover and declare urgency as required.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting a high hover because the low hover was comfortable.  
  ✔ Check OGE performance; say unable if margins are gone.

- ❌ Staying in a high hover "só mais um minuto" with decaying margin.  
  ✔ Leave early; advise descending or transitioning.

- ❌ Not telling ATC when you leave an assigned high hover.  
  ✔ Report leaving high hover so traffic sequencing can update.

- ❌ Using pan-pan language for a simple unable.  
  ✔ Unable high hover is enough unless an emergency exists.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

HEMS scene. Approach wants a two-thousand-foot high hover for sequencing. Weight and temperature are high.

**Your first task**

OGE is not available. What do you say?

**Your second task**

On a later flight OGE is available. You are holding high when Nr fluctuates. What do you say and do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, unable high hover, power limited, request orbit or delay for descent. Second task: Leave the hover immediately — transition or descend — and transmit: ANAC123, leaving high hover, descending, and upgrade to pan-pan or mayday if control or RPM is threatened. Do not stay high hoping the margin returns.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

High hover is a tool, not a parking spot.

Confirm OGE. State altitude. Keep an exit. Refuse when power says no.

In the H130 over Brazil, the high hover exposes you — to traffic plans, to density altitude, and to thin margins.

Hover high only with numbers, a purpose, and a way out. Speak the limitation before the hover becomes the emergency.
