# Steep Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0430  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Steep Approach — a helicopter approach flown at a steeper-than-normal descent angle to clear obstacles and arrive in a confined or obstructed landing area.

Steep approach is essential H130 technique for city helipads, hospital rooftops, and obstacle-rich Brazilian sites where a shallow path will not clear wires or buildings.

You must brief the path, power management, and go-around in ICAO English for Parts 2 and 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Steep Approach is a helicopter landing approach conducted at a significantly steeper descent angle than a normal approach, used to remain clear of obstacles while decelerating to a hover or touchdown in a confined or obstructed area.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aproximação íngreme / steep approach.

Aproximação de pouso de helicóptero conduzida com ângulo de descida significativamente mais íngreme que o normal, usada para permanecer livre de obstáculos enquanto se desacelera até o pairamento ou o toque em área confinada ou obstruída.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When flying a steep approach, the pilot should:

- identify obstacles and select a clear approach and go-around path
- manage rate of descent and airspeed to avoid settling with power
- plan to arrive at a hover or landing with adequate power margin
- abort early if the approach becomes unstable or too steep to recover
- advise ATC of steep approach or confined-area intentions when required

Steep approach reporting commonly includes:

- commencing steep approach
- short final, confined area
- going around, unstable steep approach
- established hover, landing

**Important:** Steep is not vertical. Excessive rate of descent at low airspeed invites vortex ring state. Brief the profile. Fly the numbers. Go around if the sink rate exceeds your recovery comfort.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support steep approaches by:

- protecting the approach corridor from other traffic
- understanding longer hover or positioning time at confined pads
- coordinating go-arounds that may differ from runway missed approaches
- providing wind and traffic information near obstacle-rich sites
- sequencing helicopters into hospital or rooftop pads

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to a rooftop helipad in São Paulo in your H130. Buildings and wires surround the pad.

You brief a steep approach to clear obstacles, then decelerate to a hover over the pad.

On short final the sink rate increases beyond your briefed limit. You go around:

Going around, unstable steep approach, remaining clear of buildings, request another approach.

You reset and fly a controlled steep path to a stable hover and landing.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, report commencing approach, rooftop traffic advised.
2. Tower: ANAC123, wind three two zero at one two, cleared approach.
3. Tower: ANAC123, roger going around, report intentions.
4. Tower: ANAC123, orbit west of the hospital, advise ready.
5. Tower: ANAC123, pad clear, cleared for another approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Steep approach clears obstacles. It does not forgive poor energy management.

In the H130, you need a plan for where the go-around goes — often not straight ahead through wires. Brief the escape before you commit.

Part 3 loves: high rate of descent, low airspeed on steep final — what is the risk? Answer: settling with power / vortex ring state. Action: reduce descent rate, increase airspeed if able, or go around with coordinated power.

Brazilian rooftop and hospital pads demand this skill weekly. Fly steep. Fly stable. Leave when it is not.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your steep approach becomes unstable with a high sink rate. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would initiate a go-around on the briefed obstacle-clear path and transmit: going around, unstable steep approach, remaining clear, request orbit. I would not continue a high sink-rate approach into a confined pad. After resetting configuration and brief, I would request another approach when ready.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Flying a near-vertical descent "to clear wires."  
  ✔ Steep and controlled — avoid vortex ring territory.

- ❌ No briefed go-around path around obstacles.  
  ✔ Always brief where the missed approach goes.

- ❌ Not telling ATC you are going around from a rooftop.  
  ✔ Report going around and remaining clear of buildings.

- ❌ Decelerating too late and arriving fast over the pad.  
  ✔ Plan deceleration early on the steep path.
