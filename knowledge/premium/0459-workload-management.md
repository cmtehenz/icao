# Workload Management

**Catalog ID:** 0459  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Workload Management — organizing tasks, prioritizing flying the aircraft, and shedding non-essential work before task saturation degrades safety.

Workload spikes on departure, approach, go-around, and abnormal procedures. When both pilots are heads-down, radio calls are missed, checklists are rushed, and situational awareness collapses. Managing workload is managing capacity.

For H130 operations — single-engine helicopter complexity, IFR approaches, offshore arrivals — workload management must appear in crew task sharing and in radio discipline: delay non-critical requests, ask ATC to standby when necessary, and prioritize aviate–navigate–communicate. Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 all apply.

## 📖 Meaning

Workload Management is the deliberate distribution and prioritization of cockpit tasks so that flying the aircraft, navigating, communicating, and managing systems remain within crew capacity, including shedding or delaying non-essential tasks when demand exceeds available attention.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gerenciamento de carga de trabalho / Workload Management.

Distribuição e priorização deliberada das tarefas de cabine para que voar a aeronave, navegar, comunicar e gerenciar sistemas permaneçam dentro da capacidade da tripulação, incluindo adiar ou eliminar tarefas não essenciais quando a demanda excede a atenção disponível.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing workload, the crew should:

- prioritize: aviate, navigate, communicate, then manage systems
- assign PF and PM roles clearly during high-workload phases
- complete checklists early when possible; do not rush critical items
- delay non-essential cabin or company calls during sterile phases
- tell ATC when unable to comply immediately: standby, unable, request delay

Workload management includes:

- anticipating busy phases — departure, approach, weather avoidance
- reducing automation surprises by briefing modes early
- using standard calls to reduce negotiation under pressure
- recognizing saturation — tunnel vision, missed radio, skipped checks

**Important:** If you need time to fly the aircraft, say so. "Standby" is professional workload management, not poor English.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers adjust when crews manage workload by:

- accepting standby during critical flight phases
- simplifying clearances when a crew reports high workload
- delaying frequency changes until the aircraft is stable
- prioritizing essential instructions during emergencies
- avoiding stacked complex clearances on short final

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are flying the H130 on a busy IFR arrival into Guarulhos. Approach issues a complex reroute, altitude, and speed in one transmission while you are configuring.

PF flies. PM says:

Approach, ANAC123, standby, configuring.

You stabilize, then:

ANAC123, ready to copy, say again clearance.

Approach repeats. You read back correctly. Workload was managed; the clearance was not lost to saturation.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading two seven zero, descend three thousand, reduce speed one two zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say again, did you copy the clearance.
3. Tower: ANAC123, continue approach, contact tower one one eight decimal five.
4. Approach: ANAC123, after go-around, expect vectors, advise when ready to copy.
5. Control: ANAC123, complex reroute available, advise when able to copy.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, standby, configuring, will call ready to copy.
2. ANAC123, left heading two seven zero, descending three thousand, speed one two zero.
3. ANAC123, continuing approach, tower one one eight decimal five.
4. ANAC123, going around, request simple vectors, high workload.
5. ANAC123, ready to copy reroute.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Workload management is capacity accounting.

Every task costs attention. Flying costs the most. Navigation next. Radio next. Everything else waits.

In the H130, approach and landing already consume high attention — especially in weather or confined areas. Do not add company calls, passenger explanations, or FMS experiments on short final.

Use the other pilot. PF flies. PM talks and configures. If both are heads-down, workload management has already failed.

"Standby" on frequency protects the aircraft. A wrong readback under saturation creates a new threat. Take the three seconds. Then copy. Then fly.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC issues a long clearance while you are hand-flying a go-around. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would prioritize flying the go-around. The PM or I would transmit: ANAC123, standby, going around. When the aircraft is stable and climbing, I would say: ANAC123, ready to copy. I would not accept a complex clearance until I can read it back correctly.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting long clearances while unstable.  
  ✔ Say standby; copy when able to read back correctly.

- ❌ Both pilots heads-down on the FMS on final.  
  ✔ PF flies; PM manages radios and configuration.

- ❌ Treating standby as rude or unprofessional.  
  ✔ Standby is standard phraseology for workload control.

- ❌ Adding non-essential tasks during sterile cockpit.  
  ✔ Shed cabin and company chatter until after landing or cruise.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Standby, Configuring, Ready to Copy Shortly

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, STAND-by, kun-FIG-yur-ing, RED-ee too KOP-ee SHORT-lee

**Word Stress**

- Standby → STAND-by
- Configuring → kun-FIG-yur-ing (stress on second)
- Ready → RED-ee
- Copy → KOP-ee

Practice:

Standby, configuring... ready to copy shortly.

Together:

ANAC123, standby, configuring, will call ready to copy.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On short final in the H130, Approach changes runway and issues a new frequency, heading, and altitude in one call. You are not yet stable.

**Your first task**

What do you say immediately?

**Your second task**

After go-around and stabilization, what do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, standby, or ANAC123, going around, request vectors — if the runway change makes the approach unsafe. Second task: ANAC123, ready to copy, request simplified clearance. Workload management means protecting the aircraft first, then accepting the new plan when you can execute it.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Workload management is knowing what not to do right now.

Fly first. Navigate. Communicate when able. Shed the rest.

In the H130, Brazilian IFR arrivals will stack tasks faster than you expect. Use standby. Use role split. Use go-around if the plan no longer fits capacity.

That is how crews stay ahead — with attention left for the next threat.
