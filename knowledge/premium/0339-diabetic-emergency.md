# Diabetic Emergency

**Catalog ID:** 0339  
**Category:** Passenger  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Diabetic Emergency — a passenger medical event involving low or high blood sugar that may cause confusion, unconsciousness, or seizures and require priority landing and medical assistance.

ICAO exams expect operational English: diabetic emergency, passenger unconscious, request medical assistance — not a lecture on insulin types.

For H130 helicopter operations under ICAO Delta academy training in Brazil, crews may encounter diabetic events on charter or passenger flights. Cabin treatment options are limited. If the passenger becomes unconscious or seizing, diversion and clear ATC communication are the professional response.

Master the phraseology so urgency, diversion, and ground medical support are coordinated without delay.

## 📖 Meaning

Diabetic Emergency is a medical crisis related to diabetes — typically hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) or severe hyperglycemia — that may cause altered consciousness, sweating, confusion, seizure, or unconsciousness and requires medical assistance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Emergência diabética / Diabetic Emergency.

Crise médica relacionada ao diabetes — tipicamente hipoglicemia (açúcar baixo no sangue) ou hiperglicemia grave — que pode causar alteração de consciência, sudorese, confusão, convulsão ou inconsciência e exige assistência médica.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a diabetic emergency occurs, the crew should:

- obtain cabin status: conscious, confused, unconscious, seizure
- allow cabin first aid / sugar if appropriate for known hypoglycemia per cabin procedures
- declare Pan Pan for urgency when the condition is serious
- divert if the passenger is unconscious, seizing, or not improving
- request medical assistance and priority landing

Diabetic emergency reporting commonly includes:

- diabetic emergency on board
- passenger unconscious / confused / seizure
- request priority landing and medical assistance
- passenger improving after treatment / still unresponsive

**Important:** You are not confirming blood glucose on frequency. Report diabetic emergency and the observable condition. Let ground medical services manage definitive care.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to diabetic emergency reports to:

- prioritize the aircraft when consciousness is impaired
- alert medical services for possible hypoglycemia or related crisis
- obtain updates that change diversion urgency
- clear the approach path of delaying traffic
- arrange ambulance meeting on landing

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are en route from Belo Horizonte toward the coast when cabin reports a known diabetic passenger who is confused and sweating, then becomes unresponsive.

You declare Pan Pan, divert to the nearest suitable airport, and request medical assistance.

Approach clears traffic and alerts ambulances. You land. Medical services meet the aircraft. The passenger is transferred.

You report condition changes only when they matter: unconscious, improving, seizing.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say if passenger is conscious.
2. Approach: ANAC123, divert approved, priority vectors, medical services alerted.
3. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, ambulances standing by.
4. Control: ANAC123, say souls on board and nature of assistance required.
5. Tower: ANAC123, vacate when able, medical team will meet the aircraft.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, diabetic emergency on board, passenger unconscious, request priority landing and medical assistance.
2. ANAC123, diverting, diabetic emergency, passenger confused, request ambulances standing by.
3. ANAC123, passenger diabetic emergency, seizure, request immediate landing.
4. ANAC123, passenger improving after treatment, still request medical assistance on landing.
5. ANAC123, on final, diabetic emergency continuing, medical team requested.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Diabetic emergency is common enough to train — and easy to mishandle on the radio.

Do not say "açúcar" and hope ATC understands. Say diabetic emergency, then the state: unconscious, confused, seizure.

Brazilian pilots may say "crise de diabetes" or "hipoglicemia." Prefer diabetic emergency on board for ICAO English, then the observable condition.

If cabin gives sugar and the passenger improves, still request medical assistance after a serious event. Improvement in flight is not a full medical clearance.

In the H130, one unresponsive passenger fills the cabin with workload. Divert early. Keep the frequency clean. Fly the approach.

Pan Pan is normally correct. Escalate if the situation becomes immediately life-threatening and you need absolute priority.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

A diabetic passenger becomes unconscious. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare Pan Pan, report diabetic emergency on board with passenger unconscious, request priority landing at the nearest suitable airport, and request medical assistance. I would provide souls on board when asked and update ATC if the passenger starts seizing or recovers consciousness.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "diabetes" or "açúcar baixo" without standard structure.  
  ✔ Use diabetic emergency on board plus conscious/unconscious status.

- ❌ Delaying diversion while waiting to see if sugar "works."  
  ✔ If unconscious or seizing, divert and request medical assistance.

- ❌ Giving insulin dosing details on frequency.  
  ✔ Report condition and assistance required — keep it operational.

- ❌ Cancelling ambulances after partial improvement.  
  ✔ Keep medical assistance for landing after a serious diabetic event.

A known diabetic passenger becomes unconscious fifteen minutes from a suitable airport.

**Your second task**

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Diabetic emergency is managed with observation, urgency, and a prompt landing.

Report diabetic emergency. Report consciousness. Divert when needed. Request medical assistance.

Avoid clinical debates on frequency. Avoid informal Portuguese as your only call.

In Brazilian H130 operations, limited cabin care makes ATC coordination and early diversion your strongest tools.

Clear words. Short updates. Professional handover on the ground.
