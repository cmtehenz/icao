# Challenge and Response

**Catalog ID:** 0462  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Challenge and Response — the structured CRM technique where one crew member challenges a condition or checklist item and the other responds with a verified status, creating a closed verification loop.

Challenge and response is the spoken backbone of SOPs: checklists, altitude calls, unstable approach gates, and safety-critical deviations. Without a required response, a challenge is only a comment.

For H130 crews, challenge and response supports Part 1 CRM language and Part 2 operations when deviations must be raised assertively — including go-around calls and ATC confirmations when the radio is involved.

## 📖 Meaning

Challenge and Response is a crew communication method in which one pilot issues a specific challenge regarding a checklist item, flight parameter, or safety concern, and the other pilot provides a required, verifiable response confirming the actual status or the corrective action.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Desafio e resposta / Challenge and Response.

Método de comunicação da tripulação no qual um piloto emite um desafio específico sobre um item de checklist, parâmetro de voo ou preocupação de segurança, e o outro piloto fornece uma resposta obrigatória e verificável confirmando o estado real ou a ação corretiva.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When using challenge and response, the crew should:

- use standard checklist challenges with required responses — not casual nods
- challenge deviations from SOPs: speed, configuration, altitude, mode
- respond with facts: "gear down, three greens," not "okay"
- escalate if there is no response: repeat, then take control if required
- include ATC when the challenge concerns clearance ambiguity

Challenge and response includes:

- normal checklist flows
- approach gate calls — stable or go around
- safety challenges — "pull up," "wrong runway," "altitude"
- training challenges from instructor to trainee with required reply

**Important:** A challenge without a response is an open safety item. Repeat it. Do not assume agreement from silence.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers hear challenge-and-response outcomes when crews:

- go around after an approach-gate challenge
- request confirmation of a disputed clearance
- report unable after an internal safety challenge
- correct a wrong readback discovered by the monitoring pilot
- declare urgency after unresolved critical deviations

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

On final in the H130, the monitoring pilot challenges:

Speed high, not stable.

No corrective response. The challenge is repeated:

Not stable, go around.

You execute and transmit:

Tower, ANAC123, going around, unstable approach.

Tower: ANAC123, roger go around, climb two thousand, runway heading.

The challenge produced a response — action and radio call.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, continue approach, wind calm, runway two seven cleared to land.
2. Tower: ANAC123, roger go around, climb and maintain two thousand, fly runway heading.
3. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you are going around, say intentions.
4. Approach: ANAC123, unable that request, advise alternate intentions.
5. Tower: ANAC123, confirm aborting landing, acknowledge.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around, unstable approach, climbing two thousand.
2. ANAC123, affirm going around, request vectors for another approach.
3. ANAC123, diverting, request clearance, not accepting another approach.
4. ANAC123, unable continue, going around.
5. ANAC123, aborting landing, climbing, request instructions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Challenge and response is assertiveness with structure.

The challenge must be specific: "speed high," "wrong altitude," "not stable." Vague comments do not demand a clear reply.

The response must be observable: correct the parameter, or go around, or confirm the status.

In the H130, approach gates arrive quickly. If the PF does not respond to "not stable," the PM must escalate to "I have control" per SOP — then communicate with ATC.

Hierarchy does not cancel challenges. Captains need challenges. First officers must give them. That is professional CRM, not conflict.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your monitoring pilot challenges "not stable" on short final and you agree. What do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would execute the go-around and transmit: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. If ATC asks intentions, I would say: ANAC123, request vectors for another approach, or divert if conditions require. The challenge is closed by action and a clear radio call.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using "okay" as a checklist response.  
  ✔ State the actual status: "flaps thirty, set" or equivalent SOP words.

- ❌ Softening critical challenges to avoid conflict.  
  ✔ Be clear: "not stable, go around."

- ❌ Ignoring a challenge because you are the captain.  
  ✔ Respond with action or explanation; never with silence.

- ❌ Challenging without waiting for or requiring a response.  
  ✔ Repeat once; escalate per SOP if still unanswered.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

At five hundred feet in the H130, your PM challenges: "Not stable." You are high and fast. Tower has cleared you to land.

**Your first task**

What is the correct response inside the cockpit?

**Your second task**

What do you say on frequency?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Acknowledge and go around — "Going around" — not "I can fix it." Second task: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. Challenge and response is complete only when the aircraft path and the radio both reflect the decision.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Challenge and response turns concern into verified action.

Specific challenge. Required response. Escalation if silence.

In the H130, unstable approaches and wrong setups will not wait for polite debate. Challenge early. Respond clearly. Tell ATC when the plan changes.

That is how crews convert CRM words into a safe flight path.
