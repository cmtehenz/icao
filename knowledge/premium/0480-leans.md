# Leans

**Catalog ID:** 0480  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers the Leans — a common vestibular illusion in which a pilot incorrectly senses a bank attitude after a sub-threshold roll or after recovering from a turn, leading to inappropriate "corrections" into a real bank.

The leans are subtle. A slow roll goes unnoticed. When instruments show wings level, the pilot still feels banked and leans the other way — creating a genuine spiral risk in IMC.

In H130 instrument flight, prolonged vectors, holding, and gentle intercepts can all set up the leans. Recognition and instrument discipline prevent a quiet illusion from becoming an unusual attitude.

Understanding how to manage the leans in ICAO-standard English — including requests for straight-and-level time and simple vectors — supports Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

The Leans is a vestibular illusion in which the pilot experiences a false sensation of bank, typically after an unnoticed gradual roll or after leveling from a turn, and may apply control inputs that create an actual opposite bank.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

The Leans / Ilusão de inclinação.

Ilusão vestibular em que o piloto experimenta uma falsa sensação de inclinação lateral, tipicamente após uma rolagem gradual não percebida ou após nivelar de uma curva, e pode aplicar comandos que criam uma inclinação real no sentido oposto.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the leans are suspected, the crew should:

- accept the attitude indicator as the sole bank truth in IMC
- hold wings level despite a strong opposite sensation
- request a period of straight flight if needed to reset vestibular cues
- avoid large aileron inputs based on bodily lean
- cross-check with the other pilot before "correcting" a felt bank

Leans prevention includes:

- monitoring bank angle during slow autopilot or manual rolls
- using a steady instrument scan in holding and vectors
- briefing the leans as a threat on long IMC arrivals
- preferring gentle, announced turns over abrupt heading changes

**Important:** The leans feel like knowledge. You "know" you are banked. You are not. If you act on that knowledge, you create the bank you feared.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers help crews affected by the leans when they:

- allow straight-ahead flight for stabilization
- issue gentle heading changes rather than abrupt turns
- query unexpected turns observed on radar
- provide simple maintain-heading and altitude instructions
- vector toward VMC when the crew requests relief from IMC maneuvering

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Holding in IMC near Porto Alegre. After rollout you feel right-wing-low. Instruments show level. You start to lift the right wing — and actually bank left.

Your first officer says: "Wings level — trust it."

You hold the attitude and call:

ANAC123, requesting straight ahead one minute, vestibular leans, stabilizing.

Radar: ANAC123, fly present heading, maintain four thousand, report ready for turn.

You respond:

ANAC123, present heading, maintaining four thousand, will report ready.

Sensation fades. Scan stabilizes. Holding resumes.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, fly present heading, maintain four thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm wings level, left turn observed.
3. Radar: ANAC123, gentle right turn heading three six zero.
4. Control: ANAC123, continue straight ahead, report when ready.
5. Approach: ANAC123, vectors to final when able, advise.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The leans are a quiet liar.

No drama. Just a persistent feeling that the world is tilted. Then a "correction" that tilts it for real.

In the H130, make wings-level a verbal confirmation in IMC: "Level on instruments." Say it. Mean it. Hold it.

If the lean will not leave, ask ATC for straight flight. There is no prize for maneuvering while disoriented.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

After leveling from an IMC turn you feel banked. Instruments show wings level. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would hold wings level on the attitude indicator and refuse to correct the sensation. If needed I would request time: ANAC123, requesting straight ahead, stabilizing. I would accept further turns only when the instrument scan is solid and the false lean has reduced.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Banking to cancel a false lean after rollout.  
  ✔ Hold instrument wings-level until the sensation fades.

- ❌ Staying silent while fighting the illusion through a hold.  
  ✔ Request straight ahead if stabilization is required.

- ❌ Making abrupt corrections when the lean returns.  
  ✔ Use small, instrument-referenced inputs only.

- ❌ Skipping a cross-check with the other pilot.  
  ✔ Confirm bank state verbally before any "correction."

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

After rolling out of an IMC hold, you feel strongly right-wing-low. Instruments show wings level. You catch yourself about to bank left.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC if you need time?

**Your second task**

Radar then assigns an immediate turn. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, requesting straight ahead, stabilizing on instruments. Second task: ANAC123, request delay turn, wings level, recovering leans. I would not invent a bank to silence a false sensation. Straight flight and instrument truth come first.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

The leans reward patience and punish "corrections."

In the H130, level means level on the panel. If your body disagrees, your body is the problem.

Hold the wings. Ask for time. Turn again only when the lie has quieted.
