# Total Electrical Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0255  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Total Electrical Failure — loss of normal electrical power affecting instruments, communication, navigation, and systems, requiring emergency checklists, possible battery or standby instrument use, and carefully managed ATC communication when radios are intermittent or restored. Highly relevant to H130 operations: electrical anomalies quickly degrade avionics and lighting, and ICAO English must describe the failure, remaining capability, and intentions under Critical priority.

Total electrical failure is one of the most demanding radio scenarios because the radio itself may be part of the problem. When you can transmit, every word must count.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master the standard Mayday/Pan Pan structure for electrical failure, including lost communication contingency thinking under DECEA/ICAO procedures.

Understanding total electrical failure in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Total Electrical Failure is the complete or near-complete loss of aircraft electrical power generation, leaving the crew dependent on remaining battery or standby systems for essential instruments and communication, requiring emergency procedures, possible squawk and light-signal contingency planning, and clear ATC notification of the failure, remaining capability, and intentions whenever communication is available.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha elétrica total / Total Electrical Failure.

Perda completa ou quase completa da geração de energia elétrica da aeronave, deixando a tripulação dependente da bateria restante ou de sistemas de reserva para instrumentos e comunicação essenciais, exigindo procedimentos de emergência, possível planejamento de contingência com squawk e sinais luminosos, e notificação clara ao ATC sobre a falha, a capacidade restante e as intenções sempre que a comunicação estiver disponível.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When total electrical failure occurs, the crew should:

- fly the aircraft using standby instruments and available visual references
- apply QRH/AFM electrical failure procedures — shed load, attempt reset/restore as published
- transmit Mayday or Pan Pan while battery power remains, if able
- report total electrical failure, partial restoration if any, and intentions to land
- consider squawk 7700, lost-comm procedures, and light signals if radios fail
- for H130: prioritize control, navigation with remaining instruments, and earliest suitable landing

Total electrical failure reporting commonly includes:

- total electrical failure / electrical failure
- operating on battery / standby instruments
- request priority landing / vectors if nav capability limited
- souls and fuel when asked
- advise if communication may be intermittent

**Important:** If you still have a radio, use it immediately. Battery time is limited. State the failure, your position if useful, and your landing intention before the frequency goes quiet.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to total electrical failure to:

- provide immediate priority landing clearance
- anticipate intermittent or lost communication
- use light signals and expected lost-comm routing when needed
- keep traffic clear around an aircraft with degraded navigation and lighting
- alert emergency services for arrival without normal systems

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Operational scenario: generators fail; you are on battery power with limited time.

While the radio still works, you transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, total electrical failure, operating on battery, request immediate vectors for priority landing.

ATC gives a short clearance to the nearest airport. You acknowledge once, fly standby instruments, and prepare for possible lost communication if the battery dies.

For H130 operations, the same urgency applies — electrical failure is a land-soon event, not a continue-as-planned event.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday total electrical failure, squawk seven seven zero zero, say intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, turn left heading two five zero, descend four thousand, nearest airport twelve o'clock, one five miles.
3. Approach: ANAC123, priority landing approved, runway one zero, emergency services standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, if radio fails, continue present heading, expect light signals on final.
5. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land runway one zero, confirm battery power remaining if able.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Total electrical failure steals your office — screens, radios, lights — while the aircraft is still an aircraft.

Fly attitude and power. Then spend battery words like money: Mayday, total electrical failure, on battery, request immediate landing. Position and souls if time allows.

Do not narrate every failed bus on the first call. ATC needs the big picture and a landing plan. Details later if the radio lives.

In the H130, electrical failure is intimate — the cockpit goes quiet and dark fast. Decide early. Transmit early. Land early.

Critical means you rehearse the short call before you need it.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You experience a total electrical failure but still have short-term battery radio capability. What do you say to ATC, and what else should you consider?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would fly the aircraft on standby instruments, apply the electrical failure checklist, and immediately declare Mayday, stating total electrical failure, that I am operating on battery, and that I request immediate priority vectors for landing. I would give position, souls, and fuel if time allows, and advise that communication may become intermittent. I would also consider squawk 7700 and lost-communication procedures, including light signals, if the radio fails before landing. The same priorities apply in helicopter operations.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saving the radio call "for later" while battery power drains.  
  ✔ Transmit the Mayday as soon as the aircraft is under control.

- ❌ Saying only "electrical problem" without "total electrical failure."  
  ✔ Use the clear ICAO phrase when that is the condition.

- ❌ Giving a long systems explanation on a dying battery.  
  ✔ Nature, battery status, immediate landing request — then stop talking and fly.

- ❌ Forgetting lost-comm contingency in the plan.  
  ✔ Brief light signals / expected routing if radio fails.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Both generators fail. Battery power remains for a limited time. Nearest suitable airport is fifteen miles away.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit immediately?

**Your second task**

ATC clears you for runway one zero and warns that light signals will be used if radio fails. What do you reply?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, total electrical failure, operating on battery, request immediate vectors for priority landing. Second task: ANAC123, runway one zero, wilco, if radio fails will continue for runway one zero and look for light signals. I would give souls and fuel if battery time allows, then minimize further transmissions and fly the approach.
