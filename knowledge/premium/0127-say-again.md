# Say Again

**Catalog ID:** 0127  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Say Again — the correct ICAO phrase to request a repetition of an ATC transmission.

In aviation, you never say "repeat." Repeat has a military meaning (fire again) and is avoided on aviation frequencies.

Say Again is the standard phrase. It is short, clear, and internationally understood.

In the H130, operating into Florianópolis or Joinville where radio frequency congestion is common, you will regularly encounter transmissions you did not fully receive. Knowing when and how to request Say Again — including partial Say Again for specific elements — is a core ICAO Level 4 skill.

## 📖 Meaning

Say Again is the ICAO-standard phrase used to request that all or part of a previous transmission be repeated. It replaces the word "repeat" in aviation radio communication and is used when a message was not received clearly, was partially lost, or needs to be confirmed.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Repita / Diga novamente.

Frase ICAO padrão para solicitar que toda ou parte de uma transmissão anterior seja repetida. Substitui a palavra "repeat" nas comunicações de rádio aeronáutico e é usada quando a mensagem não foi recebida claramente, foi parcialmente perdida ou precisa ser confirmada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a transmission is unclear, the pilot should:

- transmit say again to request a full repetition
- use say again all after [element] to request from a specific point
- use say again [specific element] to request only one value
- never guess at an unclear altitude, runway, or instruction
- never act on a clearance you did not fully receive

Say Again operations commonly include:

- altitude or level not clearly received
- runway number blocked by radio interference
- frequency or transponder code partially heard
- QNH value unclear due to static or stepped transmission
- routing or waypoint name distorted

**Important:** Never paraphrase or substitute a guess when you missed a critical value. Always request Say Again before acting on incomplete information.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers expect pilots to request Say Again because:

- radio interference and frequency congestion are normal
- stepped transmissions occur regularly
- safety depends on clear information exchange
- it is far better to ask than to act on incorrect information
- ICAO explicitly authorizes and encourages Say Again requests

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are departing Florianópolis in your H130.

Departure transmits and you hear only part of the message:

ANAC123, climb... [static]... four zero, fly heading...

You missed the altitude and heading. You transmit:

ANAC123, say again.

Departure repeats:

ANAC123, climb to flight level one four zero, fly heading zero three five.

Now you have complete information. You read back:

Climb flight level one four zero, fly heading zero three five, ANAC123.

If you had guessed "maybe they said one two zero" and climbed to the wrong level, you could have violated a traffic restriction.

Say Again prevented that.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, say again your altitude.
2. Tower: ANAC123, say again request.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say again all after "climb to."
4. Ground: ANAC123, say again last transmission, you were broken.
5. Control: ANAC123, say again squawk code.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Say again, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, say again altitude clearance.
3. ANAC123, say again all after "descend to."
4. ANAC123, say again — you were blocked.
5. ANAC123, say again the runway, please.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Say Again is a professional tool, not an admission of failure.

Brazilian pilots sometimes hesitate to ask for Say Again because they feel it reveals a weakness. They hear a partial message, guess, and press on.

That is the dangerous path.

The professional path is: I did not fully receive that transmission. I will not act on incomplete data. I will request Say Again.

There is no shame in a Say Again request. There is great risk in not making one.

In the H130, flying single-pilot in busy Brazilian terminal areas, radio congestion will step on transmissions. This is normal. Request Say Again without hesitation.

Use partial Say Again when only one element was lost:

Say again the runway, ANAC123.

Say again altitude, ANAC123.

Say again all after "cleared to," ANAC123.

These are precise, professional tools.

Use them.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the correct ICAO phrase to use when you did not clearly receive an ATC transmission? Why is "repeat" avoided in aviation?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The correct phrase is Say Again. In aviation, repeat has a historical military association meaning "fire again" and could be misinterpreted on a frequency shared by military and civil traffic. ICAO standardized Say Again to eliminate this ambiguity. If I only missed part of the message, I can say Say Again all after the last word I understood, or request the specific element I missed, such as Say Again the runway.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using "repeat" instead of the ICAO-standard phrase.  
  ✔ Use Say Again — always, without exception, on aviation frequencies.

- ❌ Guessing at an unclear altitude or runway instead of requesting Say Again.  
  ✔ Never act on incomplete information. Request Say Again before acting.

- ❌ Saying "Can you say again?" with unnecessary words.  
  ✔ Keep it concise: Say again, ANAC123. Or Say again the altitude, ANAC123.

- ❌ Not specifying which part was missed, requiring a full retransmission.  
  ✔ Use Say Again all after [word] or Say Again [specific element] to save frequency time.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Joinville Approach transmits: "ANAC123, cleared ILS runway... [static]... QNH... [blocked]."

**Your first task**

You heard "cleared ILS" but missed both the runway and the QNH. What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Approach repeats and you clearly hear "cleared ILS runway two seven, QNH one zero zero eight." Now provide the full readback.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would transmit: ANAC123, say again — you were blocked. If I needed to be specific: ANAC123, say again the runway and QNH. On the second transmission, my readback would be: Cleared ILS runway two seven, QNH one zero zero eight, ANAC123. Never assume a runway number. A confusion between runway two seven and runway zero nine could lead to a serious incident.
