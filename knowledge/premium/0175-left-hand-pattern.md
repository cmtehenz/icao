# Left-Hand Pattern

**Catalog ID:** 0175  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Left-Hand Pattern — the standard traffic circuit direction in which all turns are made to the left, as opposed to a Right-Hand Pattern where turns are made to the right.

Traffic patterns — or circuits — are the organized rectangular flight paths flown at aerodromes during visual and instrument approaches in VFR conditions.

The standard ICAO traffic pattern is a Left-Hand Pattern. At most Brazilian airports, unless otherwise published or directed by ATC, pilots expect to turn left onto the crosswind, downwind, base, and final legs.

A Right-Hand Pattern is the exception — published when terrain, obstacles, noise-sensitive areas, or conflicting traffic require traffic to turn right instead of left.

At smaller Brazilian airports near the coast and in terrain — Joinville, Navegantes — traffic pattern direction can change based on active runway and local noise procedures.

ICAO Part 2 tests pilots on traffic pattern phraseology, direction, and operational understanding. This lesson builds those skills.

## 📖 Meaning

Left-Hand Pattern is a traffic circuit in which all turns are made to the left — the aircraft turns left from the upwind leg to crosswind, from crosswind to downwind, from downwind to base, and from base to final approach.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Circuito à esquerda.

Circuito de tráfego em que todas as curvas são feitas à esquerda — a aeronave vira à esquerda do trecho de subida para o vento cruzado, do cruzado para o vento contrário, do contrário para o final de base, e da base para a aproximação final.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ATC specifies Left-Hand Pattern, the pilot should:

- confirm the active runway and pattern direction
- enter the traffic pattern at the appropriate altitude and position
- make all turns to the left at each leg of the circuit
- report each leg when required by ATC or local procedure
- yield to aircraft on final approach at all times
- maintain pattern altitude until turning final

Standard traffic pattern legs:

- **Upwind** — along the runway, climbing after takeoff
- **Crosswind** — first 90° turn to the left, climbing to pattern altitude
- **Downwind** — parallel to the runway, opposite direction, at pattern altitude
- **Base** — 90° left turn toward the runway
- **Final** — aligned with the runway, descending to landing

**Important:** At some Brazilian airports, both Left-Hand Pattern for one runway and Right-Hand Pattern for the parallel or opposite runway operate simultaneously. Know which applies to your runway before entering the pattern.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers specify Left-Hand Pattern to:

- ensure orderly traffic flow in the circuit
- prevent conflicts between aircraft on opposite pattern turns
- apply standard procedures when noise abatement or terrain requires it
- coordinate pattern direction for the active runway
- separate VFR pattern traffic from IFR approach traffic

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are returning to Joinville in your H130 after a local flight.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, report downwind runway two seven, left-hand pattern, wind two eight zero at eight knots.

You are approaching the aerodrome from the east.

You maneuver to enter the downwind leg on the left-hand side of runway two seven.

You report:

Joinville Tower, ANAC123, downwind runway two seven, left-hand pattern.

Tower responds:

ANAC123, number two, follow the Cessna on final, report base.

You watch the Cessna on final.

Passing abeam the landing threshold, you report:

ANAC123, base runway two seven.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, cleared to land runway two seven, wind two eight zero at eight.

You turn final and land.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, enter left-hand pattern runway two seven, report downwind.
2. Tower: ANAC123, left-hand pattern runway three three, number two traffic, Cessna downwind.
3. Tower: ANAC123, continue left-hand pattern, extend downwind, spacing for landing traffic.
4. Tower: ANAC123, join left-hand pattern, report final runway two seven.
5. Tower: ANAC123, right-hand pattern approved runway one five, noise abatement procedure.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, left-hand pattern runway two seven, will report downwind.
2. ANAC123, left-hand pattern runway three three, number two, traffic copied.
3. ANAC123, extending downwind, left-hand pattern.
4. ANAC123, joining left-hand pattern, will report final runway two seven.
5. ANAC123, right-hand pattern runway one five, understood.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Left-Hand Pattern is the default.

Most of the time, you are not told which pattern to use — you already know it is left.

But at certain runways, after certain approaches, or at noise-sensitive airports, you may receive an explicit direction.

Know it. Confirm it. Fly it precisely.

The H130 has excellent visibility in turns — use it.

In a left-hand pattern, the pilot in the left seat has the runway in sight through the turn. That is why left-hand is the standard — it places the runway on the captain's side.

At Joinville and Navegantes, local terrain and sea-breeze patterns sometimes require right-hand patterns for specific runways. Always check the Chart Supplement or ATIS for current pattern direction.

Maintain standard pattern altitude — 1,000 feet AGL for fixed-wing, though the H130 typically operates at lower altitudes in the circuit.

Stay on the correct side of the runway extended centerline during downwind.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe a left-hand traffic pattern and explain when ATC might specify a right-hand pattern instead.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A left-hand traffic pattern is the standard rectangular circuit in which the pilot makes all turns to the left — from the upwind leg to crosswind, crosswind to downwind, downwind to base, and base to final approach. The aircraft is always turning left and the runway is always on the pilot's left side during the downwind leg. ATC specifies a right-hand pattern as an exception when terrain or obstacles on the standard left side are too close, when noise-sensitive areas under the left pattern require avoidance, when conflicting traffic from a parallel runway uses the standard left circuit, or when the airport procedure chart specifies right-hand turns for a particular runway. In Brazil, several smaller airports have right-hand pattern requirements for specific runways published in the approach plate notes.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Not confirming pattern direction before entering, assuming left-hand always applies.  
  ✔ At airports where both directions are possible, confirm with ATC or ATIS before entering.

- ❌ Reporting "entrando no downwind" instead of standard phraseology.  
  ✔ Use: ANAC123, downwind runway two seven, left-hand pattern.

- ❌ Turning to the right in the pattern after receiving a left-hand pattern instruction.  
  ✔ Left-hand means all turns are to the left. Confirm mentally before each turn.

- ❌ Not reporting position legs when ATC requests.  
  ✔ Report downwind, base, and final when instructed: ANAC123, downwind runway two seven.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Downwind Runway Two Seven, Left-Hand Pattern

**Pronunciation:** AY-NAK wun too tree, DOWN-wind RUN-way too SEV-un, LEFT-hand PAT-urn

**Word Stress**

- Downwind → DOWN-wind
- Runway → RUN-way
- Two Seven → too SEV-un
- Left-Hand → LEFT-hand
- Pattern → PAT-urn

Practice:

ANAC123... downwind... runway two seven... left-hand pattern...

Together:

ANAC123, downwind runway two seven, left-hand pattern.

Say "left-hand" with a brief pause — it is the key safety element.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Joinville Tower transmits: ANAC123, enter left-hand pattern runway two seven, report downwind, number two, traffic is a Cessna on base.

You are three miles east of the aerodrome at one thousand five hundred feet.

**Your first task**

Read back the clearance and state what you do to enter the pattern correctly.

**Your second task**

When you reach the downwind leg, describe what you transmit to ATC and what you look for.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Readback: ANAC123, left-hand pattern runway two seven, will report downwind, number two, traffic copied. Entry: I descend to pattern altitude approximately one thousand feet AGL and maneuver to enter the downwind leg on the left side of runway two seven — that means north of the runway with the runway to my left. I look for the Cessna on base to ensure spacing. On downwind, I transmit: Joinville Tower, ANAC123, downwind runway two seven. I look for the landing runway threshold to my left, confirm gear down and pre-landing checks complete, and watch for ATC to sequence me after the Cessna.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Left-Hand Pattern is the most common default in aviation.

Know it deeply so you can spot the exception.

When ATC says left-hand — every turn is left. Runway on your left. Captain's side.

When terrain, noise, or traffic requires a right-hand pattern — ATC will say so explicitly.

The H130 is agile in the circuit. Use that agility wisely.

Maintain spacing. Report your position. Fly precise legs.

A well-flown traffic pattern is a communication of professionalism before you even touch down.
