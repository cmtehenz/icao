# Hypothetical Scenarios

**Catalog ID:** 0508  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Hypothetical Scenarios — the ICAO oral exam task in which you explain what you would do in an imagined situation, using conditional language at Level 4 or above.

Hypotheticals are not fantasy. They are structured "what if" reasoning for aviation decisions.

In Part 1, the examiner asks questions with if/would — engine failure after takeoff, weather below minima, passenger medical event. In Part 2, the same skill appears when ATC asks intentions or when you must state what you would request in a developing situation.

For H130 ICAO Delta crews, hypothetical English connects training scenarios to exam performance and live ATC intention calls.

Understanding how to handle hypothetical scenarios in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 and Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Hypothetical Scenarios is the ICAO speaking task type in which the candidate responds to imagined aviation situations using conditional language — explaining likely actions, priorities, and communications — so that grammatical range, problem-solving discourse, and fluency can be assessed at Operational Level 4 or higher.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When answering a hypothetical scenario, the candidate should:

- mark the condition clearly: If X happened, I would...
- prioritize safety actions before secondary details
- include what you would say to ATC when relevant
- keep one main plan, not five conflicting plans
- use would / could / might appropriately
- for H130: adapt actions to helicopter capabilities — autorotation, confined landing, hover
- accept examiner variations: "And if that failed?"

A Level 4 hypothetical answer usually includes:

- condition restated briefly
- immediate action
- follow-on plan
- communication with ATC or crew
- optional contingency if the first plan fails

**Important:** Do not refuse the hypothetical ("That would never happen"). Engage it. Examiners test language and reasoning, not whether you like the scenario.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Hypothetical prompts help examiners and controllers to:

- elicit conditional grammar naturally
- test prioritization under imagined pressure
- prepare candidates for intention calls when plans change
- extend Part 1 discussion and Part 2 role-play branches
- assess recovery language when the first answer is challenged

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Examiner: What would you do if you had an engine chip warning in cruise in the H130?

You answer: If I had a chip warning in cruise, I would check engine parameters and follow the checklist. I would land as soon as practical at a suitable site. I would advise ATC of the problem and request priority if needed. If parameters were abnormal, I would treat it as more urgent and land immediately.

The examiner adds: And if no suitable airport were nearby? You adapt: I would select the best available landing area for a helicopter and keep ATC informed.

Conditional. Prioritized. Communicated.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, advise intentions if weather continues to deteriorate.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say what you would request if the approach becomes unstable.
3. Examiner: ANAC123, what would you do if you lost communications in IMC?
4. Tower: ANAC123, if unable to land, advise missed approach intentions.
5. Radar: ANAC123, confirm alternate if destination closes.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Hypotheticals run on if/would. Use them deliberately.

"If the engine failed after takeoff, I would enter autorotation and land ahead" is clearer than a vague "maybe land somewhere."

Prioritize: aviate, navigate, communicate — even in exam English. Examiners listen for that order.

Part 2 compression: turn the long hypothetical into an intentions call. "If weather deteriorates, we divert" becomes a clean radio intention when ATC asks.

Do not over-condition with ten ifs. One main condition, one main plan, one backup. That is enough for Level 4.

H130 gives you rich hypotheticals — engine failure, cable strike risk, offshore deck wave height. Use realistic helicopter actions; avoid jet-only answers when the prompt is rotorcraft.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do if destination weather went below minima while you still had fuel for one hold and a divert?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If destination weather went below minima, I would not continue the approach below minima. I would request a hold only if improvement was expected soon and fuel clearly allowed it. Otherwise I would divert to the alternate. I would tell ATC my intentions clearly and keep the crew informed. Safety margins come before forcing a landing at destination.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Answering in present tense only with no conditional.  
  ✔ Use if + would for imagined situations.

- ❌ Refusing the scenario as unrealistic.  
  ✔ Engage the hypothetical and show your plan.

- ❌ Listing actions with no priority order.  
  ✔ Fly the aircraft first, then navigate, then communicate.

- ❌ Forgetting the ATC communication piece.  
  ✔ Include what you would request or report.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** If That Happened I Would Divert

**Pronunciation:** if THAT HAP-und eye wood dih-VERT

**Word Stress**

- Happened → HAP-und
- Would → wood
- Divert → dih-VERT
- Immediately → ih-MEE-dee-it-lee

Practice:

If that happened... I would divert.

Together:

If that happened, I would divert immediately and advise ATC.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**WHAT IF** — **W**ord the condition with if, **H**ighlight first safety action, **A**dd the follow-on plan, **T**ell ATC intentions, **I**nsert one backup, **F**orbear refusing the scenario.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Hypothetical: After takeoff in the H130 you have a passenger medical emergency and destination is twenty minutes away; a closer airport is eight minutes away.

**Your first task**

Answer with if/would, priorities, and ATC communication.

**Your second task**

Give the actual Part 2 call you would make.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: If a passenger had a medical emergency after takeoff, I would have the crew assist the passenger, assess severity, and divert to the closer airport if urgency required it. I would advise ATC and request priority landing and medical services. Second task: ANAC123, medical emergency on board, requesting priority landing and ambulance, diverting to nearest suitable. Hypothetical reasoning becomes a real call.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Hypothetical scenarios train conditional professional English.

If X, I would Y — with priority and a radio plan.

Engage the scenario. Keep one main plan and one backup. Compress for Part 2 when ATC asks intentions.

You already train "what if" in the H130. Say it in exam English until if/would feels automatic.

That is how hypotheticals become a High-value skill across Part 1 and Part 2.
