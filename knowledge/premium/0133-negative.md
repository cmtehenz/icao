# Negative

**Catalog ID:** 0133  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Negative — the ICAO-standard word for no in aviation radio communication.

In ICAO standard phraseology, the word "no" is never used on the radio. Negative replaces it.

Negative is clear, unambiguous, and internationally standardized. It cannot be confused with other words in a noisy radio environment the way "no" might be.

In the H130, operating into São Paulo, Curitiba, or Congonhas, you will regularly need to decline requests, correct misunderstandings, and refuse clearances that are not appropriate. Negative is the word for all of those situations — and using it confidently and correctly is a mark of ICAO Level 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Negative is the ICAO-standard word meaning no. It is used to provide a negative answer to a question, to correct an incorrect readback by ATC, to refuse an instruction you cannot or should not accept, or to deny a request. It replaces the word "no" in all aviation radio communications.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Palavra ICAO padrão que significa "não." É usada para fornecer uma resposta negativa a uma pergunta, corrigir um readback incorreto do ATC, recusar uma instrução que não se pode ou não se deve aceitar, ou negar uma solicitação. Substitui a palavra "no" (não) em todas as comunicações de rádio aeronáutico.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When to use Negative:

- to answer no to a direct question from ATC
- to correct an incorrect readback from ATC
- to decline a clearance or request you cannot accept
- to indicate a condition is not as stated
- to deny a traffic-in-sight query when you do not have the traffic

When NOT to use Negative:

- to refuse a valid clearance without cause — use Unable with reason
- as a general filler word
- to express displeasure with ATC instructions

Negative operations commonly include:

- negative ATIS — when you have not received the information
- negative traffic in sight — when you cannot see reported traffic
- negative, request different runway — when the assigned runway is unsuitable
- correcting an incorrect readback: Negative, altitude is four thousand, not three thousand

**Important:** Negative is not rude. It is accurate. When ATC reads back an incorrect altitude or clearance, saying Negative with the correction is a critical safety action. An uncorrected error in a readback can lead to altitude deviations or runway conflicts.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers use Negative to:

- correct a pilot readback that contains an error
- decline a pilot request that cannot be accommodated
- clarify that a condition stated by the pilot is incorrect
- deny permission for an action not yet cleared
- close a safety loop when the pilot's information is wrong

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are approaching Congonhas in your H130.

Approach asks:

ANAC123, traffic in your two o'clock, five miles — do you have it in sight?

You scan but cannot see the traffic:

Negative, ANAC123, traffic not in sight.

Approach issues separation instructions instead of a visual approach clearance.

Later, you read back your cleared altitude:

Descend altitude three thousand, ANAC123.

Approach corrects:

Negative, ANAC123 — altitude four thousand, not three thousand. Say again altitude four thousand.

You correct:

Descend altitude four thousand, ANAC123.

In both examples, Negative delivered critical safety information directly and clearly. If you had said "Yes, traffic in sight" when you did not see it, Approach might have cleared you for a visual approach into unconfirmed traffic. If Approach had not corrected your altitude misread, you would have descended to the wrong level.

Negative is a safety word.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, negative, altitude is five thousand, not four thousand.
2. Tower: ANAC123, negative, runway two eight — not runway three three.
3. Approach: ANAC123, negative, not cleared for approach yet, stand by.
4. Ground: ANAC123, negative, hold short of taxiway delta, not cleared to cross.
5. Approach: ANAC123, negative on the direct routing, fly radar vectors.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Negative, ANAC123 — I do not have the traffic in sight.
2. ANAC123, negative, ATIS information not received.
3. Negative, ANAC123 — unable to accept runway three three, crosswind exceeds limits.
4. ANAC123, correcting — altitude four thousand, not three thousand.
5. Negative ATIS, request information, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Negative is the most underused safety word in Brazilian aviation.

The cultural tendency is to avoid saying no — to try to find a way to say yes, to accept, to comply.

In operational communication, however, Negative is essential.

Negative traffic in sight prevents a controller from issuing a visual approach clearance into unseen traffic.

Negative, altitude four thousand corrects a readback error before the aircraft descends to the wrong level.

Negative, unable that runway prevents an approach on a runway beyond crosswind limits.

Each of these is a safety intervention.

In the H130, single-pilot, you are responsible for catching errors — including errors in ATC's readback of your request, or errors in your own readback of ATC's clearance.

When you hear something wrong — say Negative and state the correction immediately.

Do not let an error stand to avoid awkwardness.

Negative is professional. It is precise. It is safe.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How should a pilot respond if ATC reads back the wrong altitude after issuing a clearance?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately transmit: Negative, [correct altitude], ANAC123. Then wait for ATC to confirm the correction. For example, if ATC cleared me to altitude four thousand but read back three thousand in my reply, I would say: Negative, altitude four thousand, not three thousand, ANAC123. It is critical to correct readback errors immediately, before either the pilot or the controller acts on the wrong value. Uncorrected altitude errors are a leading cause of altitude deviations.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "não" or "no" on English-language frequencies instead of Negative.  
  ✔ Use Negative — always. "No" is not ICAO standard radio phraseology.

- ❌ Staying silent when ATC reads back an incorrect value, hoping it will work out.  
  ✔ Correct immediately: Negative, [correct value], ANAC123.

- ❌ Using Negative to refuse a valid clearance without providing a reason.  
  ✔ If you are declining due to a genuine limitation, use Unable with a reason.

- ❌ Confusing Negative with Unable — using Negative when you mean you cannot comply.  
  ✔ Negative answers a yes/no question. Unable addresses inability to comply with an instruction.

**NEGATIVE = No Error Passes** — When ATC or you state something wrong, **N**egative is the **E**xact word to **G**ive a correction, **A**lways **T**ransmit it **I**mmediately, **V**erify the corrected value, **E**nsure acknowledgement.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Curitiba Approach transmits your readback correction: "ANAC123, confirm altitude five thousand, QNH one zero one two?"

You were cleared to altitude four thousand and QNH one zero one four.

**Your first task**

Both values in ATC's query are wrong. What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

After your correction, Approach confirms: "ANAC123, affirm, altitude four thousand, QNH one zero one four, corrected." How do you respond?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would transmit: Negative, ANAC123 — altitude four thousand, QNH one zero one four. Both values need to be corrected clearly and immediately. ATC is verifying my readback and has wrong values — if I confirmed them, I would be accepting incorrect altimetry and incorrect altitude. After ATC corrects: I would respond Roger, ANAC123 — confirming I received the correction and now have the correct values set.
