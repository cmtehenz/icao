# Structure

**Catalog ID:** 0497  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Structure — the ICAO language skill that evaluates how well you control basic and complex grammatical patterns so that meaning stays clear in operational speaking and radiotelephony plain language.

Structure is the skeleton of your message. Weak structure forces the listener to rebuild your meaning. Strong structure lets ATC and examiners follow you on the first pass.

For H130 ICAO Delta Part 1 answers, PEEL organization plus clean grammar turns knowledge into Level 4 performance.

Understanding structure as an ICAO skill is essential for Part 1 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Structure is the ICAO language proficiency skill that assesses control of grammatical forms and sentence patterns — from simple clauses to more complex constructions — so that operational meaning remains clear, coherent, and unambiguous in speech.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Estrutura / Structure.

Habilidade de proficiência linguística da OACI que avalia o controle de formas gramaticais e padrões de frase — de orações simples a construções mais complexas — para que o significado operacional permaneça claro, coerente e inequívoco na fala.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When demonstrating structure, the pilot should:

- prefer clear short sentences under high workload
- keep subject–verb–object order in plain language
- use PEEL for Part 1 extended answers
- for H130: describe sequences with clear time markers (first, then, after)
- avoid Portuguese-influenced fragments that hide the verb
- repair a broken sentence instead of stacking more clauses

Structure in practice commonly includes:

- simple present / past for procedures and events
- conditionals for contingencies (if... then...)
- connectors: because, therefore, however, then
- question forms for clarification
- standard phraseology word order on frequency

**Important:** Level 4 allows limited errors if meaning remains clear. Creative but confusing grammar is worse than simple correct grammar.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers need structured plain language to:

- understand abnormal reports without decoding word salad
- follow intentions: what you will do, when, and where
- catch conditionals that matter — if weather worsens, we divert
- reduce say-again loops caused by incomplete sentences
- coordinate helicopter requests that include limits and reasons

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

An examiner asks what you would do if weather drops below minima on an H130 approach to Santos.

An unstructured answer jumps between fuel, passengers, and feelings. A structured answer uses PEEL: Point — I would discontinue and divert. Explain — because minima are not available and fuel allows Congonhas. Example — I would request diversion routing. Link — that keeps the operation inside company and ICAO safety standards.

On frequency the same structure becomes: unable to continue approach, diverting Congonhas, request routing.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, advise intentions if weather deteriorates.
2. Control: ANAC123, confirm diversion destination.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say again reason for diversion.
4. Tower: ANAC123, report when ready for second approach.
5. Control: ANAC123, request fuel remaining and alternate.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Structure is how professionals think out loud.

PEEL is not a school trick. It is how you keep a long answer from collapsing.

On frequency, structure is shorter: intention + reason if needed + request. Do not narrate your entire mental model to ATC.

Brazilian speakers often front the topic and delay the verb. In English aviation speech, put the action early: diverting, requesting, unable, going around.

In the H130, sequence language matters for emergencies: first enter autorotation, then mayday, then landing area. Structure saves order when stress steals memory.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Why is structure important in ICAO Aviation English, and how can you show it in a Part 1 answer?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Structure keeps meaning clear by controlling grammar and sentence order. In a Part 1 answer I use a clear pattern such as PEEL — point, explain, example, link — with simple accurate sentences and connectors. Even if I make small errors, the listener can follow my decision and my reasons without confusion.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Long Portuguese-style sentences with the main verb at the end.  
  ✔ Put the action early: I would divert because...

- ❌ Answering without a plan — jumping between ideas.  
  ✔ Use PEEL or another fixed spine every time.

- ❌ Overusing complex grammar that collapses under stress.  
  ✔ Prefer simple clear sentences when workload rises.

- ❌ Omitting subjects or verbs in plain language.  
  ✔ Complete the clause: We are diverting to Congonhas.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Weather at Santos falls below minima. Approach asks you to advise intentions if you cannot land.

**Your first task**

Give a structured radio call.

**Your second task**

Give a four-part PEEL answer to: “What would you do if destination weather goes below minima?”

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, weather below minima, diverting Congonhas, request routing. Second task: Point — I would divert to the alternate. Explain — because landing below minima is not acceptable and fuel is sufficient. Example — I would request diversion routing and advise fuel. Link — structured communication keeps the decision clear for ATC and for the crew.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Structure turns thoughts into messages other people can use.

PEEL in the oral test. Intention-plus-reason on the radio. Action early in the sentence.

Simple and clear beats complex and collapsed.

In the H130, structured speech keeps emergency sequences in order. Build the skeleton before you need it in turbulence.
