# Pinnacle Landing

**Catalog ID:** 0071  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers pinnacle landing — landing on a peak or elevated point with dropping terrain on at least one side.

Pinnacle Landing requires special technique because downwash, wind, and limited escape routes create unique hazards.

In the H130, pinnacle operations are common in Brazilian mountain rescue, offshore platform transfers, and remote site operations.

You must assess wind, vortex behavior, and departure path before landing, then inform ATC.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 operational communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Pinnacle Landing is a helicopter landing on a peak or elevated area with significant terrain drop-off, requiring specialized approach and departure techniques.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pouso em pináculo.

Pouso de helicóptero no topo de elevação com declive acentuado em pelo menos um lado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting a Pinnacle Landing, the pilot should:

- assess wind direction and speed at the pinnacle — wind may differ from reported
- approach into the wind when possible with escape route over the drop-off
- use caution with downwash over steep terrain below the landing point
- inform ATC of landing intentions and position
- plan departure with power margin — pinnacle takeoffs are often power-limited

Pinnacle Landing reporting commonly includes:

- landing pinnacle or landing mountain top site
- position and elevation if known
- request traffic advisory
- report go-around if wind or power is insufficient

**Important:** Pinnacle departures often require maximum performance. Assess power available before committing to land.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Pinnacle Landing reports to:

- provide traffic advisories in the area
- coordinate with search and rescue if applicable
- maintain awareness of non-standard landing sites
- request position and altitude reports
- prepare for possible go-around or delay reports

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are supporting a rescue operation on a mountain ridge near Curitiba in your H130.

The landing point is a narrow ridge with steep drop-off on the east side.

You orbit to assess wind — it is from the west, favoring an approach from the east over the drop-off.

You call:

Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, landing pinnacle rescue site two zero miles west of Curitiba, elevation four thousand five hundred feet, request traffic advisory.

You land on the ridge and report safe on the ground.

After the rescue, you assess power for departure into the wind over the drop-off.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger pinnacle landing, no traffic reported, report safe on the ground.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say wind at the site if able.
3. Tower: ANAC123, report go-around if unable to land.
4. Approach: ANAC123, traffic helicopter five miles east, remain clear of your area.
5. Approach: ANAC123, report departure when clear of the pinnacle.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Landing pinnacle rescue site two zero miles west, elevation four five hundred feet, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, landing pinnacle, will report safe on the ground.
3. ANAC123, unable pinnacle landing due wind, going around.
4. Tower, ANAC123, safe on the ground pinnacle landing complete.
5. ANAC123, departing pinnacle into the wind, will report clear of the ridge.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Pinnacle landing is not a normal helipad.

The ground falls away.

Downwash goes down the mountain — not into open air.

Wind on the ridge can be violent and different from the valley report.

The sequence:

Wind assessment. Approach into wind. Escape over the drop. Power check for departure.

Never land on a pinnacle without knowing how you will depart.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "pouso no topo" on frequency.

Use landing pinnacle — ICAO standard phraseology.

In the H130, calculate power required for departure at pinnacle elevation before you touch down.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What are the main hazards of pinnacle landing and what would you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Main hazards include unpredictable wind on the ridge, downwash over steep terrain, limited escape routes, and power limitations on departure. I would inform ATC of landing pinnacle, state position and elevation, request traffic advisory, assess wind on site, and report safe on the ground or go-around if conditions are unsafe.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using valley wind report without assessing ridge wind.  
  ✔ Orbit and assess actual wind at the pinnacle before landing.

- ❌ Saying "pouso em montanha" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use landing pinnacle.

- ❌ Landing without verifying departure power margin.  
  ✔ Calculate power required for departure at elevation before landing.

- ❌ Approaching with tailwind over the drop-off.  
  ✔ Approach into wind when possible with escape route planned.

You must land on a ridge at four thousand feet near Florianópolis for a medical evacuation.

**Your second task**

What do you transmit to Approach before the approach?

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Pinnacle landing is mountain flying discipline.

Assess wind on the ridge.

Plan departure power before you land.

Escape over the drop.

Tell ATC where you are.

Go around if power or wind is wrong.

The pinnacle does not forgive poor planning.

Train for departure before you land. Speak clearly. Fly with margin.
