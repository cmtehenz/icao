# Engine Fire

**Catalog ID:** 0301  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Engine Fire — fire warning or confirmed fire associated with an engine, requiring immediate memory items and checklist actions, possible engine shutdown and fire extinguishing, and unambiguous Mayday communication to ATC. Highly relevant to H130 operations: engine fire is a Critical single-engine emergency for a helicopter, and ICAO English must state the fire, the engine, and the intention to land immediately.

Engine fire is Critical because seconds matter for checklist discipline and for getting emergency services moving.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master ICAO phraseology for engine fire, then apply it directly to H130 turbine fire emergencies under DECEA operations.

Understanding engine fire in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Engine Fire is a fire warning or confirmed fire involving an aircraft engine, requiring immediate crew actions per the engine fire checklist — typically thrust idle or shutdown, fuel shutoff, and fire agent discharge as published — and prompt Mayday notification to ATC of the nature of the emergency and intentions for immediate landing.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Fogo no motor / Engine Fire.

Alerta de fogo ou fogo confirmado envolvendo um motor da aeronave, exigindo ações imediatas da tripulação conforme o checklist de fogo no motor — tipicamente redução ou corte de potência, fechamento de combustível e descarga do agente extintor conforme publicado — e notificação Mayday rápida ao ATC sobre a natureza da emergência e as intenções de pouso imediato.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an engine fire occurs, the crew should:

- fly the aircraft — control attitude, rotor/airspeed, and trajectory first
- accomplish engine fire memory items and checklist — shut down affected engine, discharge agent as published
- declare Mayday immediately
- report engine fire, which engine if applicable, and request immediate landing with emergency services
- plan forced landing or autorotation profile if power is lost on the H130
- advise ATC of smoke, shutdown complete, and evacuation intentions after landing if needed

Engine fire reporting commonly includes:

- engine fire / engine fire number one / number two
- engine shut down / fire agent discharged
- request immediate priority landing / emergency services
- souls and fuel when asked
- advise if continuing smoke or evacuating on the runway/helipad

**Important:** On the ICAO speaking test and in H130 line flying, "engine fire" is Mayday language. Do not soften it. Identify the engine if the airframe has more than one; on the H130, state engine fire and land immediately.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to engine fire reports to:

- provide immediate priority landing clearance
- alert fire and rescue services without delay
- keep traffic clear of an aircraft with fire and possible power loss
- prepare for runway or helipad blockage and evacuation
- coordinate the shortest safe path to land

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Operational scenario: climbing out of Congonhas, engine fire warning. Memory items complete — engine secured, agent discharged.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Departure, ANAC123, engine fire, returning immediately, request priority landing and emergency services.

If fire indications persist:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, engine fire, fire continuing, request immediate landing, emergency services.

For H130 reality, the same call applies with single-engine urgency — protect the landing site, keep the Mayday short, and fly the profile.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Departure: ANAC123, roger Mayday engine fire, turn left heading two five zero, descend three thousand, nearest airport approved.
2. Approach: ANAC123, priority landing runway one seven, emergency services rolling.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
4. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land runway one seven, wind calm, equipment standing by.
5. Tower: ANAC123, after landing vacate if able, advise if evacuating.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Engine fire is the call that must be clean under stress.

Fly first. Memory items second. Mayday third — but do not wait for a perfect checklist before the first Mayday if you can transmit while securing the engine.

Say "engine fire," not "possible fire warning maybe." If you are running the engine fire checklist, ATC needs engine fire.

In the H130, one engine is the whole story. Power loss and fire share the same landing urgency. Keep the radio short so your hands stay on the flying.

Brazilian examiners listen for Mayday, the malfunction name, and a clear landing intention. Deliver all three.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You have an engine fire during climb. What do you do, and what do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control of the aircraft, apply the engine fire memory items and checklist — shut down the affected engine and discharge the fire agent as published — and declare Mayday. I would report engine fire, state that I am returning or landing immediately, and request priority landing with emergency services. I would provide souls and fuel when asked and advise if smoke continues or evacuation is required. On a helicopter, I would use the same Mayday structure with immediate landing urgency for a single-engine fire.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using Pan Pan for a confirmed engine fire.  
  ✔ Engine fire is Mayday.

- ❌ Saying "problem in the engine" instead of "engine fire."  
  ✔ Use the standard term so rescue response matches the threat.

- ❌ Long radio narratives while the fire checklist is incomplete.  
  ✔ Short Mayday first; details when flying allows.

- ❌ Forgetting to request emergency services.  
  ✔ Ask for fire and rescue in the first or second transmission.
