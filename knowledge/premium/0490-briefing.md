# Briefing

**Catalog ID:** 0490  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Briefing — the structured pre-task communication that aligns the crew on plan, threats, roles, and contingencies before a takeoff, approach, or special H130 operation.

A briefing is not a speech. It is a shared mental model built in time to be useful. Too long and it is ignored; too short and it leaves traps unmentioned.

For ICAO Delta training, briefings support Part 1 structured speaking and Part 2 readiness — because a crew that briefed well transmits clearer intentions to ATC.

Understanding briefing in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 and Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Briefing is a concise, structured crew communication conducted before a critical phase of flight to establish the plan, identify threats, assign roles, define callouts and contingencies, and confirm shared understanding before execution begins.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Briefing / Briefing operacional.

Comunicação concisa e estruturada da tripulação realizada antes de uma fase crítica do voo para estabelecer o plano, identificar ameaças, atribuir funções, definir callouts e contingências, e confirmar o entendimento compartilhado antes da execução.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When conducting a briefing, the crew should:

- cover plan, threats, roles, and go-around or escape
- keep it short enough to be remembered under stress
- invite questions and challenges at the end
- for H130: brief pad, wires, wind, and escape heading for confined areas
- update the briefing when ATC changes the plan
- confirm who flies and who talks before the approach

Effective briefings commonly include:

- runway / pad and procedure
- minima and missed approach
- threats (weather, traffic, terrain, wires)
- PF / PM duties
- any special ATC expectation

**Important:** If ATC issues a last-minute runway or approach change, re-brief the differences. An old briefing is a hazard.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers indirectly rely on crew briefings because:

- aircraft that briefed well accept or refuse clearances decisively
- missed-approach and diversion intentions arrive coherent
- helicopter special ops are announced with clear limits
- frequency time is saved when the crew already knows the plan
- safety rises when crews update after ATC changes

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Congonhas in the H130. Before top of descent you brief: ILS runway two seven Left, minima, missed approach climb, threats of traffic and late runway change, you fly and the other pilot talks.

Approach later changes you to runway two seven Right. You do not “wing it.” You re-brief the differences: new localizer, same missed approach concept, confirm roles again. Then you call ready for the new approach with a clean mental model.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, expect ILS runway two seven Left.
2. Approach: ANAC123, change of plan, expect ILS runway two seven Right.
3. Tower: ANAC123, report ready for departure, advise briefing complete if required by company.
4. Approach: ANAC123, advise when ready for approach.
5. Control: ANAC123, expect holding, advise when approach briefing updated.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Briefing is CRM before the workload peaks.

Use a structure every time: plan, threats, roles, contingencies. PEEL works in Part 1 answers; a similar spine works in the cockpit.

Do not brief the entire AFM. Brief what can kill you on this approach — wires, tailwind, late clearances, unstable criteria.

In the H130, the confined-area briefing is sacred: wind, obstacles, escape, decision point. If you cannot brief the escape, you are not ready to descend into the site.

When ATC changes the plan, the professional response is a short delta brief — what changed — then fly.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What should an approach briefing include before an H130 ILS into a busy Brazilian airport?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The briefing should include the procedure and runway, minima and missed approach, main threats such as traffic and possible late changes, and clear PF and PM roles including who handles the radio. It should end with contingencies — go-around triggers — and a chance for the other crew member to challenge or ask questions.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reading the chart aloud without stating threats or roles.  
  ✔ Brief the plan and the traps; assign who does what.

- ❌ Skipping a re-brief after a runway change.  
  ✔ Always brief the differences before continuing.

- ❌ Making the briefing so long that nobody remembers it.  
  ✔ Keep it concise and operational.

- ❌ Treating “any questions?” as a formality with no pause.  
  ✔ Stop and wait — invite a real challenge.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Approach changes your expected runway from two seven Left to two seven Right ten miles from Congonhas.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

What essential points do you re-brief before calling ready for approach?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, change copied, expecting ILS runway two seven Right. Second task: Re-brief the new procedure identity, minima if different, missed approach, updated threats, and confirm PF/PM and radio roles — then call ready. A runway change without a delta brief is an invitation to fly the old plan by mistake.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Briefing builds the shared model before the aircraft needs it.

Plan. Threats. Roles. Escape. Questions.

When ATC changes the clearance, change the briefing.

In the H130, a pad without an escape brief is not an approach — it is a gamble. Brief it. Then fly it.
