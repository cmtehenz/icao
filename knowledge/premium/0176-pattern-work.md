# Pattern Work

**Catalog ID:** 0176  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Pattern Work — the ATC authorization allowing an aircraft to perform repeated closed traffic circuits around an airport, typically for training, proficiency, or practice approaches.

Pattern Work is fundamental to helicopter and light aircraft operations at Brazilian aerodromes.

It is not a single approach. It is a series of circuits — takeoff, crosswind, downwind, base, final — repeated until ATC terminates the authorization or the pilot requests departure.

At busy controlled airports like Congonhas or Florianópolis, entering pattern work requires an explicit ATC clearance that specifies the runway, direction of traffic, and any altitude or radio constraints.

Understanding how to request, receive, and respond to pattern work instructions in ICAO-standard English is essential for H130 operations at Class D and Class C aerodromes.

## 📖 Meaning

Pattern Work is an ATC authorization permitting an aircraft to conduct successive closed traffic patterns at an airport, remaining in the local traffic circuit to perform repeated approaches, touch-and-go landings, or stop-and-go landings without departing the traffic area.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Trabalho de circuito / Tráfego de circuito.

Autorização do controle de tráfego aéreo para que uma aeronave execute circuitos fechados sucessivos em um aeródromo, permanecendo no tráfego local para realizar aproximações repetidas, pousos com decolagem imediata ou pousos completos sem sair da área de tráfego.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When approved for pattern work, the crew should:

- confirm the approved runway and direction of traffic (left or right pattern)
- maintain the published or assigned circuit altitude
- report each turn in the pattern as required by local ATC procedures
- request departure from the pattern when pattern work is complete
- comply with any altitude, speed, or routing restrictions issued by ATC
- monitor for conflicting traffic on the same runway

Pattern work commonly includes:

- touch-and-go landings for currency or training
- stop-and-go landings for short-field practice
- low approaches without landing for instrument currency
- full stop landings with immediate taxi back

**Important:** Pattern work approval does not grant unlimited operations. ATC may terminate pattern work at any time due to traffic. The crew must acknowledge termination and comply immediately.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Pattern Work approvals to:

- accommodate training flights at controlled airports without issuing a new clearance for each circuit
- coordinate circuit traffic with departures and arrivals on the same runway
- assign traffic direction to prevent conflicts between multiple aircraft in the pattern
- allow efficient training operations during periods of lower traffic density
- maintain separation between pattern traffic and IFR arrivals

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are operating your H130 at Navegantes, conducting recurrency training with your co-pilot.

You call Tower:

ANAC123, H130, at the ramp, request pattern work, runway one four, VFR.

Tower responds:

ANAC123, approved for pattern work, runway one four, left-hand circuit, maintain one thousand, report downwind.

You respond:

Approved for pattern work, runway one four, left-hand circuit, maintain one thousand, wilco, ANAC123.

You depart, fly the left-hand circuit, and call downwind each time.

After three circuits, you call:

ANAC123, request departure to the north, full stop at Joinville.

Tower responds:

ANAC123, pattern work terminated, cleared to Joinville, climb and maintain three thousand, report airborne.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, approved for pattern work, runway three two, left-hand circuit, maintain one thousand five hundred.
2. Tower: ANAC123, number two for the runway, pattern work approved, follow the Cessna on left base.
3. Tower: ANAC123, pattern work terminated, wind calm, cleared to land runway one four.
4. Tower: ANAC123, extend downwind, I will call your base, traffic on final.
5. Tower: ANAC123, go around, pattern work continues, maintain one thousand, report abeam.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Pattern Work sounds simple. It is not.

Each circuit is a new clearance event. You must manage your position, report your turns, and integrate with every other aircraft ATC is working.

In the H130, the pattern is tighter and faster than fixed-wing. You can turn final much closer to the threshold. That is an advantage — and a trap. Controllers at busier airports may not expect you to roll out on final so quickly.

Communicate your turns. Tell Tower when you are downwind, base, and final.

Never assume the pattern is yours until the runway is clear and ATC has cleared you to land.

When ATC extends your downwind — they are creating spacing. Do not rush the base turn. Wait for the instruction.

And when pattern work is terminated — acknowledge and comply. The training session ends on ATC's terms.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are conducting pattern work at a controlled aerodrome. ATC instructs you to extend your downwind. Describe what this means and how you respond.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Extending the downwind means continuing to fly the downwind leg beyond the normal base turn point to create additional spacing with other traffic. ATC wants more separation between my aircraft and another on final or departing. I would respond: Extending downwind, ANAC123 — and continue flying downwind until ATC calls my base turn. I would not turn base on my own initiative. I would wait for the clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Turning base without ATC instruction when told to extend downwind.  
  ✔ Extending downwind means you fly until ATC calls your base. Wait for the instruction.

- ❌ Not reporting circuit positions as instructed.  
  ✔ If ATC says "report downwind," call them each time you are abeam the runway.

- ❌ Requesting pattern work without stating aircraft type and intentions.  
  ✔ Include aircraft type and request: ANAC123, H130, request pattern work, runway one four.

- ❌ Assuming pattern work continues without re-clearance after going around.  
  ✔ Confirm with ATC: pattern work continues, ANAC123.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Approved for Pattern Work, Runway One Four, Left-Hand Circuit

**Pronunciation:** uh-PROOVD for PAT-urn WURK, RUN-way WUN FOR, left-HAND SUR-kit

**Word Stress**

- Approved → uh-PROOVD
- Pattern → PAT-urn (stress on first syllable)
- Work → WURK
- Left-Hand → left-HAND
- Circuit → SUR-kit (two syllables)

Practice:

Approved for pattern work... runway one four... left-hand circuit.

Together:

Approved for pattern work, runway one four, left-hand circuit, maintain one thousand, ANAC123.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**CIRCUIT** — **C**onfirm runway and direction, **I**ntegrate your turns with ATC calls, **R**eport position at each leg when required, **C**omply with extend or sequence instructions, **U**nderstand pattern work ends when ATC terminates, **I**mmediately acknowledge termination, **T**ransition to departure or full stop.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Navegantes Tower transmits: "ANAC123, approved for pattern work, runway one four, left-hand circuit, maintain one thousand, report downwind."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete clearance.

**Your second task**

On your third downwind call, Tower says: "ANAC123, extend downwind, I will call your base, traffic on short final." You continue. Five minutes pass. You are getting low on fuel and must stop. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Approved for pattern work, runway one four, left-hand circuit, maintain one thousand, wilco, ANAC123. If after five minutes I needed to stop due to fuel, I would advise Tower: ANAC123, fuel state requires full stop, request landing clearance. I would not turn base on my own initiative while still holding with traffic on short final. I would communicate my constraint to ATC and follow their instruction.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Pattern Work is organized repetition.

Every circuit, the same discipline: confirm runway, fly the legs, report your position, comply with ATC.

In the H130, you have flexibility that fixed-wing pilots do not. You can turn tighter, fly slower, land shorter.

Use that advantage — but always within ATC's structure.

When they extend you — wait. When they terminate — comply. When you cannot continue — say so.

Professional training is not just repetition. It is repetition inside a controlled environment, with clear communication every time.
