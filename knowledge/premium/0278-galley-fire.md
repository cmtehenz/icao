# Galley Fire

**Catalog ID:** 0278  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Galley Fire — fire in the aircraft galley or catering area, a classic airliner cabin emergency on ICAO exams. Crews fight the fire with extinguishers, protect passengers, declare distress, and land with priority while cabin crew manage smoke and evacuation readiness.

For H130 helicopter ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, there is no airline galley. Teach the exam English — galley fire, cabin fire, fire under control / not under control — and relate it to cabin or electrical fire in the helicopter cabin when useful. Same radio structure: location, severity, intentions, landing.

Master Mayday, galley fire, request immediate landing, and clear updates on whether the fire is extinguished.

## 📖 Meaning

Galley Fire is an active fire in the galley or catering service area of an aircraft, requiring immediate firefighting by crew, possible passenger relocation, declaration of distress or urgency as appropriate, and landing at the nearest suitable airport with fire services alerted.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Fogo na galley / Galley Fire.

Fogo ativo na área da galley ou de serviço de bordo da aeronave, exigindo combate imediato pela tripulação, possível realocação de passageiros, declaração de emergência conforme a gravidade, e pouso no aeroporto adequado mais próximo com serviços de combate a incêndio alertados.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a galley fire occurs, the crew should:

- fight the fire with appropriate extinguishers per cabin/AFM procedures
- isolate electrical power to the galley if the checklist requires it
- declare Mayday for active fire; update ATC when extinguished or if uncontrollable
- request immediate landing / diversion and fire services
- report location (galley), status (under control / not under control), souls and fuel
- for H130: use cabin fire / electrical fire wording for the actual type; keep galley fire for exam English

Galley fire reporting commonly includes:

- galley fire / fire in the galley
- fire under control / fire not under control
- request immediate landing
- smoke in cabin (if present)
- evacuate after landing if required

**Important:** Update ATC when the fire status changes. "Fire under control" is useful — but still land. Do not cancel the emergency solely because flames are out if smoke or heat remains a threat.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to galley fire reports to:

- provide priority vectors and descent for immediate landing
- alert airport fire and rescue for cabin fire risk
- request fire status updates (under control or not)
- coordinate runway and evacuation support
- obtain souls and fuel for emergency planning

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Training scenario (airliner exam English): cabin crew reports fire in the galley. Flight crew coordinates checklist and diversion.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, galley fire, request immediate diversion to nearest suitable airport.

Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday, turn right heading zero niner zero, descend, say if fire is under control, souls and fuel when able.

After extinguishing:

ANAC123, galley fire under control, smoke in cabin, continuing Mayday, request shortest approach, fire services standing by.

For H130 cabin fire training, keep Mayday, location, status, and landing request — swap "galley" for the real fire location.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday galley fire, turn right heading zero niner zero, descend.
2. Approach: ANAC123, say if the fire is under control.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
4. Tower: ANAC123, runway cleared, fire services standing by for cabin fire.
5. Tower: ANAC123, after landing advise if evacuation is required.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, galley fire, request immediate diversion.
2. ANAC123, turning right zero niner zero, descending, galley fire.
3. ANAC123, galley fire under control, smoke remaining, continuing Mayday.
4. ANAC123, souls one hundred eighty, fuel two hours.
5. ANAC123, will advise evacuation after landing, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Galley fire is a cabin fire with a precise location word.

Fight it. Isolate power if required. Mayday. Land. Update whether it is under control.

Brazilian pilots may say "fogo na cozinha" or "fogo na galley." Prefer galley fire in ICAO exam English. For the H130, say cabin fire or electrical fire when that is the truth — but still know galley fire for the test.

Never treat "fire under control" as permission to continue to destination. Land, assess, evacuate if smoke or heat remains dangerous.

Cabin crew fight; flight crew fly and communicate. Both jobs matter.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Cabin crew reports a fire in the galley. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would ensure the fire is fought per procedure, isolate galley power if required, declare Mayday stating galley fire, and request immediate diversion to the nearest suitable airport. I would update ATC when the fire is under control or if it becomes uncontrollable, report smoke if present, and provide souls and fuel. After landing I would decide on evacuation based on fire and smoke status.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "problem in the cabin" without galley/fire location.  
  ✔ State galley fire (or cabin fire) clearly.

- ❌ Cancelling Mayday the moment flames are out while smoke remains.  
  ✔ Update status; continue priority landing if still a threat.

- ❌ Not telling ATC whether the fire is under control.  
  ✔ Report under control or not under control when known.

- ❌ Claiming the H130 has an airline galley.  
  ✔ Exam vocabulary yes; type configuration factual.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**GALLY** — **G**et extinguishers on it, **A**dvise Mayday with location, **L**and nearest suitable, **L**et ATC know under control or not, **Y**ield to evacuation if smoke remains.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Cabin reports galley fire. You begin diversion. Flames are extinguished but smoke remains.

**Your first task**

What is your initial Mayday call?

**Your second task**

Approach asks if the fire is under control. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, galley fire, request immediate diversion to nearest suitable airport. Second task: ANAC123, galley fire under control, smoke in cabin remaining, continuing Mayday, request shortest approach and fire services. I would still plan for possible evacuation after landing because of residual smoke.

Galley fire is cabin fire with a precise name.

Fight. Declare. Divert. Update. Evacuate if needed.
