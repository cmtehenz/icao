# Fuel Transfer Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0297  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Fuel Transfer Failure — inability to move fuel between tanks as required by procedure, which can leave usable fuel trapped, create or worsen imbalance, and force a change of destination or early landing. Transfer-failure vocabulary appears in ICAO systems and speaking tests; for H130 crews, treat this as essential exam English and map it to any inability to use or move fuel as planned.

Fuel transfer failure is Medium priority because the aircraft may still fly normally while the fuel plan quietly becomes unsafe.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master ICAO phraseology for fuel transfer failure, then transfer the same reporting structure to helicopter fuel-system limitations under Brazilian ops.

Understanding fuel transfer failure in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Fuel Transfer Failure is the inability of the fuel system to transfer fuel between tanks as required, which may leave fuel unusable, create imbalance, or reduce endurance below the planned destination requirement, requiring checklist actions and clear notification to ATC of the malfunction, remaining usable fuel, and intentions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Incapacidade do sistema de combustível de transferir combustível entre tanques conforme necessário, o que pode deixar combustível inutilizável, criar desbalanceamento ou reduzir a autonomia abaixo do necessário para o destino planejado, exigindo ações de checklist e notificação clara ao ATC sobre a pane, o combustível utilizável restante e as intenções.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When fuel transfer fails, the crew should:

- confirm transfer valve, pump, and quantity indications per AFM/QRH
- stop repeated attempts that may worsen the situation if procedure says so
- recalculate usable fuel and destination suitability
- declare Pan Pan if destination is no longer assured or imbalance develops
- report fuel transfer failure, usable fuel, and request return or diversion
- for H130: apply the same structure when fuel cannot be used from a tank as planned

Fuel transfer failure reporting commonly includes:

- fuel transfer failure / unable to transfer fuel
- usable fuel limited / fuel trapped in tank
- request return / diversion / priority if fuel critical
- souls and fuel remaining when asked
- advise if imbalance is developing

**Important:** On the ICAO exam, distinguish transfer failure from a simple imbalance. Transfer failure is the system inability; imbalance may be the result. In H130 ops, say what you cannot do with the fuel system and what that means for endurance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to fuel transfer failure reports to:

- understand reduced usable fuel even if total quantity looks high
- offer shorter routing, return, or diversion
- prioritize landing if endurance becomes marginal
- avoid assuming engine failure when the issue is fuel system
- alert emergency services if a fuel-critical landing is expected

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: you attempt to transfer fuel to correct an imbalance. Transfer does not occur. Destination fuel is no longer assured.

You transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Control, ANAC123, fuel transfer failure, usable fuel limited, request diversion to nearest suitable.

If endurance becomes critical:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, fuel transfer failure, low usable fuel, request immediate priority landing.

For H130 reality, keep the same logic — trapped or unusable fuel changes the plan even when the engine is running normally.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan fuel transfer failure, say usable fuel and intentions.
2. Control: ANAC123, diversion approved, turn right heading zero niner zero, nearest airport one two miles.
3. Approach: ANAC123, priority handling approved, expect runway two five.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Tower: ANAC123, equipment standing by, advise if fuel critical.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fuel transfer failure is quiet until it is not. The gauges may still show fuel — but not fuel you can burn where you need it.

Recalculate usable fuel immediately. Do not protect the original destination out of habit.

Tell ATC "fuel transfer failure" and "usable fuel limited." That combination explains why you need a shorter plan without sounding like an engine emergency.

In the H130, your transfer architecture may be limited or different from a jet. The English still matters for ICAO — and the decision logic still matters for line flying: if you cannot use the fuel, you do not have the fuel.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Fuel transfer fails during cruise and usable fuel for destination is no longer assured. What do you do, and what do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would apply the fuel transfer failure checklist, stop ineffective transfer attempts as published, and recalculate usable fuel and range. I would declare Pan Pan, report fuel transfer failure with limited usable fuel, and request diversion or return to a suitable aerodrome. If usable fuel becomes critically low, I would declare Mayday and request immediate priority landing. I would provide souls and fuel when asked. On a helicopter, I would use the same structure whenever planned fuel cannot be used from a tank.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting only "fuel imbalance" when the root problem is transfer failure.  
  ✔ Say "fuel transfer failure" and mention imbalance only if it is also present.

- ❌ Continuing to destination because total fuel quantity still looks high.  
  ✔ Plan on usable fuel, not trapped fuel.

- ❌ Using vague "fuel system problem" without the standard term.  
  ✔ Prefer "fuel transfer failure" plus usable-fuel status.

- ❌ Skipping the topic because H130 transfer options are limited.  
  ✔ Learn exam English; transfer unusable-fuel logic to the helicopter.
