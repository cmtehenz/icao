# Passenger Passed Out

**Catalog ID:** 0327  
**Category:** Passenger  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Passenger Passed Out — the medical situation in which a passenger loses consciousness and the crew must manage the person, the flight, and ATC communication.

Passenger Passed Out is High priority because unconsciousness may be brief fainting or the first sign of a serious medical emergency. ICAO exams expect clear English: nature of the problem, assistance required, and whether priority landing is needed.

For H130 operations, there is often no cabin crew. The pilot — or the non-flying pilot in two-crew ops — may be the first to notice and must manage passengers while aviating. Teach the exam phraseology, then apply it to helicopter reality: secure the passenger, fly the aircraft, declare urgency if required.

Understanding how to communicate a passenger passed out in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Passenger Passed Out describes a passenger who has lost consciousness, requiring immediate assessment, possible first aid, and clear communication with ATC if medical assistance or priority handling is needed.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Passageiro desmaiado / Passenger Passed Out.

Situação em que um passageiro perde a consciência, exigindo avaliação imediata, possíveis primeiros socorros e comunicação clara com o controle se for necessária assistência médica ou tratamento prioritário.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a passenger passes out, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- assign a capable person to check responsiveness, breathing, and pulse if available
- provide first aid within training limits and use any medical kit on board
- decide whether the situation requires Pan Pan, priority landing, or diversion
- advise ATC of a medical problem on board and request assistance on arrival

Passenger Passed Out communication commonly includes:

- medical problem on board, passenger unconscious
- passenger passed out, requesting medical assistance on landing
- request priority landing if the condition is serious or not improving
- souls on board and fuel remaining when asked

**Important:** On the H130, you may have only a few passengers and no flight attendant. Do not leave the controls unattended in single-pilot flight. If alone, stabilize the aircraft first, then communicate. Brief passengers before flight on how to alert you. Unconsciousness that does not resolve quickly is treated as a medical emergency.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to passenger medical reports to:

- provide priority handling or diversion routing when requested
- alert airport medical services and emergency responders
- clear traffic for an expedited approach if required
- request souls on board and nature of the medical problem
- coordinate ambulance meeting points on the apron or helipad

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are en route along the coast toward Navegantes in the H130 with three passengers and no cabin crew.

A passenger slumps unconscious in the rear seat. Another passenger alerts you.

You maintain altitude and heading, ask the alert passenger to check breathing, and assess the situation.

You transmit:

Pan Pan Pan, Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, medical problem on board, passenger unconscious, request priority landing and medical assistance.

Approach sequences you number one and alerts medical services. You land, shut down safely, and hand the passenger to the ambulance.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say nature of medical problem and souls on board.
2. Approach: ANAC123, priority approach approved, medical services alerted.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway one eight cleared to land, ambulance standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm passenger still unconscious.
5. Tower: ANAC123, after landing expect medical team at the apron.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, passenger unconscious, request priority landing and medical assistance.
2. ANAC123, medical problem on board, passenger passed out, souls on board four.
3. ANAC123, requesting diversion to Navegantes, passenger unconscious.
4. ANAC123, passenger regained consciousness, still request medical assistance on arrival.
5. ANAC123, priority landing runway one eight, medical assistance required.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Passed out is plain English that ICAO examiners understand. Use it. Then add the operational request: medical assistance, priority landing, or diversion.

Severity decides the prefix. Brief fainting that resolves may need medical meet-and-assist without Pan Pan. Ongoing unconsciousness deserves urgency — Pan Pan at minimum, Mayday if life is clearly threatened and you need immediate priority.

In airline cabins, attendants manage the passenger while pilots fly. In the H130, that separation often does not exist. Aviate. Navigate. Communicate. Use a capable passenger as your hands if you must stay on the controls.

Never invent a diagnosis on frequency. Say passenger unconscious or passenger passed out. Let medical teams diagnose on the ground.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

A passenger passes out during cruise and does not wake up. What do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would declare urgency with Pan Pan, state that there is a medical problem on board with a passenger unconscious, and request priority landing and medical assistance. I would provide souls on board when asked and continue to fly the aircraft as the first priority, especially in a helicopter without cabin crew.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "passenger sick" with no request.  
  ✔ State the problem and request medical assistance or priority landing.

- ❌ Leaving the controls to treat the passenger in single-pilot flight.  
  ✔ Fly first; use another passenger for basic checks if possible.

- ❌ Diagnosing on the radio: "he had a stroke."  
  ✔ Say passenger unconscious unless medically qualified and certain.

- ❌ Forgetting to cancel or downgrade urgency if the passenger recovers fully.  
  ✔ Update ATC if the situation changes.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Pan Pan Pan, Passenger Unconscious, Request Priority Landing

**Pronunciation:** PAN PAN PAN, PASS-en-jer un-KON-shus, reh-KWEST pry-OR-ih-tee LAND-ing

**Word Stress**

- Passenger → PASS-en-jer
- Unconscious → un-KON-shus (stress on second)
- Priority → pry-OR-ih-tee
- Landing → LAND-ing

Practice:

Passenger unconscious... request priority landing.

Together:

Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, passenger unconscious, request priority landing and medical assistance.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**FAINT** — **F**ly the aircraft first, **A**ssess responsiveness via capable help, **I**nform ATC with clear medical language, **N**eed medical assistance stated, **T**ime to priority landing if unresolved.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

A passenger passes out at cruise. He is breathing but does not respond. You are twenty minutes from Navegantes in the H130 with no cabin crew.

**Your first task**

What is your first radio call?

**Your second task**

What do you do operationally before and after that call?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, medical problem on board, passenger unconscious, request priority landing and medical assistance. Second task: I keep control of the helicopter, ask another passenger to monitor breathing if available, prepare for an expedited approach, and update ATC if the passenger's condition changes.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Passenger Passed Out is a medical event wrapped in flight discipline.

On the H130, you may be pilot and cabin all at once. That does not change the order: aircraft first, then people, then radio.

Clear English. Clear request. Medical team waiting when you land.

That is professional Part 2 and Part 3 communication.
