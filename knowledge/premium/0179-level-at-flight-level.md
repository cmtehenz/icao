# Level at Flight Level

**Catalog ID:** 0179  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Level at Flight Level — the precise ICAO phraseology used when an aircraft has completed a climb or descent and is now established in level cruise at a specific flight level.

This phrase matters because it is not just a position report. It is a confirmation to ATC that the altitude change is complete and the aircraft is stable at the new level — which directly triggers separation decisions for adjacent traffic.

In Brazilian airspace, controllers rely on this readback to coordinate traffic efficiently, particularly in high-density corridors between São Paulo, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Congonhas.

Getting the phraseology right — and knowing exactly when to say it — is a key ICAO Level 4 competency.

## 📖 Meaning

Level at Flight Level is the pilot's position and status report confirming that the aircraft has completed a climb or descent and is currently established in level flight at the stated flight level, provided to ATC to confirm compliance with an altitude clearance and to enable updated separation with adjacent traffic.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Nivelado no nível de voo.

Relatório de posição e status do piloto confirmando que a aeronave completou uma subida ou descida e está atualmente em voo nivelado na altitude declarada, fornecido ao controle de tráfego aéreo para confirmar o cumprimento de uma autorização de altitude e permitir a atualização da separação com tráfego adjacente.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When reporting level at a flight level, the crew should:

- confirm the aircraft is fully stabilized in level flight before reporting
- use the correct ICAO format: callsign, level at flight level, number
- provide the report promptly after completing the altitude change
- include any ATC-requested additional information such as time or position
- avoid reporting level when still in climb or descent — even within the last few hundred feet

Level reports are used in contexts including:

- reaching an initial cruise altitude after departure
- completing an en-route climb or descent to a new cruise level
- arriving at an assigned altitude in a holding pattern
- confirming altitude after a block altitude maneuver
- position reports on airways without radar coverage

**Important:** "Level" means the altimeter is stable and the aircraft is no longer climbing or descending. It does not mean passing through. Do not report level until the altitude is confirmed. In radar-controlled airspace, ATC can see your altitude — but the verbal confirmation is still required. In non-radar airspace, it is the only confirmation ATC has.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers request and expect level reports to:

- confirm that a cleared altitude has been reached and stabilized
- update the separation picture for adjacent aircraft
- allow release of previously blocked altitudes to other traffic
- trigger coordination with adjacent sectors in non-radar airspace
- verify compliance with altitude instructions

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You depart Florianópolis in your H130, cleared to flight level nine zero.

After a long climb through departure terminal area and en-route structure, you reach flight level nine zero.

The altimeter stabilizes. Rate of climb is zero. You are level.

You call:

ANAC123, level flight level nine zero.

Radar acknowledges:

ANAC123, roger, maintain flight level nine zero, next report at TILO.

Later, Radar instructs:

ANAC123, descend and maintain flight level five five.

You begin the descent. You reach flight level five five and stabilize.

ANAC123, level flight level five five.

Radar:

ANAC123, roger, expect further descent in fifteen miles.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, report level at flight level nine zero.
2. Control: ANAC123, roger, maintain flight level nine zero, cleared direct Congonhas.
3. Radar: ANAC123, confirm level at flight level five five, traffic on your left at five zero.
4. Control: ANAC123, report level and position over TILO.
5. Radar: ANAC123, traffic at flight level nine five, confirm you are level flight level nine zero.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Level at Flight Level is a status declaration.

You are telling ATC: the climb is finished. The descent is finished. I am stable. You can plan around me.

That matters more than it sounds.

In Brazilian airspace, routes between São Paulo and Florianópolis carry significant traffic. Controllers plan separation based on who is climbing, who is level, and who is descending.

When you report level, a blocked altitude may be released. Another aircraft may be cleared to climb through where you just were.

Report too early — while still climbing — and you create a false picture. A controller who believes you are level at flight level nine zero will plan separation on that basis. If you are actually still at eight eight thousand, the gap is gone.

In the H130, the climb rate is lower than many jets. Be precise. Wait until you are truly level.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC requests you report when level at flight level nine zero. You are currently climbing through flight level eight seven. Describe what you do and say.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would continue the climb to flight level nine zero and wait until the altimeter is stable — fully level, not climbing — before making the report. Then I would call: ANAC123, level flight level nine zero. I would not report level at eight seven or at eight nine, even if I am close. The report is only made when the altitude is confirmed and stable. ATC needs accurate position data to manage separation.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting level before the aircraft has fully stabilized at the assigned altitude.  
  ✔ Wait for a stable altimeter reading before calling level.

- ❌ Omitting the level report entirely because radar is tracking the aircraft.  
  ✔ The verbal confirmation is still required even in radar-controlled airspace.

- ❌ Using "passing through" phraseology where "level at" is required.  
  ✔ "Passing" means in transit. "Level at" means established. Use the correct phrase.

- ❌ Not including the flight level number in the level report.  
  ✔ Always state the full report: ANAC123, level flight level nine zero — not just "level."

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Level Flight Level Nine Zero

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, LEV-ul FLYT LEV-ul NYN ZE-ro

**Word Stress**

- Level → LEV-ul (two syllables, stress on first)
- Flight Level → FLYT LEV-ul
- Nine Zero → NYN ZE-ro

Practice:

ANAC123... level... flight level nine zero.

Together:

ANAC123, level flight level nine zero.

Keep it short. Keep it precise. No additional words needed.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**LEVEL** — **L**ook at the altimeter, **E**nsure rate of climb/descent is zero, **V**erify you are stable at the cleared altitude, **E**nunciate the report clearly, **L**isten for ATC acknowledgement.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are climbing from Florianópolis to flight level nine zero. You pass through flight level eight eight. You are still climbing at two hundred feet per minute.

Radar calls: "ANAC123, report level flight level nine zero."

**Your first task**

What do you say right now — while still climbing?

**Your second task**

Thirty seconds later, you stabilize at flight level nine zero. What do you say now?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

While still climbing, I would not report level. If ATC is asking because they need an update, I might say: ANAC123, climbing through flight level eight eight, approaching flight level nine zero — to keep them informed without falsely declaring level. Once stable at flight level nine zero, I report: ANAC123, level flight level nine zero.
