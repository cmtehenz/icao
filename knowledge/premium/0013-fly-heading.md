# Fly Heading

**Catalog ID:** 0013  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson introduces one of the most common ATC instructions you'll hear while under radar control.

Unlike Maintain Runway Heading, which keeps you on the departure heading, Fly Heading instructs you to turn to a specific magnetic heading assigned by ATC.

This instruction is used every day to sequence traffic, avoid conflicts, and guide aircraft safely through controlled airspace.

## 📖 Meaning

Fly Heading instructs the pilot to turn to and maintain a specific magnetic heading assigned by Air Traffic Control.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Voe no rumo indicado.

O piloto deve ajustar a aeronave para o rumo magnético informado pelo controlador e mantê-lo até receber nova instrução.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ATC issues a Fly Heading instruction, the pilot must:

- turn to the assigned magnetic heading
- maintain that heading accurately
- continue complying with assigned altitude and speed restrictions
- remain on that heading until ATC issues another clearance

A Fly Heading instruction temporarily replaces the aircraft's planned lateral navigation.

Do not continue toward the GPS route unless instructed to Resume Own Navigation or given another clearance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Fly Heading to:

- provide radar vectors
- sequence arrivals
- separate aircraft
- avoid weather
- position aircraft for an instrument approach
- simplify traffic management
- resolve conflicts efficiently

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You're flying your H130 toward Curitiba Airport.

Approach is sequencing several arriving aircraft.

Instead of allowing you to continue directly to the next waypoint, the controller transmits:

Approach: ANAC123, fly heading zero niner zero.

You immediately turn to heading 090° and maintain it.

A few minutes later, the controller says:

Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading one three zero.

You comply with the new heading.

Finally, ATC instructs:

Approach: ANAC123, resume own navigation direct REKPA.

Only then do you return to your planned route.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, fly heading zero niner zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, fly heading one eight zero.
3. Approach: ANAC123, fly heading two four zero, vectors for the ILS.
4. Departure: ANAC123, fly heading three one zero.
5. Approach: ANAC123, fly heading zero six zero to avoid weather.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Heading zero niner zero, ANAC123.
2. Heading one eight zero, ANAC123.
3. Heading two four zero, vectors for the ILS, ANAC123.
4. Heading three one zero, ANAC123.
5. Heading zero six zero to avoid weather, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Imagine you're following your GPS on a road trip.

Suddenly, a police officer closes the highway and points you toward a detour.

For the next few kilometers, you stop following your GPS and simply follow the officer's directions.

That's exactly what happens when ATC says:

Fly heading zero niner zero.

For a short period, the controller becomes your navigator.

Your responsibility is not to question the route.

Your responsibility is to fly the assigned heading accurately until a new clearance is issued.

Professional pilots understand that precision matters.

Being five or ten degrees off the assigned heading may reduce the separation ATC is trying to create.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Why would ATC instruct an aircraft to fly a specific heading?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

ATC may assign a specific heading to provide radar vectors, separate aircraft, sequence traffic, avoid weather, or position the aircraft for an instrument approach. The pilot should maintain the assigned heading until receiving another clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Turning approximately to the assigned heading.  
  ✔ Fly the assigned heading accurately.

- ❌ Returning to the GPS route without authorization.  
  ✔ Continue on the assigned heading until instructed otherwise.

- ❌ Confusing Heading with Course.  
  ✔ Heading is where the aircraft's nose points.  
  ✔ Course is the intended path over the ground.

- ❌ Reading back only: Roger.  
  ✔ Correct: Heading zero niner zero, ANAC123.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Fly Heading

**Pronunciation:** FLY HED-ing

**Word Stress**

- Fly → strong and clear
- Heading → stress the first syllable

Practice:

Fly... Heading...

Now together:

Fly Heading.

Then practice inside a complete transmission:

Heading zero niner zero, ANAC123.

Speak each number clearly and at a steady pace.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

Imagine ATC is drawing a compass on your windshield.

Instead of saying "Go over there," the controller points to one exact direction.

Your job is simply to keep the nose of the aircraft pointing there until told otherwise.

That's what Fly Heading means.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Today's lesson builds directly on Radar Vectors and Resume Own Navigation.

Think of these three concepts as one sequence:

Fly Heading → Radar Vectors → Resume Own Navigation

The controller guides you for a period of time.

Then, when traffic and airspace permit, navigation is handed back to you.

Great pilots don't just fly accurately.

They know who is responsible for navigation at every stage of the flight.
