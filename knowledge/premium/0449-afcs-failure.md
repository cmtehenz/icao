# AFCS Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0449  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers AFCS Failure — loss of the Automatic Flight Control System, including autopilot, stability augmentation, or coupled modes that normally reduce pilot workload in the H130.

When AFCS fails, the aircraft remains flyable, but the pilot must assume full manual stabilization. Workload rises sharply in IMC, turbulence, or during instrument approaches.

For H130 helicopter operations, AFCS failure requires an immediate transition to manual flight, a reassessment of weather and approach capability, and clear communication with ATC if you need vectors, a simpler approach, or a diversion.

Understanding how to report AFCS failure in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

AFCS Failure is the loss of automatic flight control system functions — such as autopilot, attitude retention, or stability augmentation — requiring the pilot to fly the helicopter fully manually without automated stabilization or coupled guidance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do AFCS (sistema automático de controle de voo).

Perda das funções do sistema automático de controle de voo — como piloto automático, retenção de atitude ou aumento de estabilidade — exigindo que o piloto voe o helicóptero manualmente, sem estabilização automatizada ou guiamento acoplado.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When AFCS fails, the pilot should:

- take manual control immediately and stabilize attitude and altitude
- disconnect or confirm AFCS modes are off per AFM procedure
- reassess IMC capability, approach minima, and crew workload
- declare Pan Pan if the failure compromises safe continuation in current conditions
- request radar vectors, a simpler approach, or diversion as required

AFCS failure reporting commonly includes:

- AFCS failure or autopilot and stability augmentation failed
- manual flight, request vectors
- request ILS or simpler approach / diversion
- souls on board when requested

**Important:** AFCS failure is not automatically Mayday. It becomes urgency or distress when manual workload plus weather or system state threatens safe flight.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support AFCS failure by:

- providing radar vectors to reduce navigation workload
- offering the simplest available approach
- facilitating diversion when IMC manual flight is unsafe
- avoiding complex clearances and last-minute changes
- coordinating priority when the crew declares urgency

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in IMC at flight level seven zero in your H130 toward Florianópolis with AFCS coupled.

AFCS fails. The helicopter pitches and rolls slightly before you take manual control and stabilize. Workload is high in cloud.

You advise Approach of AFCS failure, request radar vectors for the ILS, and ask for no speed or heading changes without notice. If conditions exceed comfortable manual IMC capability, you declare Pan Pan and request diversion to VMC.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger AFCS failure, fly heading one eight zero, vectors ILS runway one four.
2. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, divert approved, turn left heading zero nine zero.
3. Approach: ANAC123, maintain three thousand, expect ILS approach.
4. Tower: ANAC123, runway one four cleared to land, wind one five zero at one two.
5. Approach: ANAC123, say intentions and fuel remaining.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

AFCS is a workload reducer — not a substitute for hand-flying skill.

When it fails, your first job is attitude. Trim and stabilize. Then decide: can you safely continue this approach manually in these conditions?

On the H130, daylight VMC AFCS failure may be a maintenance diversion after a normal landing. Night IMC in turbulence may be Pan Pan and a request for the simplest path to VMC.

Tell ATC what you need: vectors, no complex SID/STAR gymnastics, or diversion. Do not silently accept a workload you cannot manage.

Never chase coupled modes that are clearly failed. Confirm disconnect. Fly the aircraft.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

AFCS fails in IMC on vectors to an approach. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Approach, AFCS failure, manual flight, request continued vectors for ILS and minimal heading changes. If workload becomes unsafe I would declare: Pan Pan Pan Pan, AFCS failure, request diversion to VMC.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying nothing and accepting a complex clearance after AFCS fails.  
  ✔ Advise AFCS failure and request vectors or simplification.

- ❌ Declaring Mayday for a daytime VMC AFCS failure with full control.  
  ✔ Mayday is for distress; use Pan Pan when conditions make manual flight urgent.

- ❌ Attempting to re-engage failed modes repeatedly in IMC.  
  ✔ Fly manually; troubleshoot only when stable and appropriate.

- ❌ Forgetting to reassess approach minima and personal limits.  
  ✔ Manual IMC has higher workload — divert if required.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You lose AFCS in IMC twenty miles from Florianópolis. You are stable manually but workload is high.

**Your first task**

What do you say to Approach?

**Your second task**

Turbulence increases and you cannot safely continue the ILS manually. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Approach, ANAC123, AFCS failure, manual flight, request vectors ILS runway one four. Second task: Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, AFCS failure, unable ILS in IMC, request diversion to VMC.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

AFCS failure returns the helicopter to your hands completely.

Stabilize. Advise. Simplify.

VMC may be routine after landing. IMC may be Pan Pan and diversion.

On the H130, hand-fly with honesty about your limits — and make ATC part of the solution early.
