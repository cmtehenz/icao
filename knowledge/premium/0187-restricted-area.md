# Restricted Area

**Catalog ID:** 0187  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Restricted Area — designated airspace where flight is not permitted, or is permitted only under specified conditions, usually due to military activity, security, or other hazards to aviation.

A restricted area is not a suggestion. It is a published airspace constraint. Entering without authorization can create conflict with military operations, endanger the aircraft, and result in serious regulatory consequences.

For H130 helicopter training academy flights in Brazilian airspace, restricted areas appear on charts near major cities, coastal zones, and military installations. Routes between Congonhas, Santos Dumont, Guarulhos, Curitiba, and Florianópolis may require lateral or altitude planning to remain clear — or to obtain a clearance through when the area is cold or transit is authorized.

Understanding how to discuss restricted areas, request transit, and confirm remaining clear in ICAO English supports Part 2 radiotelephony and Part 4 interaction skills.

## 📖 Meaning

A Restricted Area is a defined volume of airspace, published on aeronautical charts and in AIP documentation, within which flight of aircraft is restricted in accordance with certain specified conditions, or prohibited except for authorized aircraft, typically due to military, security, or hazardous activities.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When operating near or toward a restricted area, the crew should:

- identify restricted areas on the route during preflight and briefing using AIP and charts
- note activation status (hot/cold), vertical limits, and lateral boundaries
- plan a route that remains clear unless transit clearance is expected and obtained
- request ATC clearance for transit when the area is available and the route requires it
- comply immediately if ATC instructs to remain clear or divert around the area
- report if unable to remain clear due to weather or emergency and state intentions

Restricted area operations commonly include:

- lateral routing around an active (hot) restricted area
- altitude restrictions below or above the published block
- ATC vectors to keep traffic clear of the boundary
- temporary activation for military exercises
- transit clearances when the area is cold or specifically authorized

**Important:** Never assume a restricted area is cold because it was cold yesterday. Check NOTAMs and AIP supplements. In the H130, low-level VFR training routes are especially exposed to restricted and prohibited areas near urban and coastal zones. If ATC says remain clear, treat the boundary as a hard line unless you receive an explicit transit clearance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with restricted areas to:

- keep civil traffic separated from military or hazardous activity
- issue clearances and vectors that respect published boundaries
- coordinate transit when the controlling authority releases the area
- protect both the restricted activity and the civil aircraft
- re-route arrivals and departures when an area becomes active

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are flying from Santos Dumont toward Curitiba. Along the planned track, a restricted area is active from the surface to flight level one zero zero according to the day's NOTAM.

Approach advises that the restricted area is hot and instructs you to remain clear. You request a heading to the east to circumnavigate.

Approach issues a vector clear of the area and later clears you direct to a fix beyond the boundary.

You remain outside. No penetration. No conflict with the activity inside.

That is how restricted area awareness works in real Brazilian ops — chart knowledge, NOTAM check, ATC instruction, and a clean lateral plan.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, restricted area ahead is active, remain clear, turn right heading two seven zero.
2. Radar: ANAC123, unable direct due restricted area, expect vectors around.
3. Control: ANAC123, restricted area bravo is cold, cleared through, report exiting.
4. Approach: ANAC123, remain outside restricted area, traffic military activity in progress.
5. Radar: ANAC123, when clear of restricted area, resume own navigation, direct Curitiba.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Restricted areas are published for a reason — usually something inside that does not mix well with civil traffic.

Your first defense is the chart and the NOTAM. Know where the areas sit relative to your route before you start the engines.

Your second defense is ATC. If they say remain clear, do not edge the boundary to save two minutes. Give yourself margin — especially in the H130 at low level, where a small navigation error becomes a boundary penetration quickly.

If the area is cold and transit is cleared, read back the clearance and report exiting if requested. Transit is a privilege, not a default.

If weather forces you toward a restricted area, say so early. Request an alternative heading or altitude. Do not choose between a thunderstorm and an unauthorized penetration without talking to ATC.

Part 4 interaction matters here: you may need to clarify status, request transit, or confirm you are clear. Keep the language simple and precise.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your cleared route passes through a restricted area that NOTAMs show as active. Describe what you do before and during the flight.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

During preflight I would identify the restricted area on the chart, confirm activation status and vertical limits in the NOTAM and AIP, and plan an alternate route that remains clear. Before departure or en route, I would confirm with ATC that the area is active and request vectors or a reroute around it. If ATC later advises the area is cold and clears me through, I would read back the transit clearance and comply with any reporting instructions. I would not enter an active restricted area without an explicit clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Assuming the area is inactive because it is often cold.  
  ✔ Check NOTAMs every flight and confirm with ATC if status is unclear.

- ❌ Flying close to the boundary to "cut the corner."  
  ✔ Remain clearly outside unless cleared through; build lateral margin.

- ❌ Entering because GPS shows a shorter path.  
  ✔ Navigation shortcuts do not authorize restricted area penetration.

- ❌ Failing to report exiting when transit was cleared with a report request.  
  ✔ Report exiting as instructed so ATC can release the coordination.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are en route from Guarulhos to Florianópolis. Radar advises a restricted area on your track is active.

**Your first task**

Radar says: "ANAC123, restricted area ahead is active, remain clear, turn left heading one five zero." What is your readback?

**Your second task**

After you are clear of the area, Radar says you may resume own navigation. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, restricted area active, remaining clear, left heading one five zero. Second task: ANAC123, resume own navigation, direct Florianópolis — or the fix Radar assigns. I would not re-enter toward the restricted area after being told to remain clear, and I would only resume the original track when ATC clears me to do so.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Restricted areas are hard boundaries until ATC — and the controlling authority — say otherwise.

Know them before takeoff. Confirm them in flight. Remain clear when they are hot.

If transit is offered, read it back and fly it precisely. If vectors around are issued, take the heading and keep the margin.

In Brazilian H130 ops, low-level routes make chart discipline non-negotiable. A beautiful shortcut through a hot restricted area is not professional flying — it is a violation waiting to happen.

Stay outside. Ask early. Transit only when cleared.

That is how you respect the airspace — and keep the mission legal and safe.
