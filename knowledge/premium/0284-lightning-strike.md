# Lightning Strike

**Catalog ID:** 0284  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Lightning Strike — an electrical discharge striking the aircraft in flight, requiring immediate assessment of systems, instruments, and structural or electrical damage, plus clear ATC communication for diversion or priority handling when needed.

Lightning strikes are common ICAO exam topics and real Brazilian weather events in convective seasons. Effects range from a loud bang and temporary flash blindness to compass errors, electrical anomalies, or — rarely — structural damage. For H130 helicopter ICAO Delta academy operations, avoid known thunderstorm cells; if struck, fly the aircraft, check systems, and report.

Master: lightning strike, systems normal / systems affected, request diversion, compass unreliable.

## 📖 Meaning

Lightning Strike is an atmospheric electrical discharge that contacts the aircraft, potentially affecting electrical systems, instruments, compass, radios, or structure, and requiring immediate flight-path and systems assessment, ATC notification when operations are affected, and diversion or priority landing if damage or unreliability is present.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Descarga atmosférica / Lightning Strike.

Descarga elétrica atmosférica que atinge a aeronave, podendo afetar sistemas elétricos, instrumentos, bússola, rádios ou estrutura, e exigindo avaliação imediata da trajetória e dos sistemas, notificação ao ATC quando as operações forem afetadas, e desvio ou pouso prioritário se houver dano ou falta de confiabilidade.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a lightning strike occurs, the crew should:

- maintain aircraft control; recover from any temporary startle or flash effect
- check flight instruments, electrical systems, compass, and radios
- exit or avoid further convective weather
- declare Pan Pan if systems are unreliable or diversion is required; Mayday if critical systems fail or structural damage is evident
- report lightning strike, systems status, intentions
- for H130: highly relevant — avoid cells; after a strike, verify engine instruments, avionics, and controllability

Lightning strike reporting commonly includes:

- lightning strike
- systems normal / compass unreliable / electrical problems
- request diversion / priority landing
- request vectors clear of weather
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** A strike with all systems normal may need only a report and continuation with caution. A strike with compass or electrical failure needs Pan Pan and diversion language. Match the call to the effect.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to lightning strike reports to:

- provide vectors clear of further convective weather
- offer diversion airports and priority handling
- note possible compass or navigation unreliability for separation planning
- alert emergency services if structural or electrical damage is reported
- request systems status updates

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR in the H130 near Campinas when a bright flash and bang — lightning strike. Attitude instruments remain OK. Magnetic compass spins. GPS remains available.

You transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, Control, ANAC123, lightning strike, magnetic compass unreliable, aircraft controllable, request vectors clear of weather and diversion Campinas.

Control: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, turn left heading two two zero, radar vectors Campinas, say other systems status when able.

You update:

ANAC123, flight instruments normal, compass unreliable, continuing diversion Campinas.

If radios or primary flight instruments fail, upgrade urgency and say what is lost.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Lightning strike is weather discipline first — and systems English second.

Avoid the cell. If you are struck anyway: fly attitude, check systems, tell ATC what failed.

Brazilian pilots often say "tomou raio" or "descarga." Prefer lightning strike in ICAO English — then compass unreliable, electrical problems, or systems normal.

In the H130, convective weather is a serious threat at helicopter altitudes. Do not treat a strike as "nothing" until you have checked instruments and electrics. Do not declare Mayday for a bang with all systems green — but do not hide a failed compass either.

Report. Divert if unreliable. Stay clear of the next cell.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You experience a lightning strike. The magnetic compass is unreliable but flight instruments are normal. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control using primary flight instruments, check electrical and navigation systems, exit convective weather, and declare Pan Pan stating lightning strike with magnetic compass unreliable. I would request vectors clear of weather and diversion to a suitable airport if needed. If critical systems failed, I would declare Mayday and request immediate landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "tomou raio" without systems status.  
  ✔ Lightning strike — then systems normal or what failed.

- ❌ Continuing into the same thunderstorm cell after a strike.  
  ✔ Request vectors clear of weather; divert if needed.

- ❌ Declaring Mayday for a strike with all systems normal.  
  ✔ Match urgency to effects; Pan Pan when systems are unreliable.

- ❌ Not mentioning compass or navigation unreliability.  
  ✔ Say compass unreliable so ATC understands navigation limits.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Lightning strike is a systems check with a weather story behind it.

Fly attitude. Check everything. Say what failed. Get clear of the storm.

In Brazilian convective seasons this is real H130 risk, not only exam theory. Avoidance beats recovery every time.

Systems normal — report and continue with caution. Systems unreliable — Pan Pan and divert. Critical failure — Mayday.

That is how lightning strikes end as uneventful diversions — not as second strikes in the same cell.
