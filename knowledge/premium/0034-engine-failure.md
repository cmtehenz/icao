# Engine Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0034  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers one of the most serious events a pilot can face.

Engine Failure means the engine has stopped producing usable power — completely or to the extent that continued safe flight is in doubt.

In a single-engine helicopter like the H130, engine failure is immediately critical.

You must aviate first, then navigate, then communicate.

Your radio call must be clear, standard, and timed — after you have secured the aircraft as much as possible.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Engine Failure is the complete or near-complete loss of engine power, requiring immediate crew action and ATC notification.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de motor.

Perda completa ou parcial da potência do motor, exigindo ação imediata da tripulação.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Engine Failure occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- perform appropriate emergency procedures (autorotation in a helicopter)
- select a suitable landing area
- declare Mayday when continued safe flight is in doubt
- inform ATC of engine failure, intentions, souls on board, and fuel remaining

Engine Failure reporting commonly includes:

- Mayday Mayday Mayday for distress in single-engine operations
- we have an engine failure
- request immediate return or vectors to nearest suitable airport
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested

**Important:** In a single-engine H130, engine failure is a distress situation. Aviate first — then communicate clearly with standard phraseology.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Engine Failure declarations to:

- clear traffic from the aircraft's route
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable airport
- coordinate priority landing and emergency services
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- alert other aircraft and airport authorities

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You have just departed Navegantes in your H130 on a sightseeing flight.

At three hundred feet, the engine fails.

You lower the collective and enter autorotation toward a suitable area.

When stable, you transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Navegantes Tower, ANAC123, engine failure, autorotating, one mile south of the airport.

Tower clears all traffic and coordinates emergency services.

You complete a controlled autorotation landing and report safe on the ground.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, roger Mayday, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
2. Tower: ANAC123, all traffic cleared from your approach, runway zero seven cleared to land.
3. Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading zero nine zero, descend at your discretion.
4. Tower: ANAC123, emergency services standing by.
5. Departure: ANAC123, radar contact, fly direct Navegantes, say intentions.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Navegantes Tower, ANAC123, engine failure, request immediate return.
2. Tower, ANAC123, lost engine, autorotating.
3. We have an engine failure, ANAC123, souls on board three, fuel remaining one hour.
4. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Departure, ANAC123, engine failure, request vectors to Florianópolis.
5. ANAC123, engine failure, unable to maintain altitude, descending to nearest suitable area.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Engine failure in an H130 is not a drill.

It is immediate.

The sequence every professional pilot learns:

Aviate. Navigate. Communicate.

Do not transmit Mayday while the aircraft is out of control.

Establish autorotation first.

Then speak — clearly, once, with standard words.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "engine stopped" or "motor parou" on frequency.

Use engine failure — it is the ICAO standard term.

For multi-engine aircraft, specify which engine: lost engine number one.

For the H130, one engine means one chance.

Train your autorotation reflexes so your radio call comes from a stable aircraft, not a panicked voice.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC immediately after an engine failure on departure?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After securing the aircraft, I would declare Mayday Mayday Mayday, state my callsign, report engine failure, state my intentions such as immediate return or autorotation, and provide position. I would give souls on board and fuel remaining when ATC requests.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Declaring Mayday before establishing aircraft control.  
  ✔ Aviate first — autorotate or secure the aircraft, then communicate.

- ❌ Saying "engine stopped" instead of engine failure.  
  ✔ Use standard phraseology: engine failure or lost engine.

- ❌ Forgetting to state intentions after the emergency declaration.  
  ✔ State immediate return, autorotation, or nearest suitable airport.

- ❌ Omitting souls on board and fuel when ATC asks.  
  ✔ Provide souls on board and fuel remaining promptly.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Mayday Mayday Mayday, Engine Failure

**Pronunciation:** MAY-day MAY-day MAY-day, EN-jin FAIL-yur

**Word Stress**

- Mayday → may-DAY (say three times, clearly)
- Engine → EN-jin
- Failure → FAIL-yur (two syllables)

Practice:

Mayday... Mayday... Mayday...

Together:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Navegantes Tower, ANAC123, engine failure.

Speak slowly and clearly — controllers need every word.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**FAIL** — **F**ly the aircraft first, **A**utorotate or secure, **I**nform ATC with Mayday, **L**and or loiter as required.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are departing Congonhas at two hundred feet when the engine fails in your H130.

**Your first task**

After establishing autorotation, transmit your emergency declaration to Tower.

**Your second task**

Answer this question:

Tower asks for souls on board and fuel remaining. What do you report?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report souls on board and fuel remaining clearly: for example, souls on board three, fuel remaining forty-five minutes, ANAC123. I provide accurate information to help ATC coordinate the emergency response.

Engine Failure demands the discipline every pilot trains for.

Aviate first.

Autorotate in the H130.

State your intentions.

Answer ATC questions promptly.
