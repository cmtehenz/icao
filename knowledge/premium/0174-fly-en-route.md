# Fly En Route

**Catalog ID:** 0174  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Fly En Route — the phase of flight and associated ATC clearances that cover the portion of the journey from the departure phase through to the arrival phase, conducted along published airways, routes, or direct tracks.

The en-route phase is where most of the actual flying time occurs.

It is also where many pilots become complacent.

En route, you are responsible for altitude, speed, and lateral navigation along the cleared route. ATC monitors your track and altitude on radar, but the cockpit workload is yours.

In Brazilian airspace, en-route operations between airports like São Paulo, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Navegantes involve high-altitude airways, mandatory reporting points, RVSM vertical separation, and transitions through multiple ATC sectors.

Understanding the phraseology, responsibilities, and common clearances associated with the en-route phase is essential for ICAO Part 2 communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Fly En Route refers to the navigational phase of flight where the aircraft operates along published airways or cleared tracks between the departure and destination, following ATC-assigned altitudes, speeds, and routes while transitioning through multiple ATC sectors.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Voe em rota.

Fase de voo onde a aeronave opera ao longo de rotas publicadas ou trajetórias autorizadas entre a partida e o destino, seguindo altitudes, velocidades e rotas atribuídas pelo controle de tráfego aéreo durante a transição por múltiplos setores.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

During the en-route phase, the pilot is responsible for:

- maintaining the ATC-cleared flight level or altitude
- maintaining the ATC-cleared route — airway or direct clearance
- reporting over compulsory reporting points unless radar coverage is confirmed
- requesting amendments for weather deviations, turbulence, or performance changes
- coordinating frequency changes with each controlling sector
- monitoring for TCAS advisories and reporting conflicts to ATC

Common en-route clearances and instructions:

- **Climb to flight level** — altitude amendment
- **Direct to** — shortcut clearance bypassing intermediate waypoints
- **Proceed via** — route via specific airways or waypoints
- **Maintain flight level** — instruction to hold the current level
- **Contact [sector] on [frequency]** — handoff to next sector
- **Report position** — position report at non-radar waypoints

**Important:** En-route position reports are mandatory at compulsory reporting points unless the controller specifically states radar identification and releases the pilot from reporting. In non-radar areas of Brazilian airspace, position reports maintain separation between aircraft.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers manage en-route traffic to:

- maintain vertical and horizontal separation between aircraft
- route traffic efficiently through multiple sectors
- coordinate handoffs between approach, area, and oceanic sectors
- provide traffic information and TCAS coordination
- issue shortcuts and climb authorizations to optimize flight
- manage conflict avoidance in non-radar environments

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are climbing to flight level eight five on an IFR flight from Florianópolis to São Paulo in your H130.

São Paulo ACC transmits:

ANAC123, radar contact, climb flight level one zero zero, direct GIKAM.

You respond:

Climbing flight level one zero zero, direct GIKAM, ANAC123.

En route, you reach GIKAM and continue to the next fix. The controller hands you off:

ANAC123, contact São Paulo approach one two zero decimal three five.

You respond:

One two zero decimal three five, ANAC123.

You switch frequency and check in:

São Paulo Approach, ANAC123, passing flight level one zero zero, inbound, information Bravo.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, radar contact, descend to four thousand feet, QNH one zero one zero, expect ILS runway two eight.

En-route phase complete. Arrival phase begins.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

En route is where the professional pilot's habits are most visible.

Complacency is the enemy of good en-route flying.

The cruise phase feels calm — and that calmness lowers attention.

In the H130, the en-route cruise is typically at lower flight levels compared to jet traffic, but the responsibilities are identical.

Maintain your cleared altitude. Fly your cleared route. Make your position reports.

When ATC gives you a direct routing shortcut — take it. But load it correctly. Verify the route on the moving map. Do not blindly press "direct."

When you are handed off to a new sector — check in immediately. State your flight level, inbound waypoint, and ATIS if approaching the destination.

The en-route phase is the one where most communication happens. Practice the standard formats until they are automatic.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

When are position reports mandatory during en-route flight, and what information do they contain?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Position reports are mandatory at compulsory reporting points — marked with a solid triangle on aeronautical charts — unless the controller has confirmed radar contact and specifically released the pilot from routine reporting. A standard position report includes the aircraft callsign, position, time over the fix, flight level, and the next reporting point with the estimated time. For example: ANAC123, position REKPA, time three five, flight level eight five, estimating GIKAM at four five. In areas outside radar coverage, position reports are the only separation assurance between aircraft, so they must be accurate and timely.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Not making position reports at compulsory points in non-radar areas.  
  ✔ Position reports are mandatory at compulsory points unless released by ATC radar contact.

- ❌ Checking in on a new sector frequency without stating flight level.  
  ✔ Always state callsign, flight level, and inbound waypoint on initial contact with a new sector.

- ❌ Accepting a direct routing shortcut without loading it correctly.  
  ✔ Verify the direct clearance on the navigation display before pressing execute.

- ❌ Failing to request a flight level amendment when turbulence or performance issues occur.  
  ✔ Report conditions and request an amendment: ANAC123, request climb to flight level one zero five, turbulence at current level.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**ROUTE** — **R**eport at compulsory points, **O**bserve cleared flight level, **U**pdate ATC on weather or performance changes, **T**ransfer frequency promptly at sector boundaries, **E**stimate time to next fix accurately.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are en route from Florianópolis to Congonhas at flight level eight five.

São Paulo Control transmits: ANAC123, cleared direct GIKAM, maintain flight level eight five.

After loading GIKAM, you encounter moderate turbulence at flight level eight five and wish to climb to flight level nine five.

**Your first task**

Read back the original direct clearance.

**Your second task**

Compose the request for a flight level change due to turbulence, and anticipate a possible ATC response.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Readback: ANAC123, direct GIKAM, maintaining flight level eight five. Turbulence request: ANAC123, request climb to flight level nine five, moderate turbulence at current level. Possible ATC response: ANAC123, climb flight level nine five, report reaching. My readback: Climbing flight level nine five, will report reaching, ANAC123. If ATC is unable: ANAC123, unable higher traffic, maintain flight level eight five, advise if turbulence severe. My response: Maintaining flight level eight five, turbulence moderate at this time, ANAC123.
