# Mountain Flying

**Catalog ID:** 0099  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers mountain flying — operations near or over mountainous terrain, where turbulence, downdrafts, density altitude, and limited emergency landing options create elevated risk.

Mountain Flying requires thorough pre-flight planning, conservative weather minima, and clear ATC communication about position and intentions.

In the H130, mountain flying is relevant near the Serra do Mar escarpment close to Florianópolis and the elevated terrain surrounding Curitiba and São Paulo.

You must understand orographic effects, plan escape routes, and communicate altitude and terrain clearance intentions clearly.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2, Part 3, and Part 4 communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Mountain Flying describes helicopter and aircraft operations in areas of significant terrain elevation, where orographic lift, turbulence, downdrafts, reduced performance, and limited forced landing areas demand heightened crew awareness and planning.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Voo em região montanhosa.

Operação de helicópteros e aeronaves em áreas de elevação de terreno significativa, onde turbulência orográfica, correntes descendentes, desempenho reduzido e opções limitadas de pouso forçado exigem maior atenção e planejamento da tripulação.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Mountain Flying is planned, the pilot should:

- study terrain profiles and obstacle charts thoroughly
- plan routes along valleys to maintain engine-out landing options
- account for density altitude and performance reduction at altitude
- identify escape routes and alternate landing areas
- monitor weather closely — mountain weather changes rapidly

Mountain Flying reporting commonly includes:

- request altitude increase for terrain clearance
- encountering severe turbulence over mountains
- diverting due to deteriorating weather over terrain
- unable to maintain VMC, requesting IFR clearance

**Important:** Mountains create turbulence and downdrafts on the lee side. Do not assume a clear sky above means calm air. Cross ridgelines with excess altitude and at ninety degrees when possible.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC supports Mountain Flying by:

- providing radar traffic advisories over terrain
- approving altitude changes for terrain clearance
- relaying weather and turbulence PIREPs
- coordinating search and rescue if contact is lost
- issuing terrain clearance advisories when available

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are transiting the Serra do Mar in your H130, routing from the coast toward Curitiba.

At three thousand feet, you encounter moderate turbulence and a downdraft on the lee side of the ridge.

You decide to climb for clearance and change route.

You transmit:

Curitiba Control, ANAC123, request climb to five thousand feet, mountain turbulence, lee side downdraft.

Control approves. You cross above the turbulent layer and reach Curitiba safely.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, terrain two thousand feet within five miles, say altitude.
2. Control: ANAC123, turbulence report from previous aircraft, moderate over ridgeline.
3. Control: ANAC123, climb to five thousand feet approved, terrain clearance confirmed.
4. Control: ANAC123, radar contact lost, say position.
5. Control: ANAC123, weather report Curitiba, visibility reducing, advise intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Mountains are beautiful and dangerous.

Updraft. Downdraft. Rotor turbulence. Canyon trap. No landing option.

The sequence:

Plan the route carefully. Check weather. Know the escape. Cross ridgelines with margin.

The Serra do Mar between the São Paulo coast and the plateau catches pilots every year.

Downdrafts on the lee side can exceed your climb capability.

Always have an escape route. Always know where you can land if the engine fails.

Plan for density altitude at altitude.

Never box yourself into a canyon. Keep your exit behind you until you know the route ahead is clear.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if you encountered severe turbulence and downdrafts while flying over mountainous terrain?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would immediately request a climb to gain altitude above the turbulent layer and report the turbulence to ATC. I would state the location, altitude, and severity. If I was unable to climb sufficiently, I would reverse course to exit the mountain area and divert to a lower-terrain route, informing ATC of my intentions.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating Brazilian coastal mountains as benign because they are not high altitude.  
  ✔ The Serra do Mar produces severe orographic turbulence regardless of absolute elevation.

- ❌ Not filing a flight plan or notifying ATC before entering mountainous areas.  
  ✔ Always ensure ATC or a responsible party knows your route over terrain.

- ❌ Saying "muita turbulência" without specific location and altitude.  
  ✔ Report turbulence with location, altitude, and severity.

- ❌ Continuing into deteriorating weather over mountains hoping it will improve.  
  ✔ Reverse course early — mountain weather traps are often one-way.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are routing from Navegantes to Curitiba in your H130 via the Serra do Mar escarpment. At three thousand feet, you encounter strong downdrafts and reducing visibility ahead.

**Your first task**

What is your immediate radio call and action?

**Your second task**

ATC advises radar contact is marginal. What additional information do you provide?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would request an immediate climb to five thousand feet and report mountain turbulence and reducing visibility to ATC. If unable to climb, I would reverse course immediately. I would provide my current position, altitude, and heading to assist ATC in tracking me, and state my intention to divert to Navegantes or an alternate with a lower-terrain route.
