# Fire on Board

**Catalog ID:** 0038  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers one of the most urgent emergencies in aviation.

Fire on Board means fire exists inside the aircraft — cabin, cockpit, or cargo area.

There is no time for hesitation. Mayday is immediate.

In your H130, a cabin fire demands descent, landing, and evacuation planning — all while maintaining aircraft control.

Your radio call must be clear, specific, and immediate.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Fire on Board is an active fire inside the aircraft structure — cabin, cockpit, or cargo compartment.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Incêndio a bordo.

Fogo ativo no interior da aeronave — cabine, cockpit ou compartimento de carga.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Fire on Board occurs, the pilot should:

- declare Mayday Mayday Mayday immediately
- specify the fire location: cabin, cockpit, or cargo
- descend and navigate toward the nearest suitable airport
- execute the fire checklist and prepare for emergency landing
- correct ATC if they assume the wrong fire location

Fire on Board reporting commonly includes:

- Mayday, we have fire on board
- fire in the cabin
- request immediate return
- NEGATIVE, we have fire in the cabin — not engine fire

**Important:** Specify cabin, cockpit, or cargo. Correct ATC immediately if they assume engine fire when the fire is in the cabin.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Fire on Board declarations to:

- clear all traffic from the aircraft's route immediately
- assign unrestricted descent and vectors to the nearest airport
- alert fire and rescue services on the ground
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- coordinate emergency landing and evacuation support

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are climbing through six thousand feet in your H130 after departure from Manaus.

The passenger behind you reports smoke and flames near the rear cabin area.

You don oxygen, declare Mayday, and turn back toward Manaus.

You transmit:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Manaus Approach, ANAC123, fire in the cabin, request immediate return to Manaus.

Approach clears traffic and asks you to confirm engine fire.

You respond firmly:

NEGATIVE, we have fire in the cabin. Descending to three thousand feet, ANAC123.

You complete an emergency landing and evacuate on the runway.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, descend at your discretion, turn right heading zero six zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm you have an engine fire.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway three six cleared to land, fire services alerted.
4. Approach: ANAC123, squawk seven seven zero zero.
5. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Fire on board is not a Pan Pan situation.

It is Mayday — three times, immediately, after you have basic aircraft control.

The most common Brazilian mistake: saying fire in the airplane vaguely.

Be specific: fire in the cabin, fire in the cockpit, fire in the cargo.

The ICAO Delta exam tests NEGATIVE — when ATC assumes engine fire and you have cabin fire.

Do not accept the wrong assumption. Correct firmly: NEGATIVE, we have fire in the cabin.

In the H130, cabin fire from passenger equipment or electrical fault is a real scenario.

Descend first. Land fast. Evacuate when stopped.

Do not delay Mayday to finish checklist items below ten thousand feet — you are already below that in a helicopter.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the difference between fire on board and fire in the cabin when reporting?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Fire on board is the general emergency declaration. Fire in the cabin specifies the location. I would declare Mayday and state fire in the cabin so ATC knows it is not engine fire. If ATC assumes engine fire, I correct with NEGATIVE and restate the cabin location.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying fire in the airplane vaguely — no location given.  
  ✔ Specify cabin, cockpit, or cargo: fire in the cabin.

- ❌ Accepting ATC assumption of engine fire without correction.  
  ✔ Use NEGATIVE firmly and restate the correct fire location.

- ❌ Delaying Mayday to finish checklist items.  
  ✔ Declare Mayday immediately after securing basic aircraft control.

- ❌ Using fogo a bordo in English transmission.  
  ✔ Use fire on board or fire in the cabin — standard ICAO phraseology.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Mayday, Fire in the Cabin

**Pronunciation:** MAY-day, FYUR in the CAB-in

**Word Stress**

- Mayday → may-DAY (three times)
- Cabin → CAB-in
- NEGATIVE → NEG-a-tive (firm correction)

Practice:

Mayday... Mayday... Mayday...

Together:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Manaus Approach, ANAC123, fire in the cabin.

Speak with authority — controllers must hear every word clearly.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**FIRE** — **F**irst declare Mayday, **I**nform location, **R**equest return, **E**vacuate plan ready.
