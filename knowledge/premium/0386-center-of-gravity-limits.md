# Center of Gravity Limits

**Catalog ID:** 0386  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Center of Gravity Limits — the forward and aft boundaries of the approved CG envelope within which the aircraft must be loaded for safe and controllable flight.

CG Limits are not flexible guidelines. Forward of the limit, the aircraft may be nose-heavy and hard to rotate or flare. Aft of the limit, stability and control margins degrade — especially critical in a helicopter.

For H130 Brazilian operations — passenger seating, baggage, and fuel — CG can move out of limits even when total weight is legal. You must understand the concept for Part 2 and Part 4 exam English and operational briefings.

## 📖 Meaning

Center of Gravity Limits are the approved forward and aft positions of the aircraft's center of gravity, published in the flight manual, outside which the aircraft must not be operated.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Limites de centro de gravidade / Center of Gravity Limits.

Posições aprovadas à frente e atrás do centro de gravidade da aeronave, publicadas no manual de voo, fora das quais a aeronave não deve ser operada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing CG limits, the crew should:

- compute CG from loading stations and compare to forward/aft limits
- redistribute passengers or baggage if CG is out of limits
- refuse departure if CG cannot be corrected
- consider fuel burn effects on CG when relevant to the type
- brief the load so cabin changes do not move CG unexpectedly

CG limit management includes:

- forward limit — control and performance effects when nose-heavy
- aft limit — stability and controllability when tail-heavy
- lateral balance where the AFM requires it
- empty weight CG and moment from the weighing report
- operational loading charts for quick verification

**Important:** Total weight within maximum does not guarantee CG within limits. Always check both. An aft CG in the H130 is a controllability issue — not a paperwork issue.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

ATC rarely assigns CG limits, but controllers are involved when:

- crews delay taxi for load redistribution
- aircraft report unable to depart due to balance
- return to stand is requested after a CG discrepancy
- loading adjustments affect departure sequence
- performance or handling issues are reported after takeoff (rare, serious)

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

At a helipad in Rio, three passengers seat aft and baggage is loaded rear. Your H130 CG calculates aft of the limit.

Before start:

ANAC123, request delay, center of gravity out of limits, redistributing load.

After moving one passenger forward and relocating bags:

ANAC123, ready for departure, CG within limits.

You did not lift into an aft-CG condition to "see how it feels."

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, advise ready.
2. Ground: ANAC123, roger delay for loading, hold position.
3. Tower: ANAC123, say reason unable to depart.
4. Ground: ANAC123, taxi when balance confirmed.
5. Tower: ANAC123, confirm ready for departure.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, negative ready, CG out of limits, request delay.
2. ANAC123, holding position, redistributing load.
3. ANAC123, unable departure, center of gravity aft of limit.
4. ANAC123, ready to taxi, CG within limits.
5. ANAC123, ready for departure, weight and balance confirmed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

CG limits protect controllability.

Forward limit — you may lack authority or need excessive control input. Aft limit — stability margin shrinks. In a helicopter, that is not academic.

Load the H130 with intention. Passengers are not free to choose seats if the calculation depends on a seating plan. Brief them. Check bags. Recalculate.

If CG is out — fix it on the ground. Tell ATC you need delay for balance. Do not lift "a little" to test.

Part 4 exam English expects you to explain forward and aft effects clearly. Operational English expects you to act on them without compromise.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your calculation shows CG aft of the approved limit. Total weight is under maximum takeoff weight. Are you legal to depart? What do you tell ATC if they call ready?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

No — weight within limits does not authorize flight with CG outside the envelope. I would say: ANAC123, negative ready, center of gravity out of limits, request delay to redistribute load. I would depart only after CG is within forward and aft limits. Both weight and CG must be legal.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Checking only total weight and ignoring CG.  
  ✔ Always verify CG against forward and aft limits.

- ❌ Allowing passengers to reseat freely after the calculation.  
  ✔ Brief seating; recalculate if seats change.

- ❌ Departing slightly out of aft limit "because it is a helicopter."  
  ✔ AFM limits apply — no informal margin outside the envelope.

- ❌ Not telling ATC why you are delayed.  
  ✔ State: delay for balance / CG out of limits.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Center of gravity limits are hard stops.

Forward and aft — both matter. Weight legal is not CG legal.

Load with a plan. Recalculate after changes. Delay for redistribution when needed.

In the H130, aft CG is a handling threat. Fix it on the ground. Say it clearly to ATC.

Within the envelope — then fly. Outside — do not.

That is CG discipline.
