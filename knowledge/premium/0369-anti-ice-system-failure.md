# Anti-Ice System Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0369  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Anti-Ice System Failure — loss or degradation of aircraft ice protection that leaves the crew unable to prevent ice accretion on protected surfaces when flying in icing conditions.

Anti-ice failure turns a manageable weather exposure into an exit-now problem. Without protection, the H130 must leave icing conditions promptly, report the failure, and plan diversion or altitude change before performance erodes.

For Brazilian IFR — winter plateau routes, freezing cloud over the Serra, moist coastal layers — crews must communicate system status and intentions clearly: anti-ice failure, exiting icing, requesting descent or diversion.

Understanding this phraseology supports Part 2 radiotelephony and Part 3 abnormal-situation speaking.

## 📖 Meaning

Anti-Ice System Failure is a malfunction in which aircraft ice-protection equipment — such as engine inlet anti-ice, windshield heat, or other AFM-listed anti-ice systems — fails to operate or provides inadequate protection, requiring immediate avoidance of icing conditions, abnormal procedures, and notification to ATC when the failure affects routing or landing capability.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do sistema anti-gelo / Anti-Ice System Failure.

Malfuncionamento em que o equipamento de proteção contra gelo da aeronave — como anti-gelo de entrada do motor, aquecimento do para-brisa ou outros sistemas listados no AFM — deixa de operar ou oferece proteção inadequada, exigindo evitar imediatamente condições de gelo, procedimentos anormais e notificação ao ATC quando a falha afetar o roteamento ou a capacidade de pouso.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When anti-ice fails, the crew should:

- apply the AFM abnormal checklist for the failed system
- exit icing conditions immediately if ice is present or forecast along the route
- avoid further flight into known icing without protection
- advise ATC of anti-ice failure and request altitude change or diversion as required
- monitor for structural or inlet ice accretion after the failure
- plan an approach to conditions that do not require ice protection

Anti-ice failure scenarios commonly include:

- engine anti-ice inoperative in freezing cloud
- windshield heat failure with ice forming on the windshield
- need to descend to warmer air or divert to VMC
- priority handling if ice is already accreting

**Important:** Anti-ice failure in clear dry air may be a maintenance item. Anti-ice failure in icing conditions is an operational emergency of timing — leave the moisture and cold before the airframe or inlet collects ice you cannot shed.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use anti-ice failure reports to:

- clear altitude or routing changes out of icing
- approve diversions to better weather
- provide priority when ice is already forming
- warn other aircraft of icing along the route
- understand why a pilot cannot accept a clearance through freezing cloud

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR at five thousand feet toward Curitiba in light freezing drizzle. Engine anti-ice fails the self-test / caution illuminates per AFM logic. Ice begins to show on the windshield frame.

You transmit:

Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, anti-ice system failure, light icing, request immediate descent to three thousand and diversion to Congonhas.

Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain three thousand, diversion approved, say souls on board.

You leave the icing layer, complete the checklist, and accept vectors to better conditions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Anti-ice failure is a permission problem: you no longer have permission to stay in icing.

The checklist matters. The exit matters more.

In the H130, known icing is already a hard limitation for many configurations. Failed anti-ice removes any remaining margin for windshield or inlet protection you might have counted on.

Brazilian winter IFR will tempt you to "continue a few miles." Those miles grow ice.

Tell ATC the system failed and what you need — descent, climb to on-top if safe, or diversion. Do not accept a clearance through freezing cloud after the failure.

If ice is already on the aircraft, say so. Intensity plus system failure equals priority language ATC understands.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your anti-ice system fails while you are in light icing at four thousand feet. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would apply the AFM abnormal procedure and exit icing conditions immediately by requesting a descent, climb, or diversion as appropriate. I would advise ATC of anti-ice system failure and report any icing intensity. I would not accept further clearance into known icing without protection. If performance or visibility is affected, I would request priority handling and divert to an airport with better conditions. In the H130 I treat anti-ice failure in icing as an exit-now situation.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting only "system failure" without saying anti-ice.  
  ✔ Specify anti-ice failure so ATC understands the icing implication.

- ❌ Accepting climb into colder cloud after the failure.  
  ✔ Request a route or level clear of icing; state unable if required.

- ❌ Delaying diversion until ice is severe.  
  ✔ Divert early while options and performance remain.

- ❌ Mixing checklist talk with unclear intentions on frequency.  
  ✔ One clear request: descent / diversion / priority — then fly the procedure.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Anti-ice failure rewrites the flight plan: no more icing.

Checklist. Exit. Report. Divert if needed.

In Brazilian IFR winters, that sequence is not dramatic — it is routine professionalism.

Name the system. Name the icing. Name the request. Then fly the exit before the ice owns the aircraft.
