# METAR

**Catalog ID:** 0377  
**Category:** Weather  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers METAR — the routine aviation weather report for an aerodrome, encoding wind, visibility, weather phenomena, clouds, temperature, dew point, and pressure in a standard international format.

METAR is the snapshot that drives go/no-go, approach selection, and ATC weather talk. For H130 operations in Brazil — Congonhas, Santos Dumont, Brasília, Manaus — reading METAR correctly is Part 4 literacy; using it to request the right approach or delay is Part 2 skill.

Understanding how to decode and discuss METAR elements in ICAO English is essential for professional operations.

## 📖 Meaning

A METAR is a meteorological aerodrome report issued at fixed intervals (and as SPECI when conditions change significantly), providing current observed weather at an airport in a coded format that includes wind, visibility, runway visual range when applicable, present weather, cloud layers, temperature, dew point, and altimeter setting.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

METAR / Informe Meteorológico de Aeródromo.

Informe meteorológico de aeródromo emitido em intervalos fixos (e como SPECI quando as condições mudam significativamente), fornecendo o tempo observado atual em um aeroporto em formato codificado que inclui vento, visibilidade, alcance visual de pista quando aplicável, tempo presente, camadas de nuvens, temperatura, ponto de orvalho e ajuste do altímetro.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When using a METAR, the crew should:

- decode wind, visibility, weather, ceiling (lowest BKN/OVC), temperature/dew point, and QNH
- compare visibility and ceiling with approach minima and VFR/IFR requirements
- note remarks / TREND if present for short-term change
- update ATIS and request current weather from ATC when METAR is old or conditions are changing
- use METAR language when stating reasons for delay, diversion, or approach choice
- watch for SPECI updates after thunderstorms or rapid fog formation

METAR operational use commonly includes:

- deciding IFR vs VFR
- selecting approach type based on ceiling and visibility
- briefing icing risk from temperature and cloud
- requesting alternate when destination METAR is below minima

**Important:** METAR is observation, not forecast. A good METAR now does not guarantee a good TAF later — and the reverse. For Brazilian coastal fog and summer cells, SPECI can rewrite your plan in minutes. Read the time group.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use METAR / aerodrome weather to:

- pass official weather to aircraft on request
- determine airport operating status relative to minima
- sequence approaches when visibility or ceiling is limiting
- issue runway changes when wind shifts
- coordinate diversions when weather falls below minima

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Curitiba. Latest METAR shows wind calm, visibility 2 000 meters in mist, overcast at 400 feet, temperature 8, dew point 8, QNH 1015. Your approach minima require higher ceiling.

You transmit:

Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, request current weather Curitiba, destination below minima on last METAR, say intentions after weather.

Approach issues the update. Ceiling remains 400. You divert.

Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, diverting Florianópolis due weather below minima, request vectors.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

METAR is the truth at a moment in time — coded so the world can read it the same way.

Learn the groups cold: wind, visibility, weather, clouds, T/Td, QNH. Ceiling is the lowest broken or overcast layer that matters for minima.

In Brazil, mist and low overcast at Curitiba, fog at coastal fields, and CAVOK days that die in afternoon convection — all live in METAR and SPECI.

Part 4: decode accurately. Part 2: when weather is below minima, say diverting due weather below minima. Do not invent a landing out of optimism.

ATIS is METAR's cousin on the frequency. Copy QNH. Compare with your briefed sheet. Fly the current number.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is a METAR and which elements do you check before an instrument approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

A METAR is the routine coded weather observation for an aerodrome. Before an instrument approach I check wind for runway selection, visibility and RVR if available against minima, present weather such as fog or thunderstorms, cloud layers for ceiling, temperature and dew point for fog or icing risk, and QNH for altitude reference. I also check the time of the report and any SPECI. If ceiling or visibility is below minima, I hold, delay, or divert rather than continue to a landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reading temperature as ceiling or mixing SCT with ceiling.  
  ✔ Ceiling = lowest BKN or OVC (or vertical visibility); SCT is not ceiling.

- ❌ Using an old METAR when SPECI / ATIS has updated.  
  ✔ Always use the latest observation before committing.

- ❌ Saying "bad weather" instead of METAR elements.  
  ✔ State visibility, ceiling, and below minima when relevant.

- ❌ Ignoring dew point spread in Brazilian fog season.  
  ✔ T/Td close together warns of fog — plan early.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Request Current Weather, Destination Below Minima

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, reh-KWEST KUR-ent WETH-er, des-ti-NAY-shun bih-LOH MIN-ih-muh

**Word Stress**

- METAR → MEE-tar or MET-ar (common aviation: MAY-tar / MET-ar — use clear syllables)
- Visibility → viz-ih-BIL-ih-tee
- Ceiling → SEE-ling
- Minima → MIN-ih-muh

Practice:

Request current weather... visibility... ceiling... below minima.

Together:

ANAC123, diverting due weather below minima, request vectors to alternate.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

METAR shows OVC004 and visibility 1 500 m. Your minima are higher.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

A SPECI improves visibility to 5 km but ceiling remains 400 feet. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, destination below minima, requesting hold or diversion. Second task: Ceiling still below minima — I would not land; I would continue holding only if improvement is expected soon per TAF/ATC, otherwise divert. Visibility alone does not fix a ceiling problem.
