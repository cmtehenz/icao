# Partial Panel

**Catalog ID:** 0257  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Partial Panel — instrument flight with one or more primary flight instruments inoperative or unreliable, requiring the pilot to fly using remaining instruments, cross-check carefully, and communicate the limitation clearly to ATC when IFR capability is reduced.

Partial panel is classic ICAO exam English and real IFR survival skill. Attitude, heading, or airspeed indications may be lost or misleading. The crew must identify what failed, fly what remains reliable, and avoid clearances that demand the lost instrument capability.

For H130 helicopter ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, partial-panel scenarios appear in instrument training and in electrical or instrument failures en route. Even with modern glass cockpits, the concept remains: degraded instrument presentation, limited IFR capability, and precise radio reporting of what you can and cannot do.

Understanding how to discuss partial panel in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Partial Panel is a flight condition in which one or more primary flight instruments are inoperative or unreliable, forcing the pilot to maintain aircraft control and navigation using the remaining serviceable instruments and to advise ATC when instrument capability for IFR flight is limited.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Painel parcial / Partial Panel / Instrumentos parciais.

Condição de voo em que um ou mais instrumentos primários de voo estão inoperantes ou não confiáveis, obrigando o piloto a manter o controle e a navegação com os instrumentos restantes utilizáveis e a informar o ATC quando a capacidade IFR estiver limitada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When partial panel occurs, the crew should:

- identify which instruments failed and which remain reliable
- fly using the remaining panel and AFM abnormal procedures
- avoid relying on failed or conflicting indications
- advise ATC of partial panel and the specific limitation
- request vectors, a simpler approach, VMC if available, or diversion as needed
- declare urgency if safe IFR continuation is threatened

Partial panel scenarios commonly include:

- attitude indicator failure with remaining turn and performance instruments
- heading indicator or compass system failure requiring magnetic compass techniques
- airspeed or altimeter anomalies requiring cross-check and ATC altitude confirmation
- electrical or vacuum-related instrument loss on applicable types
- H130 note: glass or standby instrument failures still use partial-panel English on exams

**Important:** Say what failed and what you need. "Partial panel, attitude indicator unreliable, request no-gyro vectors" is useful. "Instruments bad" is not. If you cannot safely continue IFR, say so and request an alternative.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to partial-panel reports to:

- provide no-gyro or simplified vectoring when heading instruments fail
- avoid complex clearances the crew cannot fly accurately
- offer priority, diversion, or VMC routing when available
- understand reduced IFR precision on approach
- coordinate the safest available approach or landing option

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR toward Navegantes in the H130. The attitude indication becomes unreliable. Standby instruments remain usable. You are in IMC.

You transmit:

ANAC123, partial panel, attitude indicator unreliable, request no-gyro vectors and priority approach Navegantes.

Approach: ANAC123, roger partial panel, turn left, stop turn — for no-gyro vectors, expect ILS runway zero seven.

You fly the remaining instruments. You keep the radio calm. You land with a known limitation instead of chasing a failed attitude display into a spiral.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger partial panel, say which instruments failed.

4. Approach: ANAC123, expect straight-in, simplified approach instructions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Partial panel is discipline under missing information.

The failed instrument is not the emergency by itself. Believing the failed instrument is the emergency. Cover it mentally. Fly the ones that still agree. Cross-check continuously.

On the ICAO exam, examiners listen for structure: identify the failure, state the limitation, request what ATC can give you — vectors, no-gyro, priority, diversion, or VMC.

In Brazilian IMC — coastal stratus, Serra weather, night IFR — partial panel is not academic. Keep the helicopter under control first. Then speak. Short. Specific. Honest about what you can still fly.

Do not accept a complex SID, STAR, or circling instruction if your remaining instruments cannot support it. Unable is a professional word.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your attitude indicator fails in IMC and you must continue on remaining instruments. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would maintain control using the remaining reliable instruments, apply the abnormal procedure, and stop using the failed attitude indication. I would advise ATC of partial panel with attitude indicator unreliable and request no-gyro or simplified vectors and priority approach or diversion as needed. If I could not safely continue IFR, I would state that clearly and request an alternative.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying only "instrument problem" without naming the failed instrument.  
  ✔ Specify: partial panel, attitude indicator unreliable, or heading indicator failed.

- ❌ Continuing to chase a failed attitude display in IMC.  
  ✔ Ignore the failed instrument; fly the remaining reliable panel.

- ❌ Accepting complex clearances you cannot fly accurately on partial panel.  
  ✔ Request simplified vectors, no-gyro, or diversion; say unable if required.

- ❌ Delaying the ATC report until the approach is already unstable.  
  ✔ Report early so ATC can simplify the clearance and protect you.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Partial panel is controlled flying with incomplete information.

Identify what failed. Trust what still agrees. Tell ATC the limitation in plain ICAO English.

Request the help that matches the failure — no-gyro, simplified vectors, priority, or diversion.

In the H130 academy and on the exam, calm specificity beats vague panic.

Fly the remaining panel. Speak the limitation. Land the plan.

That is how partial panel stays a procedure — not an accident.
