# Loss of Control In Flight

**Catalog ID:** 0483  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Loss of Control In Flight (LOC-I) — a departure from controlled flight in which the aircraft attitude, airspeed, or flight path can no longer be maintained within normal parameters by the crew.

LOC-I is a leading fatal outcome worldwide. Causes include stall, spatial disorientation, wake encounter, icing, mechanical failure, and inappropriate control inputs. The common thread is a rapid transition from normal flight to an upset.

In H130 operations, LOC-I risk appears in low-speed flight, vortex ring state margins, IMC upsets, and emergency maneuvering. Recognition, upset recovery, and clear emergency communication to ATC are critical.

Understanding how to declare and recover from LOC-I in ICAO-standard English supports Part 2, Part 3, and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Loss of Control In Flight is an unintended departure from controlled flight, involving aircraft attitudes or flight parameters outside the normal envelope, requiring immediate recovery actions to regain a safe flight path.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Perda de controle em voo / Loss of Control In Flight (LOC-I).

Partida não intencional do voo controlado, envolvendo atitudes ou parâmetros de voo fora do envelope normal, exigindo ações imediatas de recuperação para restabelecer uma trajetória segura.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When LOC-I occurs or is imminent, the crew should:

- recognize the upset early — unusual attitude, stall warning, uncommanded rates
- apply the aircraft-specific recovery: reduce angle of attack, regain attitude, then manage power
- aviate first — do not troubleshoot systems in the middle of an upset
- advise ATC when able: loss of control, recovering, requesting altitude or vectors
- after recovery, assess damage, controllability, and diversion needs

LOC-I prevention includes:

- respecting speed and bank limits in the H130
- avoiding aggressive maneuvering near terrain or in IMC without instrument references
- training upset recognition and recovery regularly
- managing startle so the first input is correct, not instinctive and wrong

**Important:** The first seconds decide LOC-I outcomes. Push or unload as required to reduce angle of attack, level the wings using attitude references, then restore a climb or level flight. Talk to ATC after the aircraft is flying again — or with a short emergency call if altitude is critical.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support LOC-I events by:

- providing immediate altitude blocks and traffic clearance
- issuing simple headings after recovery
- coordinating emergency descent or diversion
- keeping the frequency clear during recovery
- alerting rescue services if the crew declares an emergency

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

In IMC near Curitiba you enter an unusual nose-low bank after a spatial disorientation event. Airspeed rises. The aircraft is no longer in controlled normal flight.

You recover on instruments: wings level, pitch to horizon, power as required.

Then:

ANAC123, mayday mayday mayday, loss of control, recovering, request five thousand and vectors.

Radar: ANAC123, roger mayday, climb or maintain five thousand, turn heading zero nine zero when able, traffic cleared.

You respond:

ANAC123, maintaining five thousand, heading zero nine zero when able, recovering.

Control first. Radio second. Diversion third.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, roger mayday, altitude at your discretion, say intentions when able.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm recovering, cleared any altitude, traffic alerted.
3. Radar: ANAC123, turn heading zero nine zero when able, vectors to VMC.
4. Control: ANAC123, squawk seven seven zero zero, say souls on board when able.
5. Approach: ANAC123, airport twelve o'clock, fifteen miles, advise controllability.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, mayday, loss of control, recovering, request priority.
2. ANAC123, recovering, maintaining five thousand.
3. ANAC123, heading zero nine zero when able, request vectors to VMC.
4. ANAC123, squawking seven seven zero zero, will advise souls on board.
5. ANAC123, aircraft controllable, requesting diversion and emergency services.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

LOC-I is a race between recognition and terrain.

Startle makes people pull when they should unload, or roll the wrong way on a false horizon.

In the H130, train the recovery until it is boring. Unusual attitude in IMC is not the moment to invent technique.

Fly the recovery. Then make the mayday useful: state the problem, the altitude need, and the intention.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You experience an unusual attitude in IMC and recover. What do you say to ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

As soon as the aircraft is under control, or sooner if altitude is critical, I would declare: ANAC123, mayday, loss of control, recovering, request five thousand and vectors. I would then confirm controllability and request diversion or vectors to VMC. The priority sequence remains aviate, navigate, communicate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Transmitting a long story before recovering the aircraft.  
  ✔ Recover first; use a short mayday when altitude allows.

- ❌ Pulling instinctively through a stall or nose-high upset.  
  ✔ Reduce angle of attack per the upset recovery procedure.

- ❌ Failing to declare after a serious upset because "we recovered."  
  ✔ Declare and get priority — controllability and diversion may still be needed.

- ❌ Returning to the same IMC maneuvering without reassessment.  
  ✔ Request vectors to VMC or a stable approach after recovery.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

In IMC you depart controlled flight into a nose-low bank. You recover at roughly four thousand feet.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

Radar asks intentions. The aircraft is controllable but you are shaken and still in IMC. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, mayday, loss of control, recovering, request five thousand and vectors. Second task: ANAC123, aircraft controllable, request vectors to VMC and diversion. LOC-I ends only when the aircraft is safe on the ground or in stable VMC with a new plan — not merely when the wings are level again.
