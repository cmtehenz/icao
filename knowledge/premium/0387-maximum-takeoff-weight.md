# Maximum Takeoff Weight

**Catalog ID:** 0387  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Maximum Takeoff Weight (MTOW) — the highest weight at which the aircraft is certified or performance-allowed to begin the takeoff, limited by structure, performance, or both.

MTOW is a hard limit. Exceeding it is illegal and unsafe: climb gradients, reject margins, and structural loads are all based on approved weights.

For H130 Brazilian operations — hot days, high density altitude, and short pads — the performance-limited takeoff weight is often below structural MTOW. You must know the difference for Part 2 and Part 4 English and for ramp decisions with ATC.

## 📖 Meaning

Maximum Takeoff Weight is the maximum weight approved for takeoff, which may be the structural certified maximum or a lower weight limited by runway, climb, obstacle, or environmental performance for the specific departure.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Peso máximo de decolagem / Maximum Takeoff Weight (MTOW).

Peso máximo aprovado para decolagem, que pode ser o máximo estrutural certificado ou um peso menor limitado por pista, subida, obstáculos ou condições ambientais para a partida específica.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When dealing with MTOW, the crew should:

- identify structural MTOW from the AFM
- calculate performance-limited takeoff weight for today's conditions
- use the lower of structural and performance-limited weights
- offload fuel, passengers, or cargo if above the limit
- advise ATC if departure is delayed for weight reduction

MTOW decision-making includes:

- structural MTOW vs field-length / climb-limited weight
- density altitude reducing allowable takeoff weight
- obstacle-limited departure weight
- wet surface or tailwind reducing allowable weight
- company policy more restrictive than AFM when applicable

**Important:** "We are under MTOW" is incomplete if you mean only structural MTOW. On a hot Congonhas day, performance may limit you well below the placard number.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers see MTOW issues when:

- crews delay for offload or defuel
- aircraft report unable to depart overweight
- performance-limited departures need full runway
- weight reduction changes departure readiness time
- flights request return to stand after a weight error

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Structural MTOW allows your planned H130 load. Afternoon temperature at Congonhas does not. Performance charts limit takeoff weight below your current ramp weight.

You call:

São Paulo Congonhas Ground, ANAC123, unable departure, above performance-limited takeoff weight, request delay for offload.

Ground: ANAC123, roger, hold position, advise ready.

After offloading:

ANAC123, ready to taxi, takeoff weight within limits.

You respected the lower limit — the one that matters today.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Ground: ANAC123, advise ready to taxi.
2. Ground: ANAC123, roger delay for weight reduction.
3. Tower: ANAC123, say reason unable to depart.
4. Ground: ANAC123, taxi when weight confirmed within limits.
5. Tower: ANAC123, performance limited departure, full runway available.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Maximum takeoff weight has two faces: structural and performance.

Structural is the certified ceiling. Performance is what today's runway, temperature, and obstacles allow. You fly the lower number.

In Brazil's heat, the H130 often hits performance limits first. Do not argue with the chart because the placard MTOW is higher.

If you are heavy — offload. Tell ATC: delay for weight reduction, above takeoff weight for conditions. Clear and professional.

Never "burn down to weight" by idling with a taxi clearance if you are still above limits — fix the load, then call ready.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your aircraft is below structural MTOW but above the performance-limited takeoff weight for today's density altitude. Can you depart? What do you tell Ground?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I cannot depart. The applicable limit is the lower performance-limited weight. I would say: ANAC123, negative ready, above performance-limited takeoff weight, request delay for offload. After weight is within the performance limit, I would call ready. Structural MTOW alone is not sufficient authorization.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using only structural MTOW and ignoring performance charts.  
  ✔ Use the more restrictive weight for today's conditions.

- ❌ Calling ready while still above the applicable takeoff weight.  
  ✔ Offload first; then ready to taxi.

- ❌ Not explaining the delay to ATC.  
  ✔ State: delay for weight reduction / above takeoff weight for conditions.

- ❌ Adding last-minute fuel without rechecking takeoff weight.  
  ✔ Recalculate after any fuel or load change.

Maximum takeoff weight is the lower of structure and performance.

Hot Brazilian days cut the number. Charts rule.
