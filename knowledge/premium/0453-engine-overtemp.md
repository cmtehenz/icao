# Engine Overtemp

**Catalog ID:** 0453  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Engine Overtemp — turbine temperature (T4 / TOT / ITT as applicable) exceeding published limits, indicating the engine is producing more heat than the limits allow for continued normal operation.

Overtemperature damages hot-section components quickly. The pilot must reduce power to bring temperature within limits, land as soon as practicable or possible depending on severity, and communicate clearly with ATC.

For H130 helicopter operations, engine overtemp is a critical powerplant emergency. Protect the engine, protect the landing site options, and use Mayday when temperature cannot be controlled or power is required for flight.

Understanding how to report engine overtemp in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Engine Overtemp is a condition in which engine turbine temperature exceeds the maximum permitted limit for the existing power setting or flight condition, risking hot-section damage or engine failure.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Sobretemperatura do motor (overtemp).

Condição em que a temperatura da turbina do motor excede o limite máximo permitido para a potência ou condição de voo existente, com risco de dano à seção quente ou falha do motor.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When engine overtemp occurs, the pilot should:

- reduce collective and power immediately to bring temperature within limits
- monitor Ng, T4, torque, and oil temperature
- land as soon as practicable if temperature returns to limits
- land as soon as possible and declare Mayday if temperature remains high or power must be kept high to fly
- prepare for power loss if the engine continues to degrade

Engine overtemp reporting commonly includes:

- engine overtemp or turbine temperature exceedance
- reducing power, request priority or immediate landing
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested
- possible engine failure on approach if trend continues

**Important:** Flying with sustained overtemp to "save time" destroys the engine and may leave you with none. Reduce power. Land. Speak.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to engine overtemp to:

- provide priority routing to a suitable landing site
- clear traffic from a possible engine-out approach path
- coordinate emergency services
- avoid climbs or holds that demand high power
- request souls on board and fuel remaining

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are climbing out of Congonhas in your H130.

T4 exceeds the limit. You stop the climb, reduce collective, and temperature begins to fall toward the limit. You level at a safe altitude and plan an immediate return.

You declare Pan Pan initially, then upgrade to Mayday when temperature remains near the red line with the power needed to maintain altitude. Approach clears you for priority landing. You land with minimum power and request fire services.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, engine overtemp, say intentions.
2. Approach: ANAC123, roger Mayday, turn left heading three six zero, Congonhas five miles.
3. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land runway one seven left, emergency services rolling.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say temperature trend and souls on board.
5. Tower: ANAC123, wind three four zero at one two, report short final.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, ANAC123, engine overtemp, reducing power, request priority return Congonhas.
2. Mayday Mayday Mayday, ANAC123, engine overtemp, temperature not controlling, request immediate landing.
3. ANAC123, Mayday, engine overtemp, souls on board four, returning Congonhas.
4. ANAC123, turning left heading three six zero, descending for landing.
5. Tower, ANAC123, short final, Mayday engine overtemp, request fire services.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Temperature is life for a turbine.

When T4 is past the limit, every second at high power writes damage into the hot section — or worse, sets up a failure.

On the H130, reduce collective first. Do not keep climbing into an overtemp. Level, cool, and land.

If you can maintain safe flight with temperature in limits, Pan Pan and priority landing may suffice. If you need overtemp power just to stay in the air, or temperature will not come down — Mayday.

After landing, record the exceedance. Maintenance must inspect before the next flight. Do not treat a cooled gauge as a cleared engine.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

T4 exceeds limits in climb. Temperature falls when you reduce power. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, engine overtemp, reducing power, temperature returning toward limits, request priority landing nearest suitable. If temperature stays high I would upgrade to Mayday.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing the climb to cruise with T4 over red line.  
  ✔ Reduce power immediately and land as required by the AFM.

- ❌ Saying only "engine problem" without overtemp.  
  ✔ State engine overtemp or turbine temperature exceedance.

- ❌ Declining priority because the temperature "came back."  
  ✔ Still land for inspection after an exceedance.

- ❌ Using high power on final after an overtemp event.  
  ✔ Plan a minimum-power approach consistent with safety.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Engine overtemp is a limit you do not negotiate with.

Reduce power. Cool the turbine. Land.

Pan Pan when controllable. Mayday when temperature or power leaves you no safe option.

On the H130, protect the hot section — and the landing that follows an exceedance belongs to maintenance, not to the next leg.
