# Cross-Checking

**Catalog ID:** 0461  
**Category:** CRM  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Cross-Checking — verifying critical information against an independent source so that single-point errors are trapped before they affect the flight path.

Cross-checking is how crews catch wrong altitudes, wrong runways, wrong modes, and wrong fuel figures. One pilot sets. The other verifies. Instruments confirm charts. Clearances confirm FMS. Assumptions confirm nothing.

For H130 IFR and VFR operations in Brazil, cross-checking supports Part 1 CRM vocabulary and Part 2 radio accuracy — especially when confirming altitudes, runways, and routing with ATC.

## 📖 Meaning

Cross-Checking is the systematic comparison of critical flight information against an independent source — another pilot, another instrument, a chart, or ATC confirmation — to detect and correct errors before they result in an undesired aircraft state.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Checagem cruzada / Cross-Checking.

Comparação sistemática de informações críticas de voo com uma fonte independente — outro piloto, outro instrumento, uma carta ou confirmação do controle — para detectar e corrigir erros antes que resultem em um estado indesejado da aeronave.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When cross-checking, the crew should:

- verify altitude alerter and MCP or bug settings against the clearance
- compare FMS routing with the charted procedure and ATC clearance
- confirm runway and approach identifier before arming or briefing
- check fuel figures against totalizer and planned remaining
- challenge mismatches immediately and resolve before continuing

Cross-checking includes:

- pilot-to-pilot verification of mode and target changes
- raw data versus computed navigation
- ATC confirmation when the clearance is ambiguous
- independent instrument comparison after failures

**Important:** If two sources disagree, do not average them. Identify the correct source, or ask ATC / use raw data until resolved.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support cross-checking when crews:

- confirm runway, altitude, or squawk after doubt
- report altitude for verification against radar
- request confirmation of complex clearances
- advise discrepancies between clearance and published procedure
- correct readback errors discovered by the monitoring pilot

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You load the ILS for runway one six at Confins. Approach clears you for runway one four.

Your first officer cross-checks the clearance against the FMS and charts.

You transmit:

Approach, ANAC123, confirm cleared ILS runway one four, we had one six loaded.

Approach: ANAC123, affirm ILS runway one four, report established.

You reload, cross-check identifiers, and continue. The mismatch was trapped.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, cleared ILS runway one four, report established.
2. Approach: ANAC123, affirm runway one four, not one six.
3. Radar: ANAC123, confirm altitude, radar shows you high.
4. Tower: ANAC123, confirm cleared to land runway two eight left.
5. Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain four thousand, confirm leaving three thousand.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, confirm cleared ILS runway one four, reloading.
2. ANAC123, cleared ILS one four, will report established.
3. ANAC123, leveling four thousand, altitude checks.
4. ANAC123, cleared to land runway two eight left, confirm.
5. ANAC123, leaving three thousand, climbing four thousand.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Cross-checking is distrust with manners.

Not distrust of people — distrust of single sources. One wrong digit is enough.

Set. Verify. Point. Agree. That rhythm belongs on every altitude and runway change.

In the H130, with high workload near terrain, the monitoring pilot's cross-check is your second brain. Invite it. Never punish a challenge.

If the FMS and the chart disagree, the chart and the clearance win until proven otherwise. Magenta is not truth. It is a suggestion that must be cross-checked.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The FMS shows runway one six but ATC cleared runway one four. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would not continue on the wrong procedure. I would cross-check the clearance with the chart and transmit: ANAC123, confirm cleared ILS runway one four. After affirmation, I would reload, cross-check the identifier with the other pilot, and then continue.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Trusting the FMS without checking the clearance.  
  ✔ Clearance and chart first; FMS must match both.

- ❌ Changing altitude bugs without the other pilot verifying.  
  ✔ Call the set; require a cross-check response.

- ❌ Ignoring a small mismatch "to save time."  
  ✔ Resolve mismatches before the next phase of flight.

- ❌ Feeling offended when challenged.  
  ✔ Treat challenges as cross-checks, not criticism.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**CHECK** — **C**ompare two sources, **H**alt if they disagree, **E**nquire with ATC if needed, **C**orrect the setup, **K**eep flying only when matched.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

ATC clears you to four thousand. Your altitude alerter shows three thousand. Radar later asks you to confirm altitude.

**Your first task**

What cross-check and radio call do you make when you notice the mismatch?

**Your second task**

Radar says you appear high. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Stop the incorrect setup, verify the clearance, set four thousand, and if unsure transmit: ANAC123, confirm maintain four thousand. Second task: ANAC123, leveling four thousand, altitude checks — or report actual altitude if still climbing. Cross-checking closes the gap between what was said and what was set.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Cross-checking is how single errors die quietly.

Two sources. One truth. Immediate challenge.

In the H130, wrong runway or wrong altitude is not a paperwork issue — it is terrain and traffic. Verify every critical set. Confirm with ATC when doubt remains.

That is CRM you can hear on the frequency and see on the panel.
