# Rotor Brake Engagement

**Catalog ID:** 0413  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Rotor Brake Engagement — applying the rotor brake after landing or shutdown to stop main rotor rotation safely and allow passengers, ground crew, and maintenance to approach.

Rotor brake engagement is a ground procedure with radio implications: you must not clear passengers or report "rotors stopped" until the rotor is actually secured.

For H130 operations in Brazil — Congonhas, Santos Dumont, hospital pads, and offshore decks — clear English about rotor status prevents ground personnel from walking into a turning disc.

Understanding how to report rotor brake engagement and rotor status in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Rotor Brake Engagement is the application of the helicopter rotor brake after landing or engine shutdown to decelerate and stop the main rotor, confirming a safe condition for passenger egress and ground operations around the aircraft.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Acionamento do freio de rotor.

Aplicação do freio de rotor do helicóptero após o pouso ou o desligamento do motor para desacelerar e parar o rotor principal, confirmando condição segura para desembarque de passageiros e operações de solo ao redor da aeronave.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When engaging the rotor brake, the crew should:

- complete landing and shutdown checklist items before brake application as per AFM
- apply rotor brake only within approved RPM and wind limits
- confirm rotors stopped before reporting passengers clear to disembark
- advise ground or ATC when ready for shutdown complete / rotors stopped if required
- never signal approach to the aircraft while blades are still turning

Rotor brake reporting commonly includes:

- landed, shutting down
- rotors stopping
- rotors stopped, passengers disembarking
- ready for ground handling

**Important:** "Landed" is not "safe to approach." Only report rotors stopped when the disc is fully stopped and the brake is set as required by procedure.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers and ground agencies care about rotor status to:

- know when the pad or stand is clear for the next movement
- coordinate passenger and ambulance approach on HEMS pads
- sequence the next helicopter into a single-pad site
- confirm shutdown complete for noise or curfew reporting
- avoid clearing ground vehicles toward a turning rotor

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You land your H130 on the hospital helipad in São Paulo after a HEMS flight. Tower asks when the pad will be clear for the next arrival.

You shut down, engage the rotor brake within limits, and wait until the blades stop.

Tower, rotors stopped, passengers disembarking, pad will be clear in five minutes.

Ambulance approaches only after your confirmation. The next helicopter holds until you report clear.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, report when rotors stopped and pad available.
2. Tower: ANAC123, advise shutdown complete.
3. Ground: ANAC123, confirm safe for ambulance approach.
4. Tower: ANAC123, next helicopter inbound in one zero minutes, say pad status.
5. Tower: ANAC123, roger rotors stopped, report when vacating pad.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, wilco, will report rotors stopped.
2. ANAC123, shutdown complete, rotors stopped.
3. ANAC123, safe for ambulance approach, rotors stopped.
4. ANAC123, pad occupied, clear in approximately five minutes.
5. ANAC123, vacating pad, ready for next arrival.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Rotor brake English is ground-safety English.

ATC and medical crews will push for speed on a busy Brazilian hospital pad. Your job is accurate status, not optimistic status.

In the H130, engage the brake per the AFM — correct RPM band, correct technique. Then say "rotors stopped" only when it is true.

If wind or a hot brake delays the stop, say so: rotors still turning, stand by. Never clear an ambulance into a turning disc to save face on frequency.

Shutdown complete and rotors stopped are two different milestones. Know which one they asked for.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

After landing on a hospital helipad, Tower asks when the pad will be available. What do you report regarding the rotor brake?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would shut down and engage the rotor brake per procedure, then report only when accurate: rotors stopped, passengers disembarking, pad clear in five minutes. If blades are still turning, I would say: rotors still turning, stand by for stopped. I would not clear ground personnel until the rotor is stopped.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "pode aproximar" / "safe to approach" while blades still move.  
  ✔ Wait for full stop; then report rotors stopped, safe for approach.

- ❌ Confusing "landed" with "shutdown complete."  
  ✔ Landed means on the pad; rotors stopped means the disc is secured.

- ❌ Rushing brake engagement outside AFM limits.  
  ✔ Follow RPM and wind limits; report delay if needed.

- ❌ Not updating Tower when pad clearance time changes.  
  ✔ Give a revised estimate so the next helicopter can plan.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**BRAKE** — **B**rake only in limits, **R**eport stopped not landing, **A**llow approach only after stop, **K**eep pad status updated, **E**gress passengers when disc is secure.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You land at a busy São Paulo hospital pad. An ambulance is waiting. Tower wants the pad for another HEMS inbound.

**Your first task**

Ambulance asks by radio relay if they can approach. Blades are still turning. What do you say?

**Your second task**

Rotors are now stopped. Tower asks pad status. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, negative, rotors still turning, stand by — do not clear the ambulance yet. Second task: ANAC123, rotors stopped, safe for ambulance approach, pad clear in approximately five minutes after passenger transfer. Accurate rotor status protects lives on the ground.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Rotor brake engagement ends the flight and starts ground safety.

Engage in limits. Confirm the stop. Then speak.

In the H130 on Brazilian pads, people will crowd the disc if you let them. Your radio call is the barrier.

Landed is not stopped. Stopped is not optional wording. Say it only when it is true.
