# Cleared Straight-In Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0103  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers cleared straight-in approach — an ATC clearance authorizing an aircraft to proceed directly to the runway or landing area from the current position without entering a traffic pattern or circuit.

Cleared Straight-In Approach is issued when the aircraft is already aligned on a final approach course or positioned for a direct final.

In the H130, cleared straight-in approach is common at Navegantes, Florianópolis, and Joinville during IFR arrivals or when ATC sequences you directly to final.

You must understand the clearance, readback correctly, and execute the approach to the designated runway without breaking off for a circuit.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 critical phraseology.

## 📖 Meaning

Cleared Straight-In Approach is an ATC clearance authorizing the pilot to proceed directly from the current position to a landing on the specified runway or landing area, without executing a traffic pattern or circuit entry.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Autorizado aproximação direta (straight-in approach).

Autorização do ATC para que o piloto prossiga diretamente de sua posição atual para pouso na pista ou área de pouso designada, sem executar um circuito de tráfego.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Cleared Straight-In Approach is issued, the pilot should:

- acknowledge the clearance with runway designation and callsign
- confirm current position allows a stable straight-in final
- establish on the final approach course and stabilize
- advise ATC if unable to accept the straight-in
- report final when established if required by local procedures

Cleared Straight-In Approach reporting commonly includes:

- readback cleared straight-in runway designation
- report final established
- unable straight-in, request circuit
- going around from straight-in approach

**Important:** A straight-in approach bypasses the circuit. Ensure you are stable, configured, and on speed before crossing the threshold. Do not attempt a straight-in if you are not in a position for a stabilized approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC issues Cleared Straight-In Approach to:

- reduce circuit workload and improve traffic flow
- sequence IFR aircraft directly to the runway
- accommodate aircraft already established on final
- expedite arrivals during busy traffic periods
- accommodate instrument approach procedures

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Navegantes from the north in your H130, descending from two thousand feet.

Approach advises you are number one and vectors you directly to final for runway two nine.

Tower transmits:

ANAC123, cleared straight-in approach runway two nine, wind two three zero at twelve, report final.

You readback:

Cleared straight-in runway two nine, ANAC123, wilco.

You establish on final, report final, and land.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, cleared straight-in approach runway two nine, wind two three zero at one two.
2. Tower: ANAC123, cleared straight-in, report final.
3. Tower: ANAC123, number one, cleared straight-in runway two nine, no delay.
4. Tower: ANAC123, straight-in runway two nine approved, cleared to land.
5. Tower: ANAC123, confirm able to accept straight-in approach.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Cleared straight-in runway two nine, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, cleared straight-in runway two nine, wilco, will report final.
3. ANAC123, unable straight-in, request circuit, not stabilized.
4. Tower, ANAC123, final, straight-in runway two nine.
5. ANAC123, going around from straight-in, request another approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Straight-in approach — no circuit. No pattern. Directly to final.

ATC sends you straight. You must be ready.

The sequence:

Hear the clearance. Check configuration. Confirm you can stabilize. Accept or decline.

Brazilian pilots sometimes accept a straight-in when they are too high or too fast.

Decline if not in position. Request a circuit or go around.

A forced straight-in that is not stabilized is worse than a missed approach.

At Navegantes, straight-ins from the north are common when the ILS is in use.

Know the phrase. Know your stabilization criteria. Make the right call.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you do if ATC cleared you straight-in but you were too high for a stabilized approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would advise ATC that I am unable to accept the straight-in due to a high and unstable approach, and request a circuit to establish properly. I would say: ANAC123, unable straight-in, request circuit, too high for stabilized approach. A go-around and circuit is always preferable to a forced unstabilized landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting a straight-in clearance when positioned too high or fast.  
  ✔ Advise unable if not in a position for a stabilized straight-in.

- ❌ Readback only with "entendido" or callsign without the runway.  
  ✔ Readback: cleared straight-in runway (designation), ANAC123.

- ❌ Not reporting final when required by Tower.  
  ✔ Report final when Tower requests: ANAC123, final runway two nine.

- ❌ Continuing an unstabilized straight-in rather than going around.  
  ✔ Go around early if the approach is not stabilized by the gate altitude.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Cleared Straight-In Runway Two Niner, ANAC123

**Pronunciation:** kleerd STRAYT-in RUN-way too NY-ner, AY-nak wun too three

**Word Stress**

- Cleared → kleerd
- Straight-in → STRAYT-in
- Runway → RUN-way

Practice:

Straight-in... runway... two nine...

Together:

Cleared straight-in runway two nine, ANAC123.

Speak clearly — straight-in must be distinct from other approach types.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**FINAL** — **F**ly directly to the threshold, **I**nstability means go around, **N**o circuit required, **A**cknowledge with runway and callsign, **L**and or go around — no middle ground.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are inbound from the north to Navegantes at two thousand feet. Tower clears you: ANAC123, cleared straight-in approach runway two nine.

**Your first task**

You are currently five miles out at two thousand feet in a helicopter. Are you able to accept the straight-in?

**Your second task**

If you cannot stabilize for the straight-in, what is your radio call?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

At two thousand feet, five miles out in the H130, I would assess my descent capability. If I can configure and stabilize by five hundred feet AGL, I would accept. If not, I would advise: ANAC123, unable straight-in, request circuit, high and not configured. A go-around and circuit protects the approach stability standard.
