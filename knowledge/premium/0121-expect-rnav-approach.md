# Expect RNAV Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0121  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Expect RNAV Approach — ATC's advisory that a satellite or area navigation approach has been assigned for your arrival.

RNAV approaches are increasingly common at Brazilian airports, including Curitiba, Navegantes, Joinville, and São Paulo.

Unlike the ILS, an RNAV approach uses GPS or FMS-computed guidance, which means different minima, different monitoring, and different briefing requirements.

This lesson connects Part 2 phraseology with Part 4 operational language — you must speak correctly about navigation type and approach characteristics.

## 📖 Meaning

Expect RNAV Approach is an ATC advisory indicating that an Area Navigation approach, typically GPS-based, has been assigned for the destination runway, allowing the crew to load, verify, and brief the procedure before the formal clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Espera aproximação RNAV.

Aviso do controle de tráfego aéreo indicando que uma aproximação por navegação de área, tipicamente baseada em GPS, foi atribuída para a pista de destino, permitindo que a tripulação carregue, verifique e realize o briefing antes da autorização formal.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew receives Expect RNAV Approach, they should:

- load and activate the RNAV approach procedure in the FMS or GPS
- verify the approach type: RNAV (GNSS), LPV, LNAV/VNAV, or LNAV
- review the applicable minima based on available vertical guidance
- brief the missed approach procedure
- confirm GPS RAIM prediction or system readiness

Expect RNAV Approach briefings commonly include:

- approach type and minima line
- final approach course and step-down or vertical path
- missed approach altitude, heading, and holding fix
- system annunciation to expect during the approach
- any NOTAM-based GNSS restrictions in the area

**Important:** RNAV approaches require system validation before commencement. Confirm GPS reception, FMS database currency, and RAIM availability. An RNAV approach without valid system integrity must be refused.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Expect RNAV Approach to:

- assign a non-precision or APV approach at airports without ILS
- provide approach planning information in areas with GNSS infrastructure
- allow the crew to verify and load the procedure before the clearance
- sequence arrivals efficiently when ILS approaches are not available
- specify approach type clearly when multiple RNAV variants exist

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Joinville in your H130.

The aerodrome has an RNAV (GNSS) approach to runway one five.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, expect RNAV approach runway one five, advise GNSS available.

You check your GPS: system normal, RAIM available.

You respond:

Expect RNAV approach runway one five, GNSS available, ANAC123.

You load the approach, brief the procedure, confirm minima and missed approach.

Approach later issues the clearance.

You fly the approach fully briefed, confident in the system and the procedure.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The RNAV approach is modern aviation's most common approach type at regional airports.

And yet many pilots brief it less carefully than the ILS.

That is a mistake.

The RNAV approach has its own traps: RAIM faults, database expiry, incorrect minima selection, and autopilot mode confusion.

When ATC says "expect RNAV approach," your first question to yourself is: is my system valid?

Check RAIM. Check the database date. Confirm the correct approach is loaded.

In the H130, you may be flying the approach with raw GPS data.

Know the difference between LNAV minima and LPV minima.

Know which your system can support.

Brief both the approach and the missed approach as if the missed is likely.

That discipline makes the difference.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

How would you confirm readiness for an RNAV approach after receiving the Expect advisory?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After reading back the advisory, I would confirm that the GPS or FMS showed normal operation, that RAIM was available for the approach, and that the navigation database was current. I would load and verify the correct RNAV approach procedure, brief the minima appropriate to my system capability, and complete the missed approach brief. If the system showed any degradation or RAIM fault, I would advise ATC that I was unable to accept the RNAV approach and request an alternative.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting an RNAV approach without verifying RAIM availability.  
  ✔ Confirm RAIM and system integrity before accepting.

- ❌ Using LNAV minima when LPV is available and the system supports it.  
  ✔ Select the lowest applicable minima your system is certified for.

- ❌ Treating RNAV approach advisory as an ILS advisory — same brief style.  
  ✔ RNAV has unique considerations: system type, database, RAIM, step-downs.

- ❌ Not advising ATC of GNSS unavailability until already on the approach.  
  ✔ Advise ATC as early as possible if GNSS is degraded or RAIM unavailable.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Expect RNAV Approach

**Pronunciation:** eks-PEKT AR-nav uh-PROHCH

**Word Stress**

- Expect → eks-PEKT
- RNAV → AR-nav (two syllables, stress on first)
- Approach → uh-PROHCH

Practice:

Expect... RNAV... Approach... Expect RNAV Approach.

Together:

Expect RNAV approach runway one five, ANAC123.

Avoid: *R-N-A-V* (letter by letter) — say AR-nav as two syllables in conversational context.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**RAIM** — **R**RAIM available confirmed, **A**pproach loaded and verified, **I**dentify correct minima, **M**issed approach briefed.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Joinville Approach transmits: "ANAC123, expect RNAV approach runway one five, advise GNSS available."

**Your first task**

Your GPS shows normal operation and RAIM is available. What do you say?

**Your second task**

Your GPS shows a RAIM fault warning. What do you say to ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

If GNSS is available, I would respond: Expect RNAV approach runway one five, GNSS available, ANAC123. If RAIM was faulted, I would advise: ANAC123, unable RNAV approach, GNSS not available, request alternate approach. Informing ATC early allows time for an ILS or other approach to be assigned before the arrival sector.
