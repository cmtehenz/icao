# Top of Descent

**Catalog ID:** 0185  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Top of Descent — the geographic point along the flight path where descent from cruise altitude begins in order to arrive at the destination or initial approach fix at the correct altitude.

Top of Descent is not just a FMS waypoint. It is a planning discipline.

A well-planned descent begins at the right point, follows the right profile, and arrives at the terminal area at the correct altitude — neither too high nor too low. An unplanned or late descent creates pressure on the crew, forces steep descents to catch up, and puts the aircraft behind the approach.

For H130 helicopter operations, top of descent is relevant primarily in IFR cruise operations at flight levels. While the descent profile is more flexible for a helicopter than for a jet, the discipline of calculating, briefing, and executing a planned top of descent is part of professional IFR operations on every flight.

Understanding how to discuss top of descent in ICAO-standard English — including requests, advisories, and reports to ATC — is essential for Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Top of Descent is the calculated point along the flight path at which an aircraft should begin its descent from cruise altitude in order to arrive at the terminal area or initial approach fix at the required altitude, calculated using the aircraft's descent performance, the distance to the destination, and any ATC altitude constraints along the route.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Ponto de início de descida / Top of Descent (TOD).

Ponto calculado ao longo da trajetória de voo no qual a aeronave deve iniciar a descida da altitude de cruzeiro para chegar à área terminal ou ao fix de aproximação inicial na altitude necessária, calculado com base no desempenho de descida da aeronave, na distância até o destino e em quaisquer restrições de altitude do controle ao longo da rota.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When planning and executing the top of descent, the crew should:

- calculate the top of descent during cruise using current ground speed, altitude to lose, and target altitude
- load the TOD point into the FMS or note the geographic reference for manual calculation
- brief the descent profile: target altitudes at each waypoint, expected ATC descent clearances, STAR restrictions
- request descent clearance from ATC before reaching TOD if no clearance has been received
- advise ATC when commencing descent: ANAC123, leaving flight level nine zero, descending to flight level six zero
- monitor altitude profile throughout the descent and advise ATC if unable to comply with restrictions

Top of Descent planning includes:

- standard three-to-one descent rule: three nautical miles per one thousand feet of altitude to lose
- adjustment for head and tailwind component
- STAR altitude restrictions that may require earlier or steeper descent
- holding patterns that may precede the terminal area
- ATC-assigned altitudes that may alter the TOD calculation

**Important:** If ATC has not issued a descent clearance by the time you reach your calculated top of descent, request descent. Do not begin descending without clearance. ATC may have a reason to hold you at cruise level — traffic below, airspace restrictions, sequencing. Request early and allow time for ATC to respond.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with top of descent to:

- sequence arriving aircraft with appropriate spacing from cruise level to the terminal area
- assign descent clearances at the optimal time for traffic flow
- coordinate between en-route sectors and approach control
- prevent late, steep descents that reduce stability for approach sequencing
- manage the transition between cruise altitude and terminal area flow

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at flight level nine zero from Congonhas to Florianópolis, one hundred and twenty miles to run.

You calculate top of descent: ninety thousand feet divided by three is thirty nautical miles from the destination per thousand feet. Ninety times three: two hundred and seventy nautical miles — but you only have ninety miles. Adjust for H130 descent rate. You plan to begin descent in forty miles.

At one hundred miles from Florianópolis you call:

ANAC123, one hundred miles from Florianópolis, request descent.

Radar: ANAC123, standby for descent.

At eighty miles, you are approaching your planned top of descent.

ANAC123, approaching top of descent, request descent clearance.

Radar: ANAC123, descend and maintain flight level six zero, expect further descent in twenty miles.

You respond:

ANAC123, leaving flight level nine zero, descending flight level six zero.

You begin the descent. Planned. Controlled. On profile.

5. Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain four thousand, expect ILS runway one four.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Top of descent is where the approach begins.

Not at the initial approach fix. Not when you load the ILS. At the top of descent — when you commit to the altitude profile that gets you to the terminal area correctly.

Begin descent too late — you arrive high, rushing, and out of profile. You load the crew with workload and pressure the approach.

Begin descent too early — you arrive low on energy, potentially below safe crossing altitudes for terrain.

The three-to-one rule is a tool. Three nautical miles per thousand feet of altitude to lose, adjusted for wind.

Use it. But also listen for ATC restrictions. A STAR with a restriction at sixty miles may require an earlier descent. An ATC hold may delay the TOD entirely.

In the H130, the descent rate is highly variable — you can steepen quickly if needed. That flexibility does not replace planning. Use it to recover. Do not use it as a reason not to plan.

Request descent before you need it. ATC may hold you longer than expected. Give yourself margin.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You are cruising at flight level nine zero with ninety nautical miles to your destination. ATC has not issued a descent clearance. Describe what you do.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would calculate my top of descent based on the three-to-one rule and H130 descent performance. With ninety thousand feet to lose to reach traffic pattern altitude — approximately roughly flight level nine zero to two thousand — I would need substantial distance. I would request descent with sufficient margin: ANAC123, ninety miles from destination, request descent. If ATC asks me to standby, I would advise when I am approaching my calculated TOD: ANAC123, approaching top of descent, request clearance. If descent is still not received at TOD, I would advise: ANAC123, at top of descent, require descent clearance for altitude profile. ATC may then expedite or ask me to hold for sequencing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Waiting until directly over the destination to request descent.  
  ✔ Request descent eighty to one hundred miles out to give ATC time to coordinate.

- ❌ Beginning descent without ATC clearance because the TOD has passed.  
  ✔ Never descend without clearance. Request first, advise urgency if needed.

- ❌ Not monitoring the altitude profile against STAR restrictions during descent.  
  ✔ Cross-check published STAR altitudes at each fix against your descent profile.

- ❌ Not advising ATC when leaving cruise altitude.  
  ✔ Report: ANAC123, leaving flight level nine zero, descending flight level six zero.

- Approaching → uh-PRO-ching (three syllables, stress on second)
- Top → TOP

Practice:

Approaching top of descent... request descent clearance.

Together:

ANAC123, approaching top of descent, request descent clearance, flight level nine zero to Florianópolis.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**DESCENT** — **D**etermine TOD before cruise ends, **E**stimate using three-to-one rule, **S**tart descent request eighty miles out, **C**onfirm STAR restrictions on the descent profile, **E**nunciate leaving cruise when cleared, **N**ever descend without clearance, **T**rack altitude profile throughout.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are cruising at flight level nine zero from Curitiba to Florianópolis. You have one hundred miles to run. Radar has issued no descent clearance.

**Your first task**

At eighty miles, you calculate your top of descent is at sixty miles. What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

Radar responds: "ANAC123, expect descent in ten miles, stand by." You reach your calculated TOD at sixty miles with no clearance. Traffic is reported below. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, eighty miles from Florianópolis, request descent. Second task: ANAC123, at top of descent, require descent clearance, current flight level nine zero — to advise ATC of my situation without descending without clearance. I would not descend into the traffic below without authorization. If ATC still cannot issue clearance immediately, I might request: ANAC123, able to hold for sequencing if required. The decision to descend belongs to ATC. My job is to communicate my profile requirements clearly and promptly.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Top of descent marks the transition from cruise to arrival.

Everything before it is route. Everything after it is the approach — starting with the descent profile.

Plan it. Calculate it. Brief it.

Request descent before you arrive at the TOD point, not after you pass it.

If ATC is not ready — advise them of your profile requirement. They will sequence you as efficiently as possible.

In the H130, you have flexibility. But flexibility is not permission to be unplanned.

Know your TOD. Know your profile. Request early. Descend on clearance.

That is how you arrive at the terminal area in control of the aircraft — not chasing it.
