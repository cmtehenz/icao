# Past Experience Narrative

**Catalog ID:** 0509  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Past Experience Narrative — the ICAO oral exam task in which you tell a clear story about something that happened in your flying past, using past tenses at Level 4 or above.

Past experience narrative is not a random memory dump. It is a controlled story with a beginning, middle, and end.

In Part 1, the examiner asks about a difficult landing, a weather delay, a memorable training flight, or a problem you solved. You must select relevant details, keep chronology clear, and finish with what you learned or what the outcome was.

For H130 ICAO Delta candidates, narrative skill shows you can move beyond phraseology into extended discourse — a core Level 4 requirement.

Understanding how to narrate past experience in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Past Experience Narrative is the ICAO speaking task type in which the candidate recounts a real or plausible aviation experience in chronological order — setting the scene, describing events, and stating the outcome or lesson — so that past-tense control, coherence, and fluency can be assessed at Operational Level 4 or higher.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When narrating a past experience, the candidate should:

- set the scene: when, where, aircraft type, flight phase
- tell events in time order using past simple and past continuous
- keep the story to one main problem or theme
- include what you did, not only what happened around you
- close with outcome and brief lesson learned
- for H130: use helicopter experiences when natural — confined area, IFR cruise, offshore
- stop after two minutes of clear story rather than wandering

A Level 4 narrative frame:

- setting
- complication
- actions taken
- result
- lesson

**Important:** Inventing an impossible epic is unnecessary. A simple true-to-life story told clearly scores higher than a dramatic confused tale. If you lack a real event, use a realistic training scenario and keep it coherent.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Past narrative tasks help examiners to:

- elicit extended discourse beyond short answers
- test past-tense accuracy under speaking pressure
- assess coherence and relevance
- open follow-up interaction about decisions made
- distinguish Level 4 storytelling from memorized phrase lists

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Examiner: Tell me about a time weather affected your flight.

You narrate: Last year I was flying an H130 from Congonhas to a hospital helipad. While we were cruising, fog formed at the destination. I checked fuel and the alternate, coordinated with ATC, and diverted to the airport. We landed safely and the patient transferred by ground. I learned to brief diversion triggers earlier in the flight.

Scene. Problem. Action. Result. Lesson. The examiner can ask one follow-up and you are ready.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Examiner: ANAC123, tell me about a past flight when you had to divert.
2. Radar: ANAC123, confirm you diverted due weather on that occasion — say again the alternate used.
3. Approach: ANAC123, report when you previously held for weather, advise typical delay.
4. Examiner: ANAC123, describe what happened after the abnormal indication.
5. Tower: ANAC123, say again how you vacated after that landing.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, on that flight I diverted to Congonhas due fog at destination.
2. ANAC123, alternate used was Congonhas, weather was below minima at destination.
3. ANAC123, typical weather hold was about ten minutes, then diversion.
4. ANAC123, after the abnormal indication I landed as soon as practical.
5. ANAC123, after landing I vacated via the taxiway as instructed.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Narratives need a spine: setting, problem, action, result.

Without a spine, candidates jump around in time and lose the examiner. Use time markers: first, then, after that, finally.

Past continuous sets the background ("I was cruising"); past simple moves the plot ("I diverted"). That mix sounds natural at Level 4.

Keep yourself in the story as the decision-maker. "Things happened" is weak. "I decided / I requested / I landed" is strong.

Do not confess regulatory violations for drama. Tell a professional story. The exam is language assessment, not a safety investigation.

H130 stories are rich: site reconnaissance that changed the plan, a go-around from a pad, a medical transfer. Choose one and tell it cleanly.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Tell me about a difficult approach you flew. Structure a Level 4 narrative.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Two years ago I flew an H130 visual approach to a rooftop helipad in gusty wind. While I was on final, the wind shifted and my approach became unstable. I decided to go around, climbed to a safe altitude, and requested another approach. On the second attempt I adjusted my aim point and landed safely. I learned to brief go-around paths carefully for rooftop sites and to go around early when the approach is unstable.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Mixing present and past with no control.  
  ✔ Stay in past tenses for the story; use present only for the lesson now.

- ❌ Starting in the middle with no setting.  
  ✔ Open with when, where, and aircraft.

- ❌ Endless detail with no ending.  
  ✔ Close with result and one lesson learned.

- ❌ Telling someone else's story with no personal action.  
  ✔ Include what you did and decided.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** While I Was Cruising I Decided to Divert

**Pronunciation:** wyl eye wuz KROO-zing eye dih-SY-did too dih-VERT

**Word Stress**

- Cruising → KROO-zing
- Decided → dih-SY-did
- Divert → dih-VERT
- Safely → SAYF-lee

Practice:

While I was cruising... I decided to divert.

Together:

While I was cruising, I decided to divert and we landed safely.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

The examiner asks for a past experience with an abnormal indication in flight.

**Your first task**

Give a Level 4 narrative with setting, problem, action, result, and lesson.

**Your second task**

If ATC had asked your intentions during that event, what would you have said?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Last year in an H130 I noticed abnormal vibration during cruise. I checked instruments, reduced severity per procedure, and diverted to the nearest suitable airport. We landed safely and maintenance found a defect. I learned to treat unusual vibration early and communicate clearly. Second task: ANAC123, abnormal vibration, requesting diversion and priority landing. Narrative for Part 1; intentions for the radio.
