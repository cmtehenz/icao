# Runway Contaminated

**Catalog ID:** 0169  
**Category:** Airport Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Runway Contaminated — the formal aerodrome condition advisory indicating that a significant portion of the runway is covered with standing water, slush, snow, or ice to a depth and extent that creates serious performance hazards.

Runway Contaminated is more severe than Runway Wet.

Runway Wet means a film of water reduces friction, but the pavement is still the dominant surface your tires contact.

Runway Contaminated means the pavement itself is no longer your primary braking surface. You are braking on the contaminant — standing water, slush, compacted snow, or ice — which has dramatically different friction properties.

The threshold is precise: 25% or more of the runway surface area in the relevant zone, covered with 3mm or more of standing water, slush, dry snow, or compacted snow — or any ice formation.

In southern Brazil, Curitiba occasionally sees contamination events in winter with frost and compacted moisture. Understanding this distinction is critical for safe operations and ICAO-level communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Runway Contaminated is an aerodrome surface condition indicating that 25% or more of the runway surface area in the touchdown zone, mid-runway, or rollout zone is covered with a contaminant of 3mm depth or more — including standing water, slush, dry or compacted snow, or ice — substantially increasing stopping distance.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Runway Contaminated is reported, the pilot should:

- identify the type and depth of contaminant
- apply the contaminated runway landing distance correction — typically much larger than the wet correction
- determine if the corrected landing distance fits within the available runway
- assess the risk of aquaplaning (hydroplaning) if standing water is the contaminant
- consider diversion if performance limits are exceeded
- advise ATC of the decision — go-around or diversion

The key performance difference:

- **Runway Wet** — typical landing distance increase: 10–20%
- **Runway Contaminated** — typical landing distance increase: 25–100% depending on contaminant type and depth

Standing water 3mm or deeper causes dynamic hydroplaning. At typical approach speeds, the tires ride on a pressurized water film and make almost no contact with the pavement. Braking is negligible.

**Important:** Contaminated runway performance data in the aircraft manual is based on specific contaminant types and depths. Always match the reported condition to the correct column in the performance tables.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers report Runway Contaminated to:

- alert pilots to severe surface degradation requiring major performance corrections
- comply with ICAO Annex 14 and GRF mandatory reporting requirements
- prevent runway overruns by ensuring performance awareness before landing
- support coordination for runway closures if contamination is unacceptable
- differentiate clearly from the less severe Runway Wet advisory

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on approach to Curitiba in your H130 during an unusual winter fog event.

ATIS reports:

Runway one five, runway contaminated, standing water eight to ten millimeters, touchdown zone.

You open your H130 performance supplement.

For standing water 10mm, your required landing distance correction is 45%.

Normal landing distance: 1,200 meters. Corrected: 1,740 meters.

Available runway one five: 1,800 meters. Marginal — but within limits.

You call Approach:

ANAC123, copied runway contaminated, runway one five, performing performance check. We can accept, request latest braking action.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, braking action Poor, reported by previous aircraft.

A Poor braking action report with already marginal performance — the decision is clear.

You declare:

ANAC123, unable to accept runway one five contaminated and braking action Poor, request diversion to Congonhas.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Contaminated is the line between normal operations and high-risk operations.

Wet: you adjust and proceed with care.

Contaminated: you calculate with precision and decide with discipline.

The H130 is a light helicopter — your performance tables are based on its specific weight and tire characteristics. The contaminant tables in your manual exist for a reason.

Use them.

Standing water at 10mm depth creates a real hydroplaning risk. You are not braking on pavement anymore. You are gliding on a water film.

If the calculation says no — it means no.

Diversion is not failure. It is the correct answer when contamination exceeds limits.

Do not accept a contaminated runway under social pressure, schedule pressure, or wishful thinking.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What actions would you take if ATC advised runway contaminated with standing water before your landing?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would acknowledge the advisory and immediately calculate my landing distance using the contaminated runway performance data for the reported water depth. If the corrected landing distance exceeds the available runway, I would request a go-around and diversion to an alternate with a dry or safe runway. If the corrected distance is within limits, I would also request the latest braking action report from the previous aircraft. If braking action is Poor combined with already marginal performance, I would divert. I would advise ATC clearly of my decision: either I can accept with corrected performance, or I am unable to accept and request an alternate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating Runway Contaminated the same as Runway Wet.  
  ✔ Contaminated requires far larger performance corrections. The two conditions are fundamentally different.

- ❌ Applying a standard wet-runway correction to a contaminated runway.  
  ✔ Use the contaminated runway section of the performance manual, matched to the correct contaminant type and depth.

- ❌ Accepting a contaminated runway without knowing the available landing distance.  
  ✔ Always verify your corrected stopping distance fits within the available runway before accepting.

- ❌ Not communicating a decision to divert clearly.  
  ✔ Advise ATC: ANAC123, unable to accept runway contaminated, request diversion to [alternate].

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Curitiba Tower advises: ANAC123, runway contaminated, standing water ten millimeters, runway one five, braking action Medium reported by previous light aircraft.

Your H130 performance supplement shows: standing water 10mm correction is +45%. Available runway one five: 1,800 meters. Your normal landing distance: 1,200 meters.

**Your first task**

Calculate your corrected landing distance. Can you accept the runway?

**Your second task**

ATC offers you runway three three with a dry surface and a 15-knot tailwind component. Your tailwind correction is +15%. Calculate the corrected landing distance for runway three three and advise ATC of your choice.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Runway one five: 1,200 multiplied by 1.45 equals 1,740 meters. Available 1,800 meters — marginal acceptance, but technically within limits. However, combined with a Medium braking action from a light aircraft, the risk is elevated. Runway three three with tailwind: 1,200 multiplied by 1.15 equals 1,380 meters. Available on runway three three: 2,100 meters. Runway three three with the tailwind correction is the safer choice. I would advise: ANAC123, request runway three three, performance more comfortable despite tailwind, contaminated runway one five marginal with braking action Medium.
