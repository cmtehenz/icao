# Traffic in Sight

**Catalog ID:** 0142  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Traffic in Sight — the pilot's response when ATC issues a traffic advisory and the pilot visually acquires the reported traffic.

Traffic in Sight is a short, unambiguous phrase that closes the loop on a traffic call.

When ATC says "traffic, twelve o'clock, five miles," they are giving you information and expecting a response.

In the H130, flying in and out of Congonhas, Joinville, or São Paulo area, traffic advisories are common — especially in busy terminal airspace.

Knowing how to respond — and what your response means operationally — is a fundamental Part 2 skill.

## 📖 Meaning

Traffic in Sight is the pilot's verbal confirmation that the aircraft or vehicles described in an ATC traffic advisory have been visually acquired by the flight crew.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Tráfego à vista.

Confirmação verbal do piloto de que a aeronave ou veículos descritos em um aviso de tráfego do controle foram visualmente adquiridos pela tripulação.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Traffic in Sight is reported, the pilot should:

- visually confirm the traffic before transmitting
- report callsign and Traffic in Sight
- continue to monitor the traffic visually
- advise ATC if traffic is subsequently lost
- understand that visual separation may be delegated after this report

Traffic in Sight applies to:

- conflicting IFR traffic called by Approach or Control
- VFR traffic advisories in terminal areas
- runway incursion advisories from Tower
- traffic pointed out for visual separation
- crossing traffic during approach or departure

**Important:** Traffic in Sight means you see it. Do not say Traffic in Sight if you have not visually acquired the traffic. A false report can lead ATC to terminate the traffic advisory, leaving you without protection.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue traffic advisories to:

- alert pilots to potential conflicting traffic
- allow pilots to apply visual separation
- satisfy separation requirements with visual confirmation
- enhance situational awareness in complex terminal areas
- support sequencing during approach

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on a visual approach to Joinville runway two seven in your H130.

Joinville Approach transmits:

ANAC123, traffic, eleven o'clock, three miles, westbound, altitude three thousand, unknown type.

You scan the area. Eleven o'clock left, three miles.

You see a small aircraft tracking westbound.

You transmit:

Traffic in sight, ANAC123.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, maintain visual separation from that traffic, continue approach.

You monitor the traffic, keep it in sight, and land normally.

Three words. Traffic awareness. Approach maintained.

4. Control: ANAC123, traffic, two o'clock, ten miles, same direction, same altitude.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Traffic in Sight is not a formality.

It is a declaration that you have eyes on the conflicting traffic.

When you say it, ATC may delegate visual separation to you — meaning the responsibility for avoiding that aircraft shifts to the flight deck.

That is a significant operational consequence.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say Traffic in Sight before they actually see the traffic — to avoid the follow-up conversation or because they expect to see it soon.

Do not do this.

If you have not seen it, say: Negative contact, ANAC123 — or: Looking, ANAC123.

ATC will continue the advisory.

Once you say Traffic in Sight, monitor it. If you lose it: ANAC123, lost traffic, request advisory.

In the H130, visual scanning around Congonhas requires discipline. Traffic density is high. Head-down time is fatal.

Look first. Report what you see. Mean what you say.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What does Traffic in Sight mean operationally, and what responsibilities does it carry?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Traffic in Sight means I have visually acquired the traffic ATC described. Reporting it confirms to ATC that I can see the conflicting aircraft. After this report, ATC may instruct me to maintain visual separation — which transfers responsibility for collision avoidance from ATC to me. I must only say Traffic in Sight if I have actually seen the traffic. If I lose visual contact, I immediately advise ATC: ANAC123, lost traffic.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting Traffic in Sight without actually having visual contact.  
  ✔ Only report when confirmed visually. A false report removes ATC protection.

- ❌ Not monitoring the traffic after reporting it in sight.  
  ✔ Continue to monitor. If lost, report immediately.

- ❌ Saying "Roger" instead of "Traffic in sight."  
  ✔ The specific phrase closes the ATC advisory loop. "Roger" does not confirm visual acquisition.

- ❌ Forgetting to include callsign in the report.  
  ✔ Traffic in sight, ANAC123 — always include your callsign.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Congonhas Approach calls: "ANAC123, traffic, two o'clock, two miles, southbound, altitude three thousand, appears to be a light aircraft."

**Your first task**

You scan and acquire the traffic — a small propeller aircraft tracking south. What do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Approach says: "ANAC123, maintain visual separation, continue approach." Thirty seconds later the traffic turns and you lose it behind cloud. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After visual acquisition I transmit: Traffic in sight, ANAC123. When Approach delegates visual separation, I acknowledge: Maintaining visual separation, ANAC123. When I lose the traffic behind cloud, I immediately report: ANAC123, lost traffic, request advisory. ATC will then resume traffic advisory responsibility and may provide further instructions for separation.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Traffic in Sight is three words and a responsibility.

When you say them, ATC may hand you the separation task.

See the traffic first.

Report it honestly.

Monitor it continuously.

Advise ATC when you lose it.

Your eyes are the final layer of protection in visual separation.

Use them deliberately. Report truthfully. Fly safely.
