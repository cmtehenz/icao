# VOR Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0192  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers VOR Failure — loss of usable VOR navigation information due to airborne receiver failure, ground station unserviceability, or flag indications that make VOR-based tracking or approach guidance unreliable.

VOR failure matters whenever your clearance or approach depends on that aid. If the needle is flagged or the station is NOTAMed out, you cannot pretend the procedure is still available. You must advise ATC and request an alternative.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations in Brazil, VOR remains a key conventional backup around Congonhas, Guarulhos, Curitiba, Florianópolis, and Santos Dumont — especially when RNAV is unavailable or as a cross-check. Crews must know how to report VOR failure and transition to radar vectors, another aid, or a different approach.

Understanding VOR failure phraseology and explanation skills supports Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

VOR Failure is the inability to receive or use valid VHF Omnidirectional Range guidance for navigation or approach, indicated by warning flags, loss of bearing information, abnormal course indications, or ground station unserviceability, requiring the crew to discontinue VOR-dependent tracking and obtain an alternative clearance or navigation method.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha de VOR / VOR Failure.

Incapacidade de receber ou utilizar orientação válida de VHF Omnidirectional Range para navegação ou aproximação, indicada por bandeiras de aviso, perda de informação de rumo, indicações anormais de curso ou inoperância da estação no solo, exigindo que a tripulação interrompa o seguimento dependente de VOR e obtenha uma autorização ou método de navegação alternativo.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When VOR failure occurs or is identified, the crew should:

- recognize flags, loss of ident, or unreliable bearing information
- confirm whether the problem is airborne (receiver) or ground-based (NOTAM / ATC advice)
- stop using the failed VOR for primary lateral navigation
- advise ATC: VOR unserviceable / unable VOR approach, request alternative
- select backup navigation: second VOR if available, RNAV if capable, or radar vectors
- execute missed approach if already on a VOR approach and guidance is lost

VOR failure scenarios commonly include:

- receiver flag during en-route tracking to a VOR
- ground station outage advised by ATC or NOTAM
- loss of guidance inside a VOR approach final segment
- inability to identify the station morse ident
- need to request ILS, RNAV, or visual approach instead

**Important:** Identify the station before you trust it. No ident, no use. In the H130, if you lose VOR on a conventional approach, go missed if you are not in a position to continue safely by other approved means, and tell ATC immediately. Do not chase a flagged needle toward minimums.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers work with VOR failure reports to:

- stop clearing aircraft for procedures that require that VOR
- provide radar vectors or clearances via other aids
- advise when a ground VOR is unserviceable
- resequence arrivals to ILS, RNAV, or visual approaches
- maintain separation when an aircraft can no longer hold or track a VOR radial

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cleared for a VOR approach into Curitiba. On intermediate segment, the VOR flag appears and the bearing becomes unreliable. You confirm loss of ident.

You execute the missed approach as published, climb, and report: missed approach, VOR failure, request radar vectors for an alternate approach.

Approach acknowledges, vectors you for the ILS, and sequences you again.

You did not continue to minimums on a dead needle. You went missed, declared the failure, and accepted the alternative.

That is professional contingency management on conventional aids.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger VOR failure, climb via missed approach, fly heading zero niner zero.
2. Radar: ANAC123, VOR unserviceable, expect radar vectors, ILS available.
3. Control: ANAC123, unable VOR routing, cleared via RNAV route, or radar vectors.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm able ILS or request another approach.
5. Radar: ANAC123, VOR back in service, advise if you require it.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

4. ANAC123, able ILS, request ILS approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

A flagged VOR is not a suggestion to try harder. It is a stop sign for that guidance source.

Identify first. If the ident is gone or the flag is in view, the aid is unusable for primary navigation.

If you are established on a VOR approach and guidance fails, missed approach is usually the correct path unless you have another approved way to continue and are in a safe position to do so. Tell ATC why you went missed: VOR failure.

In Brazilian training ops, treat VOR as a valued conventional tool — and treat its failure as routine contingency, not drama. Ask for ILS, RNAV, or vectors. Brief the new plan. Continue.

Part 3 answers should cover recognition, missed approach if required, ATC notification, and alternate approach selection.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You lose VOR guidance on final approach segment of a VOR approach in IMC. Describe your actions.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would discontinue the approach, execute the missed approach procedure immediately, and advise ATC that I am going missed due to VOR failure. I would not continue to minimums without valid course guidance in IMC. After completing the missed approach, I would request radar vectors for an alternate approach such as ILS or RNAV if available, brief the new procedure, and continue only with a valid navigation source.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing the approach with a flagged VOR because "almost there."  
  ✔ Go missed in IMC without valid guidance; declare VOR failure.

- ❌ Using a VOR without confirming the morse ident.  
  ✔ Identify the station before tracking or descending on the procedure.

- ❌ Saying "navigation failure" without specifying VOR.  
  ✔ State VOR failure so ATC can plan the correct alternative.

- ❌ Holding on a VOR radial after the receiver has flagged.  
  ✔ Advise unable and request a published alternate hold or radar vectors.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are cleared for the VOR approach at Florianópolis. On final, the VOR flags and ident is lost in IMC.

**Your first task**

What do you do and what do you transmit?

**Your second task**

Approach offers ILS runway one four with vectors. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Execute missed approach and transmit: ANAC123, missed approach, VOR failure, request radar vectors. Second task: ANAC123, request ILS runway one four, standing by for vectors. I would not continue the VOR approach without guidance in IMC, and I would brief the ILS fully before accepting the subsequent approach clearance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

VOR failure is simple when you respect the flag.

No ident, no needle trust. No guidance in IMC, no continuation to minimums.

Go missed if required. Say VOR failure. Take the alternate — ILS, RNAV, or vectors.

In H130 Brazilian ops, conventional aids still matter. So does contingency discipline.

Identify. Detect. Declare. Divert the plan — not the airplane into uncertainty.

That is how you handle a dead VOR like a professional.
