# Magnetic Compass Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0199  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2, Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Magnetic Compass Failure — the loss or unreliability of the magnetic compass used as a primary or backup heading reference.

In modern cockpits the magnetic compass is often a standby instrument. When the primary heading system fails, or when you must cross-check heading, the magnetic compass becomes critical. If the compass itself fails — stuck, unreadable, or erratic — heading reference may be degraded.

For H130 helicopter operations, heading awareness remains essential for IFR, night VFR, and ATC-assigned headings in busy Brazilian terminal areas such as Congonhas, Guarulhos, and Santos Dumont.

This lesson trains Part 2 reports and Part 3 explanations: how to tell ATC you have limited heading reference, how to request vectors or a simpler clearance, and how to manage the aircraft safely.

## 📖 Meaning

Magnetic Compass Failure is the loss, jamming, or unreliable indication of the magnetic compass, reducing or removing a fundamental heading reference and requiring the pilot to use alternate heading sources and to advise ATC when heading-keeping capability is affected.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha da bússola magnética / Magnetic Compass Failure.

Perda, travamento ou indicação não confiável da bússola magnética, reduzindo ou eliminando uma referência fundamental de proa e exigindo fontes alternativas de direção, com comunicação ao controle quando a capacidade de manter proa estiver afetada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When magnetic compass failure occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control using attitude instruments and remaining heading references
- identify whether primary gyro/EHSI heading remains available
- avoid relying on the failed compass for turns or assigned headings
- inform ATC if heading reference is limited or unreliable
- request radar vectors, progressive instructions, or visual routing as appropriate
- minimize unnecessary heading changes until a stable reference is confirmed

Magnetic compass failure management includes:

- recognizing common failure modes: stuck card, excessive oscillation, fluid leakage, unreadable markings
- using GPS track as a supplementary cue when available — remembering track is not magnetic heading
- requesting "confirm heading" or radar monitoring if uncertain
- planning a landing at a suitable aerodrome if IFR heading reference becomes inadequate

**Important:** A failed magnetic compass with a working primary heading system is a degraded backup. A failed compass plus failed primary heading system is a serious navigation problem — report it and request assistance early.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use heading-reference failure reports to:

- provide radar vectors with closer monitoring
- avoid complex route clearances that demand precise self-navigation by heading
- offer shorter routings to landing
- confirm aircraft track on radar when the pilot reports heading uncertainty
- coordinate simpler approaches and traffic sequencing

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are VFR from Santos Dumont toward Congonhas in the H130 under special VFR in marginal visibility.

Your magnetic compass card sticks and will not turn. Primary heading from the electronic system still works, but you have lost the standby compass.

You continue with electronic heading and transmit when appropriate:

ANAC123, magnetic compass failure, primary heading available, request continued vectors to Congonhas.

Approach monitors you on radar and issues headings to the circuit. You land without using the failed standby compass.

If both electronic heading and magnetic compass were unreliable, you would declare a more urgent navigation limitation and request immediate radar assistance to land.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

The magnetic compass is old technology — and still part of the safety net.

When it fails, ask one question: do I still have a reliable heading reference?

If yes, report the failure as a system degradation and continue with care. If no, you have a heading problem, not a trivia problem. Tell ATC you need radar vectors and a simple path to land.

Do not pretend precision you do not have. If you cannot reliably hold an assigned heading, say so. Controllers would rather give you continuous radar service than discover you drifted through a final approach course.

In the H130, vibration and maneuvering can make compass reading harder even when the instrument is healthy. Know the difference between a hard-to-read compass and a failed one.

For ICAO speaking, describe the operational effect: limited heading reference, request radar assistance, continue with remaining instruments. That is Level 4 clarity.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your magnetic compass fails in flight. Your electronic heading indicator still works. What do you do and what do you tell ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would continue using the electronic heading indicator as my primary heading reference and treat the magnetic compass as failed. I would inform ATC of magnetic compass failure and confirm that primary heading is still available. I would request radar vectors if operating in IMC or complex airspace, and I would avoid relying on the failed compass for any heading cross-check. If the electronic heading system later became unreliable, I would report reduced heading capability immediately and request priority radar assistance to land.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Ignoring compass failure because "the GPS shows track."  
  ✔ Track is not heading; report compass failure and use proper heading sources.

- ❌ Not telling ATC when both compass and primary heading are degraded.  
  ✔ Report limited heading reference and request radar vectors early.

- ❌ Making large unverified turns after compass failure in IMC.  
  ✔ Use remaining heading instruments; request radar confirmation if uncertain.

- ❌ Using vague language like "instrument problem" only.  
  ✔ Specify magnetic compass failure and remaining heading capability.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are IFR from Curitiba to Florianópolis. The magnetic compass becomes stuck and unreadable. Your electronic heading still works. Approach assigns heading two four zero.

**Your first task**

What do you report to ATC about the failure?

**Your second task**

Later the electronic heading also becomes erratic. What do you say, and what do you request?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, magnetic compass failure, primary heading available. Second task: ANAC123, heading reference unreliable, request radar vectors and priority for landing. I would then follow radar headings as closely as possible using remaining cues, minimize unnecessary maneuvering, and land at the nearest suitable aerodrome with radar assistance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Magnetic compass failure is easy to underestimate.

When the backup fails, you still have a story to tell ATC. When the primary heading fails with it, you have a mission change: get radar help and land.

Report capability. Fly remaining instruments. Do not invent precision.

In H130 Brazilian operations, clear heading communication keeps you sequenced safely through Congonhas, Guarulhos, and coastal routes.

Know your heading source. Protect it. Report when it fails.
