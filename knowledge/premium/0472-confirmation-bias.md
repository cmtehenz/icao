# Confirmation Bias

**Catalog ID:** 0472  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Confirmation Bias — the human tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that supports an existing belief while discounting contradictory evidence.

Confirmation bias is not stubbornness alone. It is a cognitive filter that can make a wrong runway, wrong frequency, or wrong weather assessment feel correct because it matches the plan already in the crew's mind.

In H130 operations across Brazil — busy terminals, VFR corridors, and IFR arrivals — confirmation bias can turn a single incorrect assumption into a chain of reinforcing errors. A crew that "knows" the weather is improving may ignore a deteriorating METAR. A crew that "knows" they are on the correct radial may dismiss a conflicting CDI.

Understanding how to challenge confirmation bias in ICAO-standard English — including clarification requests, readback discipline, and intention statements — supports Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Confirmation Bias is a cognitive bias in which pilots favour information that confirms their current expectation or plan and undervalue or ignore cues that contradict that expectation, increasing the risk of navigational, procedural, and decision-making errors.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Viés de confirmação / Confirmation Bias.

Viés cognitivo em que os pilotos favorecem informações que confirmam sua expectativa ou plano atual e subestimam ou ignoram indícios que contradizem essa expectativa, aumentando o risco de erros de navegação, procedimento e tomada de decisão.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When confirmation bias may be present, the crew should:

- actively seek disconfirming evidence before committing to a decision
- cross-check independent sources: instruments, charts, ATC, and the other pilot
- use challenge-and-response language when something "almost" matches the plan
- request confirmation from ATC when identity, clearance, or weather is uncertain
- delay irreversible actions until contradictory cues are resolved

Confirmation discipline includes:

- reading back clearances fully, not only the expected parts
- verifying runway, approach, and frequency against the plate, not memory
- treating "it looks right" as a trigger for a second check
- inviting the other pilot to argue the opposite case briefly

**Important:** If the evidence only fits your plan after you force it, stop. Ask ATC. Recheck the chart. The bias feels like confidence. Professional crews treat unexplained mismatches as warnings, not noise.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers help counter confirmation bias when they:

- require full readbacks of critical clearance elements
- challenge unexpected position or altitude reports
- restate runway, approach, or holding instructions clearly
- ask the crew to confirm intentions when the plan seems inconsistent
- provide weather updates that may contradict the crew's earlier picture

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Santos Dumont expecting runway two zero. Approach clears you for the ILS runway one three.

You almost read back runway two zero because that is what you briefed.

Your first officer challenges: "Clearance was one three."

You call:

ANAC123, confirm ILS runway one three.

Approach: ANAC123, affirmative, cleared ILS runway one three, report established.

You respond:

ANAC123, cleared ILS runway one three, will report established.

You rebuild the brief around the actual clearance, not the expected one.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, confirm cleared ILS runway one three.
2. Tower: ANAC123, confirm runway in use, runway two zero.
3. Radar: ANAC123, say again destination, confirm Florianópolis.
4. Approach: ANAC123, confirm intentions, weather now below minima.
5. Control: ANAC123, verify altitude, maintain four thousand.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, confirming cleared ILS runway one three.
2. ANAC123, runway two zero confirmed, ANAC123.
3. ANAC123, destination Florianópolis confirmed.
4. ANAC123, requesting alternate, weather below minima.
5. ANAC123, maintaining four thousand, altitude verified.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Confirmation bias loves a good brief.

Once the crew has a story — this runway, this weather, this routing — every cue is bent to fit it.

In the H130, keep the brief alive. When ATC changes the plan, kill the old story immediately. Do not patch it.

Ask the question that hurts: "What would prove we are wrong?"

If you cannot answer that, you are defending a belief, not flying a clearance.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC issues an approach clearance different from the one you briefed. You nearly read back the briefed runway. What do you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would stop and confirm with ATC rather than force the expected runway into the readback: ANAC123, confirm ILS runway one three. After affirmative confirmation, I would rebrief the actual approach and discard the previous plan. I would thank the challenge from the other pilot and treat the mismatch as a bias trap, not a minor wording issue.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reading back the expected clearance instead of the spoken one.  
  ✔ Read back exactly what ATC said, then challenge mismatches.

- ❌ Ignoring a METAR that contradicts the "improving" mental picture.  
  ✔ Update the plan when official weather disagrees with expectation.

- ❌ Dismissing the other pilot's challenge to protect the brief.  
  ✔ Treat challenges as safety data, not personal criticism.

- ❌ Continuing because "it looks familiar."  
  ✔ Verify identity of runway, fix, and frequency against current documents.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You briefed ILS runway two zero. Approach clears ILS runway one three. You catch yourself about to read back two zero.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

Approach confirms runway one three. Weather is also worse than briefed. What do you say next if you need time?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, confirm cleared ILS runway one three. Second task: ANAC123, request delay vectors to rebrief approach. I would not protect the original brief. Confirmation bias is broken by verifying the real clearance and rebuilding the plan around facts.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Confirmation bias turns hope into procedure.

The cure is deliberate doubt: confirm, cross-check, and invite challenge.

In the H130, the brief is a tool, not a contract with reality. When reality changes, change the brief.

Hear what was said. Verify what you believe. Fly the clearance you actually have.
