# Windshield Heat Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0240  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Windshield Heat Failure — loss of windshield heating that can allow ice, frost, or fogging to obscure forward vision, especially in icing, rain, or cold-soak conditions, requiring AFM procedures, possible diversion, and clear ATC communication when visibility through the windshield is reduced.

Windshield heat failure is a vision problem before it is a systems trivia question. If you cannot see the approach lights, the hover reference, or the landing area, the approach is not safe — regardless of how good the instruments are.

For H130 helicopter operations in Brazil — coastal approaches into Santos Dumont, winter arrivals into Curitiba, rain and mist around Congonhas — forward visibility through the windshield is critical for the visual segment and for VFR segments of training flights. ICAO Part 2 and Part 3 expect precise language for the failure and the intention.

## 📖 Meaning

Windshield Heat Failure is a malfunction in which heating of the windshield is lost or inadequate, allowing ice, frost, or condensation to form on the windshield and reduce forward visibility, which may require abnormal procedures, reduced approach minima considerations where applicable, diversion, and notification to ATC when visual references are compromised.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do aquecimento do para-brisa / Windshield Heat Failure.

Malfuncionamento em que o aquecimento do para-brisa é perdido ou inadequado, permitindo que gelo, geada ou condensação se formem no para-brisa e reduzam a visibilidade frontal, o que pode exigir procedimentos anormais, considerações de mínimos de aproximação quando aplicável, desvio e notificação ao ATC quando as referências visuais estiverem comprometidas.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When windshield heat fails, the crew should:

- apply the AFM abnormal procedure and attempt any alternate defog or heat options
- assess forward visibility and whether ice or fog is forming on the windshield
- avoid prolonged flight in icing that will obscure the windshield further
- advise ATC of windshield heat failure and reduced forward visibility if relevant
- plan a diversion or approach to better conditions if the visual segment will be unsafe
- go around or discontinue if windshield vision is insufficient for landing

Windshield heat failure scenarios commonly include:

- ice accretion on the windshield in freezing cloud or drizzle
- internal fogging in humid Brazilian coastal approaches
- inability to see the runway or helipad at minima
- need for an instrument approach to better weather or a diversion
- H130: visual hover and landing references degraded — do not force the landing

**Important:** Instruments can take you to minima. They cannot replace a clear windshield for the landing. If forward vision is inadequate, go around, divert, or declare the situation as required. Tell ATC early: windshield heat failure, reduced forward visibility, requesting diversion or priority approach to better conditions.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use windshield heat / visibility reports to:

- provide vectors to better weather or alternate airports
- prioritize approaches when vision is degrading
- avoid unnecessary holds in icing that worsen windshield ice
- coordinate emergency services if a landing with limited vision is attempted
- understand go-around reasons related to windshield obstruction

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are inbound to Curitiba in light freezing drizzle. Windshield heat fails. Ice begins to build on the screen. Forward vision is deteriorating.

You transmit:

ANAC123, windshield heat failure, ice on windshield, reduced forward visibility, request vectors for immediate approach or diversion to warmer conditions.

Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading three six zero, descend three thousand, expect ILS runway one five, advise visual status.

On final, you still cannot see enough of the runway environment.

You go around:

ANAC123, going around, insufficient forward visibility due windshield ice, request vectors for diversion to Congonhas.

You refuse a blind landing. That is professionalism, not failure.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger windshield heat failure, say forward visibility and intentions.
2. Approach: ANAC123, expect ILS runway one five, report runway in sight.
3. Tower: ANAC123, go around, climb two thousand, fly runway heading.
4. Approach: ANAC123, diversion approved, expect vectors Congonhas.
5. Approach: ANAC123, advise if windshield still obscured.

4. ANAC123, diverting Congonhas, requesting vectors.
5. ANAC123, windshield still partially obscured, request longer final if available.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Windshield heat failure is about whether you can see what you must see.

In cruise, you may continue with instruments while you plan. In the visual segment of an approach — or in a helicopter hover and landing — a frozen or fogged windshield can end the maneuver safely only one way: discontinue.

Do not negotiate with a white windshield at fifty feet. Go around. Tell ATC why. Divert to conditions where ice is not winning.

In Brazilian humidity and temperature swings, fogging can be as dangerous as external ice. Use every AFM option. If vision is still inadequate, the radio call must include the operational effect: reduced forward visibility — not only the system name.

Examiners want the link: failure → effect → intention → request. Practice that chain until it is automatic.

The H130 lands where you can see the references. No windshield vision, no landing — unless it is a declared emergency with no better option, and even then you manage risk deliberately.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Windshield heat fails on approach. Ice is forming and you may not see the runway at minima. What do you tell ATC and what might you do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would advise ATC of windshield heat failure with reduced forward visibility and request an immediate approach to the best available conditions or a diversion to an alternate with warmer or clearer weather. If I reached minima without sufficient forward visual reference, I would go around and request vectors for diversion. I would not continue to land without the visual references required for a safe landing.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Reporting only "system failure" without mentioning visibility.  
  ✔ State windshield heat failure and reduced forward visibility.

- ❌ Continuing below minima hoping the windshield will clear.  
  ✔ Go around if visual references are insufficient.

- ❌ Holding in icing while windshield ice increases.  
  ✔ Request immediate approach or exit icing / divert.

- ❌ Forcing a helicopter landing with obscured hover references.  
  ✔ Discontinue and divert; vision is part of the landing system.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Windshield heat failure is a visibility emergency wearing a systems caption.

Checklist. Assess vision. Approach only if you can see what landing requires.

If not — go around, explain, divert.

In Brazilian coastal and winter weather, ice and fog on the screen are common enough to respect and rare enough to take seriously.

See to land. If you cannot see, do not land.

That is the whole lesson — in the H130 and in ICAO English.
