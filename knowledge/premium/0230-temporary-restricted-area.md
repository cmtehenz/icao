# Temporary Restricted Area

**Catalog ID:** 0230  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Temporary Restricted Area — a defined volume of airspace activated for a limited period by NOTAM or AIP supplement, within which flight is restricted or prohibited except for authorized aircraft, usually due to military exercises, security events, airshows, or other temporary hazards.

A temporary restricted area is not on every chart as a permanent fixture. It appears for hours or days, then disappears. Crews who only memorize permanent restricted areas will fly into temporary ones. The defense is current NOTAMs and ATC advisories.

For H130 helicopter training academy flights in Brazilian airspace, temporary restricted areas frequently affect coastal corridors, major-city events, and military exercise zones. Low-level VFR training is especially exposed. Routes between Congonhas, Santos Dumont, Curitiba, and Florianópolis may need lateral or vertical deviation when a temporary area goes hot.

Understanding how to remain clear, request deviation, and discuss temporary restrictions in ICAO English supports Part 2 and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

A Temporary Restricted Area is a defined volume of airspace activated for a specified period by NOTAM or similar aeronautical information, within which flight of aircraft is restricted under stated conditions or prohibited except for authorized flights, due to temporary military, security, public event, or hazardous activity.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Área Restrita Temporária / Temporary Restricted Area.

Volume definido de espaço aéreo ativado por período especificado mediante NOTAM ou informação aeronáutica similar, dentro do qual o voo de aeronaves é restrito sob condições estabelecidas ou proibido exceto para voos autorizados, devido a atividade militar, de segurança, evento público ou perigosa temporária.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When operating near a temporary restricted area, the crew should:

- identify temporary activations in the preflight NOTAM package
- note lateral boundaries, vertical limits, and active times (hot/cold schedule)
- plan a route that remains clear unless transit is authorized
- request ATC vectors or reroute early if the cleared route conflicts
- comply immediately with remain-clear instructions
- report if weather or emergency prevents remaining clear and state intentions

Temporary restricted area operations commonly include:

- military exercises activated for a published window
- VIP or security cordons around cities and airports
- airshow and sporting event airspace
- temporary hazard areas (e.g., blasting, rocket activity)
- altitude floors that allow transit above the block

**Important:** Temporary means the chart you flew last week may be incomplete. Check NOTAMs every flight. In the H130, do not assume you can slip under a temporary area at low level — vertical limits often start at the surface. If ATC says remain clear, treat the boundary as hard until you receive an explicit transit clearance.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers manage temporary restricted areas to:

- keep civil traffic clear of temporary hazardous or sensitive activity
- issue vectors and reroutes around active (hot) areas
- coordinate with military or security agencies during activation
- advise inbound aircraft of NOTAM-based restrictions in real time
- reopen the corridor when the area goes cold

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are flying the H130 from Santos Dumont toward Florianópolis along a familiar coastal VFR route. Preflight NOTAM showed a temporary restricted area active from the surface to four thousand feet until 1800 UTC for a military exercise.

Radar: remain clear of the temporary restricted area, turn right heading two two zero for deviation.

You turn, stay outside the boundary, and continue when clear. You do not cut the corner to save fuel. Temporary restricted means temporary — but while it is hot, it is as real as a permanent restricted area.

After 1800 UTC, ATC advises the area is cold. Your return route can use the normal corridor again. NOTAM times matter.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Radar: ANAC123, temporary restricted area active, remain clear, turn left heading one eight zero.
2. Control: ANAC123, cleared via flight planned route, temporary restricted area cold.
3. Radar: ANAC123, unable direct due temporary restricted area, expect vectors around.
4. Approach: ANAC123, remain at or above five thousand, temporary restricted area below active.
5. Radar: ANAC123, temporary restricted area now cold, resume own navigation.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Temporary restricted areas punish outdated briefs.

Permanent restricted areas are on the chart. Temporary ones live in the NOTAM and in ATC's mouth. Both can stop your route.

Know the times. An area hot until 1800 UTC is cold after that — unless extended. Do not guess. Ask ATC if status is unclear.

In the H130 near Brazilian coasts and cities, temporary activations for exercises and events are common. Build NOTAM review into every academy sortie, not only cross-country IFR.

Remain clear means remain clear. Transit requires an explicit clearance. Weather is not a free pass — if you cannot remain clear, declare the problem and request help early.

When the area goes cold, confirm before you slide back onto the old track. Extensions happen. A quick confirmation with ATC costs seconds and prevents an airspace bust.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

A NOTAM activates a temporary restricted area across your planned VFR route. Describe how you plan and what you say to ATC if the area is active when you approach it.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would note the lateral and vertical limits and the active times from the NOTAM, and plan a lateral or vertical route that remains clear. Approaching the area, I would confirm with ATC that it is still active and request vectors or a reroute around it. If ATC instructs me to remain clear, I would comply and read back the heading or routing. I would not enter the area without an explicit transit clearance.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Checking only permanent restricted areas on the chart and ignoring temporary NOTAMs.  
  ✔ Temporary activations are NOTAM-driven. Review them every flight.

- ❌ Entering because "it was cold yesterday" or "the exercise usually ends early."  
  ✔ Confirm status with NOTAM times and ATC. Do not assume.

- ❌ Cutting the corner of the boundary to shorten the route.  
  ✔ Remain clear of the published or ATC-stated boundary with margin.

- ❌ Climbing into cloud or terrain while trying to stay above a temporary block without a plan.  
  ✔ Brief a safe lateral alternative, not only a vertical squeeze.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are VFR along the coast. Radar advises a temporary restricted area ahead is active from the surface to three thousand feet.

**Your first task**

Radar says remain clear and offers a heading. What do you say?

**Your second task**

Twenty minutes later Radar says the area is cold. What do you say if you want to resume the original coastal track?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, remaining clear, heading two two zero — or whatever heading was assigned. Second task: ANAC123, request resume own navigation via the coastal route, temporary restricted area cold. I would only re-enter the former volume after ATC confirms it is cold or clears me through.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Temporary restricted areas are real while they are hot — and invisible on last week's memory.

Read the NOTAM. Note times and limits. Remain clear unless cleared through.

In the H130, low-level training meets these activations often. Brief them. Ask ATC. Do not cut corners.

Hot means stay out. Cold means confirm, then resume. That is the discipline.
