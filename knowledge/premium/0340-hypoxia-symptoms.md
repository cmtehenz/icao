# Hypoxia Symptoms

**Catalog ID:** 0340  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Hypoxia Symptoms — the human-factors signs of insufficient oxygen to the brain and body, critical for high-altitude operations, depressurization scenarios, and ICAO exam English even when flying an unpressurized H130.

Hypoxia impairs judgment before the pilot fully recognizes the problem. That is why recognition, oxygen, descent, and clear communication are trained together.

For H130 ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, most flights are at altitudes where hypoxia is unlikely — but Part 2 and Part 3 still test the vocabulary: hypoxia, oxygen, emergency descent, rapid decompression. You must speak the concept correctly for exams and for any pressurized or high-altitude operation.

Master symptom language and the radio call that follows recognition.

## 📖 Meaning

Hypoxia Symptoms are the physical and cognitive signs of inadequate oxygen supply — such as euphoria, impaired judgment, tingling, cyanosis, headache, or loss of consciousness — requiring immediate oxygen and descent when altitude-related.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Sintomas de hipóxia / Hypoxia Symptoms.

Sinais físicos e cognitivos de oxigenação inadequada — como euforia, julgamento prejudicado, formigamento, cianose, dor de cabeça ou perda de consciência — exigindo oxigênio imediato e descida quando relacionados à altitude.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When hypoxia symptoms are recognized, the crew should:

- don oxygen immediately if available
- initiate descent to a safe altitude (commonly ten thousand feet or below in pressurized scenarios)
- declare Mayday for rapid decompression / severe hypoxia; Pan Pan may apply for gradual onset with control maintained
- inform ATC of hypoxia or depressurization and descent intentions
- request priority handling and medical assistance if occupants are affected

Hypoxia-related reporting commonly includes:

- hypoxia / symptoms of hypoxia
- rapid decompression / requesting lower altitude
- crew on oxygen, descending
- passenger hypoxia, request medical assistance

**Important:** Do not wait to "feel sure." Impaired judgment is itself a hypoxia symptom. Oxygen first, descend, then talk.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to hypoxia-related calls to:

- clear altitude blocks for immediate descent
- prioritize the aircraft to lower safe levels
- coordinate medical services if occupants are impaired
- obtain souls on board and nature of injuries
- protect other traffic from the emergency descent path

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam scenario: you are in a pressurized profile at high altitude when cabin altitude rises and you notice tingling, slowed thinking, and cyanosis in a passenger.

You don oxygen, begin emergency descent, and declare the emergency.

Control clears traffic below. You level at a safe altitude, assess occupants, and request medical assistance if needed.

In day-to-day H130 Brazilian VFR cruise this scenario is uncommon — the phraseology remains exam-critical.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday, descend at your discretion, say souls on board.
2. Control: ANAC123, all traffic cleared below, report leveling at ten thousand.
3. Approach: ANAC123, priority vectors, say if crew is on oxygen.
4. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, medical services standing by.
5. Control: ANAC123, confirm hypoxia due to depressurization or other cause if known.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, hypoxia, rapid decompression, descending to ten thousand feet.
2. ANAC123, crew on oxygen, emergency descent, request lower altitude immediately.
3. Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, symptoms of hypoxia, requesting descent and priority handling.
4. ANAC123, passenger hypoxia symptoms, request medical assistance on landing.
5. ANAC123, leveled ten thousand, crew recovering, request vectors to nearest suitable airport.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Hypoxia is a liar. It tells you that you are fine.

That is why checklists put oxygen and descent before long radio explanations.

Brazilian pilots may say "falta de oxigênio" or "hipóxia." Prefer hypoxia or symptoms of hypoxia in ICAO English, plus the action: on oxygen, descending.

Know the classic signs: euphoria, impaired judgment, tingling fingers, blue lips/fingernails, headache, drowsiness.

In the H130, respect density altitude and night fatigue as related human-factor risks even without pressurization — but for exam items, speak the high-altitude script correctly.

If only a passenger shows symptoms after a cabin event, still protect the crew with oxygen and get down.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You recognize hypoxia symptoms at high altitude. What do you do and what do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would don oxygen immediately, initiate emergency descent to a safe altitude, and declare Mayday or Pan Pan as appropriate. I would report hypoxia or rapid decompression, state that I am descending, and request priority handling. I would request medical assistance if passengers or crew remain affected.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Explaining symptoms at length before donning oxygen.  
  ✔ Oxygen and descent first — then communicate.

- ❌ Using only "falta de ar" without standard terms.  
  ✔ Use hypoxia / symptoms of hypoxia / rapid decompression as applicable.

- ❌ Leveling high because "we feel better for a moment."  
  ✔ Descend to a safe altitude and reassess on oxygen.

- ❌ Forgetting medical assistance after occupant hypoxia.  
  ✔ Request medical assistance if anyone remains impaired.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

In an exam pressurized scenario at high altitude you feel tingling and slowed thinking. Cabin altitude is rising.

**Your first task**

What are your first actions and your distress call?

**Your second task**

Control asks if you are on oxygen. What do you reply while descending?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Don oxygen, begin emergency descent, then: Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, hypoxia, rapid decompression, descending to ten thousand feet. Second task: ANAC123, crew on oxygen, descending to ten thousand, request all traffic cleared below. I would not delay oxygen to finish a longer transmission.
