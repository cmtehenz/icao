# Rapid Decompression

**Catalog ID:** 0250  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Rapid Decompression — a sudden loss of cabin pressure that can cause fog, flying debris, ear and sinus pain, and immediate hypoxia risk, requiring oxygen, emergency descent, and urgent ATC communication. The H130 is not typically operated as a high-altitude pressurized airliner; this lesson still teaches Critical ICAO exam English and notes cabin-altitude and environmental awareness where useful for helicopter crews.

Rapid decompression is a classic ICAO oral and radiotelephony scenario. Examiners expect Mayday, oxygen actions, emergency descent, and clear intentions.

For ICAO Delta / Captain Delta training in Brazil, master the standard phraseology exactly, then show you can transfer urgency management to any time-critical systems emergency on the H130.

Understanding rapid decompression in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Rapid Decompression is a sudden and significant loss of cabin pressurization, creating an immediate risk of hypoxia and requiring crew oxygen, passenger oxygen if installed, emergency descent to a safe altitude, and immediate distress communication with ATC including nature of the emergency and intentions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Descompressão rápida / Rapid Decompression.

Perda súbita e significativa da pressurização da cabine, criando risco imediato de hipóxia e exigindo oxigênio da tripulação, oxigênio dos passageiros se instalado, descida de emergência para uma altitude segura e comunicação imediata de socorro com o ATC, incluindo a natureza da emergência e as intenções.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When rapid decompression occurs, the crew should:

- don crew oxygen immediately and establish communication
- confirm cabin altitude and initiate emergency descent
- declare Mayday: rapid decompression, commencing emergency descent
- ensure passenger oxygen as applicable on transport aircraft
- request traffic cleared below and diversion to nearest suitable airport
- for H130 training: retain exam vocabulary; transfer time-critical declare-and-descend logic to applicable emergencies

Rapid decompression reporting commonly includes:

- rapid decompression
- oxygen masks deployed / crew oxygen on
- commencing emergency descent
- request priority / diversion
- souls and fuel when asked

**Important:** Rapid decompression is time-critical. The first radio call must be short and complete enough for ATC to act. Nature. Action. Request. Then fly and update.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to rapid decompression to:

- clear vertical airspace for unrestricted emergency descent
- provide immediate priority routing to a suitable airport
- coordinate sector handoffs during a fast-changing altitude profile
- alert emergency and medical services for possible hypoxia effects
- obtain souls and fuel for arrival support

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam-style scenario: a bang, cabin fog, and masks drop at flight level three three zero.

You don oxygen, start the emergency descent, and call:

Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, rapid decompression, oxygen masks deployed, commencing emergency descent, request clear traffic below, diverting nearest suitable.

Control clears traffic and issues unrestricted descent. You report passing key levels and later advise cabin altitude under control while continuing for priority landing.

For H130 reality, keep the Mayday structure for any equally time-critical failure — the exam word remains "rapid decompression."

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger Mayday rapid decompression, descend unrestricted, traffic cleared below.
2. Control: ANAC123, report cabin altitude status when able.
3. Approach: ANAC123, priority vectors approved, expect runway two eight, medical services advised.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining.
5. Tower: ANAC123, emergency services standing by, advise passenger condition on arrival.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Mayday Mayday Mayday, Control, ANAC123, rapid decompression, commencing emergency descent, request traffic cleared below.
2. ANAC123, descending unrestricted, leaving flight level three three zero, oxygen masks deployed.
3. ANAC123, cabin altitude decreasing, continuing emergency descent, priority runway two eight.
4. ANAC123, souls one six zero, fuel one hour forty-five minutes.
5. ANAC123, cabin altitude under control, leveling flight level one zero zero, request vectors for landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Rapid decompression is violence in the cabin — then silence in the brain if you delay oxygen.

Mask. Communicate. Descend. Declare. In that spirit, even if the exact order flexes with your checklist, oxygen and aircraft control come before storytelling.

On the radio, "rapid decompression" is the correct ICAO label. Not "the cabin broke." Not "we lost pressure a little."

ATC will move mountains of traffic if you give them a clean Mayday. Give them that Mayday early.

For the H130 oral: answer the decompression scenario correctly, then add one sentence of transfer — you understand hypoxia time pressure and emergency descent English even if your daily machine differs.

Critical means no improvisation on the key words.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Describe what rapid decompression is and what you would say to ATC if it occurred in cruise.

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Rapid decompression is a sudden loss of cabin pressurization with immediate hypoxia risk. I would don oxygen, commence an emergency descent, and declare Mayday, stating rapid decompression, that oxygen masks have deployed if applicable, that I am commencing emergency descent, and that I request traffic cleared below and priority to the nearest suitable airport. I would update cabin altitude status and provide souls and fuel when asked. Even on a helicopter without typical airliner pressurization, I would keep the same time-critical communication structure for equivalent emergencies.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "fast depressurization" or mixing Portuguese structure into the call.  
  ✔ Use ICAO-standard "rapid decompression."

- ❌ Delaying the Mayday until after a long technical diagnosis.  
  ✔ Declare early: nature, emergency descent, request.

- ❌ Forgetting to request traffic cleared below.  
  ✔ Explicitly ask ATC to clear traffic during unrestricted descent.

- ❌ Dismissing the topic as irrelevant to helicopters.  
  ✔ Learn it for ICAO Critical items; transfer the urgency logic.
