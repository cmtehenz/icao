# Unstable Approach

**Catalog ID:** 0484  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Unstable Approach — an approach that does not meet defined stabilization criteria for speed, sink rate, configuration, power, and flight-path alignment by a required gate, typically at one thousand feet in IMC or five hundred feet in VMC.

An unstable approach is a leading precursor to runway excursions, hard landings, and CFIT. Continuing is almost never the professional option. The go-around is.

In H130 operations into confined pads, short runways, and busy Brazilian terminals, stabilization gates protect the landing. ICAO English must make the go-around call immediate, clear, and unambiguous for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

An Unstable Approach is an approach that fails to meet prescribed stabilization parameters — including lateral and vertical path, airspeed, descent rate, configuration, and power setting — at or after the applicable altitude gate, requiring a go-around.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Aproximação instável / Unstable Approach.

Aproximação que não atende aos parâmetros de estabilização prescritos — incluindo trajetória lateral e vertical, velocidade, taxa de descida, configuração e potência — na altitude-porta aplicável ou após ela, exigindo arremetida.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an approach is unstable, the crew should:

- call "unstable" at the gate without negotiation
- execute a go-around immediately — do not try one more correction through the flare
- configure and fly the missed-approach or rejected-landing profile
- advise ATC: going around, unstable approach
- rebrief before the next attempt; do not repeat the same energy mistake

Stabilization criteria typically include:

- on lateral and vertical path
- speed within briefed limits
- sink rate appropriate for the approach
- landing configuration complete
- unique pitch and power for the H130 approach type

**Important:** "It might still work" is not a stabilization criterion. If the gate is missed, go around. The runway will still be there after a safe missed approach.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support unstable-approach management by:

- accepting go-around calls immediately
- providing missed-approach and re-sequencing instructions
- avoiding pressure that encourages continued unstable approaches
- issuing wind and runway updates that may explain energy problems
- coordinating a second approach or diversion without delay

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Short final into Congonhas. You are high and fast at five hundred feet. Sink rate is excessive.

Your first officer calls: "Unstable."

You go around:

ANAC123, going around, unstable approach.

Tower: ANAC123, go around, fly runway heading, climb three thousand, contact approach.

You respond:

ANAC123, going around, runway heading, climbing three thousand.

No debate. No save. No pride. Safe missed approach.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, go around, fly runway heading, climb three thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, radar contact, say intentions.
3. Tower: ANAC123, confirm going around.
4. Approach: ANAC123, vectors for second approach, climb four thousand.
5. Tower: ANAC123, wind check, confirm able to continue or go around.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, going around, runway heading, climbing three thousand.
2. ANAC123, requesting vectors for second approach.
3. ANAC123, going around, unstable.
4. ANAC123, climbing four thousand, vectors for second approach.
5. ANAC123, going around, unable to continue.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Unstable approach is a decision gate, not a discussion forum.

At the gate, the answer is binary: stable or go around.

In the H130, brief the numbers before the approach so the call is automatic. High, fast, unconfigured, excessive sink — any one can be enough.

The worst landings begin with "we can still save it."

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

At five hundred feet you are high and fast. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would execute a go-around and report: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. I would fly the missed-approach profile, then request vectors for another approach or diversion if conditions or fuel require it. I would not continue hoping to correct through the flare.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing because the runway is in sight and close.  
  ✔ Go around at the gate regardless of how close you feel.

- ❌ Negotiating the unstable call from the other pilot.  
  ✔ Treat "unstable" as an action item, not an opinion.

- ❌ Making a vague radio call without "going around."  
  ✔ Lead with the action: going around, then the reason if needed.

- ❌ Repeating the same hot approach without changing the plan.  
  ✔ Rebrief energy management before the next attempt.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

At five hundred feet on final you are high, fast, and above target sink rate. Your first officer calls unstable. Passengers expect a landing.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Your second task**

After the go-around, approach asks intentions. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, going around, unstable approach. Second task: ANAC123, request vectors for second approach. Passenger expectation is not a stabilization criterion. The gate was missed; the go-around is mandatory.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Unstable approaches are where professionalism becomes visible.

Anyone can continue. The professional goes around.

In the H130, brief the gate, honor the call, fly the missed approach, and rebrief. That is how unstable approaches end as stories — not accidents.
