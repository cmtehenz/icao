# Contact

**Catalog ID:** 0137  
**Category:** Radio Communication  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Contact — the ATC instruction directing a pilot to establish two-way communication with a specified unit on a given frequency.

Contact is the most common handoff instruction in aviation.

It transfers you from one controller to the next.

In the H130, flying between Congonhas, Navegantes, Florianópolis, and Curitiba, you will receive Contact multiple times every flight as you pass through different sectors and control areas.

Executing Contact correctly — with the right readback, the right callsign, and the right opening transmission — is a fundamental ICAO Part 2 skill.

## 📖 Meaning

Contact is an ATC instruction directing the pilot to establish two-way radio communication with a specified ATC unit, usually on a stated frequency, as part of a sector handoff or service transfer.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Chame / Entre em contato.

Instrução do controle para o piloto estabelecer comunicação bidirecional com uma unidade ATC específica, geralmente em uma frequência determinada, como parte de uma transferência de setor ou de serviço.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Contact is received, the pilot should:

- acknowledge the instruction with the frequency and unit name
- switch to the new frequency promptly
- establish two-way contact with the receiving unit
- include callsign, altitude, and routing in the initial call
- not leave the current frequency until acknowledged by the new station

Contact instructions commonly occur during:

- sector handoffs during climb or cruise
- approach transfers from Area Control to Approach
- transfers from Approach to Tower before landing
- transfers from Tower to Ground after landing
- transfers from Ground to Tower before departure

**Important:** After receiving Contact, do not delay. Switch promptly. The receiving controller is expecting your call. Delays cause gaps in radar coverage and positional uncertainty.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Contact to:

- transfer service responsibility to the next sector
- ensure the pilot checks in on the correct frequency
- maintain continuous ATC coverage along the route
- sequence the aircraft into the receiving sector's traffic flow
- terminate responsibility on the current frequency

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at flight level nine zero inbound to Navegantes in your H130.

Curitiba Control transmits:

ANAC123, contact Navegantes Approach on one one nine decimal one.

You readback:

Contact Navegantes Approach one one nine decimal one, ANAC123.

You switch frequencies and call:

Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, flight level nine zero, inbound, estimating Navegantes one five.

Approach replies:

ANAC123, Navegantes Approach, radar contact, descend to altitude five thousand, QNH one zero one eight.

The handoff is complete.

One readback. One opening call. You are in the new sector.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Contact is the most repeated handoff phrase in aviation.

You will hear it dozens of times in a single IFR flight.

The trap is routine: because you hear it so often, you stop paying attention to the frequency.

Misread the frequency. Miss a digit. Tune the wrong channel.

Now you are talking to a frequency no one is on — and the controller waiting for your call is holding your clearance.

The discipline is: read back every digit. Verify before you switch. Call promptly.

In the H130 between Congonhas and Florianópolis, you may receive four or five Contact instructions per sector. Treat each one as its own discipline moment.

When you call the new unit, include altitude, routing, and intentions. Do not say "ANAC123, hello." Say who you are, where you are, and where you are going.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the correct procedure when ATC says "contact Approach on 119.7"?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

When ATC instructs me to contact Approach on 119.7, I readback the instruction: Contact Approach one one nine decimal seven, ANAC123. I then switch to that frequency and call: Approach, ANAC123, with you, altitude and routing details. I include my callsign, current altitude, and intention. I do not switch without reading back, and I call the new unit promptly after switching.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Switching frequency without reading back the instruction.  
  ✔ Always readback: Contact [unit] [frequency], ANAC123.

- ❌ Calling the new unit with only a callsign and no position information.  
  ✔ Include altitude and routing: ANAC123, flight level nine zero, inbound Navegantes.

- ❌ Delaying the switch to finish a task in the cockpit.  
  ✔ Switch promptly. The receiving controller is expecting your call.

- ❌ Reading back the wrong frequency after mishearing a digit.  
  ✔ If uncertain, ask for a repeat: Say again frequency, ANAC123.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**SWITCH** — **S**ay the frequency back, **W**ait for handoff acknowledgment, **I**mmediately tune the new frequency, **T**ransmit a full opening call, **C**onfirm radar contact, **H**old until acknowledged.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

São Paulo Control transmits: "ANAC123, contact Curitiba Approach on one two zero decimal four, good day."

**Your first task**

What do you transmit before switching? What do you say on the new frequency?

**Your second task**

You switch and call, but there is no reply after two attempts. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Before switching I transmit: Contact Curitiba Approach one two zero decimal four, ANAC123, good day. On the new frequency I call: Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, flight level nine zero, inbound, with you. If there is no reply after two attempts, I try again on that frequency, then consider returning to the last frequency for a repeat of the instruction, or attempting the guard frequency to confirm my position.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Contact is brief, routine, and critical.

Every handoff is a new controller.

A new set of eyes on your radar blip.

A new responsibility for your separation.

Give them a proper opening call.

Say who you are. Say where you are. Say what you are doing.

And always — always — read back the frequency before you switch.

One missed digit is all it takes to be talking to no one.
