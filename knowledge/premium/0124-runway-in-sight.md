# Runway in Sight

**Catalog ID:** 0124  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Runway in Sight — the pilot report confirming visual contact with the runway during an instrument approach.

These three words carry enormous operational weight.

At Florianópolis or Curitiba in reduced visibility, Runway in Sight is the transition point between instrument flight and visual landing.

It determines whether you continue the approach to land or execute the missed approach.

It is also the required report for some ATC visual approach clearances and transitions from IFR to visual.

Every instrument pilot must know when to say it, what it means, and what it commits you to.

## 📖 Meaning

Runway in Sight is a pilot report stating that the pilot has acquired sufficient visual contact with the runway to continue the approach and land, typically made at or before the applicable minima on an instrument approach.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Pista à vista.

Reporte do piloto informando que obteve contato visual suficiente com a pista para continuar a aproximação e pousar, tipicamente feito em ou antes dos mínimos aplicáveis em uma aproximação por instrumentos.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

A Runway in Sight report is used when:

- the pilot has acquired the runway during an instrument approach at or before minimums
- ATC requests confirmation of visual contact for a visual approach clearance
- the pilot is transitioning from IFR to visual flight for the approach and landing
- ATC needs confirmation before issuing a landing clearance under special conditions

Runway in Sight operations commonly include:

- report made at or before the decision height or minimum descent altitude
- confirmation that the required visual references are established
- ATC clearance to land following the pilot report
- go-around executed if the runway is not in sight at minimums

**Important:** Runway in Sight is not the same as airport in sight. The required visual reference depends on the approach type and applicable minima. The runway, or its approach lighting, must be identifiable — not just the general airport environment.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers use the Runway in Sight report to:

- transition an aircraft from IFR to a visual approach or landing
- confirm visual contact before issuing a landing clearance
- manage the flow between instrument and visual approaches
- provide pilot-in-command responsibility confirmation
- coordinate between approach and tower when visibility is marginal

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on the ILS approach to Florianópolis runway one four.

Ceiling is reported at five hundred feet.

Visibility is twelve hundred meters.

You are descending through the glide slope, monitoring instruments, waiting.

At three hundred feet, you look up.

The approach lights appear in the mist.

At two hundred fifty feet, you acquire the runway threshold.

You transmit:

ANAC123, runway in sight.

Tower responds:

ANAC123, cleared to land runway one four, wind calm.

You continue visually and land.

Had the runway not appeared by decision height, you would have transmitted:

Going around, ANAC123.

The three words Runway in Sight made the difference between landing and going around.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, report runway in sight.
2. Approach: ANAC123, advise runway in sight for visual approach.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway one four in sight, cleared to land.
4. Tower: ANAC123, if runway in sight, cancel IFR and report final.
5. Approach: ANAC123, radar service terminated, maintain VFR, report runway in sight.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, runway in sight.
2. ANAC123, runway in sight, request visual approach.
3. Runway one four in sight, ANAC123.
4. ANAC123, runway in sight, canceling IFR, visual final.
5. ANAC123, runway not in sight, going around.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Runway in Sight is one of the most important reports in instrument flying.

It marks the moment your eyes take over from the instruments.

Before that moment: fly the instruments. Precisely. Without looking outside prematurely.

The mistake is looking out too early, losing instrument scan, and discovering you are below minimums with no runway in sight.

Fly instruments until you see the runway.

Then — and only then — say runway in sight and transition to visual.

In the H130, approaching Florianópolis in low visibility, resist the temptation to look out at 500 feet.

Fly the needles.

At decision height, if the runway is there — say it. Land it.

If not — go around immediately. Not in two seconds. Now.

The runway will be there on the next approach.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is the significance of the Runway in Sight report during an ILS approach?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

The Runway in Sight report during an ILS approach indicates that I have acquired the required visual references to continue the approach and land. This report is made at or before the decision height. If the runway or its approach lighting is in sight at decision height, I continue to a landing. If the runway is not in sight by decision height, I must execute the missed approach immediately. The report transitions the approach from instrument to visual flight and is required before continuing below minimums.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying runway in sight when only the airport environment or city lights are visible.  
  ✔ The runway, or its approach lights, must be positively identified — not just the general area.

- ❌ Looking outside too early and losing the instrument scan below minimums.  
  ✔ Fly instruments precisely until decision height, then acquire the runway.

- ❌ Delaying the go-around when the runway is not in sight at minimums.  
  ✔ If the runway is not in sight at decision height, go around immediately.

- ❌ Not reporting runway in sight when asked by ATC.  
  ✔ Respond clearly: ANAC123, runway in sight or ANAC123, runway not in sight, going around.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Runway in Sight

**Pronunciation:** RUN-way in SITE

**Word Stress**

- Runway → RUN-way
- In → in (short)
- Sight → SITE (rhymes with "night")

Practice:

Runway... In... Sight... Runway in Sight.

Together:

ANAC123, runway in sight.

Avoid: *runway in side* — the word is sight (SITE), meaning you can see it. Practice the T at the end.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**SEE** — **S**ight the runway at minimums, **E**nter visual flight, **E**xecute missed approach if not visible.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are on the ILS to Curitiba runway one five. Decision height is two hundred feet. You reach two hundred feet and acquire the approach lights but cannot yet see the runway threshold.

**Your first task**

Is this sufficient to report runway in sight and continue? Explain.

**Your second task**

What do you transmit to ATC if you reach decision height and see nothing?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Approach lighting alone may be sufficient visual reference to continue the approach, depending on the applicable regulatory requirement and approach category. Under ICAO standards, a pilot may continue below decision height if they can maintain the required visual reference, which for certain approaches includes approach lighting. If the runway is not identifiable and no usable visual reference exists at decision height, I would execute the missed approach immediately and transmit: Going around, ANAC123.
