# Stress Management

**Catalog ID:** 0466  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Stress Management — recognizing acute and chronic stress responses and using CRM techniques so that stress narrows attention without narrowing safety margins.

Stress is not only emotion. It changes breathing, scanning, time perception, and radio performance. Under acute stress, crews omit readbacks, fixate on one instrument, and stop hearing the other pilot.

For H130 operations — emergencies, weather, confined areas, passenger medical events — stress management supports Part 1 human-factors language, Part 2 clear radio under pressure, and Part 4 crew support. Phraseology must stay clean when stress is high.

## 📖 Meaning

Stress Management is the recognition and control of psychological and physiological stress responses in flight operations, using breathing, task prioritization, CRM support, and standardized procedures so that performance remains adequate for safe decision making and communication.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Gerenciamento de estresse / Stress Management.

Reconhecimento e controle das respostas psicológicas e fisiológicas de estresse nas operações de voo, usando respiração, priorização de tarefas, apoio de CRM e procedimentos padronizados para que o desempenho permaneça adequado à tomada de decisão e à comunicação seguras.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When managing stress, the crew should:

- recognize signs: tunnel vision, rushed speech, missed callouts, anger, freeze
- return to priorities: aviate, navigate, communicate
- use SOP memory items and checklists instead of improvisation
- ask for help: "you have the radios," "confirm checklist"
- keep ATC messages short, standard, and closed-loop

Stress management includes:

- acute stress from failures, GPWS, traffic conflicts, medical events
- chronic stress from roster, life events, organizational pressure
- tactical tools — breathe, slow the speech, simplify the plan
- strategic tools — fitness, sleep, support, remove from duty if impaired

**Important:** Under stress, shorter standard phraseology is safer than long explanations. Fly first. Then speak clearly.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support stressed crews when they:

- hear urgency or emergency calls and provide priority
- simplify clearances on request
- avoid non-essential frequency congestion during emergencies
- confirm critical instructions with hearback
- coordinate assistance after diversion or priority landing

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

In the H130, a caution light and weather on approach spike your stress. Speech accelerates. You catch yourself.

PF flies. You breathe once. Then:

Approach, ANAC123, request delay vectors, working a system caution, will advise.

Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading three six zero, maintain three thousand, advise when ready.

You run the checklist, regain control of pace, then continue or divert with a clear plan.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading three six zero, maintain three thousand, advise ready for approach.
2. Approach: ANAC123, roger priority, cleared present position direct airport, descend two thousand.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway two eight cleared to land, services standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, say again slowly, confirm squawk and intentions.
5. Control: ANAC123, simplified clearance available, advise when ready to copy.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Stress shrinks the world to one problem. CRM expands it again.

Name the priority out loud: "I fly. You talk." That single sentence manages stress better than a speech.

In the H130, startle and stress often arrive together — a warning horn, a passenger event, a sudden cloud. Use SOP. Use the other pilot. Use short ICAO calls.

If your voice is climbing, slow it down on purpose. ATC understands standard words at a steady pace better than fast broken English.

Part 4 skill: tell your colleague you are overloaded. That is strength. Silence is how stress wins.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You feel acute stress after a system warning on approach. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would fly the aircraft first, assign roles, and use the checklist. On the radio I would keep it short: ANAC123, request vectors, working a system problem, will advise. When ready, I would state intentions: continue, go around, or divert. I would not transmit a long stressed explanation while the aircraft is unstable.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Talking faster and longer under stress.  
  ✔ Slow down; use standard short phraseology.

- ❌ Fixating on the warning and ignoring altitude.  
  ✔ Aviate first; then checklist; then ATC.

- ❌ Refusing help from the other pilot.  
  ✔ Delegate radios and checklists explicitly.

- ❌ Translating complex emotions on frequency.  
  ✔ State operational needs: delay, priority, divert, unable.

- Request → reh-KWEST

- Working → WUR-king
- Problem → PROB-lem

Practice:

Request vectors... working a system problem.

Together:

ANAC123, request vectors, working a system problem, will advise.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On final in the H130, a master caution and passenger shout hit at once. You feel tunnel vision. Tower asks if you can continue.

**Your first task**

What do you do in the cockpit?

**Your second task**

What do you say to ATC?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: Aviate — go around if unstable — assign PM to checklist and cabin coordination. Second task: ANAC123, going around, request vectors, working a problem — or ANAC123, request delay, will advise. Stress management means shrinking the radio message while expanding crew support.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Stress management keeps human factors from rewriting the flight path.

Recognize the squeeze. Return to priorities. Speak short standard English.

In the H130, Brazilian operations will give you real stress — weather, systems, people. Use CRM. Use SOP. Use ATC for what you need, not for what you feel.

Fly. Simplify. Then decide.
