# De-Icing System Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0238  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers De-Icing System Failure — loss or malfunction of aircraft systems that remove or prevent ice on critical surfaces in flight, requiring immediate assessment of icing exposure, AFM abnormal procedures, and clear communication with ATC when continued flight in icing is unsafe.

This is an aircraft systems failure, not ground fluid treatment. Ground de-icing/anti-icing removes or prevents ice on the ramp. Airborne de-icing or anti-ice systems protect the aircraft in flight. When those systems fail, the operational answer is usually: exit icing, land if required, and tell ATC what you have lost and what you need.

For H130 helicopter training academy operations, many aircraft have limited ice protection compared with transport jets. A system failure — or the absence of certification for known icing — makes exiting icing and reporting even more important. ICAO exams still expect precise English for system failure and icing avoidance.

## 📖 Meaning

De-Icing System Failure is a malfunction or loss of capability of aircraft equipment designed to remove or prevent ice accretion on critical surfaces or sensors in flight, which may leave the aircraft unprotected in icing conditions and require exit from icing, application of abnormal procedures, and notification to ATC when operational capability is limited.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do sistema de de-icing / De-Icing System Failure.

Malfuncionamento ou perda de capacidade do equipamento da aeronave destinado a remover ou impedir o acúmulo de gelo em superfícies ou sensores críticos em voo, o que pode deixar a aeronave desprotegida em condições de gelo e exigir saída do gelo, aplicação de procedimentos anormais e notificação ao ATC quando a capacidade operacional estiver limitada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When a de-icing or anti-ice system fails, the crew should:

- identify the failed system and apply the AFM abnormal checklist
- assess whether you are in or near icing conditions
- exit icing immediately if unprotected — climb, descend, or divert as appropriate
- advise ATC of the system failure and request altitude or routing to exit icing
- avoid continued flight into known icing when unprotected
- plan precautionary landing if ice accretion or performance degradation continues

De-icing system failure scenarios commonly include:

- anti-ice valve, bleed, or electrical heat failure affecting protected surfaces
- inability to clear or prevent ice on critical areas in cloud at freezing temperatures
- need for priority descent or diversion due unprotected icing exposure
- confusion between ground de-icing needs and airborne system failure reports
- H130 context: limited ice protection — treat icing exposure as urgent

**Important:** Do not use the phrase "de-icing" with ATC without clarifying airborne system failure versus ground treatment. Say what failed and what you need: unable anti-ice, requesting descent to exit icing, requesting diversion. If not certified for known icing, system status does not authorize staying in ice.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to de-icing/anti-ice system failure reports to:

- provide altitudes and routes to exit icing layers
- issue priority handling for unprotected aircraft in icing
- coordinate diversions to suitable airports
- alert other traffic with relevant PIREP information
- request souls, fuel, and icing status when the situation escalates

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are IFR at seven thousand feet toward Florianópolis in cloud. Outside air temperature is minus three. You notice the anti-ice or de-ice system caption and confirm failure per checklist.

Ice begins to form on unprotected areas. You must leave the icing layer.

You transmit:

ANAC123, de-icing system failure, moderate icing, request descent to four thousand to exit icing.

Approach: ANAC123, descend and maintain four thousand, report clear of icing.

You descend into warmer air. Ice stops accreting. You update:

ANAC123, clear of icing, requesting vectors to Florianópolis for precautionary landing.

You land with a failed system, not with an unexplained performance problem in cloud.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger system failure, descend and maintain four thousand, say icing intensity.
2. Center: ANAC123, advise when clear of icing, say intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, diversion approved, expect vectors to Congonhas.
4. Approach: ANAC123, previous aircraft reported icing five to seven thousand, say altitude request.
5. Approach: ANAC123, say souls on board and fuel remaining if diverting.

4. ANAC123, request descent to three thousand to exit icing, anti-ice inoperative.
5. ANAC123, souls four, fuel one hour thirty minutes, diverting.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Airborne system failure and ground de-icing are different worlds. Keep them separate in your English.

If the system that protects you in icing fails, your first operational job is not a long technical lecture on the radio. It is: are we in icing? If yes — exit. Then tell ATC what failed and what altitude or routing you need.

In the H130, you may have little certified ice protection to begin with. A failure makes the rule simpler: do not stay in icing. Descend, climb, or turn — whatever exits the conditions — with clearance.

Examiners listen for structure: nature of the problem, effect on flight, intention, and request. "System problem" is weak. "De-icing system failure, requesting descent to exit icing" is professional.

If ice continues and performance degrades, upgrade urgency. Pan Pan or Mayday is about whether safe flight is in doubt — not about pride.

Exit icing. Apply the checklist. Speak clearly. Land if the aircraft is no longer suitable for the weather ahead.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Your anti-ice or de-icing system fails in cloud at freezing temperature. What do you do and how do you communicate with ATC?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would apply the AFM abnormal procedure, assess icing, and exit icing conditions as soon as practicable by requesting climb, descent, or diversion. I would advise ATC of the system failure, report icing intensity if known, and state my intentions. If performance became critically degraded or safe flight were in doubt, I would declare an urgency or distress message and request priority vectors to the nearest suitable airport.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Staying in icing to "see if the system comes back."  
  ✔ Exit icing immediately when unprotected; troubleshoot while clear if able.

- ❌ Reporting only "system failure" without saying icing or intentions.  
  ✔ State: de-icing system failure, icing, request descent/diversion.

- ❌ Confusing a request for ground de-icing with an airborne system failure call.  
  ✔ Specify airborne system failure and the ATC service you need.

- ❌ Continuing the original IFR cruise through known icing after protection is lost.  
  ✔ Change altitude or route; divert if you cannot remain clear of icing.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**EXIT** — **E**valuate system failure via AFM, **X**out of icing now, **I**nform ATC with clear limitation, **T**erminate exposure — divert if still icing.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are at six thousand feet in cloud, temperature minus two. The de-icing system fails and light icing begins.

**Your first task**

What do you say to Approach?

**Your second task**

After descent you are still in icing at four thousand. What do you say next?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, de-icing system failure, light icing at six thousand, request descent to exit icing. Second task: ANAC123, still in icing at four thousand, request further descent or diversion to nearest suitable airport. I would not continue in icing while unprotected, and I would declare Pan Pan or Mayday if performance or control became critical.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

De-icing system failure turns icing from a weather problem into a systems-and-decision problem.

Checklist. Exit. Report. Divert if needed.

Do not stay in the cloud negotiating with ice while unprotected. Do not bury the message in vague English.

In Brazilian IFR training routes, warmer air or a diversion is often available — if you ask early.

Know what failed. Leave the ice. Tell ATC what you need.

That is how you handle airborne ice-protection failure like a professional in the H130 and on the ICAO test.
