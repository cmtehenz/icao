# Describing a Picture

**Catalog ID:** 0502  
**Category:** ICAO Speaking  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Describing a Picture — the ICAO oral exam task in which you observe an aviation-related image and produce a clear, organized spoken description at Level 4 or above.

Describing a picture is not listing random objects. It is structured observation.

In Part 1, the examiner shows a photograph or drawing — often an airport scene, cockpit, weather event, or helicopter operation — and expects you to describe what you see, where things are, and what may be happening. Organization, vocabulary, and fluency matter more than artistic detail.

For H130 ICAO Delta candidates, picture description is a high-value Part 1 skill: it tests aviation vocabulary under time pressure without requiring memorized ATC scripts.

Understanding how to describe a picture in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 1 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Describing a Picture is the ICAO speaking task type in which the candidate observes an aviation-related image and produces a coherent oral description — covering main elements, location, actions, and relevant context — so that vocabulary range, structure, and fluency can be assessed at Operational Level 4 or higher.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When describing a picture, the candidate should:

- start with the overall scene in one sentence
- move from general to specific: background, then main subjects, then details
- use location language: in the foreground, on the left, behind, above
- name aviation elements accurately when visible: runway, rotor, hangar, tower
- describe actions with present continuous when something is happening
- speculate carefully if needed: "It seems that..." / "They appear to be..."
- for H130 context: use helicopter terms correctly when the image shows rotorcraft

A Level 4 picture description typically includes:

- setting: airport, helipad, en-route, weather scene
- people and roles: pilot, marshaller, passengers, ATC tower
- equipment and aircraft features
- weather or environmental conditions if visible
- a short closing inference about what is happening

**Important:** Do not invent a long story that the picture does not support. Describe what you see first. Speculation is optional and should be marked as speculation.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Picture-based speaking tasks help examiners to:

- elicit aviation vocabulary without a scripted radio exchange
- test organization of ideas under time limits
- assess ability to describe spatial relationships clearly
- probe follow-up questions from the same visual prompt
- separate memorized phraseology from flexible plain English

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

The examiner places a photo on the table: an H130 on a helipad, a marshaller with wands, a hangar in the background, light rain on the pavement.

You begin: This picture shows a helicopter on a helipad. In the foreground there is a marshaller giving signals. Behind the aircraft I can see a hangar. The surface looks wet, so it may be raining.

You continue with rotor, doors, and possible boarding. You do not invent an emergency that is not in the image.

The examiner nods and asks one follow-up about wet-helipad operations. Your description created the bridge for interaction.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Examiner: ANAC123, look at the picture and describe what you see.
2. Examiner: ANAC123, focus on the aircraft in the foreground and continue.
3. Tower: ANAC123, confirm helipad in sight, report when ready for landing.
4. Ground: ANAC123, marshaller is in position, follow signals to the pad.
5. Examiner: ANAC123, what do you think is happening in this scene?

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Picture description rewards structure.

Candidates who panic name five objects with no order. Examiners hear chaos. Candidates who open with the scene, then locate elements, then add one inference sound Level 4.

Use a simple frame: What is this place? What is the main subject? Where are the other elements? What is happening? What might happen next — only if asked or if clearly suggested.

Vocabulary beats poetry. "Helicopter on the helipad, marshaller on the left, hangar behind" is better than searching for rare adjectives.

If you do not know a word, describe the function: "the person who guides the aircraft on the ground" instead of freezing on "marshaller."

In H130 training you already brief visual scenes — landing sites, obstacles, wind indicators. Transfer that briefing habit to the exam picture.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

The examiner shows a picture of an airport apron with a helicopter, a fuel truck, and ground crew. How do you organize a Level 4 description?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would start with the overall scene: This picture shows an airport apron. Then the main subject: There is a helicopter parked near the center. Then related elements: On the right there is a fuel truck, and ground crew are standing near the aircraft. Then conditions or actions: It looks like the helicopter is being prepared for departure. I would keep sentences short, use location words, and stop after a clear structure so the examiner can ask follow-ups.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Listing objects with no spatial organization.  
  ✔ Use foreground / background / left / right systematically.

- ❌ Inventing a dramatic story not supported by the image.  
  ✔ Describe visible facts first; mark speculation clearly.

- ❌ Long silence while searching for perfect vocabulary.  
  ✔ Paraphrase unknown words and keep speaking.

- ❌ Describing only the aircraft and ignoring people or weather.  
  ✔ Cover setting, subjects, actions, and conditions.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** In the Foreground There Is a Marshaller

**Pronunciation:** in thuh FOR-ground thair iz uh MAR-shuh-ler

**Word Stress**

- Foreground → FOR-ground (stress on first)
- There → thair
- Marshaller → MAR-shuh-ler
- Signals → SIG-nulz

Practice:

In the foreground... there is a marshaller.

Together:

In the foreground there is a marshaller giving signals to the helicopter.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**PICTURE** — **P**lace first in one sentence, **I**dentify the main subject, **C**atalog left-right and near-far, **T**ell actions with present continuous, **U**se aviation words you know, **R**eserve speculation for the end, **E**nd cleanly for follow-ups.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

The examiner shows a picture: an H130 on final to a rooftop helipad, city buildings around, light haze.

**Your first task**

Give the opening two sentences of a Level 4 description.

**Your second task**

The examiner asks what risks you see. Answer in interaction style. Include one radio-style intention if you were the pilot: what would you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: This picture shows a helicopter on final approach to a rooftop helipad. Around the pad there are tall buildings, and the visibility looks slightly reduced by haze. Second task: The main risks are obstacles, limited go-around options, and reduced visual references. If I were the pilot I might report: ANAC123, helipad in sight, continuing approach — then monitor obstacles and be ready to go around. Keep the answer structured and stop for the next question.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Describing a picture is structured seeing.

Open with the scene. Locate the main subject. Place the details. Add action. Speculate only when useful.

Short clear sentences beat long confused ones.

You already brief landing sites in the H130. Use the same eyes and the same order in the exam.

That is how picture description becomes a Level 4 strength instead of a panic task.
