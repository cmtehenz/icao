# Whiteout

**Catalog ID:** 0074  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers whiteout — a visibility condition as dangerous as brownout, but in snow and ice environments.

Whiteout occurs when snow or ice particles suspended by rotor downwash eliminate visual contrast with the horizon and surface.

In the H130, whiteout risk exists in southern Brazilian highland operations and any snow-covered landing site.

You must recognize whiteout risk, use instruments, and communicate clearly with ATC.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Whiteout is a visibility condition where snow or ice particles obscure visual references, eliminating contrast between surface and sky during helicopter operations.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Whiteout.

Condição de visibilidade em que partículas de neve ou gelo obscurecem referências visuais, eliminando contraste entre superfície e céu.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Whiteout conditions exist, the pilot should:

- identify whiteout risk before approach — snow-covered or icy surfaces
- use instruments and defined hover references during landing
- inform ATC of whiteout risk at the landing site
- go around immediately if spatial disorientation occurs
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday if unable to maintain aircraft control

Whiteout reporting commonly includes:

- whiteout conditions on landing or departure
- position and landing site description
- going around due whiteout
- Pan Pan if disoriented in whiteout

**Important:** Whiteout can cause complete loss of visual reference in seconds. Go-around is always an option.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Whiteout reports to:

- clear traffic from the landing area
- coordinate if aircraft goes around or becomes disoriented
- maintain awareness of delayed reports in whiteout conditions
- request position and intentions
- prepare for possible urgency or distress declarations

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are landing at a snow-covered helipad in the southern highlands near Curitiba in your H130.

Fresh snow covers the pad and surrounding area.

Before approach, you transmit:

Curitiba Approach, ANAC123, landing snow-covered helipad one five miles south, caution whiteout conditions, will report safe on the ground.

On final, downwash lifts snow and visual references disappear.

You maintain control using the pad edge markers and instruments.

You touch down and report safe when visibility improves.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger whiteout caution, report safe on the ground.
2. Tower: ANAC123, no traffic reported, cleared to land at your discretion.
3. Approach: ANAC123, say if maintaining visual reference.
4. Tower: ANAC123, report go-around if unable to land.
5. Approach: ANAC123, traffic advise when clear of the whiteout area.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Landing snow-covered helipad, caution whiteout conditions, ANAC123.
2. ANAC123, whiteout on final, maintaining instrument reference, will report touchdown.
3. Pan Pan Pan Pan, ANAC123, whiteout, disoriented, going around.
4. Tower, ANAC123, safe on the ground, whiteout landing complete.
5. ANAC123, unable landing due whiteout, going around for reattempt.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Whiteout is brownout in snow — and equally deadly.

The horizon disappears.

The surface disappears.

You may feel level when you are not.

The sequence:

Identify snow risk. Inform ATC. Instruments and references. Go around if lost.

Never descend into whiteout without a go-around plan at every altitude.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "neve" or "perdi o horizonte" on frequency.

Use whiteout conditions or caution whiteout — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130, trust your instruments when the world turns white.

Pan Pan if disoriented — do not press on blind.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is whiteout and how does it differ from brownout?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Whiteout occurs when snow or ice particles obscure visual references, eliminating contrast between surface and sky. Brownout is the same phenomenon with dust or sand. Both require stabilized approach technique, hover references, and readiness to go around. I would inform ATC of caution whiteout conditions, maintain instrument reference, and declare Pan Pan if disoriented.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating snow landing like a normal helipad approach.  
  ✔ Identify whiteout risk and inform ATC before approach.

- ❌ Saying "nevoeiro branco" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use whiteout conditions or caution whiteout.

- ❌ Continuing descent when horizon and surface are invisible.  
  ✔ Go around immediately if spatial disorientation occurs.

- ❌ Not trusting instruments when visual references are lost.  
  ✔ Use instruments and defined hover references in whiteout.
