# V2 Takeoff Safety Speed

**Catalog ID:** 0394  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers V2 Takeoff Safety Speed — the target speed to be reached by screen height after takeoff with one engine inoperative on a multi-engine aeroplane, providing the climb performance required by certification.

V2 is fixed-wing multi-engine exam language, tightly linked to V1 and VR in ICAO Part 2 and Part 4 performance discussions.

For H130 operations: the helicopter does not use V2. Climb-out speeds follow the AFM — Vy, VTOSS where published for category procedures, or normal takeoff profile speeds — not V2. Learn V2 for ICAO English; use helicopter performance speeds in actual H130 flying and radio work.

## 📖 Meaning

V2 is the takeoff safety speed: the calibrated airspeed that must be attained at screen height following a continued takeoff after engine failure, and maintained to achieve the required one-engine-inoperative climb gradient on a multi-engine aeroplane.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Velocidade de segurança de decolagem V2 / V2 Takeoff Safety Speed.

Velocidade calibrada que deve ser atingida na altura de tela após uma decolagem continuada com falha de motor, e mantida para obter o gradiente de subida com um motor inoperante exigido em aviões multimotores. O H130 não utiliza V2.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When discussing V2, the crew should:

- calculate V2 with takeoff performance data (aeroplane)
- brief target V2 after rotation for engine-out climb (aeroplane SOP)
- maintain V2 to acceleration altitude as required by procedure
- for H130: brief AFM takeoff and climb speeds — not V2
- explain V2 correctly in exams without applying it to the H130 on frequency

V2-related concepts include:

- relationship V1 ≤ VR ≤ V2 (typical ordering constraints)
- screen height (commonly 35 feet dry / as certified)
- OEI climb gradient at V2
- flap configuration and V2 for the takeoff flap setting
- H130: use AFM climb speeds; category A profiles use type-specific speeds if applicable — still not called V2 in H130 radio

**Important:** V2 assumes a continued takeoff after engine failure on a multi-engine aeroplane. The H130 cannot continue a takeoff after engine failure in that sense — autorotation replaces OEI climb at V2.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

ATC does not assign V2, but related events affect control when:

- an aeroplane continues after engine failure and requests priority
- climb performance is limited and altitude restrictions cannot be met
- crews report unable to meet SID gradient after engine failure
- emergency return follows a continued takeoff with failure
- missed climb capability affects departure sequencing

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Exam context: after V1 engine failure, rotate at VR, fly V2 to acceleration altitude.

H130 context at Congonhas: normal takeoff, climb at AFM recommended speed. If the engine fails — autorotation, not "maintain V2."

If performance-limited on climb gradient (all engines), you say:

ANAC123, unable SID climb gradient, request alternate departure.

You never say "unable V2" in the H130. Correct concept, correct vocabulary for the type.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, cleared for takeoff, wind three four zero at eight.
2. Departure: ANAC123, climb via SID, advise able to meet gradient.
3. Radar: ANAC123, confirm engine failure, say intentions.
4. Approach: ANAC123, priority handling, vectors for return.
5. Departure: ANAC123, stop climb, maintain three thousand — advise able.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, cleared for takeoff, wind three four zero at eight.
2. ANAC123, climb via SID, wilco — or unable gradient if limited.
3. ANAC123, Mayday, engine failure, autorotating — (H130).
4. ANAC123, requesting priority return, engine failure — (multi-engine OEI).
5. ANAC123, maintaining three thousand, unable higher at this time.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

V2 is the safety speed of the continued takeoff.

After engine failure above V1, you fly V2 to get the certified OEI climb. That sentence belongs in the oral exam and in jet/turboprop SOPs.

In the H130, rewrite the sentence: after engine failure, you do not have an OEI climb at V2. You have autorotation. Performance speeds for normal takeoff are AFM speeds — learn their names from the manual, not from jet mnemonics.

Part 4 loves relationships: V1, VR, V2. Practice explaining them in order. Then state clearly that helicopters use different performance speed schedules.

Precision in vocabulary is ICAO Level 4. Mixing types is confusion.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What is V2? How does it relate to V1? Does the H130 use V2?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

V2 is the takeoff safety speed flown after a continued takeoff with one engine inoperative to achieve the required climb gradient. V1 is the decision speed before that continued takeoff logic applies. Typically V1 is at or below VR, and V2 is at or above VR. The H130 does not use V2; after engine failure it autorotates, and normal climb uses AFM speeds. I keep exam definitions and helicopter procedures separate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Calling V2 on H130 takeoff.  
  ✔ Use AFM takeoff and climb speeds; no V2 callout.

- ❌ Defining V2 as decision speed.  
  ✔ V1 is decision speed; V2 is takeoff safety / OEI climb speed.

- ❌ Forgetting screen height and climb gradient in exam answers.  
  ✔ Mention screen height and OEI gradient when defining V2.

- ❌ Implying the H130 can continue takeoff after engine failure at V2.  
  ✔ State clearly: single-engine helicopter — autorotation, not OEI climb.
