# Hydraulic Boost Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0447  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Hydraulic Boost Failure — loss of hydraulic assistance to the flight controls, resulting in significantly higher control forces and increased pilot workload.

The aircraft may remain controllable, but every input becomes heavier. Prolonged flight without boost can lead to fatigue, overcontrol, and degraded precision in the hover or on approach.

For H130 helicopter operations, hydraulic boost failure requires an immediate assessment of controllability, a landing as soon as practicable, and clear communication with ATC using Pan Pan or Mayday depending on control authority remaining.

Understanding how to report hydraulic boost failure in ICAO-standard English is essential for Part 2 and Part 3 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Hydraulic Boost Failure is the loss of hydraulic power assistance to the flight controls, requiring the pilot to move the controls against much higher mechanical forces without normal boost.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha do reforço hidráulico (boost).

Perda da assistência hidráulica aos comandos de voo, exigindo que o piloto movimente os comandos contra forças mecânicas muito maiores, sem o reforço normal.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When hydraulic boost fails, the pilot should:

- maintain control with smooth, deliberate inputs — expect heavy forces
- avoid abrupt cyclic or collective movements
- land as soon as practicable at a suitable site
- declare Pan Pan if the aircraft remains controllable
- declare Mayday if control forces prevent safe continued flight or landing

Hydraulic boost failure reporting commonly includes:

- hydraulic boost failure or loss of hydraulic assistance
- controls heavy, request priority landing
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested
- request a longer final if needed to manage workload

**Important:** Controllable does not mean continue the mission. Land soon while you still have the strength and precision to flare and touch down safely.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers respond to hydraulic boost failure to:

- provide priority routing and an uncomplicated approach
- avoid tight vectors, holds, or last-minute runway changes
- coordinate emergency services for a possible hard landing
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- give wind and runway information early to reduce workload

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are in cruise at two thousand feet in your H130 from Curitiba toward Joinville.

Cyclic and collective forces suddenly become very heavy. You recognize hydraulic boost failure, slow your control inputs, and plan a precautionary landing.

You declare Pan Pan, report hydraulic boost failure with heavy controls, and request priority landing at Joinville with a long final. Approach cooperates. You fly a stable approach with deliberate inputs and land safely.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, hydraulic boost failure, say souls on board.
2. Approach: ANAC123, proceed direct Joinville, expect long final runway one five, priority approved.
3. Tower: ANAC123, runway one five cleared to land, emergency services standing by.
4. Approach: ANAC123, wind zero nine zero at zero eight, no speed restrictions.
5. Tower: ANAC123, report short final, wind calm.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Without boost, you are flying a much heavier machine.

Smooth is mandatory. Jabbing the cyclic will fight you and waste strength. Plan a simple approach: long final, stable descent, minimal last-second corrections.

On the H130, land as soon as practicable. Do not accept a complex circuit or extended VFR tour. Fatigue is part of the emergency.

If you cannot safely control the aircraft for landing, it is Mayday — not stubborn Pan Pan.

Tell ATC you need a long final and priority. That single request reduces workload more than any clever radio speech.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Hydraulic boost fails in cruise. Controls are heavy but usable. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Pan Pan Pan Pan, Approach, hydraulic boost failure, controls heavy but controllable, request priority landing nearest suitable with long final. I would state souls on board when asked and upgrade to Mayday if control becomes unsafe.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Continuing a long cross-country with heavy controls.  
  ✔ Land as soon as practicable before fatigue sets in.

- ❌ Accepting tight vectors and a short final.  
  ✔ Request long final and simple routing.

- ❌ Making large, abrupt control inputs out of habit.  
  ✔ Use smooth, deliberate inputs only.

- ❌ Saying only "hydraulic problem" without boost or control status.  
  ✔ State hydraulic boost failure and whether controls remain usable.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Hydraulic boost failure turns a light helicopter into hard work.

Pan Pan when you can still fly it. Mayday when you cannot.
