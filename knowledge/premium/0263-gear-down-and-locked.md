# Gear Down and Locked

**Catalog ID:** 0263  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Medium  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Gear Down and Locked — the confirmed safe landing-gear configuration in which all landing gear legs are extended and mechanically locked for landing, and the standard phraseology used to report or confirm that status to ATC.

Gear down and locked is confirmation language. It is how crews and controllers share confidence that the aircraft is configured for a normal landing. It appears in readbacks, tower queries, and checklist callouts that become radio reports when ATC asks.

For H130 helicopter ICAO Delta academy operations in Brazil, many H130s use skids and do not have a retractable gear to lock. Still teach the phrase for ICAO Part 2 exams and for multi-type careers. On skids, your "configured for landing" callouts differ — but "gear down and locked" remains essential wheeled-aircraft English you must speak correctly.

Understanding this confirmation phrase is essential for Part 2 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Gear Down and Locked is the landing-gear status in which all gear legs are fully extended and locked in the landing position, confirmed by cockpit indications (typically green lights) and required before a normal wheeled landing.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Trem baixado e travado / Gear Down and Locked.

Status do trem de pouso em que todas as pernas estão totalmente estendidas e travadas na posição de pouso, confirmado pelas indicações do cockpit (tipicamente luzes verdes) e exigido antes de um pouso normal com rodas.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When confirming gear down and locked, the crew should:

- select gear down at the appropriate point on approach
- verify green down-and-locked indications for all gear
- complete landing checklist configuration callouts
- report gear down and locked when ATC requests confirmation
- do not report down and locked if any unsafe or missing indication remains
- for skid-equipped H130s: know the phrase for exams; use accurate configuration language in real ops

Gear down and locked commonly appears when:

- tower asks "confirm gear down" on final
- crew reports configuration after extension
- ATC coordinates after a previous unsafe indication that is now resolved
- training flights practice standard landing reports
- examiners test Part 2 confirmation phraseology

**Important:** Only say "gear down and locked" when indications confirm it. Saying it from memory without checking is a classic error. If unsafe, say unsafe — never fake the greens.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers use gear-down confirmation to:

- verify landing configuration on final, especially for training or after anomalies
- reduce the risk of wheels-up landings from configuration omission
- standardize tower–pilot confirmation on short final
- close the loop after previous gear warnings
- support safe landing clearances

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Wheeled-aircraft training approach to Navegantes. Tower wants confirmation on short final.

Tower: ANAC123, confirm gear down.

You have three greens. Checklist complete.

You respond:

ANAC123, gear down and locked.

Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land runway zero seven.

Simple. Correct. Verified.

In the skid-equipped H130 on the same approach, tower may not ask gear — but on the ICAO exam, you will still produce the phrase cleanly when the scenario is a wheeled aircraft.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, confirm gear down.
2. Tower: ANAC123, report gear down and locked.
3. Tower: ANAC123, cleared to land, confirm gear down.
4. Approach: ANAC123, before transfer to tower, confirm gear extended.
5. Tower: ANAC123, gear down and locked copied, cleared to land.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, gear down and locked.
2. ANAC123, gear down and locked, cleared to land.
3. ANAC123, cleared to land, gear down and locked.
4. ANAC123, gear extended, three greens, down and locked.
5. ANAC123, gear down and locked, runway zero seven.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Gear down and locked is a verification sentence, not a habit sound.

Look at the lights. Then speak. The order matters.

On Part 2 tests, this is basic professionalism. Controllers ask because wheels-up landings still happen from configuration omission worldwide. Your job is a true confirmation.

In the H130 academy with skids, do not radio "gear down and locked" as if you had retractable wheels. Learn the phrase for the exam. In the aircraft, use the configuration language that matches your rotorcraft.

Three greens. Then the words. Never the words first.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

On short final, tower asks you to confirm gear down. Your indications show three greens. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would verify the down-and-locked indications, then report gear down and locked. I would only use that phrase if the indications confirm all gear are locked. If any unsafe indication remained, I would report the unsafe status instead of confirming down and locked.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Saying "gear down and locked" without checking indications.  
  ✔ Verify greens first, then report.

- ❌ Answering only "roger" when tower asks to confirm gear.  
  ✔ Give the full confirmation: gear down and locked.

- ❌ Reporting down and locked with an unsafe light still on.  
  ✔ Report unsafe indication; do not fake confirmation.

- ❌ Using wheeled gear callouts in a skid H130 as routine radio.  
  ✔ Match real aircraft configuration; keep exam phraseology for exam scenarios.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** ANAC123, Gear Down and Locked

**Pronunciation:** AY-nak WUN TOO THREE, GEER DOWN and LOKT

**Word Stress**

- Gear → GEER
- Down → DOWN
- Locked → LOKT (clear final consonant)

Practice:

Gear down... and locked.

Together:

ANAC123, gear down and locked.

Short final. Tower: "Confirm gear down." You see three green lights. Checklist complete.

**Your first task**

What do you transmit?
