# Altitude Restriction

**Catalog ID:** 0158  
**Category:** ATC Phraseology  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Altitude Restriction — the ATC instruction or published constraint that requires an aircraft to be at or comply with a specific altitude requirement.

Altitude restrictions appear in two contexts: issued directly by ATC during a clearance, and published on procedure charts as part of SID, STAR, and approach procedures.

At Brazilian airports including Florianópolis, Navegantes, Joinville, and Curitiba, altitude restrictions appear constantly in IFR operations — in departure clearances, STAR profiles, and approach sequencing.

Understanding altitude restrictions — the difference between "at or above," "at or below," and "at" — and knowing how to read them back and comply with them professionally is fundamental ICAO knowledge.

## 📖 Meaning

An Altitude Restriction is an ATC instruction or published procedure requirement specifying that an aircraft must be at a particular altitude, not above a particular altitude, or not below a particular altitude at a defined point or within a defined segment of flight.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Restrição de altitude.

Instrução do controle de tráfego aéreo ou requisito de procedimento publicado especificando que uma aeronave deve estar em uma altitude específica, não acima de uma altitude específica, ou não abaixo de uma altitude específica em um ponto definido ou dentro de um segmento de voo definido.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When the crew encounters an altitude restriction, they should:

- determine whether the restriction is an ATC instruction or a published chart restriction
- confirm whether the restriction is "at," "at or above," or "at or below"
- load or note the restriction in the FMS altitude constraint if applicable
- comply with the restriction at or before the specified fix or segment
- advise ATC if unable to comply before reaching the restricted fix

Altitude restriction types commonly encountered:

- at: cross the fix at exactly that altitude — not above, not below
- at or above: cross the fix at or higher than the specified altitude
- at or below: cross the fix at or lower than the specified altitude
- between: cross the fix within a defined altitude band (dual restriction)
- maintain: hold that altitude continuously through a segment

**Important:** "Cross LUMEN at or above six thousand" and "cross LUMEN at six thousand" are different instructions. The first gives you room to be higher. The second requires a precise altitude crossing. Read the restriction type correctly — it determines your entire descent or climb strategy for that segment.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers and procedure designers use altitude restrictions to:

- ensure terrain and obstacle clearance throughout the procedure
- maintain vertical separation between successive aircraft
- coordinate traffic between adjacent sectors at defined altitudes
- protect approach corridors from conflicting traffic above or below
- optimize noise abatement by keeping aircraft high over populated areas

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on the STAR inbound to Florianópolis in your H130.

Approach transmits:

ANAC123, cross IDAMO at or below flight level nine zero, descend and maintain four thousand.

You load the altitude restriction at IDAMO in the GPS: at or below flight level nine zero.

You begin your descent from flight level one two zero, targeting flight level eight five at IDAMO to give yourself margin while complying with "at or below."

You respond:

Cross IDAMO at or below flight level nine zero, descend and maintain four thousand, ANAC123.

You cross IDAMO at flight level eight five — below nine zero, restriction complied with.

You continue descent to four thousand as cleared.

At four thousand, you level off awaiting further approach clearance.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, cross LUMEN at or below six thousand.
2. Control: ANAC123, cross IDAMO at or above flight level nine zero.
3. Approach: ANAC123, cross BRASA at five thousand, descend and maintain three thousand.
4. Control: ANAC123, maintain altitude restriction as published on the STAR.
5. Approach: ANAC123, altitude restriction at LUMEN cancelled, descend and maintain three thousand.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Altitude restrictions are not suggestions.

Whether published on a chart or issued by ATC, they define your authorized flight path.

The most critical skill is identifying which type of restriction you are dealing with.

"At or above" — you have a floor. Be at or above that floor.

"At or below" — you have a ceiling. Be at or below that ceiling.

"At" — you have a point. Be at that point. Neither above nor below.

In the H130 operating into Brazilian airports with terrain-critical approaches — Navegantes, Joinville, Florianópolis — altitude restrictions define your minimum safe path over terrain. Violating them is not a procedural mistake. It is a safety failure.

Brief every published restriction before the descent. Program altitude constraints in the FMS where possible. Cross-check the GPS altitude at each waypoint.

If you will not make a restriction — tell ATC before you reach the fix, not after.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

ATC instructs you to cross a fix at or above six thousand. What does this mean and what would happen if you crossed at five thousand five hundred?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

"Cross at or above six thousand" means I must be at an altitude of six thousand feet or higher when my aircraft is at that fix. If I crossed at five thousand five hundred, I would be below the restriction — a violation of the ATC clearance. This could compromise separation from traffic below, create a terrain conflict, or violate a minimum altitude requirement protecting the approach corridor. If I anticipated not meeting the restriction — due to performance, winds, or configuration — I would advise ATC before reaching the fix: ANAC123, unable to cross LUMEN at or above six thousand, request revised clearance. ATC would then adjust my routing or altitude assignment.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Treating "at or above" as "at exactly" and stopping descent to hit the altitude precisely.  
  ✔ "At or above" means you may be higher. Use the flexibility, but meet the minimum.

- ❌ Not reading back the restriction type — omitting "or above" or "or below."  
  ✔ The type changes the meaning entirely. Always include it: cross LUMEN at or below six thousand.

- ❌ Not programming published altitude restrictions into the FMS.  
  ✔ Load them as FMS altitude constraints to get automated crossing alerts.

- ❌ Descending through a restriction without ATC clearance because "it looked right."  
  ✔ Altitude restrictions are clearances. Never descend below a restriction without authorization.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Cross LUMEN at or Below Six Thousand

**Pronunciation:** kros LOO-men at or beh-LO siks THOW-zund

**Word Stress**

- Cross → kros
- LUMEN → LOO-men (stress on first syllable)
- At or Below → at or beh-LO (stress on "below")
- Six Thousand → siks THOW-zund

Practice:

Cross LUMEN... at or below... six thousand.

Together:

Cross LUMEN at or below six thousand, ANAC123.

Speak "at or below" as a connected phrase — not as separate words with pauses between them.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**CROSS** — **C**heck the restriction type: at, at or above, at or below, **R**ead back the full restriction including type, **O**perational planning starts early — brief it before descent, **S**ay something if you cannot comply before reaching the fix, **S**et FMS altitude constraints for each restricted fix.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Navegantes Approach transmits: "ANAC123, cross ARCOS at or above four thousand, then descend and maintain two thousand five hundred."

**Your first task**

Read back the complete instruction.

**Your second task**

At ARCOS your aircraft is at three thousand eight hundred due to a strong tailwind that accelerated your descent. The published restriction is at or above four thousand. What do you do?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would read back: Cross ARCOS at or above four thousand, then descend and maintain two thousand five hundred, ANAC123. If I crossed ARCOS at three thousand eight hundred, I was below the restriction — a deviation. I would immediately report: ANAC123, crossed ARCOS at three thousand eight hundred, below restriction, advise. ATC needs that information to assess separation and obstacle clearance. If I recognized before reaching ARCOS that I would be below four thousand, I would have advised in advance: ANAC123, unable to meet ARCOS restriction, passing three thousand eight hundred, request clearance.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Altitude restrictions define your authorized altitude at precise points along your route.

They protect you from terrain, protect other traffic from you, and structure the arrival flow.

Know the type. Brief the value. Program the FMS. Cross at the right altitude.

And if you cannot comply — speak up before the fix.

That is professional altitude management.

Every restriction crossed correctly is a piece of the safety system working as designed.
