# Controlled Flight Into Terrain

**Catalog ID:** 0482  
**Category:** Emergency  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Controlled Flight Into Terrain (CFIT) — an accident or near-accident in which an airworthy aircraft under pilot control is flown into terrain, water, or obstacles with no prior awareness of the collision course.

CFIT is not a loss of control. The aircraft is flying. The crew is flying it. The path is simply incompatible with the Earth.

In H130 operations near escarpments, offshore platforms, night coastal routes, and IMC approaches, CFIT remains a primary killer. Terrain awareness, MSA discipline, GPWS response, and assertive go-around culture are the barriers.

Understanding how to communicate CFIT threats and recoveries in ICAO-standard English — climb calls, terrain warnings, and missed approaches — is essential for Part 2, Part 3, and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Controlled Flight Into Terrain is an event in which an airworthy aircraft, under the control of the flight crew, is inadvertently flown into terrain, water, or an obstacle, without the crew being aware of the impending collision until it is too late or nearly too late.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Voo controlado contra o terreno / Controlled Flight Into Terrain (CFIT).

Evento em que uma aeronave aeronavegável, sob controle da tripulação, é inadvertidamente conduzida contra terreno, água ou obstáculo, sem que a tripulação tenha consciência da colisão iminente até ser tarde ou quase tarde demais.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When CFIT risk is detected, the crew should:

- climb immediately to a safe altitude — maximum performance if required
- respect GPWS, TAWS, and MSA warnings without delay
- abandon the approach or visual path that created the conflict
- advise ATC: terrain warning, climbing, or going around
- rebrief route, MSA, and approach only after clear of terrain

CFIT prevention includes:

- knowing MSA and sector minimum altitudes along the route
- using instrument procedures instead of hopeful night visuals
- monitoring altitude versus distance on approach
- challenging any descent below safe altitude without positive terrain clearance

**Important:** CFIT happens in controlled flight. Smoothness is not safety. If terrain awareness and the clearance disagree, climb first and explain second.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers help prevent and mitigate CFIT by:

- issuing low altitude alerts and immediate climb instructions
- assigning altitudes at or above MSA and approach minima profiles
- providing terrain-related advisories in mountainous sectors
- clearing missed approaches without delay
- coordinating minimum vectoring altitudes during radar vectors

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

IMC descent toward Belo Horizonte. You are below the published profile. GPWS sounds: "Terrain, terrain."

You pull and climb:

ANAC123, terrain warning, climbing, going around.

Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain five thousand, fly heading three six zero, radar vectors.

You respond:

ANAC123, climbing five thousand, heading three six zero.

Later you discover the step-down fix was missed. Controlled flight. Wrong path. Terrain almost won.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, low altitude alert, climb immediately five thousand.
2. Radar: ANAC123, climb and maintain minimum safe altitude, say intentions.
3. Approach: ANAC123, go around, climb five thousand, heading three six zero.
4. Control: ANAC123, terrain in your twelve o'clock, climb now.
5. Approach: ANAC123, confirm altitude, you are below profile.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. ANAC123, climbing immediately five thousand, terrain warning.
2. ANAC123, climbing minimum safe altitude, going around.
3. ANAC123, going around, climbing five thousand, heading three six zero.
4. ANAC123, climbing now, terrain acknowledged.
5. ANAC123, correcting altitude, climbing to profile.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

CFIT is the accident of the competent and the busy.

Nobody intends to hit the mountain. They intend to finish the approach, save time, or trust a familiar visual picture.

In the H130, treat every night and IMC descent as a CFIT rehearsal. MSA is law. GPWS is law. Ego is not.

Climb is always available. Impact is not reversible.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You receive a terrain warning on approach in IMC. What do you say and do?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would execute an immediate climb and missed approach, then report: ANAC123, terrain warning, climbing, going around. I would not continue descending to analyze the chart. After clear of terrain, I would accept vectors and rebrief before any further approach.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Delaying climb to verify the warning on the chart.  
  ✔ Climb first; verify later when safe.

- ❌ Continuing a night visual below MSA without positive terrain clearance.  
  ✔ Stay at or above safe altitude until on a published path.

- ❌ Softening the radio call during a terrain escape.  
  ✔ State climbing and going around clearly and immediately.

- ❌ Re-attempting the same unstable path without a new brief.  
  ✔ Rebrief MSA, fixes, and vertical profile before the next approach.

## 🧠 Memory Trick

**CLIMB** — **C**limb on warning without debate, **L**eave the unsafe path, **I**nform ATC immediately, **M**aintain MSA thereafter, **B**rief before any re-approach.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

On an IMC approach you are below profile. GPWS calls terrain. Mountains are ahead.

**Your first task**

What do you say to ATC while climbing?

**Your second task**

Approach asks your intentions after you are level at five thousand. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, terrain warning, climbing, going around. Second task: ANAC123, request vectors for another approach, need time to rebrief. CFIT is prevented by immediate climb and a new plan — not by hoping the old path was almost right.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

CFIT is controlled flight on an uncontrolled collision course.

The aircraft was fine. The path was not.

In the H130, MSA, warnings, and go-arounds are your terrain firewall. When in doubt, climb. When warned, climb. When low, climb.

Survive first. Explain second. Rebrief third.
