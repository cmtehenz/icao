# Radar Vectors

**Catalog ID:** 0010  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers one of the most common services provided by Air Traffic Control.

Imagine you're approaching an unfamiliar airport in poor weather. Instead of navigating every waypoint yourself, ATC begins giving you headings one by one.

This is called Radar Vectors.

Understanding this concept is essential because it appears frequently in real operations and is a common topic during ICAO English assessments.

## 📖 Meaning

Radar Vectors are navigational instructions issued by Air Traffic Control, usually in the form of magnetic headings, to guide an aircraft toward a specific route, approach, or destination.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Vetoração radar.

Instruções de rumo fornecidas pelo controle para conduzir a aeronave até uma rota, aproximação ou posição desejada.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When an aircraft is under Radar Vectors, ATC temporarily assumes responsibility for lateral navigation.

Instead of navigating by GPS, VOR, RNAV, or published procedures, the pilot follows the headings assigned by the controller.

Radar vectors are commonly used for:

- sequencing arriving aircraft
- positioning aircraft for an instrument approach
- avoiding conflicting traffic
- avoiding adverse weather
- assisting aircraft experiencing navigation problems
- simplifying complex traffic situations

The pilot must continue to comply with assigned altitudes, speeds and other ATC instructions.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Radar Vectors to:

- establish aircraft on final approach
- provide traffic separation
- reduce pilot workload
- sequence arrivals efficiently
- reroute aircraft around weather
- assist aircraft with navigation difficulties
- expedite traffic flow

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You're flying your H130 toward Navegantes Airport.

Weather conditions are marginal, and several aircraft are arriving simultaneously.

Instead of instructing you to fly the complete published arrival, Approach says:

"ANAC123, fly heading zero niner zero."

A few minutes later:

"ANAC123, turn left heading zero six zero."

Then:

"ANAC123, five miles from the final approach fix, cleared ILS approach runway zero seven."

During this entire sequence, ATC is navigating your aircraft using radar vectors.

You simply fly the assigned headings until cleared for the approach.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, fly heading zero niner zero.
2. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading zero six zero.
3. Approach: ANAC123, turn right heading one two zero.
4. Approach: ANAC123, expect radar vectors for the ILS approach.
5. Approach: ANAC123, radar vectors to final.

## 🎙 Professional Pilot Readbacks

1. Heading zero niner zero, ANAC123.
2. Left heading zero six zero, ANAC123.
3. Right heading one two zero, ANAC123.
4. Expecting radar vectors for the ILS, ANAC123.
5. Radar vectors to final, ANAC123.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Imagine you're driving in a city you've never visited before.

Normally, you follow your GPS.

But suddenly, a police officer begins directing traffic because there's a major accident ahead.

Instead of following the GPS, you follow the officer's hand signals.

Radar Vectors work exactly like that.

ATC temporarily becomes your navigator.

You stop following the published route and simply fly the headings they assign.

When the controller says:

"Fly heading zero niner zero."

Your job is simple.

Turn to that heading.

Maintain the assigned altitude.

Continue listening carefully for the next instruction.

Eventually, ATC will guide you back onto the published approach or route.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Why would ATC provide radar vectors to an aircraft?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

ATC provides radar vectors to simplify navigation, sequence arriving traffic, maintain separation between aircraft, avoid weather, or position an aircraft for an instrument approach. The pilot follows the assigned headings until receiving further instructions.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Thinking Radar Vectors cancel altitude restrictions.  
  They don't. Continue complying with all assigned altitudes unless ATC changes them.

- ❌ Returning to the GPS route without authorization.  
  Remain on the assigned heading until ATC issues another instruction or clears you to resume own navigation.

- ❌ Reading back only: Roger.  
  ✔ Correct: Heading zero niner zero, ANAC123.

- ❌ Confusing "heading" with "course."  
  A heading is the direction the aircraft's nose is pointing. A course is the intended path over the ground.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

ATC says:

Approach: ANAC123, fly heading zero niner zero.

Pilot: _______________________

Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading zero six zero.

**Your first task**

Read back both instructions exactly as a professional pilot.

**Your second task**

Answer this question:

While receiving radar vectors, should you continue following your GPS flight plan? Why or why not?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

No. While receiving radar vectors, I should follow the headings assigned by ATC. The controller is providing lateral navigation, and I should not return to my GPS route until instructed to resume own navigation or cleared for the approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Today you learned that navigation is sometimes a shared responsibility.

Most of the time, you navigate the aircraft.

During Radar Vectors, ATC temporarily helps guide you by assigning headings that improve safety and efficiency.

Remember these three key ideas:

- Follow the assigned heading precisely.
- Continue complying with altitude and speed restrictions.
- Do not resume your planned route until ATC instructs you to do so.

Great pilots don't just know where they're going—they know who is responsible for the navigation at every moment of the flight.
