# Landing Performance

**Catalog ID:** 0384  
**Category:** Performance  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Landing Performance — the calculated capability of the aircraft to approach, touch down, and stop or come to a safe hover/landing within the available landing distance under current weight, weather, and surface conditions.

Landing Performance decides whether the runway or pad is usable — not whether it looks long enough from downwind.

For H130 Brazilian operations — wet Congonhas runways, short helipads, and high density altitude — landing performance can force a runway change, weight reduction, or diversion. You must discuss those limits in ICAO English with ATC.

This lesson prepares you for Part 2, Part 3, and Part 4 communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Landing Performance is the assessed ability of the aircraft to complete a safe landing within the available landing distance or pad, considering landing weight, density altitude, wind, surface condition, approach path, and required margins.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Desempenho de pouso / Landing Performance.

Capacidade avaliada da aeronave de completar um pouso seguro dentro da distância ou área disponível, considerando peso de pouso, altitude-densidade, vento, condição da superfície, trajetória de aproximação e margens exigidas.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When assessing landing performance, the crew should:

- calculate landing weight and density altitude before approach
- check runway length, condition, and wind component
- request a longer runway or into-wind runway if performance-limited
- go around or divert if landing distance is insufficient
- advise ATC of performance limitations early on approach

Landing Performance planning includes:

- maximum landing weight for the field and conditions
- wet or contaminated runway factors
- tailwind limits and headwind benefits
- approach angle and obstacle clearance on final
- helicopter-specific pad size and surrounding obstacles

**Important:** A wet runway or unexpected tailwind can invalidate a marginal landing calculation. Recalculate when ATIS or tower wind changes. If unable, go around and say why.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support landing performance by:

- assigning the most suitable runway for wind and length
- providing accurate wind and surface condition reports
- approving runway changes for performance reasons
- sequencing go-arounds without pressure to force a landing
- coordinating diversion when the destination is performance-limited

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Inbound to Santos Dumont. ATIS reports wet runway and a light tailwind on the active.

Your H130 landing performance at current weight is marginal for the wet surface with tailwind.

You call:

Santos Dumont Tower, ANAC123, performance limited for wet runway with tailwind, request runway opposite or delay for wind check.

Tower: ANAC123, roger, expect runway change, make orbit east of the field.

You respond:

ANAC123, orbiting east, expecting runway change.

You protected the landing by speaking before short final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Landing performance is decided before you configure for landing — not on short final under pressure.

Weight, wind, wet surface, pad size. If the numbers are marginal, change the plan: different runway, less fuel/weight next time, or divert.

In Brazil, Santos Dumont and Congonhas combine short distances with busy traffic. The H130 can land in small spaces — but obstacles, wind, and density altitude still rule.

If tower offers a downwind landing "for traffic," and your charts say no — say unable, request into-wind runway. That is safety language, not stubbornness.

Go around is always available. A performance-limited landing forced onto a wet short runway is not.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

Tower clears you to land with a reported tailwind on a wet runway. Your landing performance is insufficient. What do you say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would decline and state the reason: ANAC123, unable to land, performance limited wet runway with tailwind, request opposite runway or orbit for wind. If no suitable runway is available, I would request diversion. I would not attempt a landing the performance calculation does not support. Clear communication allows ATC to re-plan without surprise on short final.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Accepting a tailwind landing when charts show insufficient performance.  
  ✔ State unable, request into-wind runway or diversion.

- ❌ Ignoring wet runway factors in the landing calculation.  
  ✔ Apply wet/contaminated factors and recalculate when surface is reported wet.

- ❌ Waiting until short final to advise performance limitation.  
  ✔ Advise ATC early on approach or when ATIS is received.

- ❌ Treating a go-around as failure instead of a performance decision.  
  ✔ Go around and communicate: going around, performance / unable to land.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Santos Dumont Tower clears you to land runway two zero. Wind is tailwind at six knots, runway wet. Your calculation shows insufficient landing performance.

**Your first task**

What do you say?

**Your second task**

Tower says opposite runway is unavailable for ten minutes. What do you say?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

First task: ANAC123, unable to land runway two zero, performance limited wet runway with tailwind, request opposite runway. Second task: ANAC123, request orbit or diversion to alternate — I would not hold in a position that pressures an unsafe landing. If fuel and alternate allow, divert. Performance limits are not negotiable on short final.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Landing performance is a calculated yes or no.

Weight. Wind. Surface. Distance. Obstacles.

If the answer is no — say unable, request another runway, or divert. Go around without ego.

In the H130 over short Brazilian runways, the charts protect you only if you use them and speak them.

Calculate before final. Communicate early. Land only when the numbers work.

That is professional landing performance.
