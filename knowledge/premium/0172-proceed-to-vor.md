# Proceed to VOR

**Catalog ID:** 0172  
**Category:** Navigation  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Proceed to VOR — the ATC instruction directing a pilot to navigate directly to a specified VHF Omnidirectional Range station, used for routing, holding, approach sequencing, and traffic management.

VOR navigation remains a core competency in Brazilian airspace.

Brazil's domestic route structure still includes significant VOR-based airways and approach procedures. At airports like Congonhas, Curitiba, and Joinville, VOR approaches and VOR-based holds are regularly flown alongside RNAV procedures.

Proceed to VOR is more than a navigational instruction — it is a clearance. It assigns the VOR as your next navigational fix. All previous lateral instructions are superseded at the moment you begin tracking toward the VOR.

Understanding this instruction, reading it back correctly, and flying it with precision is fundamental ICAO navigation vocabulary.

## 📖 Meaning

Proceed to VOR is an ATC instruction directing the pilot to navigate directly to the specified VHF Omnidirectional Range station, tracking the VOR as the next navigational fix and superseding any previous lateral routing instructions.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When ATC instructs Proceed to VOR, the pilot should:

- identify the VOR named in the instruction
- select and identify the VOR station on the navigation receiver
- tune, identify, and confirm the VOR identifier (listen for the Morse code tone)
- navigate directly to the VOR using the CDI centered indication
- report reaching the VOR unless ATC specifies otherwise
- confirm any altitude or speed restrictions associated with the instruction

Proceed to VOR clearances commonly include:

- routing to a VOR for a published approach
- routing to a VOR for holding
- routing to a VOR fix as part of an en-route clearance
- transition from radar vectors to VOR navigation

**Important:** Always identify the VOR by listening to the Morse code identifier before using the station for navigation. A misidentified VOR leads to navigation in the wrong direction. The H130 has dual NAV receivers — use both for VOR identification when available.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

Controllers issue Proceed to VOR to:

- route aircraft to a published fix for sequencing or approach
- provide a simple, unambiguous lateral clearance
- transition traffic from radar vectors to published navigation
- route aircraft around weather or restricted areas via a VOR fix
- set up traffic for VOR or VOR/DME approach procedures

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are on an IFR flight from Navegantes to Congonhas in your H130.

São Paulo Approach transmits:

ANAC123, proceed to CGH VOR, expect VOR runway one seven approach.

You tune NAV 1 to CGH VOR frequency 115.9, center the course deviation indicator, and listen for the CGH Morse code tone — confirm the identifier.

You respond:

Proceeding to CGH VOR, expect VOR runway one seven approach, ANAC123.

You track inbound on the VOR, crossing overhead the station.

At station passage, you report:

ANAC123, CGH VOR, overhead.

Approach responds:

ANAC123, cleared VOR runway one seven approach.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Proceed to VOR is an instruction that comes with a responsibility.

That responsibility is identification.

Every VOR in Brazil has a Morse code identifier. You must listen to it.

Not guess it. Listen to it.

CGH — dash dot dash dash, dash dot dash dash dash dash, dot dot dot dot. You learn the identifiers for your regular routes.

VOR navigation is still fundamental in Brazil — RNAV and GPS backup every flight, but the VOR is the regulatory foundation.

When the H130's GPS is unreliable or RNAV is not approved for the procedure, VOR is your primary tool.

Practice identifying and tracking VORs in the H130 regularly — not just using GPS to fly the course.

Know the difference between the VOR needle centered on the OBS and a GPS to-waypoint indication.

They fly the same — but the identification requirement is different.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What does a pilot do after receiving a Proceed to VOR instruction, and why is VOR identification important?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After receiving a Proceed to VOR instruction, the pilot tunes the VOR frequency on the navigation receiver, listens for and confirms the VOR station's Morse code identifier to verify the correct station is selected, centers the course deviation indicator to navigate directly to the VOR, and readbacks the instruction. VOR identification is critical because tuning to an incorrect frequency or an adjacent station with a similar frequency could cause the pilot to track toward the wrong navaid. In Brazil, several VOR stations are in proximity along certain routes, and an unidentified VOR provides no assurance that the correct station is selected.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Tuning the VOR frequency without listening to the Morse code identifier.  
  ✔ Always confirm the identifier. Navigation on an unidentified VOR is unreliable.

- ❌ Reading back "proceeding direct" without including the VOR name.  
  ✔ Include the full VOR identifier: proceeding to CGH VOR, ANAC123.

- ❌ Forgetting to report reaching the VOR when ATC expects a position report.  
  ✔ Report station passage unless ATC has specifically stated otherwise.

- ❌ Confusing Proceed to VOR with a heading assignment.  
  ✔ Proceed to VOR is a fix clearance — navigate directly to the station, not on a specific heading.

## 🗣 Pronunciation Coaching

**Target Phrase:** Proceeding to CGH VOR, Maintaining Four Thousand Feet

**Pronunciation:** pro-SEED-ing too SEE-JEE-AYCH VEE-oh-AR, men-TAYN-ing for TOW-zund feet

**Word Stress**

- Proceeding → pro-SEED-ing
- CGH → spelled out: SEE-JEE-AYCH
- VOR → VEE-oh-AR (three syllables)
- Maintaining → men-TAYN-ing
- Four Thousand → for TOW-zund

Practice:

Proceeding to... CGH VOR... maintaining... four thousand feet...

Together:

ANAC123, proceeding to CGH VOR, maintaining four thousand feet.

VOR is spelled out: V-O-R. Do not say "vor" as one word in operational phraseology.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

Approach transmits: ANAC123, proceed to FLN VOR, maintain three thousand feet, QNH one zero one zero, expect VOR approach runway two eight.

You are currently at four thousand feet on a heading of two four zero.

**Your first task**

Read back the clearance completely.

**Your second task**

Describe step by step what you do in the cockpit to comply with the instruction, from receiving it to station passage.

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

Readback: Proceeding to FLN VOR, maintaining three thousand feet, QNH one zero one zero, expect VOR approach runway two eight, ANAC123. Cockpit actions: I tune NAV 1 to FLN VOR frequency, rotate the OBS to center the CDI on a to-the-station indication, and listen for the FLN Morse code identifier to confirm the correct station. I begin descending to three thousand feet and set QNH one zero one zero on the altimeter. I navigate directly toward FLN, monitoring the CDI centered on the inbound course. As the station passage indicator confirms I am overhead, I report: ANAC123, FLN VOR, overhead. I then await the approach clearance or further instructions.
