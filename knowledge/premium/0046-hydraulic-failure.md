# Hydraulic Failure

**Catalog ID:** 0046  
**Category:** Aircraft Systems  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers a critical aircraft systems emergency.

Hydraulic Failure means the hydraulic system has lost pressure or fluid — reducing or eliminating control assistance, landing gear operation, or other hydraulically powered functions.

In the H130, hydraulic systems support flight controls and other critical components.

A total hydraulic failure can make the aircraft difficult to control and may require an immediate precautionary landing.

You must aviate first, then assess severity, then communicate with the correct urgency call.

This lesson prepares you for ICAO Part 2 phraseology and Part 3 emergency communication.

## 📖 Meaning

Hydraulic Failure is the loss of hydraulic pressure or fluid, affecting aircraft systems that depend on hydraulic power.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Falha hidráulica.

Perda de pressão ou fluido hidráulico, afetando sistemas que dependem de energia hidráulica.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When Hydraulic Failure occurs, the pilot should:

- maintain aircraft control as the first priority
- identify which systems are affected
- perform appropriate emergency procedures from the flight manual
- declare Pan Pan or Mayday based on severity
- inform ATC of hydraulic failure, intentions, and position

Hydraulic Failure reporting commonly includes:

- hydraulic failure or hydraulic fluid leak
- hydraulic pressure decreasing
- request vectors to nearest suitable airport
- souls on board and fuel remaining when requested

**Important:** A slow pressure drop may warrant Pan Pan. A rapid leak or total loss affecting control may require Mayday. Aviate first — then communicate clearly with standard phraseology.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses It

ATC responds to Hydraulic Failure declarations to:

- clear traffic from the aircraft's route
- provide vectors to the nearest suitable airport
- coordinate priority landing and emergency services
- request souls on board and fuel remaining
- alert airport authorities and ground emergency crews

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising at two thousand five hundred feet in your H130 on a charter flight from Curitiba to Navegantes.

The hydraulic pressure gauge drops rapidly and a low-pressure warning activates.

You feel increased control forces but maintain stable flight.

When stable, you transmit:

Pan Pan Pan Pan, Navegantes Approach, ANAC123, hydraulic pressure decreasing, request vectors to Navegantes.

Approach clears traffic and provides direct routing.

You complete a precautionary landing and report safe on the ground.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Approach: ANAC123, roger Pan Pan, say nature of hydraulic problem and souls on board.
2. Approach: ANAC123, turn left heading two seven zero, descend at your discretion, Navegantes three zero miles.
3. Tower: ANAC123, all traffic cleared from your approach, runway two nine cleared to land.
4. Tower: ANAC123, emergency services standing by, wind two seven zero at one two knots.
5. Departure: ANAC123, radar contact, say fuel remaining and intentions.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Hydraulic failure is not always immediate catastrophe.

Sometimes it is a slow leak.

Sometimes it is sudden and total.

The sequence every professional pilot learns:

Aviate. Assess. Communicate.

Do not declare Mayday while the aircraft is unstable.

Establish control first.

Then choose your urgency call.

A slow pressure drop — Pan Pan.

A rapid leak or loss of control — Mayday.

Brazilian pilots sometimes say "hidráulica falhou" or "perdeu pressão" on frequency.

Use hydraulic failure or hydraulic pressure decreasing — ICAO standard terms.

In the H130, increased control forces mean you work harder on the cyclic and collective.

Train the assessment reflex so your radio call comes from a stable aircraft, not a panicked voice.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

What would you tell ATC if you noticed hydraulic pressure dropping during cruise?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

After securing the aircraft, I would declare Pan Pan Pan Pan, state my callsign, report hydraulic pressure decreasing or hydraulic failure, state my position, and request vectors to the nearest suitable airport. I would upgrade to Mayday if control becomes seriously degraded. I would provide souls on board and fuel remaining when ATC requests.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Declaring Mayday for a minor pressure fluctuation without assessing severity.  
  ✔ Use Pan Pan for urgency; reserve Mayday for distress when safe flight is in doubt.

- ❌ Saying "perdeu pressão hidráulica" instead of standard English.  
  ✔ Use hydraulic failure or hydraulic pressure decreasing.

- ❌ Forgetting to state intentions after the urgency declaration.  
  ✔ State request for vectors, immediate landing, or nearest suitable airport.

- ❌ Omitting souls on board and fuel when ATC asks.  
  ✔ Provide souls on board and fuel remaining promptly.

## 🎯 Captain Challenge

You are ten miles south of Congonhas at one thousand eight hundred feet when the hydraulic pressure warning activates in your H130.

**Your first task**

After establishing stable flight, transmit your urgency declaration to Approach.

**Your second task**

Answer this question:

Approach asks for the nature of the hydraulic problem. What do you report?

**Expected ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would report clearly: hydraulic pressure decreasing, ANAC123. I would describe whether control forces have increased and whether I can maintain safe flight. I would request vectors to Congonhas for precautionary landing.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Final Debrief

Hydraulic Failure demands calm assessment.

Aviate first.

Feel the controls.

Choose Pan Pan or Mayday based on severity.

State your intentions.

Answer ATC questions promptly.

The radio call does not restore hydraulic pressure — aircraft control does.

But a clear call helps ATC clear the sky and bring help to the ground.

Train the reflexes. Fly the aircraft. Speak like a professional.
