# Low-G Condition

**Catalog ID:** 0432  
**Category:** Helicopter Operations  
**Priority:** Critical  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 2 · Part 3

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Low-G Condition — a flight state in which rotor thrust and mast loading are reduced, potentially leading to mast bumping in certain rotor systems and loss of controlled flight if mishandled.

Low-G awareness is critical for H130 crews during abrupt pushovers, turbulence, and recovery from unusual attitudes in Brazilian mountain and coastal operations.

You must explain recognition, recovery, and emergency communication in ICAO English for Parts 2 and 3.

## 📖 Meaning

Low-G Condition is a helicopter flight state in which the rotor system produces little or no positive thrust loading on the mast, often following an abrupt nose-down cyclic input or turbulence, which can reduce control effectiveness and, in some designs, risk mast bumping or loss of attitude control.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Condição de baixo G / low-G.

Estado de voo de helicóptero em que o sistema de rotor produz pouca ou nenhuma carga positiva de empuxo no mastro, frequentemente após entrada abrupta de cíclico para baixo ou turbulência, o que pode reduzir a eficácia dos controles e, em alguns projetos, risco de mast bumping ou perda de controle de atitude.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When encountering or recovering from a low-G condition, the pilot should:

- recognize light or weightless cyclic feel and unusual roll tendency
- apply gentle aft cyclic to reload the rotor — avoid abrupt lateral cyclic
- restore positive G before using large roll inputs
- avoid unnecessary pushovers and abrupt attitude changes
- declare emergency if control is lost or structural concern exists

Low-G related reporting commonly includes:

- encountering severe turbulence, requesting altitude / routing change
- recovering from unusual attitude, request priority
- declaring emergency, control difficulty
- diverting, request vectors

**Important:** Recovery is reload the disc first. Lateral cyclic in low-G can be catastrophic in susceptible rotor systems. Know your AFM. Train recognition. Never demonstrate low-G intentionally outside approved training.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support low-G related events by:

- granting altitude or routing changes for turbulence avoidance
- providing priority handling after unusual attitude recovery
- coordinating emergency diversion and landing
- alerting other traffic to weather that caused the event
- maintaining SAR awareness if control is degraded

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

You are cruising in your H130 through mountain wave turbulence near the Serra do Mar.

An abrupt downdraft unloads the rotor. The aircraft feels light. A roll tendency begins.

You gently apply aft cyclic, reload the rotor, regain controlled flight, and transmit:

Encountering severe turbulence, request climb / deviation, remaining clear of terrain.

You do not chase the roll with aggressive lateral cyclic while unloaded.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Control: ANAC123, roger turbulence, climb and maintain six thousand, report conditions.
2. Control: ANAC123, deviation approved left of course, report when able.
3. Control: ANAC123, roger priority, say intentions.
4. Control: ANAC123, squawk seven seven zero zero if declaring, say souls and fuel when able.
5. Approach: ANAC123, cleared direct field, expect straight-in, emergency services advised.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Low-G is a silent trap.

The disc unloads. The world feels light. The wrong instinct is to slam lateral cyclic to stop a roll.

In systems susceptible to mast bumping, that instinct can end the flight. Reload with aft cyclic. Restore positive G. Then correct attitude.

The H130 AFM and training define your specific recovery. Know it cold. Part 3 loves: what do you do in low-G? Answer: gentle aft cyclic to reload, avoid abrupt lateral inputs, then recover attitude, communicate turbulence or emergency as required.

Brazilian mountain routes and coastal wave make this real. Smooth flying prevents most of it. Correct recovery saves the rest.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

You experience a low-G unload in turbulence. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would gently apply aft cyclic to reload the rotor and avoid large lateral cyclic until positive G is restored. After regaining control, I would transmit: encountering severe turbulence, request climb or deviation. If control remains in doubt, I would declare an emergency and request priority landing. Aviate first, then communicate.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Using abrupt lateral cyclic while the disc is unloaded.  
  ✔ Reload first. Then correct roll.

- ❌ Pushing over aggressively after a climb or flare.  
  ✔ Smooth attitude changes prevent low-G.

- ❌ Saying nothing after a serious upset.  
  ✔ Request deviation, priority, or declare as needed.

- ❌ Continuing into known severe mountain wave without options.  
  ✔ Avoid, climb, or divert early.
