# Somatogravic Illusion

**Catalog ID:** 0479  
**Category:** Human Factors  
**Priority:** High  
**ICAO Parts:** Part 1 · Part 2 · Part 4

## ✈ Mission Brief

Today's lesson covers Somatogravic Illusion — a vestibular false sensation of pitch caused by linear acceleration or deceleration, commonly perceived as a nose-up attitude during acceleration and a nose-down attitude during deceleration.

Somatogravic illusion is especially dangerous on night takeoff and go-around. Acceleration feels like climbing. The pilot pushes forward. The aircraft descends or fails to climb while the body insists it is pitched up.

In H130 operations — accelerating from hover, departing dark pads, or executing a night go-around — somatogravic illusion can put the aircraft into the surface within seconds. Instrument pitch and power discipline are the defense.

Understanding how to recognize and communicate somatogravic illusion in ICAO-standard English supports Part 1, Part 2, and Part 4 proficiency.

## 📖 Meaning

Somatogravic Illusion is a false perception of pitch attitude produced by linear acceleration acting on the otolith organs, typically creating a sensation of pitching up during acceleration and pitching down during deceleration in the absence of reliable visual references.

## 🇧🇷 Portuguese Meaning

Ilusão somatogravic / Somatogravic Illusion.

Falsa percepção de atitude de arfagem produzida por aceleração linear atuando nos órgãos otolíticos, tipicamente criando sensação de cabrar durante a aceleração e de picada durante a desaceleração na ausência de referências visuais confiáveis.

## 🛩 Operational Meaning

When somatogravic illusion may occur, the crew should:

- set and hold a target pitch attitude on instruments during acceleration
- resist the urge to push forward based on a false nose-up sensation
- cross-check airspeed, altitude, and vertical speed during night go-around
- brief somatogravic risk before night departures over water or dark terrain
- advise ATC if an unexpected descent develops during climb or go-around

Somatogravic defenses include:

- predetermined pitch and power targets for takeoff and go-around
- flying pilot calls: "pitch attitude" confirmed on instruments
- avoiding visual lean on a featureless night horizon
- using flight director pitch cues when available

**Important:** On a dark night go-around, acceleration can feel like a steep climb. If you push to "correct" that feeling, you may fly into the ground. Pitch to the target. Confirm climb on the instruments.

## 🎯 Why ATC Uses This Concept

Controllers support crews during high-risk acceleration phases by:

- issuing early climb clearances with simple altitude targets
- querying unexpected descent after takeoff or go-around
- providing priority and vectors when a crew reports disorientation
- keeping instructions short during night missed approaches
- coordinating terrain-safe altitudes immediately

## 🌍 Real Operational Context

Night go-around from a coastal approach. You add power. Your body screams nose-high. You almost push.

Your first officer calls: "Pitch — climb attitude on instruments."

Altitude begins to increase. You transmit:

ANAC123, going around, climbing three thousand, somatogravic illusion recognized, maintaining instrument pitch.

Approach: ANAC123, climb and maintain three thousand, fly runway heading.

You respond:

ANAC123, climbing three thousand, runway heading.

The illusion was strong. The instruments were stronger.

## 📡 Real ATC Phraseology

1. Tower: ANAC123, go around, climb and maintain three thousand.
2. Approach: ANAC123, confirm climbing, you appear level.
3. Radar: ANAC123, climb immediately, low altitude alert.
4. Tower: ANAC123, fly runway heading, contact approach.
5. Approach: ANAC123, maintain three thousand, say intentions when able.

## 👨‍✈️ Captain Delta Teaching

Somatogravic illusion is the night go-around trap.

Power on. Acceleration. False climb. Push. Impact.

In the H130, memorize pitch targets for go-around and departure. Do not invent pitch from your inner ear on a black night.

If it feels steep and the altimeter is not rising, you are not climbing — you are being lied to.

## ❓ Common ICAO Speaking Question

During a night go-around you feel strongly nose-up. What do you do and say?

**Good ICAO Level 4 Answer**

I would set the briefed go-around pitch and power on instruments, confirm a positive climb on the altimeter and vertical speed, and resist pushing on the false sensation. I would report: ANAC123, going around, climbing three thousand. If altitude were not increasing, I would correct pitch immediately and advise ATC of the low-altitude recovery.

## 🇧🇷 Common Brazilian Mistakes

- ❌ Pushing forward on a false climb sensation during acceleration.  
  ✔ Hold target pitch and verify climb on instruments.

- ❌ Using a featureless night horizon as the pitch reference.  
  ✔ Use the attitude indicator as primary pitch reference.

- ❌ Delaying the go-around pitch call between pilots.  
  ✔ Cross-check pitch attitude out loud during night go-around.

- ❌ Ignoring a low-altitude alert while "feeling" high.  
  ✔ Believe the instruments and climb immediately.
